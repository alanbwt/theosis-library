The Evidence

Every major primary source statement on whether divinity can be shared with humanity. Original language, English translation, manuscript source. Organized by position.

For: Divinity Can Be Shared

For theosis
καὶ ὁ λόγος σὰρξ ἐγένετο
“And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us.”
John (attributed) c. 90–110 AD Gospel of John 1:14

God entered humanity. If divinity can become flesh, the boundary between divine and human is permeable. The orthodox read this as unique to Christ; the Gnostics read it as paradigmatic.

For theosis
ἔδωκεν αὐτοῖς ἐξουσίαν τέκνα θεοῦ γενέσθαι
“He gave them the right to become children of God.”
John (attributed) c. 90–110 AD Gospel of John 1:12

Divine sonship is available to those who receive the Logos. The Gnostics read this as awakening the divine spark; the orthodox read it as adoption by grace.

ἐγὼ εἶπα θεοί ἐστε
“I said, you are gods.”
John (attributed), quoting Psalm 82:6 c. 90–110 AD Gospel of John 10:34

Jesus quotes scripture calling human beings 'gods.' The most direct claim that divinity is not exclusive. Cited by every major theosis theologian from Clement to Palamas.

“That they all may be one, just as you, Father, are in me and I in you, that they also may be in us.”

Christ prays that the mutual indwelling between Father and Son extend to all believers. The most explicit theosis prayer in the New Testament.

Peter (attributed) c. 100–120 AD 2 Peter 1:4

The most explicit theosis statement in the New Testament. Koinonoi physeos = sharers in the nature (ontological, not merely moral). The proof-text for Eastern Orthodox divinization.

For theosis
Verbum Dei, Dominus noster Iesus Christus, qui propter immensam suam dilectionem factus est quod sumus nos, uti nos perficeret esse quod est ipse.
“The Word of God, our Lord Jesus Christ, who through his transcendent love became what we are, that he might bring us to be what he himself is.”
Irenaeus of Lyon c. 180 AD Against Heresies III.19.1

The earliest systematic theosis formula. More radical than Athanasius: not 'God' in the abstract but 'what he himself is.' The verb perficeret (might perfect/complete) implies a process.

For theosis
Αὐτὸς γὰρ ἐνηνθρώπησεν, ἵνα ἡμεῖς θεοποιηθῶμεν
“He became man that we might be made God.”
Athanasius of Alexandria c. 335 AD On the Incarnation, ch. 54

The most famous sentence in the history of the theosis doctrine. The verb theopoiethomen (might be made god) is active and ontological, not metaphorical.

misit scintillam, quae correxit hominem et suscitauit et fecit eum uiuere
“He sent a spark, which corrected man and raised him up and made him live.”
Filastrius of Brescia (reporting Saturninus) c. 385 AD Diversarum Hereseon Liber, ch. 31

The Gnostic divine spark (scintilla): a fragment of divine light sent into the body made by angels. The spark alone is saved; all else is abandoned. The most concise summary of Gnostic soteriology.

“He holds that the soul alone is saved, but that the body of man is not saved.”
Filastrius of Brescia (reporting Valentinus) c. 385 AD Diversarum Hereseon Liber, ch. 38

The Valentinian position: only the spiritual element in humanity participates in the divine. The body, made by the Demiurge, is excluded. A radical theosis limited to the soul.

Against: Divinity Is Exclusive

Against theosis
μονογενὴς θεὸς ὁ ὢν εἰς τὸν κόλπον τοῦ πατρὸς
“The only-begotten God, the one who is in the bosom of the Father, he has made him known.”
John (attributed) c. 90–110 AD Gospel of John 1:18 (Sinaiticus reading)

Sinaiticus reads 'only-begotten God' (monogenes theos), not 'only-begotten Son.' If Christ is the unique God, divinity may be his exclusive property. Key textual variant.

Against theosis
panānumma Ūta-napištim amilu / inanna Ūta-napištim u sinništassu lū ēmūū kīma ilīni anaḥnīti
“Previously Utnapishtim was a mortal man. Now let Utnapishtim and his wife become like the gods, like us.”
Sin-leqi-unninni (attributed) c. 1200 BCE Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet XI, lines 241–245

The Mesopotamian answer: divinity (immortality) was granted once, to one man, and never again. Gilgamesh is refused. The oldest argument that the divine-human boundary is permanent.

The second Mesopotamian argument against theosis: Adapa had the chance at immortality and was tricked into refusing it. Humanity's exclusion from divinity as cosmic accident, not divine decree.

Ambiguous: Claimed by Both Sides

Ambiguous
ἐγὼ καὶ ὁ πατὴρ ἕν ἐσμεν
“I and the Father are one.”
John (attributed) c. 90–110 AD Gospel of John 10:30

The neuter 'one' (hen, not heis) means 'one thing,' not 'one person.' Unity of substance or merely of will? The Arians, Nicenes, and Gnostics all claimed this verse.

Ambiguous
σὺ εἶ ὁ υἱός μου ὁ ἀγαπητός · ἐν σοὶ εὐδόκησα
“You are my beloved Son; in you I am well pleased.”
Mark (attributed) c. 65–75 AD Gospel of Mark 1:11

The divine voice at the baptism. Addressed to Jesus alone ('You are'), not declared publicly ('This is'). The adoptionists read this as the moment Christ received divinity; the orthodox read it as confirmation of pre-existing sonship.

Foundational
ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος · καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν · καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος
“In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.”
John (attributed) c. 90–110 AD Gospel of John 1:1

Establishes the divinity of the Logos. The Word is not a lesser emanation but is itself God. Foundation of both orthodox Christology and Gnostic Logos theology.
