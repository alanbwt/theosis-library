Theosis in 10 Texts

Can humanity participate in the divine nature? The complete arc of the argument, from ancient Mesopotamia to the Nicene era, in 10 primary source passages. Each links to the full text with manuscript scans.

  1. 1
    c. 1200 BCE · Akkadian

    Gilgamesh XI: "Let Utnapishtim become like the gods"

    The oldest text in the debate. Enlil grants one mortal immortality, once, never again. Gilgamesh is refused. The Mesopotamian answer: the divine-human boundary is permanent.

  2. 2
    c. 65 AD · Greek · Mark

    Mark 1:9-11: The heavens torn open

    At the baptism, the barrier between divine and human is ripped apart (schizo). The Spirit descends into Jesus. The moment the divine enters the human.

  3. 3
    c. 80 AD · Greek · Matthew

    Matthew 5:48: "Be perfect as your Father is perfect"

    The command to become teleios: complete, having reached the telos. Not a moral aspiration but an ontological claim about human possibility.

  4. 4
    c. 90 AD · Greek · John

    John 1:14: "The Word became flesh"

    If divinity can become flesh, the boundary is permeable. The Prologue is the foundation: God entered humanity. The question is whether it goes both ways.

  5. 5
    c. 90 AD · Greek · John

    John 10:34: "I said, you are gods"

    Jesus quotes Psalm 82 calling human beings gods. The most direct scriptural claim that divinity is not exclusive.

  6. 6
    c. 90 AD · Greek · John

    John 17:21: "That they may be one, as we are one"

    Christ prays that the Father-Son unity extend to all believers. Theosis universalized.

  7. 7
    c. 100 AD · Greek

    2 Peter 1:4: "Partakers of the divine nature"

    The most explicit theosis statement in the New Testament. Koinonoi physeos = sharers in the nature. Ontological, not metaphorical.

  8. 8
    c. 180 AD · Latin

    Irenaeus: "He became what we are"

    The first systematic theosis formula. Not "God" in the abstract but "what he himself is." The theological ancestor of all later formulations.

  9. 9
    c. 335 AD · Greek

    Athanasius: "He became man that we might become God"

    The most famous sentence in the history of the doctrine. The verb theopoiethomen: "be made god." Active, ontological, not metaphorical.

  10. 10
    c. 385 AD · Latin · First English translation

    Filastrius: The divine spark (scintilla)

    The Gnostic answer: a fragment of divine light trapped in the body made by angels. The spark is saved; everything else is abandoned. The radical alternative.
