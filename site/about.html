About

The Project

Theosis Library is a primary source database for one of the most consequential questions in the history of Western thought: who possesses the divine? Is divinity the exclusive property of Christ, or does a spark of the divine reside within every human being?

The earliest Christian writers debated this question with extraordinary intensity. The Church Fathers, the Gnostic teachers they opposed, and the councils that attempted to settle the matter left behind a vast literature in Greek and Latin. Most of it has never been translated into English. Much of what has been translated exists only in outdated 19th-century prose that obscures as much as it reveals.

Theosis Library brings these texts together in one place, with three layers of verification for every passage: scans of the original manuscripts or critical editions, the clean original text in Greek or Latin, and a modern English translation with scholarly annotations. Every rendering can be checked against the source. Every passage is citable and shareable.

Methodology

Each translation is produced through a rigorous process:

  1. Source identification. Texts are identified from the Patrologia Graeca, Patrologia Latina, critical editions, and other scholarly sources. Only texts for which manuscript scans or critical edition scans are available are included in the library.
  2. AI-assisted draft. An initial draft translation is generated from the original Greek or Latin using Claude by Anthropic. The AI is given specific instructions about theological terminology, structural fidelity to the original, and the identification of ambiguous or disputed passages.
  3. Review against the original. The draft is reviewed and corrected against the Latin text (and Greek where available), with particular attention to theological precision and ambiguous passages. AI-generated translations of patristic texts contain errors, and every passage is checked against the original.
  4. Scholarly annotation. Notes are added on key terms and why they were rendered as they were, ambiguous passages and how they were resolved, variant readings between manuscripts, scriptural allusions, connections to other texts in the library, and theological significance.
  5. Publication with full sources. The translation is published alongside scans of the source edition and the clean original text, with links to the critical edition, so that any reader can verify the work independently. Transparency is not optional. It is the foundation of the project.

Why This Matters

The Patrologia Graeca alone contains 161 volumes of Greek Christian writing spanning 1,400 years, from the Apostolic Fathers through the fall of Constantinople. The Patrologia Latina runs to 221 volumes. The Nag Hammadi library, discovered in 1945, revealed dozens of Gnostic texts that had been thought lost for centuries. The earliest Gospel manuscripts preserve readings that differ from later standardised versions.

These are not abstract theological curiosities. They are the record of people wrestling with the deepest questions human beings can ask. What is the relationship between the human and the divine? Can a human being participate in the nature of God? The Gnostic teachers who were ultimately declared heretics often gave the most radical answers, and the Church Fathers who described Gnostic beliefs in the process of refuting them inadvertently preserved ideas whose original texts are largely lost.

The primary sources exist. They are sitting in libraries and digital archives, locked away in Greek and Latin, accessible to a diminishing number of specialists. Theosis Library makes them available to anyone.
