2 Corinthians 5:17-21: New Creation

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Second Corinthians 5:17–21 announces the most radical ontological claim in the Pauline corpus: “If anyone is in Christ—new creation.” The Greek has no verb; it is a declaration, not a description. The old has passed away; the new has come. Paul is not speaking of moral improvement or psychological renewal but of a new order of being. The term kainē ktisis (καινὴ κτίσις) echoes the creation account of Genesis and the prophetic promises of Isaiah 65:17, where God declares, “I create new heavens and a new earth.”

The passage culminates in the “great exchange” of verse 21: God made the sinless one to be sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God. This is not forensic fiction—Paul says we become (γενώμεθα) the righteousness of God. The exchange is real, ontological, and mutual. What Christ became for us (sin), we become in him (divine righteousness). Athanasius would later crystallize this logic: “He became what we are, so that we might become what he is.”

English

Therefore we are ambassadors on behalf of Christ, as though God were making his appeal through us. We implore you on behalf of Christ: be reconciled to God. The one who knew no sin, he made to be sin on our behalf, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §5:17: The phrase kainē ktisis (καινὴ κτίσις) lacks a verb in the Greek, creating a startling verbless declaration. Most translations supply “he is” (“he is a new creation”), but the absence of the verb is deliberate. Paul is not describing a predicate; he is announcing a new state of affairs. The term ktisis can mean either “creation” or “creature”; the cosmic sense (“new creation”) is preferable, as Paul envisions not just individual transformation but a new order of reality.
  2. §5:21: This verse contains the “great exchange” formula that became the engine of patristic soteriology. The symmetry is precise: Christ was made sin (ἁμαρτίαν ἐποίησεν), we become righteousness (δικαιοσύνη θεοῦ γενώμεθα). The verb “become” (γενώμεθα) resists any merely forensic or declarative reading. Irenaeus, Athanasius, and the Cappadocians all build their exchange christology on this foundation.
