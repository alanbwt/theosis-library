Testament of Levi 18: The New Priest

Anonymous (Enochic), c. 3rd c. BCE

Introduction

The Testament of Levi is part of the Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs, a Jewish text with likely Christian interpolations, composed in Greek between the second century BCE and the second century CE. Each patriarch gives a deathbed speech to his sons. Levi’s testament is the longest and most theologically ambitious, as befits the ancestor of the priestly line.

Chapter 18 is the climactic prophecy: Levi foresees a “new priest” whom God will raise up in the last days. This figure will open the gates of paradise, remove the threatening sword, give the saints to eat from the tree of life, bind Beliar, and grant his children the authority to trample evil spirits. Whether this new priest is a messianic figure, a reference to the Qumran Teacher of Righteousness, or a Christian interpolation pointing to Christ is hotly debated. What is beyond dispute is that the passage describes a human being who exercises divine prerogatives—opening paradise, bestowing immortality, defeating cosmic evil. This is deification language, applied to a priestly figure.

Translator's Notes

  1. §18:5-7: The heavens opening, the Father’s voice, and the spirit descending “in the water” clearly echo the Synoptic accounts of Jesus’ baptism (Mark 1:10-11 and parallels). Most scholars consider this section a Christian interpolation into an originally Jewish text. However, the core prophecy of a new priest may be genuinely pre-Christian, reflecting the same messianic priestly expectations found at Qumran.
  2. §18:10-11: The opening of paradise and the restoration of the tree of life reverse the expulsion from Eden in Genesis 3:24. This is a theme found across Second Temple literature: the eschaton is the restoration of Eden. The fact that a human priest performs these acts—opening paradise, removing the angelic sword, feeding the saints from the tree of life—places him in a role that is functionally divine.
  3. §18:12-14: The binding of Beliar (the chief evil spirit) echoes the binding of Azazel in 1 Enoch and anticipates the binding of Satan in Revelation 20. The power given to “children” to tread upon evil spirits echoes Luke 10:19. The final vision of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob exulting alongside the saints suggests a final communion of all the righteous—a corporate theosis.
