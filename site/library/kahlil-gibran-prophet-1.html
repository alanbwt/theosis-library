Introduction

Kahlil Gibran’s The Prophet (1923) is a collection of poetic essays on love, work, freedom, and death, spoken by the sage Almustafa as he prepares to leave the city of Orphalese. It has been translated into over 100 languages.

§On Love · Gibran, The Prophet, ‘On Love’
Manuscript
Greek

[Original English text as published, Alfred A. Knopf, 1923]

English

When love beckons to you, follow him, though his ways are hard and steep. And when his wings enfold you yield to him, though the sword hidden among his pinions may wound you. And when he speaks to you believe in him, though his voice may shatter your dreams as the north wind lays waste the garden. For even as love crowns you so shall he crucify you.

Translator's Notes

  1. §On Love: Gibran synthesizes Sufi mysticism, Nietzschean vitalism, and Christian imagery into a universalist spiritual philosophy. His Maronite Christian-Lebanese background, combined with immersion in American Transcendentalism, makes The Prophet a genuinely cross-cultural work of spiritual literature.
