Orphic Hymn to Nature: All-Parent, Ancient and Divine

Orphic tradition, c. 6th c. BCE-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The Orphic Hymn to Nature (Hymn 10 in the standard collection) belongs to a corpus of 87 hymns attributed to the mythical poet Orpheus, composed in Greek hexameter during the late Hellenistic or early Roman Imperial period (2nd–3rd century CE). The hymns were used in ritual contexts by Orphic and Neoplatonic religious communities. The Hymn to Nature (Physis, Φύσις) addresses the goddess as the all-pervading, self-generating principle of the cosmos—“all-parent, ancient and divine.”

The theological vision is of a divinity that is not separate from the natural world but identical with it. Nature is not the creation of an external god but is herself divine: self-born, self-moving, containing all things within herself. For the theosis tradition, the Orphic vision represents the Greek mystical insight that the sacred pervades the material world. The contempt for matter that characterises some Gnostic and Platonic traditions is absent here; instead, matter itself is the body of the divine.

§Lines 1–7 · Orphic Hymns 10.1–7 (Quandt edition)
Manuscript
Greek

Ὤ Φύσι, παμμήτειρα, πολύτεχνε, θεία, / πρεσβείρα, πολύπλαγκτε δαῖμον, ἀγλαόμορφε / πανδαμάτειρα, ἀδάμαστε, κυβερνήτειρα, / πανεπόπτειρα, φαεσφόρε, παγκράτειρα.

English

O Nature, all-parent, ancient and divine, O much-contriving mother, queenly, sublime! You who tame all things, untamed yourself—all-seeing, all-bright, all-powerful. Endless, first-born, ancient, night-wandering, rich in skill—you bring all things to completion and are yourself all things.

§Lines 8–14 · Orphic Hymns 10.8–14
Manuscript
Greek

αὐτοπάτειρα, ἄπατορ, παντερπής, / μακαίρα, πολυαυξής, μεγαλώνυμε καρποτόκε.

English

Self-fathered, without father, all-delightful, blessed, rich in growth, bearing fruit of great renown. Friend of all, giver of increase, mature, sovereign. Tireless, you nurture all things. Wise father, nourishing mother, both maiden and nurse of all. Swift in bringing forth, blessed seed-giver, mover of seasons, craftswoman of many forms.

§Lines 15–21 · Orphic Hymns 10.15–21
Manuscript
Greek

παντρόφε, παντελές, βασίλεια / πάντων, ἄφθιτε, ἀεικίνητε, πανρεύστε.

English

All-nourishing, all-accomplished, you are the queen of all. Imperishable, ever-turning, all-flowing, circling in endlessly changing motion. Blessed goddess, rich in increase, hear our prayers. Grant us growth, health, and seasons of gentle peace. Send to us the end that is forever joyful.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 1–7: The epithet pammeiteira (“all-parent”) establishes Nature not as one goddess among many but as the generative principle underlying all divine and physical reality. The Orphic Physis is closer to the Stoic concept of natura naturans (nature as creative process) than to the Platonic model of a transcendent craftsman. The emphasis on Nature as both “taming all things” and “untamed herself” captures the paradox of a power that orders everything while itself being beyond all ordering.
  2. §Lines 8–14: The description of Nature as “self-fathered, without father” (αὐτοπάτειρα, ἄπατορ) echoes the self-origination theme found in the Egyptian Hymn to Amun-Re and in the Hermetic tradition. The Orphic hymns move freely between masculine and feminine attributes—Nature is father and mother, maiden and nurse—suggesting a divinity that transcends gender categories, much as the Upanishadic Brahman transcends all dualities.
