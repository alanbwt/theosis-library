Introduction

Prudentius’s Contra Symmachum (c. 402 CE) is a verse refutation of Symmachus’s plea for the Altar of Victory. Prudentius argues that Rome’s greatness was providentially ordained to prepare the world for Christ, not earned by pagan sacrifice.

§Book II, Lines 578–596 · Prudentius, Contra Symmachum II.578–596
Manuscript
Greek

Quaeris iter? Christus via est. Hoc ipse probavit, monstravitque viam: “Via sum” dixit. Ille viam patefecit: eas qua monstrat eundum. Quid per inaccessa erras? Patet area lata.

English

Do you ask what road to take? Christ is the road. He himself said so, he pointed the way: “I am the Way.” He opened the path; follow the revealed road. Why do you wander through trackless wilds? The path lies open.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Book II, Lines 578–596: Prudentius directly answers Symmachus’s “one cannot arrive by one road alone” with Christ’s own claim from John 14:6. The exchange between Symmachus and his Christian opponents (Ambrose, Prudentius) is the defining literary confrontation between pagan pluralism and Christian exclusivism.
