Introduction

The Electra of Sophocles (c. 410 BCE) tells of Electra’s long wait for her brother Orestes to return and avenge their father Agamemnon’s murder by their mother Clytemnestra. The play explores justice, grief, and the moral cost of vengeance.

§Lines 86–102 · Sophocles, Electra
Manuscript
Greek

ὦ φάος ἁγνὸν καὶ γῆς ἰσόμοιρ' ἀήρ

English

O holy light and air, equal sharer of the earth, how many dirges have you heard from me, how many blows upon my bloodied breast, when dark night withdraws! And my wretched bed in this hateful house knows well my all-night vigils, how I bewail my unhappy father.

§Lines 1126–1142 · Sophocles, Electra
Manuscript
Greek

ὦ φιλτάτου μνημεῖον ἀνθρώπων ἐμοὶ ψυχῆς

English

O relic of the dearest of all souls to me, O last remaining hope of Orestes’ life! How differently I sent you forth from how I receive you back! For now I hold you as nothing in my hands, but when I sent you from this house, child, you were radiant.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 1126–1142: Electra’s lament over what she believes is Orestes’s funeral urn is one of the most powerful speeches in Greek tragedy. She does not yet know that the living Orestes stands before her. The dramatic irony—grief over what is in fact present—mirrors the human condition as the Platonists understood it: mourning what we already possess but fail to recognize.
