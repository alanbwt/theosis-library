Introduction

The Lamentations of Isis and Nephthys (c. 4th century BCE, Papyrus Bremner-Rhind) is a liturgical text performed by two women impersonating the goddesses at the Osiris festival. They mourn the dead god and call him back to life through the power of grief.

Greek

jmj r pr=k, jmj r pr=k! pā nfr-ḥr, jmj r pr=k, mā=k wj. jnk sn.t=k mr.t(j)=k

English

Come to your house, come to your house! O Beautiful Youth, come to your house, that you may see me. I am your sister whom you love. You shall not be far from me. Gods and men turn their faces toward you and weep for you together.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Opening lament: The ritual lament performed by living women in the roles of Isis and Nephthys is one of the earliest examples of liturgical drama. The emotional intensity—“come to your house, that you may see me”—anticipates later traditions of sacred grief, from the Christian Stabat Mater to Shi’a mourning for Husayn.
