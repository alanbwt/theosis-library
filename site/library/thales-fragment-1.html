Introduction

Thales of Miletus (c. 624–546 BCE), traditionally regarded as the first philosopher, proposed that water is the principle (arche) of all things. No direct writings survive; this testimony comes from Aristotle’s reconstruction.

§Testimony · Aristotle, Metaphysics 983b6–27 (DK 11 A12)
Manuscript
Greek

Θαλῆς μὲν ὁ τῆς τοιαύτης ἀρχηγὸς φιλοσοφίας ὕδωρ φησὶν εἶναι τὴν ἀρχήν

English

Thales, the founder of this kind of philosophy, says the principle is water—for which reason he also declared that the earth rests on water—perhaps taking this supposition from seeing that the nourishment of all things is moist, and that heat itself is generated from moisture and lives by it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Testimony: Aristotle’s report is the primary source for Thales’s cosmology. The claim that “all things are full of gods” (DK 11 A22) is also attributed to Thales, suggesting a form of hylozoism—the view that matter itself is alive.
