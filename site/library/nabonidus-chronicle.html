The Nabonidus Chronicle: The Fall of Babylon to Cyrus

        
The Nabonidus Chronicle: The Fall of Babylon to Cyrus

        
Babylonian Astronomers, c. 7th century BCE

        
British Museum (BM 35382)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Nabonidus Chronicle provides the Babylonian perspective on one of the most pivotal events in the ancient world: the fall of Babylon to Cyrus the Great in October 539 BCE. The chronicle confirms the biblical account in Daniel 5, where Belshazzar (acting regent for the absent Nabonidus) sees the writing on the wall on the night the city falls.
Most remarkably, the chronicle states that Babylon fell 'without battle' — Cyrus' army simply entered the city. This peaceful conquest explains both Cyrus' ability to issue his famous decree of religious tolerance (the Cyrus Cylinder) and the Jewish community's warm view of Cyrus as God's 'anointed' (Isaiah 45:1).

Translation

        
        

          §1 · nabonidus-chronicle
          
In the month of Tashritu, when Cyrus attacked the army of Akkad in Opis on the Tigris, the inhabitants of Akkad revolted. On the fourteenth day, Sippar was seized without battle. Nabonidus fled. On the sixteenth day, Gobryas, the governor of Gutium, and the army of Cyrus entered Babylon without battle. Afterwards Nabonidus was arrested in Babylon when he returned. In the month of Arahshamnu, the third day, Cyrus entered Babylon. Green twigs were spread in front of him. The state of peace was imposed upon the city. Cyrus sent greetings to all of Babylon.
