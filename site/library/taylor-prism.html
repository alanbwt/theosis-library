The Taylor Prism: Sennacherib Besieges Jerusalem

        
The Taylor Prism: Sennacherib Besieges Jerusalem

        
Babylonian Astronomers, c. 7th century BCE

        
British Museum (BM 91032)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Taylor Prism, a six-sided clay cylinder inscribed with the annals of the Assyrian king Sennacherib, provides an independent account of one of the most dramatic events in the Hebrew Bible: the Assyrian siege of Jerusalem in 701 BCE. The biblical account (2 Kings 18-19, Isaiah 36-37) describes how Sennacherib's army was miraculously destroyed by the angel of the Lord, saving the city.
The Assyrian account is remarkable for what it does and does not say. It confirms the siege and Hezekiah's defiance. It boasts of conquering 46 other Judean cities. But unlike every other Assyrian campaign account, it conspicuously fails to claim that Jerusalem fell. Sennacherib says only that he shut Hezekiah up 'like a bird in a cage' — the earliest known example of spin in military propaganda.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · taylor-prism
          
As for Hezekiah the Judahite, who did not submit to my yoke: I besieged and took forty-six of his strong, walled cities, by means of well-stamped earth ramps, battering-rams, infantry attacks, mines, breaches, and siege machines. I drove out of them 200,150 people, young and old, male and female, together with horses, mules, donkeys, camels, big and small cattle beyond counting, and considered them as spoil. Hezekiah himself I shut up in Jerusalem, his royal city, like a bird in a cage. I erected fortifications against him. Anyone coming out of his city gate I turned back. His towns which I had plundered I cut off from his land.
