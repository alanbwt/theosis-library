Gospel of Thomas, Saying 108: Become Like Me

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

Saying 108 of the Gospel of Thomas makes a claim so direct it borders on blasphemy: “Whoever drinks from my mouth will become like me. I myself shall become that person, and the hidden things will be revealed to that one.” This is not imitation or discipleship in any ordinary sense. It is ontological identification—the disciple becomes the teacher, and the teacher becomes the disciple. The boundary between the divine revealer and the human recipient dissolves entirely.

This saying has no canonical parallel, though it resonates with John 7:37 (“If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink”) and the Pauline formula “it is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me” (Galatians 2:20). The Coptic text survives in Nag Hammadi Codex II. For the theosis tradition, this is among the most radical statements in any early text: not that the human becomes godlike, but that the boundary between human and divine collapses from both sides simultaneously.

English

Jesus said, “Whoever drinks from my mouth will become like me. I myself shall become that person, and the hidden things will be revealed to that one.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §108: The metaphor of “drinking from my mouth” implies receiving teaching as a living substance—not text but transmission. The image recalls Wisdom literature where personified Wisdom invites seekers to eat her bread and drink her wine (Proverbs 9:5, Sirach 24:21). But Thomas pushes beyond Wisdom’s invitation: the drinker does not merely gain wisdom but becomes the source of wisdom. The mutual identification (“I myself shall become that person”) has no real parallel in Jewish or Hellenistic wisdom traditions. It anticipates—or perhaps inspired—the most extreme claims of the later Christian mystical tradition, from Meister Eckhart’s “The eye with which I see God is the same eye with which God sees me” to the Orthodox doctrine of theosis as mutual indwelling (perichoresis).
