Annals I.1-4: Republic to Monarchy in Disguise

Tacitus, c. 56-120 AD

Introduction

The opening chapters of the Annals are Tacitus’s devastating account of how the Roman Republic died—not with a dramatic overthrow but with a quiet surrender. Augustus did not abolish republican institutions; he hollowed them out. The Senate still met, the consuls still held office, the laws still bore republican names. But real power had passed irrevocably to one man, and everyone knew it. Tacitus writes with the cold fury of a historian who sees tyranny disguised as continuity.

For the governance tradition, Tacitus represents the essential counterpoint to Cicero’s optimism about the mixed constitution. Cicero argued that Rome’s blended government was the most stable form; Tacitus shows what happened to it. The mixed constitution did not evolve into monarchy through constitutional crisis—it was absorbed. Augustus’s genius was to leave the forms standing while draining them of content, so that Romans could tell themselves nothing fundamental had changed. Tacitus’s opening is the greatest analysis of how republics die: not when tyrants seize power by force, but when free citizens prefer security to liberty.

§I.1 (The End of Republican History) · Tacitus, Annals I.1
Manuscript
Latin

Urbem Romam a principio reges habuere; libertatem et consulatum L. Brutus instituit. Dictaturae ad tempus sumebantur; neque decemviralis potestas ultra biennium, neque tribunorum militum consulare ius diu valuit. Non Cinnae, non Sullae longa dominatio; et Pompei Crassique potentia cito in Caesarem, Lepidi atque Antonii arma in Augustum cessere, qui cuncta discordiis civilibus fessa nomine principis sub imperium accepit.

English

Rome at the beginning was ruled by kings. Liberty and the consulship were established by Lucius Brutus. Dictatorships were held for a season; the power of the Ten did not last beyond two years, nor was the consular authority of the military tribunes of long duration. The despotism of Cinna was brief, and that of Sulla as well. The power of Pompey and of Crassus soon gave way to Caesar, and the armed might of Lepidus and Antony to Augustus, who, when the world was wearied by civil strife, subjected it to dominion under the title of “Prince.”

§I.2 (The Consolidation of Power) · Tacitus, Annals I.2
Manuscript
Latin

Qui postquam Bruto et Cassio caesis, nulla iam publica arma, Pompeius apud Siciliam oppressus, exutoque Lepido, interfecto Antonio, ne Iulianis quidem partibus nisi Caesar dux reliquus, posito triumviri nomine, consulem se ferens et ad tuendam plebem tribunicio iure contentum, ubi militem donis, populum annona, cunctos dulcedine otii pellexit, insurgere paulatim, munia senatus magistratuum legum in se trahere.

English

When he had seduced the army with gifts, the people with grain, and all men with the sweetness of peace, he rose higher by degrees, drawing to himself the functions of the Senate, the magistrates, and the laws. No one opposed him; the boldest spirits had fallen in battle or by proscription, while the remaining nobles, finding that the readier their submission the greater their advancement, preferred the safety of the present to the dangers of the past. Nor did the provinces dislike the new order, since they distrusted the government of the Senate and People, on account of the rivalries of the powerful and the greed of the magistrates.

§I.3–4 (The Succession Problem) · Tacitus, Annals I.3–4
Manuscript
Latin

Domi res tranquillae, eadem magistratuum vocabula; iuniores post Actiacam victoriam, etiam senes plerique inter bella civium nati: quotus quisque reliquus qui rem publicam vidisset?

English

At home all was tranquil, and there were magistrates with the same titles as before. There was not a man alive who had seen the Republic. The state had been utterly transformed, and there was nothing left of the good old way of life. Stripped of equality, all men looked to the commands of the prince with no apprehension for the present, while Augustus in the vigour of life maintained his own power, that of his family, and the peace. When in advanced old age he was worn out by bodily sickness, and the end was near with new prospects and new hopes, a few people talked idly about the blessings of freedom; more feared war; others desired it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1: Tacitus’s compressed opening sentence covers seven hundred years of Roman history in a single breath. The effect is deliberate: the entire republican tradition is reduced to a prelude. The word princeps (“first citizen”) is loaded with irony; Augustus chose the title precisely because it was not “king” (rex) or “dictator”—both terms Romans found intolerable. Tacitus exposes the euphemism: nomine principis sub imperium accepit, “under the name of Prince he subjected [the world] to dominion.” The constitutional fiction is the tyranny.
  2. §I.2: The phrase “seduced the army with gifts, the people with grain, and all men with the sweetness of peace” (militem donis, populum annona, cunctos dulcedine otii pellexit) is Tacitus’s anatomy of how liberty dies. It does not require violence once the population has been made dependent. The three instruments—military patronage, bread distribution, and the sheer relief of peace after decades of civil war—made opposition not just dangerous but genuinely unpopular.
