Introduction

The Myth of Er (Republic 614b–621d) closes Plato’s masterwork with a vision of the afterlife. The soldier Er returns from death to report what he saw: the judgment of souls, the cosmic spindle of necessity, and the choice of future lives.

§614b–615a · Plato, Republic 614b2–615a3 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Ἀλλ᾽ οὐ μέντοι σοι Ἀλκίνου γε ἀπόλογον ἐρῶ, ἀλλὰ ἀλκίμου μὲν ἀνδρός, Ἤρὸς τοῦ Ἀρμενίου

English

Er, son of Armenius, a native of Pamphylia, was slain in battle. When the dead were taken up on the tenth day, his body was found undecomposed. Brought home, he came to life on the funeral pyre on the twelfth day and told what he had seen in the other world. His soul went forth and journeyed with many others to a marvelous place where there were two openings in the earth and two in the heaven above.

Translator's Notes

  1. §614b–615a: The Myth of Er is Plato’s most elaborate eschatological vision. Its themes of postmortem judgment, cosmic order, and the soul’s choice of future lives influenced Virgil’s Aeneid Book 6, Cicero’s Dream of Scipio, and the entire tradition of Christian and Islamic afterlife literature.
