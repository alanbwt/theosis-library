Odyssey, Book XI: The Descent to the Dead

Odyssey, Book XI: The Descent to the Dead
Homer, c. 8th century BCE
Venetus A (Marcianus Graecus 454)

Introduction

The Nekyia (Book XI of the Odyssey) is the foundational Western text on the afterlife. Odysseus sails to the edge of the world, digs a trench, pours blood offerings, and speaks with the shades of the dead. Achilles' famous declaration — that he would rather be a living serf than king of the dead — expresses the Greek view of death with devastating clarity. This passage shaped all subsequent Western conceptions of the underworld.

§11.488-491 · Od. 11.488-491
Greek

μὴ δή μοι θάνατόν γε παραύδα, φαίδιμ᾿ Ὀδυσσεῦ. βουλοίμην κ᾿ ἐπάρουρος ἐὼν θητευέμεν ἄλλῳ, ἀνδρὶ παρ᾿ ἀκλήρῳ, ᾧ μὴ βίοτος πολὺς εἴη, ἢ πᾶσιν νεκύεσσι καταφθιμένοισιν ἀνάσσειν.

English

Do not try to console me for death, glorious Odysseus. I would rather be a serf laboring for another man — a man without a plot of land, without much to live on — than be king over all the wasted dead.

§11.204-208 · Od. 11.204-208
Greek

αὕτη δίκη ἐστὶ βροτῶν, ὅτε τίς κε θάνῃσιν· οὐ γὰρ ἔτι σάρκας τε καὶ ὀστέα ἶνες ἔχουσιν, ἀλλὰ τὰ μέν τε πυρὸς κρατερὸν μένος αἰθομένοιο δαμνᾷ.

English

This is the way of mortals when they die: the sinews no longer hold the flesh and the bones together, but the fierce power of blazing fire destroys them.
