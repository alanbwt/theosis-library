On the Holy Spirit 9.23: Through the Spirit We Become God

Περὶ τοῦ Ἁγίου Πνεύματος
Basil of Caesarea, c. 330–379 AD

Introduction

Basil of Caesarea’s On the Holy Spirit (De Spiritu Sancto), written in 375 CE, is the decisive fourth-century defence of the Spirit’s full divinity. The Pneumatomachians (“Spirit-fighters”) accepted the Son’s divinity after Nicaea but denied the Spirit’s. Basil’s argument is that the Spirit must be divine because the Spirit does what only God can do — and chief among the Spirit’s works is theosis.

Chapter 9, section 23 contains the crucial claim: through the Holy Spirit we become like God, and beyond all desire, we become God. This is not a careless phrase. Basil is making a pneumatological argument with ontological force. If the Spirit makes us God, the Spirit must be God.

§9.23 · De Spiritu Sancto 9.23 (Pruche ed., SC 17bis)
Manuscript
Greek

Δια του αγίου Πνεύματος η εις παράδεισον αποκατάστασις, η εις βασιλείαν ουρανών ανάβασις, η εις υιοθεσίαν επάνοδος, η παρρησία του καλείν Πατέρα τον Θεόν, το κοινωνον γενέσθαι της χάριτος του Χριστου, το τέκνον φωτος χρηματίζειν, το αιδίου δόξης μετέχειν· δια του Πνεύματος η προς Θεον ομοίωσις, και το υπερ πασαν επιθυμίαν, Θεον γενέσθαι.

English

Through the Holy Spirit comes our restoration to Paradise, our ascent to the Kingdom of heaven, our adoption as sons, our freedom to call God our Father, our becoming partakers of the grace of Christ, our being called children of light, our sharing in eternal glory, and, in a word, our being brought into a state of all fullness of blessing, both in this world and in the world to come. Contemplating as in a mirror the blessings that are in store, which are promised to us through faith, we await the enjoyment of them as though they were already present, so extraordinary is the pleasure we take in them. If this is the pledge, what must the perfection be? And if the first-fruits are so great, what must the full harvest be? Through the Spirit we become like God; through the Spirit, what is beyond all desire: we become God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §9.23: The climactic phrase Θεον γενέσθαι (“to become God”) is as direct as any statement in patristic literature. Basil is careful to embed it in a Trinitarian framework: it is through the Spirit that this happens, not through human effort or philosophical ascent. The rhetorical structure builds through a catalogue of lesser blessings — restoration, ascent, adoption, freedom — before arriving at the ultimate claim. Basil’s point is that all of these blessings are the Spirit’s work, and the Spirit’s greatest work is making humans divine.
