John 14:15-21: I Am in My Father, and You in Me

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

John 14:15–21 contains the most explicit statement of mutual indwelling in the New Testament. Jesus promises not merely proximity to his disciples but ontological union: “I am in my Father, and you in me, and I in you.” This is not metaphor. The Greek preposition en (ἐν) denotes real presence within, and the chain of indwelling—Father in Son, Son in believers, believers in Son—establishes a participatory structure that the Greek Fathers would later call perichoresis, mutual interpenetration.

The passage also introduces the “another Paraclete” (ἄλλον παράκλητον), the Spirit of truth who will dwell in the disciples. The indwelling is therefore trinitarian: Father, Son, and Spirit all take up residence within the believer. This is the foundation text for the Eastern Orthodox doctrine of theosis and for every subsequent theology of divine indwelling in the Christian tradition.

English

In that day you will know that I am in my Father, and you in me, and I in you. The one who has my commandments and keeps them, that one is the one who loves me. And the one who loves me will be loved by my Father, and I will love him and will manifest myself to him.

Translator's Notes

  1. §14:16: The term parakletos (παράκλητος) has been rendered variously as “Comforter,” “Advocate,” “Helper,” and “Counselor.” The word literally means “one called alongside.” Jesus says he will send another Paraclete, implying that he himself has been the first. The Spirit is thus not a replacement for Christ but a continuation of his presence in a different mode.
  2. §14:20: The chain of mutual indwelling—“I in my Father, you in me, I in you”—became the primary proof text for the patristic doctrine of theosis. Athanasius, Cyril of Alexandria, and Maximus the Confessor all cite this verse to argue that believers genuinely participate in the divine nature through Christ. The preposition en (ἐν) is not weakened to mere association; it indicates real ontological presence.
