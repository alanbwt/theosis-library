Revelations of Divine Love: The Hazelnut

Julian of Norwich, 1343-1416 AD

Introduction

Julian of Norwich (c. 1343–c. 1416) was an English anchoress who, following a series of visions received during a near-fatal illness in May 1373, spent the next twenty years composing the Revelations of Divine Love—the first book known to have been written by a woman in the English language. Chapter 5 contains one of her most celebrated images: God shows her a tiny thing, the size of a hazelnut, lying in the palm of her hand. She is told that it is “all that is made.” Three truths are revealed about it: God made it, God loves it, God preserves it.

Julian’s theology is a theology of radical divine intimacy. God is not distant or wrathful but “homely”—a Middle English word meaning both “familiar” and “domestic.” The creation is infinitely small compared to the Creator, yet God cherishes it. The theosis tradition in Julian takes the form of an insistence that all shall be well—that the love which created and sustains all things will ultimately reconcile all things to itself.

English

And in this he showed me a little thing, the quantity of a hazelnut, lying in the palm of my hand, as it seemed. And it was as round as any ball. I looked upon it with the eye of my understanding, and I thought: “What may this be?” And it was answered generally thus: “It is all that is made.”

§Chapter 5 (excerpt 3) · Revelations of Divine Love, Chapter 5
Manuscript
Middle English

In this lytil thyng I saw thre propertees. The furst is that god made it. The secunde that god loueth it. The thirde that god kepeth it. But what is that to me. Sothly the maker the louer and the keper. For tylle I am substancially onyd to hym I may neuer haue full rest ne very blysse.

English

In this little thing I saw three properties: the first is that God made it; the second is that God loves it; the third is that God preserves it. But what is that to me? Truly, the maker, the lover, and the preserver. For until I am substantially united to him, I can never have full rest nor true bliss.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 5, excerpt 3: The phrase “substantially united to him” (substancially onyd to hym) is Julian’s direct statement of theosis. The word “substantially” is not casual; it carries the Scholastic sense of a union at the level of being, not merely of feeling or understanding. Julian is claiming that the soul cannot rest until it participates in God’s own substance. This is a remarkably bold claim for a medieval English writer, and it places her squarely in the tradition of Meister Eckhart and the Rhineland mystics.
  2. §Chapter 5, general: The hazelnut vision exemplifies Julian’s characteristic method: she begins with a concrete sensory image (a tiny thing in the palm of her hand) and draws from it the most far-reaching metaphysical conclusions. The smallness of creation is not a sign of its worthlessness but of its utter dependence on God. Everything that exists is held in being at every moment by divine love; if God withdrew that love, creation would instantly collapse into nothing. This is the Augustinian doctrine of creatio continua (continuous creation) rendered as mystical experience.
