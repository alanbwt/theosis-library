Descent of Ishtar: Stripped of Divinity

Anonymous (Babylonian), c. 1100 BCE

Introduction

The Descent of Ishtar to the Underworld is one of the oldest surviving narratives of divine self-emptying. The goddess Ishtar—Queen of Heaven, goddess of love and war—descends through the seven gates of the underworld to confront her sister Ereshkigal, Queen of the Dead. At each gate she is stripped of one article of her divine regalia: crown, earrings, necklace, breast ornaments, girdle, bracelets, and finally her garment. She arrives before Ereshkigal naked and powerless, and is killed and hung on a hook.

The Akkadian text (a condensed version of the older Sumerian Descent of Inanna) survives on tablets from Nineveh and Assur. The theological structure is remarkable: divinity can only enter the realm of death by being stripped of divinity. The goddess must become not-goddess in order to reach the deepest place. This is the same logic that Paul articulates in Philippians 2:6–8—the divine figure who “emptied himself” (kenosis) and descended to the point of death. The parallel is not coincidental; it is structural.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 42–62: The seven gates correspond to the seven layers of divine identity being removed. Each article of clothing or jewelry is not merely decorative but a melammu—a component of the divine radiance that marks a god as a god. As each piece is removed, Ishtar becomes progressively less divine. The stripping is systematic: crown (sovereignty), earrings (divine hearing), necklace (divine speech), breast ornaments (nurturing power), girdle (fertility), bracelets (divine action), garment (the divine body itself). By the seventh gate she is naked—not just unclothed but de-divinised.
  2. §Lines 11–38: Ishtar’s threat to “raise up the dead and they shall eat the living” is not bluster. As goddess of both love and war, she holds power over the boundary between life and death. The threat reveals that the underworld’s gates exist not to keep the dead in but to keep the gods out. The entire architecture of death is a containment system for divinity. This inverts the usual assumption: it is not mortality that is the problem but immortality in the wrong place.
