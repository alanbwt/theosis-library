De Docta Ignorantia III.12: The Coincidence of Opposites

Nicholas of Cusa, 1401-1464 AD

Introduction

Nicholas of Cusa’s De Docta Ignorantia (On Learned Ignorance, 1440) is one of the great philosophical treatises of the Renaissance. Its central insight is that God transcends all categories of human thought, including the categories of opposition. In God, maximum and minimum coincide; affirmation and negation are one; the infinite and the finite are not opposed but united. Cusa calls this the coincidentia oppositorum—the coincidence of opposites. Book III applies this principle to Christology: because maximum and minimum coincide in God, the infinite divine nature and the finite human nature can be united in one person without contradiction.

Cusa’s relevance to the theosis tradition is direct. If God transcends opposition, then the opposition between Creator and creature is not ultimate. In Christ, the maximum (God) and the minimum (man) have already coincided. What happened in Christ can happen, by participation, in every human being who is united to him. Cusa thus provides a philosophical framework for the patristic claim that “God became man so that man might become God.”

Latin original and English translation.
§III.12.1 · De Docta Ignorantia, Book III, Chapter 12
Manuscript
Latin

Quoniam in Christo humana natura divinae naturae, quae est maximum absolutum, unita est, constat per Christum humanam naturam ad suprema esse elevatam.

English

Since in Christ human nature is united to the divine nature, which is the absolute maximum, it is clear that through Christ human nature has been elevated to the highest. For human nature in Christ is, through union with the maximum, everything that it can be.

§III.12.2 · De Docta Ignorantia, Book III, Chapter 12
Manuscript
Latin

Humana igitur natura in Christo supra omnia opera Dei elevata in unione maximi quiescit.

English

Human nature, elevated in Christ above all the works of God, rests in union with the maximum. Therefore no creature can attain a higher degree of elevation than this: to be united to the maximum in unity of person. And this has been achieved in Jesus.

§III.12.3 · De Docta Ignorantia, Book III, Chapter 12
Manuscript
Latin

Cum maximum et minimum coincidant, ut ostendimus, sequitur quod maxima humanitas, quae sine divinitate esse non potest, medium est.

English

Since the maximum and the minimum coincide, as we have shown, it follows that maximum humanity, which cannot exist without being divinity, is the means by which all human beings can attain the highest union with God. For this is the purpose of the Incarnation: that all might be elevated through him who is the maximum.

§III.12.4 · De Docta Ignorantia, Book III, Chapter 12
Manuscript
Latin

Christus est igitur primogenitus omnis creaturae, in quo omnia creata sunt et in quo omnia constant.

English

Christ is therefore the first-born of all creation, in whom all things were created and in whom all things consist. He is the means through which God descends to humanity and humanity ascends to God. Through him, the maximum descends to the minimum without confusion, and the minimum ascends to the maximum without presumption.

§III.12.5 · De Docta Ignorantia, Book III, Chapter 12
Manuscript
Latin

Sequitur quod omnis intellectualis natura quae per Christum unionem cum maximo absoluto attingit, perfectionem suae naturae attinget.

English

It follows that every intellectual nature which, through Christ, attains union with the absolute maximum, will reach the perfection of its nature. For the intellect, which desires to know, finds its rest only in the infinite truth. And the will, which desires the good, finds its rest only in the infinite good. In God alone does the intellect know and the will love without any deficiency.

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.12.1: Cusa’s argument depends on the principle established in Books I and II: the absolute maximum is that which cannot be greater, and since it cannot be greater, it coincides with the minimum (which cannot be lesser). This is not a logical trick but a claim about the nature of infinity: the truly infinite is not limited by opposition to the finite. Applied to Christology, this means that the union of divine and human natures in Christ is not a contradiction but the natural consequence of divine infinity.
  2. §III.12.3: The phrase “maximum humanity, which cannot exist without being divinity” captures Cusa’s distinctive contribution. For the earlier Scholastics, humanity at its maximum is still infinitely distant from divinity. For Cusa, the maximum of any nature coincides with the absolute maximum, which is God. This means that the perfection of human nature is not merely analogous to divinity but identical with it—a claim that brings Cusa close to Eckhart, whom he read and admired.
