Introduction

Isidore of Seville’s Etymologies (c. 630 CE) is a vast encyclopedia covering everything from grammar to agriculture, organized by etymological derivation. It was the most widely used reference work of the medieval period.

§1.1.1–3 · Isidore, Etymologiae 1.1
Manuscript
Greek

Disciplina a discendo nomen accepit. Ars vero dicta est quod artis praeceptis regulisque consistat

English

A discipline (disciplina) takes its name from learning (discendo). An art (ars) is so called because it consists of strict rules and precepts. Others say the word derives from the Greek arete, that is, virtue. Knowledge is of things known. The seven liberal arts are: Grammar, Rhetoric, Dialectic, Arithmetic, Music, Geometry, and Astronomy.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1: Isidore’s etymological method—deriving the nature of a thing from the origin of its name—is linguistically fanciful but philosophically revealing. It assumes language encodes reality, a view shared by many ancient cultures. The Etymologies transmitted classical knowledge in accessible form throughout the medieval West.
