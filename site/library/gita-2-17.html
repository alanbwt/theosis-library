Bhagavad Gita 2.17-20: The Eternal Self

Vyasa (attributed), traditional

Introduction

The Bhagavad Gita opens on a battlefield. Arjuna, the greatest warrior of his age, refuses to fight because his enemies are his own kinsmen. Krishna, his charioteer and divine friend, responds not with a pep talk but with a complete metaphysics. Verses 2.17–20 contain the core of that metaphysics: the Self (ātman) is eternal, indestructible, and unchanging. It cannot be cut, burned, wetted, or dried. It was never born and will never die.

This teaching is the foundation of everything that follows in the Gita. If the Self is eternal, then death is an illusion—not in the sense that bodies do not perish, but in the sense that what you truly are cannot perish. This is theosis as ontological fact rather than spiritual achievement: you do not become immortal; you already are immortal and always have been. The only question is whether you know it.

§2.17 · Bhagavad Gita 2.17
Manuscript
Sanskrit

avināśi tu tad viddhi yena sarvam idaṃ tatam | vināśam avyayasyāsya na kaścit kartum arhati

English

Know that to be indestructible by which all this is pervaded. No one can bring about the destruction of this imperishable reality.

§2.18 · Bhagavad Gita 2.18
Manuscript
Sanskrit

antavanta ime dehā nityasyoktāḥ śarīriṇaḥ | anāśino’prameyasya tasmād yudhyasva bhārata

English

These bodies of the embodied Self, which is eternal, indestructible, and immeasurable, are said to have an end. Therefore fight, O Bharata.

§2.19 · Bhagavad Gita 2.19
Manuscript
Sanskrit

ya enaṃ vetti hantāraṃ yaś cainaṃ manyate hatam | ubhau tau na vijānīto nāyaṃ hanti na hanyate

English

He who thinks the Self is the slayer and he who thinks it is slain—both are ignorant. It does not slay, nor is it slain.

§2.20 · Bhagavad Gita 2.20
Manuscript
Sanskrit

na jāyate mriyate vā kadācin nāyaṃ bhūtvā bhavitā vā na bhūyaḥ | ajo nityaḥ śāśvato’yaṃ purāṇo na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre

English

It is not born, nor does it ever die. Having been, it will never cease to be. Unborn, eternal, permanent, and primeval, it is not slain when the body is slain.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2.17: The phrase yena sarvam idaṃ tatam (“by which all this is pervaded”) echoes the Isha Upanishad’s īśā vāsyam idaṃ sarvam. The Gita is here consolidating Upanishadic teaching into a single, systematic presentation. The Self that pervades all things is not a distant deity but the innermost reality of every creature.
  2. §2.19: This verse is almost a direct quotation from the Katha Upanishad (2.19): hantā cen manyate hantuṃ hataś cen manyate hatam | ubhau tau na vijānīto nāyaṃ hanti na hanyate. The Gita frequently absorbs Upanishadic verses, recontextualizing them within the dramatic setting of the battlefield. What was abstract philosophy in the Upanishad becomes, in the Gita, an urgent practical teaching: you must act, and you can act without guilt, because the Self is beyond harm.
  3. §2.20: The accumulation of negatives—not born, does not die, never ceases to be, unborn, eternal, permanent, primeval, not slain—is not redundancy but emphasis. Krishna is stripping away every possible misconception about the Self. It is not a soul that was created at some point and will last forever. It is not a substance that can be modified. It simply is, outside the categories of time, change, and causation.
