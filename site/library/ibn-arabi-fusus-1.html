Introduction

Ibn Arabi's Fusus al-Hikam ("Bezels of Wisdom") is the most influential and controversial work of Sufi metaphysics. Each of its 27 chapters associates a particular aspect of divine wisdom with a different prophet, from Adam to Muhammad.

Translation

§Chapter 1: The Bezel of Divine Wisdom in the Word of Adam · Fusus al-Hikam, Chapter 1

The Real (al-Haqq) wished to see the essences of His Most Beautiful Names—or, you could say, to see His own essence in a comprehensive object that, being endowed with existence, would manifest the whole of the divine order, so that He might behold Himself through it. For the seeing of a thing by itself is not the same as its seeing itself in something else, which acts as a mirror. So Adam was the very polishing of that mirror, and the spirit breathed into that form was nothing other than the divine gaze directed at this supreme mirror.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: Ibn Arabi's doctrine that humanity is the mirror through which God sees Himself is the most radical articulation of theosis in the Islamic tradition. It implies that human beings are not merely God's servants but the means by which God achieves self-knowledge—a claim that orthodox critics condemned as pantheism but that Ibn Arabi's followers insist is a subtle distinction between the Being of God and the forms through which Being manifests.
