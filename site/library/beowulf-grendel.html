Introduction

The Grendel episode (lines 710–836) is the first great combat of Beowulf. The hero waits in the darkened hall Heorot as the monster approaches, then seizes Grendel in a grip that no weapon can match, tearing the creature’s arm from its socket.

§Lines 710–727 · Beowulf, Lines 710–727
Manuscript
Greek

Ðā cōm of mōre under mist-hleoþum Grendel gongan, Godes yrre bær; mynte se mān-scaða manna cynnes sumne besyrwan in sele þam hēan.

English

Then from the moor, under misty slopes, Grendel came stalking; he bore God’s anger. The wicked ravager meant to ensnare some of the race of men in the high hall. He strode under the clouds until he could see most clearly the gold-hall of men, plated with gold.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 710–727: The phrase “he bore God’s anger” (Godes yrre bær) is one of the clearest Christian interpolations in Beowulf. Grendel is elsewhere described as a descendant of Cain (line 107), placing the monster within biblical genealogy. The poem’s Christianity and its Germanic heroic worldview exist in unresolved tension.
