Maitri Upanishad 6.17: Brahman Is the Whole Universe

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The Maitri Upanishad (also called the Maitrayaniya Upanishad) is one of the later principal Upanishads, composed perhaps in the last centuries BCE. Chapter 6.17 contains one of the clearest Upanishadic statements of the all-pervading nature of Brahman: “This Brahman is indeed the whole universe.” The text synthesises earlier Upanishadic teachings with elements of Samkhya philosophy and yoga practice, producing a comprehensive map of the path from ignorance to realisation.

The Maitri Upanishad’s significance for the theosis library lies in its explicit identification of the individual self with the cosmic reality. The text does not merely argue that Brahman created the world; it argues that Brahman is the world, and that the individual soul’s deepest identity is Brahman itself. Liberation is not escape from the world but the recognition that the world and the self are both expressions of one undivided reality.

§6.17a · Maitri Upanishad 6.17 (Cowell edition)
Manuscript
Sanskrit

ब्रह्म वा इदमग्र आसीत्। तदेकं सन्नाशक्त।

English

In the beginning this universe was Brahman alone. Being One, he was not sufficient. He created a still superior form: the dharma, the law that governs even the rulers. There is nothing higher than dharma. Through dharma a weak man prevails over a stronger, as through a king.

§6.17b · Maitri Upanishad 6.17
Manuscript
Sanskrit

सर्वं खल्विदं ब्रह्म। तच्छान्त उपासीत यत एतद् यतोन्यजायत यत्र शक्तो यत्र प्राणिति।

English

This Brahman is indeed the whole universe. One should meditate on it in tranquillity as that from which one came forth, as that into which one will be dissolved, and as that in which one breathes. For surely the self (atman) of each person is Brahman—one who knows this, having departed from this world, merges into that Self of intelligence.

§6.18 · Maitri Upanishad 6.18
Manuscript
Sanskrit

द्वे वाव ब्रह्मणो रूपे। मूर्तं चामूर्तं च। मर्त्यं चामृतं च।

English

There are two forms of Brahman: the formed and the formless, the mortal and the immortal, the stationary and the moving, the actual and the beyond. That which is other than the sun and other than the moon, other than lightning—that is the fullness. From the fullness, fullness arises. When fullness is taken from fullness, fullness alone remains.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6.17b: The statement “This Brahman is indeed the whole universe” (sarvam khalvidam brahma) echoes the Chandogya Upanishad 3.14.1 and is one of the mahavakyas (great sayings) of Vedantic tradition. It asserts a non-dual ontology: there is no reality separate from Brahman. The path from ignorance to liberation is the recognition of what was always the case.
  2. §6.18: The paradox of fullness—“from the fullness, fullness arises; when fullness is taken from fullness, fullness alone remains”—is adapted from the Isha Upanishad invocation. It expresses the infinite nature of Brahman: unlike material resources, the divine is not diminished by giving. This principle resonates with the Christian concept of the inexhaustible divine nature and with the Neoplatonic doctrine that the One produces all things without itself being diminished.
