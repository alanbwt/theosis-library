Introduction

The Tale of Sinuhe (c. 1875 BCE) is considered the masterpiece of Egyptian literature. A court official flees Egypt after the death of Amenemhat I and lives in exile before being summoned home. It explores identity, belonging, and the pull of homeland.

pr.n=j m grḥ n msšrw. ḥāw spā ḥā.t=j s.t-Ptn, āḥā.n=j m jw n Km-wr. jb.t ḥr.t(j) wj

English

I set out at evening time. When day broke I reached Peten and halted at the island of the Great Black Bull. Thirst overtook me; I was parched, my throat was dry. I said, “This is the taste of death.” Then I lifted my heart and gathered my limbs, for I heard the sound of cattle lowing and saw nomads.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines B 1–20: Sinuhe’s flight and the moment of near-death in the desert (“This is the taste of death”) is one of the most psychologically vivid passages in ancient literature. The tale’s concern with Egyptian identity—can an Egyptian remain Egyptian outside Egypt?—parallels the later Jewish question of maintaining identity in exile.
