Sermon 166.4: The Western Theosis with Qualifications

Sermo 166
Augustine of Hippo, 354–430 AD

Introduction

Augustine of Hippo is the dominant figure of Western theology, and his relationship to the theosis tradition is complex. He uses the language of deification — deus factus est homo ut homo fieret deus, “God became man so that man might become God” — but he surrounds it with qualifications that distinguish his position from the Eastern Fathers. Sermon 166, preached around 416 CE, contains one of his clearest statements of the exchange formula along with his characteristic caution.

Augustine insists that human deification does not mean becoming God by nature. We become gods by grace, by adoption, not by generation from the divine substance. This distinction would shape the entire Western tradition’s reception of theosis language, from Aquinas’s participatio to the Reformers’ suspicion of the concept entirely.

§166.4 · Sermo 166.4 (PL 38.909)
Manuscript
Latin

Factus est Deus homo, ut homo fieret Deus. Sed ne quid indignum intellegas, addit per apostolum: ego dixi, dii estis, et filii Excelsi omnes. Fecit nos deos adoptando, non de se generando. Unus enim est Filius Dei natura, qui propter nos factus est filius hominis, ut nos qui eramus filii hominum natura, per illum efficeremur filii Dei gratia. Ille enim incommutabilis manens, naturam nostram suscepit, ut in ea nos susciperet ad se; et tenens suam divinitatem, particeps factus est nostrae infirmitatis, ut nos in melius commutati, quod peccatum et mortalitas nostra est, eius iustitiae et immortalitatis participatione amitteremus, et quod boni in natura nostra ille plantaverat, bono maiore divini muneris perfectum servaremus.

English

God became man so that man might become God. But lest you think this means something unworthy, he adds a clarification through the Apostle: you are gods, and all of you children of the Most High. He made us gods by adopting us, not by generating us from himself. For there is only one Son of God by nature, who for our sake became the Son of Man, so that we who were sons of men by nature might become sons of God through him by grace. For he, remaining immutable, took on our nature so that in it he might take us up to himself, and, holding fast his own divinity, became a partaker of our weakness, so that we, being changed for the better, might by participating in his righteousness and immortality lose our own sinfulness and mortality, and preserve whatever good nature he had planted in us, now perfected by the greater good of his divine gift.

Translator's Notes

  1. §166.4: Augustine’s qualification — adoptando, non de se generando (“by adopting, not by generating from himself”) — draws a line that the Eastern tradition also honours but does not emphasize as sharply. The Eastern Fathers speak of “real participation” in the divine nature; Augustine insists that this participation is always by grace and adoption, never by nature. Both positions affirm theosis while guarding against pantheism, but the emphasis differs. Augustine’s anxiety is that the language of deification could be read as claiming that humans become God in the same way that the Son is God. His repeated qualifications would later make Western theologians more cautious about theosis language than their Eastern counterparts.
