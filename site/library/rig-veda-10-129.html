Rig Veda 10.129: The Hymn of Creation

Vedic Rishis, c. 1500-1200 BCE

Introduction

The Nasadiya Sukta (Rig Veda 10.129) is the oldest surviving philosophical hymn in any Indo-European language, composed sometime between 1500 and 1200 BCE. It asks the most fundamental question possible: what existed before existence itself? The hymn’s answer is radical agnosticism. There was no being, no non-being, no death, no immortality. Something breathed without breath, by its own impulse. Then desire (kama) arose as the first seed of mind—and from that primal wanting, the world unfolded.

What makes this hymn extraordinary is its final turn. After tracing the origin of all things, the poet refuses to claim certainty. “Who really knows? Who here can say?” Even the gods came after creation, so even they cannot tell us what came before. The overseer in the highest heaven—perhaps he knows, or perhaps even he does not. This is not atheism; it is intellectual honesty of the highest order. Three thousand years before the Enlightenment, a Vedic poet stood at the edge of human knowledge and admitted that the origin of existence may be unknowable. The hymn does not answer the theosis question directly, but it sets the stage: if the source of all reality is a single, undifferentiated “That One” (tad ekam), then every differentiated being is an expression of it.

§10.129.1 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

nāsad āsīn no sad āsīt tadānīṃ nāsīd rajo no vyomā paro yat | kim āvarīvaḥ kuha kasya śarmann ambhaḥ kim āsīd gahanaṃ gabhīram

English

There was neither being nor non-being then. There was no air, nor the sky beyond it. What covered it? Where was it? In whose protection? Was there water, fathomlessly deep?

§10.129.2 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

na mṛtyur āsīd amṛtaṃ na tarhi na rātryā ahna āsīt praketāḥ | ānīd avātaṃ svadhāyā tad ekaṃ tasmād dhānyan na paraḥ kiṃ canāsa

English

There was no death then, nor immortality. There was no sign of night or day. That One breathed, without breath, by its own impulse. Other than that, there was nothing at all.

§10.129.3 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 3

Sanskrit

tama āsīt tamasā gūḷham agre ‘prakeataṃ salilaṃ sarvam ā idam | tucchyenābhv apihitaṃ yad āsīt tapasas tan mahinājāyataikam

English

Darkness was hidden by darkness in the beginning. All this was an undistinguished sea. That which was covered by the void, That One, was born through the power of heat (tapas).

§10.129.4 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 4

Sanskrit

kāmas tad agre sam avartataādhi manaso retaḥ prathamaṃ yad āsīt | sato bandham asati nir avindan hṛdi pratīṣyā kavayo manīṣā

English

Desire (kama) arose in the beginning, the first seed of mind. Poets, searching in their hearts with wisdom, found the bond of being in non-being.

§10.129.5 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 5

Sanskrit

tiraścīno vitato raśmir eṣām adhaḥ svid āsīd upari svid āsīt | retodhā āsan mahimāna āsan svadhā avastāt prayatiḥ parastāt

English

Their cord was stretched across. Was there a below? Was there an above? There were seed-bearers, there were powers. There was impulse from below, there was giving forth from above.

§10.129.6 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 6
Manuscript
Sanskrit

ko addhā veda ka iha pra vocat kuta ājātā kuta iyaṃ visṛṣṭiḥ | arvāg devā asya visarjanena athā ko veda yata ābabhūva

English

Who really knows? Who here can say? When was it born? From where did this creation come? The gods came afterwards, with the creation of this world. So who knows from where it arose?

§10.129.7 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 129, Verse 7

Sanskrit

iyaṃ visṛṣṭir yata ābabhūva yadi vā dadhe yadi vā na | yo asyādhyakṣaḥ parame vyoman so aṅga veda yadi vā na veda

English

From where this creation arose—whether he made it or did not—he who surveys it from the highest heaven, he alone knows. Or perhaps even he does not know.

Translator's Notes

  1. §10.129.1: The opening negation—nāsad āsīn no sad āsīt, “there was neither non-being nor being”—transcends the basic categories of ontology. This is not simply saying “nothing existed.” It is saying that the categories of existence and non-existence themselves did not apply. This kind of radical negation reappears in the Buddhist concept of śūnyatā (emptiness) and in the apophatic theology of the Christian mystics.
  2. §10.129.2: The phrase tad ekam (“That One”) is the hymn’s name for the source of all things. It is deliberately vague—not a god, not a force, not a substance, but simply “That One.” The later Upanishads will develop this into the concept of Brahman: the singular, undifferentiated ground of all being.
  3. §10.129.4: The claim that desire (kāma) is the first seed of mind is remarkable. Before there is consciousness, before there is a world, there is wanting. This makes desire not a corruption of an originally pure state (as in later Buddhist and some Hindu ascetic traditions) but the very engine of creation.
  4. §10.129.7: The final doubt—“or perhaps even he does not know”—is unique in ancient religious literature. No other hymn in the Rig Veda, and virtually no text from ancient Mesopotamia, Egypt, or Greece, ends by questioning whether the supreme being has knowledge of origins. This verse alone places the Nasadiya Sukta in a class by itself.
