Revelation 22:1-5: They Shall See His Face

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

Revelation 22:1–5 presents the final vision of the redeemed cosmos: the river of the water of life flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb, the tree of life bearing fruit in perpetuity, and the servants of God who “shall see his face, and his name shall be on their foreheads.” This is the beatific vision—the direct, unmediated sight of God that Moses was denied (Exodus 33:20) and that the entire biblical narrative has been building toward.

The promise that God’s servants will see his face reverses the fundamental limitation of the old covenant. In the new creation, the barrier between Creator and creature is removed—not by abolishing the distinction between them, but by transforming the creature into one capable of bearing the divine presence. The name of God written on the forehead signifies total belonging and total transformation: the servants bear God’s identity upon themselves. They reign forever, not as autonomous sovereigns but as participants in the divine rule, illuminated not by sun or lamp but by the Lord God himself.

English

Then he showed me the river of the water of life, bright as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb through the middle of the street of the city. On either side of the river was the tree of life, bearing twelve kinds of fruit, yielding its fruit each month; and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations.

English

No longer will there be anything accursed. The throne of God and of the Lamb will be in it, and his servants will worship him. They shall see his face, and his name shall be on their foreheads. And night will be no more; they will need no light of lamp or sun, for the Lord God will be their light, and they will reign forever and ever.

Translator's Notes

  1. §22:4: The promise “they shall see his face” (ὄψονται τὸ πρόσωπον αὐτοῦ) reverses the prohibition of Exodus 33:20: “No one can see my face and live.” In the new creation, the creature can bear what was previously fatal. This is the culmination of the transformative process described in 2 Corinthians 3:18: beholding the glory of God transforms the beholder, and the fully transformed beholder can see God face to face. Gregory of Nyssa and Maximus the Confessor both treat the beatific vision as the goal of theosis.
  2. §22:2: The tree of life (ξύλον ζωῆς) recalls the tree of Genesis 2–3 from which humanity was barred after the Fall. Access is now fully restored. The detail that its leaves are “for the healing of the nations” (εἰς θεραπείαν τῶν ἐθνῶν) implies that the new creation is not merely the absence of disease but active, ongoing restoration—a healing that continues even in eternity, perhaps anticipating Gregory of Nyssa’s teaching of eternal progress into the infinite divine life.
