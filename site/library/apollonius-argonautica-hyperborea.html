Argonautica IV.611-618: The Argonauts Near Hyperborea

Apollonius of Rhodes, c. 295-215 BCE

Introduction

Apollonius of Rhodes, the third-century BCE poet and librarian of Alexandria, includes a brief but evocative reference to the Hyperboreans in Book IV of his Argonautica, the epic retelling of Jason’s quest for the Golden Fleece. As the Argonauts make their labyrinthine return voyage—traveling up the Danube, across to the Adriatic, and through a network of rivers that defies ordinary geography—they pass near the land of the Hyperboreans. The passage is fleeting, but its placement within the narrative is significant.

The Argonauts’ journey home is itself a kind of spiritual odyssey, a return through increasingly strange and sacred landscapes. Their proximity to Hyperborea comes at a moment when the voyage has left the realm of ordinary geography entirely. For the theosis tradition, this passage matters because it places Hyperborea on the boundary of the known world—close enough to be sensed but never quite reached. The Argonauts pass near it but do not enter, just as mortals may approach the divine state without fully attaining it. Hyperborea remains the land glimpsed from the edge of human experience.

Greek

καί ρ’ ἐπ’ Ἠριδανοῖο βαθυρρόου προχοαῖσιν ἵκοντο· ἔνθα ποτὲ Φαέθων στεροπῆς βλημένος προτὶ στήθεα κεραυνῷ, ἡμιδαὴς ὅχεων ὅλον ἤριπεν ἀφ’ ἅρματος Ἠελίοιο. Κελτοὶ δ’ ἐπὶ τοῖσι λόγον τόνδε συνῆψαν· ωἷς τά γε δάκρυα Λητοῦς Ἀπόλλωνος εἰσὶ τὰ φερόμενα δίνῃσιν, ἃ δὴ μυρία χεῦεν ἱερὸν γένος Ὑπερβορέων ἰών.

English

And they came to the deep lake of Eridanus, where once Phaethon, struck in the breast by a blazing thunderbolt, fell half-consumed from the chariot of the Sun into the mouth of that deep lake. And even now it belches up heavy steam from the smoldering wound. No bird can spread its light wings to fly over that water, but in mid-course it plunges into the flame. And all around the maidens, the daughters of the Sun (Hēliades), enclosed in tall poplars, wail bitterly, and from their eyes they shed on the ground drops of amber. These are dried by the sun on the sand, but when the waters of the dark lake rise and flood the shore, the drops roll into Eridanus on the swelling current. And the Celts have attached this story to them: that these are the tears of Leto’s son Apollo, which are carried along by the eddies—the countless tears he shed when he came to the sacred race of the Hyperboreans and left shining heaven at the admonition of his father, being in wrath concerning his son whom divine Coronis bore in bright Lacereia by the waters of the Amyrus.

Translator's Notes

  1. §IV.611–618: Apollonius weaves together three myths in a single passage: Phaethon’s fall, the weeping of the Heliades (daughters of the Sun), and Apollo’s journey to the Hyperboreans. The amber tears of the Heliades are identified by the Celts with the tears of Apollo himself, shed when he traveled to the “sacred race of the Hyperboreans” (ἱερὸν γένος Ὑπερβορέων). The phrase “sacred race” is significant—it echoes Pindar’s “sacred race” (ἱερᾷ γενεᾷ) in Pythian 10 and confirms the Hyperboreans’ status as a people set apart by their relationship to the divine.
  2. §IV.611 (amber): The identification of amber with divine tears is ancient and widespread. Baltic amber was traded southward to the Mediterranean along routes that passed through Celtic and Scythian territories—the same regions associated with the Hyperborean offering chain described by Herodotus. Apollonius’s version, in which amber is Apollo’s tears shed while visiting the Hyperboreans, collapses the trade route and the myth into a single image: every piece of amber that reaches Greece is a trace of the god’s sojourn in paradise.
  3. §IV.611 (Apollo’s exile): The reference to Apollo leaving “shining heaven at the admonition of his father” alludes to the myth that Zeus banished Apollo from Olympus after Apollo killed the Cyclopes in retaliation for Zeus’s killing of Asclepius. During this exile, Apollo went to the Hyperboreans—paradise served as the god’s refuge. This inverts the usual relationship: normally mortals seek the divine; here the divine seeks the Hyperboreans. Even a god, in his hour of grief and exile, turns to the land beyond the north wind for consolation.
