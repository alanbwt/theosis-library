Epistle of Barnabas 6.12: Creating Us Afresh

Author of the Epistle of Barnabas, c. 70–130 AD

Introduction

The Epistle of Barnabas, likely composed between 70 and 135 CE, is one of the earliest attempts to interpret the Hebrew scriptures as a Christian text. In chapter 6, the author reaches the climax of his argument: through the remission of sins, Christ has made believers into “another type”—a new kind of being, with the souls of children, as if created afresh. The language is that of re-creation, not merely forgiveness.

The Greek text survives in Codex Sinaiticus itself (where it was included as part of the New Testament) and in a later manuscript. The passage presented here contains the key formula: God has “remoulded” (anaplasso) the believer, creating a second creation that surpasses the first. This is not moral improvement. It is ontological re-manufacture—the human being taken apart and reassembled with a different nature. The Epistle of Barnabas stands as evidence that the theosis impulse is not a late development in Christianity but is present from its earliest literary witnesses.

§6.11 · Epistle of Barnabas 6.11 (Kraft/Prigent, SC 172)
Manuscript
Greek

ἐπεὶ οὖν ἀνακαινίσας ἡμᾶς ἐν τῇ ἀφέσει τῶν ἁμαρτιῶν ἐποίησεν ἡμᾶς ἄλλον τύπον · ὡς παιδίων ἔχειν τὴν ψυχήν · ὡς ἂν δὴ ἀναπλάσσοντος αὐτοῦ ἡμᾶς ·

English

Since, then, he renewed us by the remission of sins, he made us another type (typon, τύπον), so that we should have the soul of children, as if he were creating us afresh (anaplassontos, ἀναπλάσσοντος).

§6.12 · Epistle of Barnabas 6.12 (Kraft/Prigent, SC 172)
Manuscript
Greek

λέγει γὰρ ἡ γραφὴ περὶ ἡμῶν ὡς λέγει τῷ υἱῷ · ποιήσωμεν κατ’ εἰκόνα καὶ καθ’ ὁμοίωσιν ἡμῶν ἄνθρωπον ·

English

For the scripture says about us, as he said to the Son: “Let us make humankind according to our image and according to our likeness, and let them rule over the beasts of the earth, and the birds of the heaven, and the fish of the sea.” And the Lord said, on seeing our good creation: “Increase and multiply and fill the earth.” These things he said to the Son.

§6.13–14 · Epistle of Barnabas 6.13–14 (Kraft/Prigent, SC 172)
Manuscript
Greek

πάλιν ἐπιδείξω σοι πῶς πρὸς ἡμᾶς λέγει ὁ κύριος · δευτέραν πλάσιν ἐπ’ ἐσχάτων ἐποίησεν · λέγει δὲ κύριος · ἰδοὺ ποιῶ τὰ ἔσχατα ὡς τὰ πρῶτα ·

English

Again I will show you how the Lord speaks concerning us. He made a second creation (deuteran plasin, δευτέραν πλάσιν) in the last days. And the Lord says: “Behold, I make the last things as the first.” For this reason, then, the prophet proclaimed: “Enter into a land flowing with milk and honey, and rule over it.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §6.11: The verb anaplasso (ἀναπλάσσω) means “to form anew, to remould.” It uses the same root as plasso (πλάσσω)—the verb used in Genesis 2:7 (LXX) for God forming Adam from clay. The prefix ana- means “again.” The theological claim is explicit: what God did in Eden, God is doing again in Christ. But the second creation produces “another type” (ἄλλον τύπον)—not a repair of the old model but a new species. The “soul of children” echoes Jesus’s teaching in Matthew 18:3: “Unless you become like children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven.”
  2. §6.13–14: The “second creation” (δευτέρα πλάσις) is the theological key to the passage. The author of Barnabas reads Genesis not as history but as prophecy: the creation of Adam was always a prefiguration of the re-creation in Christ. “I make the last things as the first” means that the eschaton recapitulates the beginning—but surpasses it. The second creation is not a return to Eden but an advance beyond it. This is the same logic Irenaeus will later develop into his doctrine of recapitulation: Christ does not merely undo the Fall but completes the creation that Adam’s fall interrupted.
