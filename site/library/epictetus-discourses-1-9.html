Introduction

In Discourses I.9, Epictetus argues that human beings are akin to God because they share in the divine reason. This kinship is not bodily but intellectual: the rational faculty within each person is a fragment of the divine nature.

§I.9.1–6 · Epictetus, Discourses I.9.1–6 (Schenkl)
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ ταῦτα ἀληθῆ λέγεται ὑπὸ τῶν φιλοσόφων περὶ τῆς συγγενείας τοῦ θεοῦ καὶ ἀνθρώπων

English

If what the philosophers say about the kinship of God and humanity is true, then what remains for people but the answer of Socrates when asked where he was from—not “Athenian” or “Corinthian,” but “a citizen of the cosmos”? You are a fragment of God; you carry a part of him within you.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.9.1–6: The claim that humans carry a “fragment of God” (ἀπόσπασμα τοῦ θεοῦ) directly parallels the Christian and Neoplatonic doctrine that the human soul participates in the divine nature. The early Church Fathers drew extensively on Stoic language of divine kinship.
