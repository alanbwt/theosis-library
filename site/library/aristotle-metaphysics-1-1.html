Introduction

The Metaphysics opens with Aristotle’s famous declaration that all human beings by nature desire to know. He traces the ascent from sensation through memory and experience to the theoretical sciences, culminating in wisdom (sophia).

§980a–981a · Aristotle, Metaphysics 980a21–981a12 (Ross, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

πάντες ἄνθρωποι τοῦ εἰδέναι ὀρέγονται φύσει

English

All human beings by nature desire to know. A sign of this is the delight we take in our senses; for apart from their usefulness, they are loved for their own sake, and above all the sense of sight. The animals other than man live by appearances and memories but have little share of experience; the human race lives also by art and reasoning.

Translator's Notes

  1. §980a: The opening sentence became one of the most quoted lines in the history of philosophy. The verb ὀρέγονται (“desire,” “reach for”) implies not mere curiosity but a fundamental orientation of human nature toward understanding—a claim the Neoplatonists took as evidence that the soul is naturally drawn upward toward the intelligible.
