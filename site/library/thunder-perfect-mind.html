Thunder, Perfect Mind: I Am the First and the Last

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

Thunder, Perfect Mind is unlike anything else in the Nag Hammadi library—or in ancient literature. A female divine figure speaks in a torrent of paradoxical self-declarations: “I am the first and the last. I am the honored one and the scorned one. I am the whore and the holy one.” The text has no narrative, no mythology, no cosmological system. It is pure revelation in the form of contradiction, a voice that refuses to be placed in any single category because it encompasses all categories.

The speaker has been identified variously as Sophia, as Isis, as the divine Feminine, or as a figure with no parallel in any known tradition. The Coptic text survives in Nag Hammadi Codex VI. The rhetorical form—the extended “I am” declaration—recalls the aretalogies of Isis and the “I am” statements of Christ in the Gospel of John. What makes this text extraordinary for the theosis question is its insistence that the divine is not found by escaping contradiction but by inhabiting all of it simultaneously.

Translator's Notes

  1. §13.16–14.9: The paradoxical “I am” declarations follow the rhetorical pattern of the Isis aretalogies—hymns in which the goddess Isis proclaims her attributes and powers. But the Isis aretalogies accumulate positive attributes (“I am the queen of every land, I am she who gave laws to mankind”), while Thunder systematically pairs each positive with its opposite. This is not mere literary play. The theology is precise: the divine contains and transcends every opposition. This echoes the Neoplatonic understanding of the One as beyond all predication, knowable only through the coincidence of opposites.
  2. §18.6–20: The declaration “I am knowledge and ignorance” is particularly striking in a Gnostic context, where gnosis (knowledge) is the supreme value and ignorance is the fundamental problem. For this speaker to claim both is to stand outside the Gnostic system even while speaking from within it. The text resists every attempt at doctrinal domestication—it cannot be made to serve any single theological program, which may be precisely its point.
