Introduction

Lactantius’s Divinae Institutiones (c. 304–311 CE) is a systematic Christian apologetic composed during the Great Persecution. Book I attacks pagan religion by arguing that the pagans themselves, through their own philosophers, have recognized the one true God.

Greek original and English translation.
§Book I.5.1–3 · Lactantius, Divinae Institutiones I.5.1–3
Manuscript
Greek

Diximus falsum esse deorum cultum, quoniam homines fuerunt. Nunc doceamus unum solum Deum colendum. Ne inter ipsos quidem philosophos quisquam fuit paulo insignior qui non videret unum esse Deum qui universa moderetur.

English

We have said that the worship of the gods is false, because they were men. Let us now show that the one God alone is to be worshipped. Even among the pagans there was no philosopher of any prominence who did not perceive that the universe is governed by one God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Book I.5.1–3: Lactantius’s strategy of citing pagan philosophers as unwitting witnesses to monotheism was enormously influential. This “preparation for the Gospel” argument allowed Christians to claim the best of classical philosophy as their own inheritance.
