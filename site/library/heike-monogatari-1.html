The Tale of the Heike: The Bells of Gion

The Tale of the Heike: The Bells of Gion
Japanese Court Scribes, c. 8th century AD
Kakuichi recension (1371)

Introduction

The Tale of the Heike opens with the most quoted lines in Japanese literature — a Buddhist meditation on impermanence that sets the frame for the epic destruction of the Taira warrior clan. Composed in the 13th century and chanted by blind monks to biwa accompaniment, it occupies the same place in Japanese culture that the Iliad holds in the West.

§1.1 · Heike 1.1
Japanese

祇園精舎の鐘の声、諸行無常の響きあり。沙羅双樹の花の色、盛者必衰の理をあらわす。

English

The sound of the bells of Gion echoes the impermanence of all things. The color of the sala flowers reveals the truth that the prosperous must decline.
