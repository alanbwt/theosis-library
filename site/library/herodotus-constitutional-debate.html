Histories III.80-82: Democracy vs Monarchy vs Oligarchy

Herodotus, c. 484-425 BCE

Introduction

The Persian Constitutional Debate in Herodotus’ Histories (III.80–82) is the earliest recorded discussion of comparative government in Western literature. Set in 522 BCE after the overthrow of the false Smerdis, three Persian nobles—Otanes, Megabyzus, and Darius—debate what form of government Persia should adopt. Otanes argues for democracy (the rule of the many), Megabyzus for oligarchy (the rule of the best few), and Darius for monarchy (the rule of one excellent man). Darius wins the argument, and Persia remains a monarchy.

Whether this debate actually took place is doubtful—Herodotus himself reports that some Greeks refuse to believe Persians would discuss democracy. But the historical question is less important than the intellectual one. The debate lays out the core arguments for and against each form of government with a clarity that later political philosophy would elaborate but never fundamentally surpass. Each speaker identifies the characteristic virtue of his preferred system and the characteristic vice of the alternatives: monarchy is decisive but prone to tyranny, oligarchy is competent but breeds faction, democracy is egalitarian but descends into mob rule.

§III.80 (Otanes for Democracy) · Herodotus, Histories III.80
Manuscript
Greek

Ὄτανης μὲν ἐκέλευε ἐς μέσον Πέρσῃσι καταθεῖναι τὰ πρήγματα, λέγων τάδε· “ἐμοὶ δοκέει ἕνα μὲν ἡμέων μηδένα μούναρχον γενέσθαι· οὔτε γὰρ ἡδὺ οὔτε ἀγαθόν.”

English

Otanes urged the Persians to put the government into the hands of the people. He said: “I hold that one man should no longer be sole ruler over us. That is neither pleasant nor good. You saw the insolence of Cambyses, how far it went, and you have had experience of the insolence of the Magus. How can monarchy be a well-ordered thing, when the ruler can do what he wishes with no accountability? Give this power to the best of men, and it would stir him to unaccustomed thoughts. Insolence is bred in him by the good things that he possesses, and envy is planted in man from the beginning. Having these two, he has all wickedness.”

§III.80 (Otanes concludes) · Herodotus, Histories III.80
Manuscript
Greek

πλῆθος δὲ ἄρχον πρῶτα μὲν οὔνομα πάντων κάλλιστον ἔχει, ἰσονομίην.

English

“But the rule of the many has, first, the fairest of all names—equality before the law (isonomia, ἰσονομίη). And second, it does none of the things that a monarch does. It holds office by lot, it renders account of its authority, and it refers all deliberation to the public. I therefore propose that we abolish monarchy and raise the people to power; for in the many lies the whole.”

§III.81 (Megabyzus for Oligarchy) · Herodotus, Histories III.81
Manuscript
Greek

Μεγάβυζος δὲ ὀλιγαρχίῃ ἐκέλευε ἐπιτρέπειν, λέγων τάδε· “ὅσα μὲν Ὄτανης εἶπε τυραννίδα παύων, λελέχθω κἀμοὶ ταῦτα.”

English

Megabyzus spoke in favour of oligarchy: “What Otanes says to put an end to tyranny, let that be my opinion also. But when he urges us to give the power to the multitude, he has strayed from good judgment. For there is nothing more lacking in understanding or more insolent than a useless mob. To escape the insolence of a tyrant only to fall into the insolence of an unbridled populace—that is intolerable. The tyrant at least knows what he does; the mob does not even know that much. Let us choose a company of the best men and invest them with power. For among the best men will be found the best counsels.”

§III.82 (Darius for Monarchy) · Herodotus, Histories III.82
Manuscript
Greek

Δαρεῖος δὲ τρίτος ἀπεδείκνυτο γνώμην· “ἐμοὶ δὲ τὰ μὲν Μεγάβυζος ἐς τὸ πλῆθος εἶπε ὀρθῶς δοκέει εἰρῆσθαι, τὰ δὲ ἐς ὀλιγαρχίην οὐκ ὀρθῶς.”

English

Darius spoke third: “What Megabyzus said against the rule of the people seems right to me, but not what he said about oligarchy. Take the three forms of government—democracy, oligarchy, monarchy—and suppose each to be the best of its kind. I say the best monarchy far surpasses the others. For nothing can appear better than the one best man. Using the best judgment, he will govern the multitude blamelessly; and in this form of government, plots against enemies are best kept secret. In an oligarchy, where many men compete for distinction, violent personal feuds are apt to arise, which lead to factions, and from factions come murders. From murders it comes round to monarchy—and this shows how much that form is the best.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.80: Otanes uses the term isonomia (ἰσονομίη), “equality before the law,” rather than demokratia to describe popular government. Some scholars argue that isonomia was the older and more respectable term, and that demokratia was originally coined as a hostile label by the aristocratic opposition, meaning “mob-power.” If so, the word “democracy” began as a slur.
  2. §III.82: Darius’s argument contains a proto-version of the cycle of constitutions that Polybius would later systematize: oligarchy breeds faction, faction produces tyranny, and tyranny (when enlightened) is simply good monarchy. The circularity of the argument—monarchy is best because all other forms eventually collapse into it—is either a profound observation about political entropy or a self-serving justification by the man who became king. Herodotus leaves the reader to judge.
