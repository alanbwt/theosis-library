2 Corinthians 4:6-7: Light in Our Hearts

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

In 2 Corinthians 4:6–7, Paul makes one of the most extraordinary theological connections in all his letters. He reaches back to the first act of creation—“Let light shine out of darkness” (Genesis 1:3)—and declares that the same God who spoke light into existence has now shone that light in the hearts of believers. The light of creation and the light of Christ are the same light. The implication is staggering: the person who encounters Christ is not receiving new information but experiencing a second Genesis, a new creation at the most fundamental level.

Then comes the qualification that prevents this from becoming triumphalism: “But we have this treasure in earthen vessels.” The divine light dwells in clay pots. The glory of God inhabits fragile, breakable, mortal human bodies. This is the Pauline version of the theosis paradox: the highest reality dwells in the lowest container. The text is from Codex Sinaiticus.

English

But we have this treasure in earthen vessels (ostrakinois skeuesin, ὀστρακίνοις σκεύεσιν), so that the surpassing greatness of the power may be of God and not from us.

Translator's Notes

  1. §4:6: Paul’s chain of genitives—“the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ”—is not clumsy writing but careful theology. Each term nests inside the next: the face of Christ reveals the glory of God; the glory of God is apprehended as knowledge (gnosis, γνῶσις); that knowledge manifests as light (photismos, φωτισμός). Paul uses the Gnostic keyword gnosis without hesitation, but he grounds it in a specific face—the face of the historical Jesus. The Gnostic tradition would later detach knowledge from incarnation; Paul insists they are inseparable.
  2. §4:7: The “earthen vessels” (ostrakina skeue) are cheap clay jars—the disposable containers of the ancient world. The image is deliberately undignified. Paul has just described the most exalted reality imaginable—the light of creation dwelling in human hearts—and immediately insists that the containers are fragile, common, and breakable. This is the anti-Gnostic move: the Gnostics sought to escape the material vessel; Paul says the treasure requires the vessel. The fragility is the point. If the vessel were golden, the observer would credit the vessel. Because it is clay, the power is obviously God’s.
