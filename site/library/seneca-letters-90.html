Introduction

In Epistulae Morales 90, Seneca debates whether philosophy invented the practical arts. He argues against Posidonius that philosophy’s true domain is not technical innovation but moral wisdom—teaching human beings how to live in accordance with nature.

§Ep. 90.1–5 · Seneca, Epistulae Morales 90.1–5 (Reynolds, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Quis dubitare potest quin vitam deorum immortalium munere acceperimus, philosophiae quod bene vivimus?

English

Who can doubt that life is a gift of the immortal gods, but that living well is a gift of philosophy? Philosophy’s single concern has always been the soul. She has investigated nature not to sharpen the skills of artisans but to understand the laws that govern the universe and to teach the soul its place within the whole.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ep. 90.1–5: The distinction between living (vita) as a gift of the gods and living well (bene vivere) as a gift of philosophy captures the Stoic view that nature provides the raw material but wisdom alone shapes it into a good life.
