Ladder of Divine Ascent, Step 30: Love Is Theosis

John Climacus, c. 579-649 AD

Introduction

John Climacus (c. 579–649) was the abbot of the Monastery of Saint Catherine on Mount Sinai and the author of the Ladder of Divine Ascent (Klimax tou Paradeisou), one of the most widely read books in Eastern Christianity. The Ladder describes thirty steps of spiritual progress, corresponding to the thirty years of Christ’s hidden life before his public ministry. Each step addresses a specific virtue or vice, moving from renunciation of the world (Step 1) to the summit of the spiritual life (Step 30).

Step 30 is the crown of the entire work. It treats the three supreme virtues—faith, hope, and love—and identifies love as the highest. In a passage of extraordinary compression and beauty, John declares that love is God himself, that whoever possesses love possesses God, and that the person who has reached the thirtieth step has become, insofar as is possible for human nature, like God. The final step of the Ladder is theosis itself: the human being who has climbed through all the stages of purification and illumination arrives at union with the divine through love.

Now we have reached the end of the journey. I have reached the summit of the Ladder. And here at the top stand three, bound together in one: faith, hope, and love. But the greatest of these is love, for God himself is called Love.

§30.2 · PG 88, col. 1153
Manuscript
Greek

Ἀγάπη κατὰ μὲν ποιότητα ὁμοίωσίς ἐστι Θεοῦ

English

Love, by its very nature, is a resemblance to God, insofar as that is humanly possible. In its activity it is inebriation of the soul. In its distinctive properties, it is a fountain of faith, an abyss of patience, a sea of humility.

§30.3-4 · PG 88, col. 1156
Manuscript
Greek

Ἀγάπη ἐξορισμός πάσης ἐναντιότητος

English

Love is the banishment of every form of contrariness, for love thinks no evil. Love, the holy state of the soul, is the door to contemplation. The person who has attained love has attained God. For “God is love.”

§30.5-7 · PG 88, col. 1156
Manuscript
Greek

Εἴ τις με ἐρωτήσειεν, τί ἐστιν ἀγάπη

English

If someone were to ask me what love is, I would say: it is the banishment of every thought contrary to love. Love is a state of the soul that goes beyond nature and beyond words. One who has tasted it knows that it is fire. And one who has not tasted it cannot imagine what it is.

§30.8-10 · PG 88, col. 1157
Manuscript
Greek

Ἀγάπη προφητείας πρόξενος. Ἀγάπη θαυμάτων αἰτία.

English

Love is the cause of prophecy. Love is the cause of miracles. Love is the abyss of illumination. Love is the fountain of fire: the more it gushes forth, the more the thirsty one burns with desire. Love is the establishment of angels. Love is the progress of eternity.

§30.11-14 · PG 88, col. 1160
Manuscript

Εἰπὲ ἡμῖν, ὦ ἀγάπη, τυφλοί γὰρ χωρὶς σοῦ

English

Tell us, O love, for we are blind without you. Tell us, O holy love, where do you pasture your flock? Where do you rest at noon? Enlighten us, quench our thirst, guide us, take us by the hand. §30.15-18 · PG 88, col. 1160
Manuscript
Greek

Ὁ τούτου τοῦ βαθμοῦ ἐπιβάς

English

The one who has reached this step, let him ask for more, if indeed there is anything beyond this. He has set his foot, while still in the flesh, in the courts of heaven. Let him now consider: is this the end, or is there still a beginning? For I believe that this is only the beginning of the light, and that the fullness of light is that which eye has not seen, and ear has not heard, and which has not entered into the heart of man.

Translator's Notes

  1. §30.2: John’s definition of love as “a resemblance to God, insofar as that is humanly possible” (ὁμοίωσις Θεοῦ) deliberately echoes Genesis 1:26 (“Let us make man in our image and likeness”). The distinction between “image” (eikon) and “likeness” (homoiosis) was fundamental in patristic anthropology: the image is given at creation; the likeness is achieved through the spiritual life. Love is the actualization of the divine likeness.
  2. §30.5-7: The admission that love is “beyond nature and beyond words” places John in the apophatic tradition: the highest realities cannot be captured in language. But he immediately follows with the experiential claim that “one who has tasted it knows.” This tension between the inadequacy of language and the certainty of experience is characteristic of the greatest mystical writers.
  3. §30.15-18: The final passage is remarkable for its refusal to close the journey. Even at the summit of the Ladder, John insists that this is only “the beginning of the light.” The citation of 1 Corinthians 2:9 points beyond all human experience to a fullness that remains eschatological—not yet, not in this life. The Ladder ends not with arrival but with anticipation, and this is perhaps the most honest thing that can be said about theosis: it is a journey whose destination exceeds every stage of the journey.
