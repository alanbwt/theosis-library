Myth of Adapa: Immortality Refused

Anonymous (Babylonian), c. 1100 BCE

Introduction

The Myth of Adapa is one of the oldest stories about a human being offered immortality and failing to receive it. Adapa, the sage of Eridu and priest of the god Ea, is summoned before the supreme god Anu after breaking the wing of the South Wind. Ea, his patron, warns him not to eat or drink anything offered in heaven, claiming it will be the food and water of death. But Anu, impressed by Adapa’s wisdom, offers him the food and water of life—eternal life. Following his god’s deceptive counsel, Adapa refuses. He is sent back to earth, mortal.

The Akkadian text survives in fragments from the library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh (seventh century BCE) and from the Amarna archive in Egypt (fourteenth century BCE). The parallel to Genesis 3 is unmistakable: a human in a garden-like state, a test involving food, divine knowledge gained but immortality lost. But where Genesis blames human disobedience, the Adapa myth blames divine deception—Ea deliberately prevented his own priest from becoming immortal. The gods, it seems, do not want company.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragment B, i.1–14: The phrase “created him as a model of men” uses the Akkadian banitu, the same verb used for divine creation in the Enuma Elish. Adapa is not merely a wise man; he is a specially created being, a prototype. The parallel to the creation of Adam in Genesis 2:7 is direct. Both are formed by a god, both serve in a sacred precinct, and both face a test involving forbidden consumption. The key difference: Adam is forbidden to eat and does; Adapa is forbidden to eat and obeys—yet both lose immortality.
  2. §Fragment B, iii.60–75: Anu’s laughter is one of the most theologically disturbing moments in Mesopotamian literature. The supreme god is not angry—he is amused. He offered genuine immortality, and Adapa refused it on the advice of his own patron deity. The implication is devastating: the gods do not prevent human deification through hostility but through the very structures of divine-human relationship. Ea’s “protection” of his priest is the instrument of that priest’s loss. The story suggests that the barrier between mortal and immortal is maintained not by cosmic law but by divine politics.
