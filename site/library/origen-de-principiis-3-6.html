De Principiis III.6.1: The End Is Like the Beginning

Περὶ Ἀρχῶν
Origen of Alexandria, c. 185–253 AD

Introduction

Origen’s De Principiis (On First Principles), composed around 220–230 CE, is the first systematic theology ever written. Much of it survives only in Rufinus’s Latin translation or in fragments preserved by later authors, since Origen’s theology was condemned at the Fifth Ecumenical Council in 553. Book III, chapter 6 addresses the consummation of all things — the apokatastasis, the final restoration.

Origen’s vision here is breathtaking in scope: the end mirrors the beginning, and God will ultimately be “all in all” (1 Corinthians 15:28). This is theosis understood not as an individual achievement but as a cosmic event. The passage was controversial in its own time and remains so, because it implies universal salvation — that nothing and no one falls permanently outside God’s final unity.

§III.6.1 · De Principiis III.6.1 (Koetschau ed., GCS 22; Greek fragments via Justinian, Ep. ad Mennam)
Manuscript
Greek

Τέλος αεί τη αρχή όμοιον. Ώσπερ ουν εν πάντων τέλος, ουτως μίαν αρχήν πάντων νοείν δεί· και ως ενός τέλους πολλών όντος, ουτως απο μιας αρχής πολλαί διαφοραί και ποικιλίαι, αι πάλιν δια της χρηστότητος του Θεου αποκαθίστανται εις εν τέλος όμοιον τη αρχή· ίνα η ο Θεός τα πάντα εν πασιν.

English

The end is always like the beginning. As there is one end of all things, so we must understand that there is one beginning of all things, and as there is one end of many things, so from one beginning arise many differences and varieties, which in their turn are restored, through God’s goodness, through their subjection to Christ and their unity with the Holy Spirit, to one end which is like the beginning. That is to say, all those who bend the knee to the name of Jesus and thereby declare their subjection to him shall, in the restitution of all things, be gathered into one end. And God shall be all in all.

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.6.1: The Greek survives only in fragments; the full passage is known primarily through Rufinus’s Latin translation, which Rufinus himself admitted he had softened to make Origen sound more orthodox. The phrase “God shall be all in all” (ο Θεός τα πάντα εν πασιν) quotes 1 Corinthians 15:28. For Origen, this is not a distant hope but a metaphysical certainty: the structure of reality requires that multiplicity return to unity. The later condemnation focused on whether this implies that even the devil would be saved — a question Origen raised but never definitively answered.
