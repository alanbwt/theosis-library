Bardo Thodol: The Clear Light of Death

Gautama Buddha (attributed), c. 563-483 BCE

Introduction

The Bardo Thodol (Liberation through Hearing in the Intermediate State), commonly known as the Tibetan Book of the Dead, is a Tibetan Buddhist text attributed to Padmasambhava (8th century CE) and rediscovered as a terma (treasure text) by Karma Lingpa in the 14th century. It describes the experiences of consciousness between death and rebirth, and serves as a practical guide read aloud to the dying and the recently dead.

The text’s central teaching is that the clear light that appears at the moment of death is the luminous nature of mind itself—Buddha-nature (tathagatagarbha). If the dying person recognises this light as their own awareness rather than something external and terrifying, they achieve liberation. The theological parallel to theosis is precise: the divine is not distant but is the deepest nature of consciousness itself, and the spiritual task is recognition, not acquisition.

English

O nobly born, listen without distraction. At this moment, the clear light of the true nature will dawn upon you. Recognise it. O nobly born, your present awareness—this very instant of naked, luminous, empty knowing—is the dharmakaya, the body of truth. Your own awareness, naturally void, not formed as anything whatsoever, is the real.

§Recognition of the Clear Light · Karma Lingpa, Bardo Thodol, f. 2a–2b
Manuscript
Tibetan

རིག་པ་གསལ་གཉིས་བགརང་བ་འདི་ཀུན་བཟང་པོ་སངས་རྒྱས་ཀི་འོད་གསལ་ཡིན།

English

This naked awareness, bright and empty, dwelling in no particular form, is the light of Samantabhadra, the primordial Buddha. Recognising it is liberation. Not recognising it, you will wander in the six realms of existence. At this very moment, the nature of your mind is the Buddha. There is nothing to seek outside yourself.

§Instructions for the Bardo · Karma Lingpa, Bardo Thodol, f. 5a
Manuscript
Tibetan

རིགས་ཀི་བུ་ཁྱོད་སངས་བ་མ་ཡེངས་བསངས་རྒྱས་ཀི་མཐ་མ།

English

O nobly born, whatever terrifying visions may appear—wrathful deities, thundering sounds, blazing lights—recognise them all as the natural radiance of your own mind. They are not external. They are not other. They are the energy of your own awareness, appearing in forms shaped by your karma. Do not flee from them. Do not grasp at them. Rest in recognition, and you are free.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Moment of Death: The identification of present awareness with the dharmakaya (the “truth body” of the Buddha) is the central soteriological claim of the Bardo Thodol. It rests on the Dzogchen teaching that the nature of mind is primordially pure and awakened. Liberation is not a change of state but a recognition of what has always been the case—a structure remarkably parallel to the Christian teaching that theosis restores the divine image already present in human nature.
  2. §Instructions for the Bardo: The instruction not to flee from terrifying visions but to recognise them as projections of one’s own mind is the practical core of the text. Fear arises from mistaking one’s own mental projections for external realities. Liberation arises from seeing through this mistake. The parallel to apophatic theology is striking: in both traditions, the obstacle to union with the divine is not distance but misperception.
