On Loving God: The Four Degrees of Love

Bernard of Clairvaux, 1090-1153 AD

Introduction

Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153) was the most influential Christian figure of the twelfth century: a Cistercian monk who founded sixty-eight monasteries, preached the Second Crusade, arbitrated papal elections, and wrote some of the most passionate mystical theology in the Western tradition. His treatise De Diligendo Deo (On Loving God), written around 1126–1141, maps the soul’s journey through four degrees of love.

Chapter 10 describes the fourth and final degree: the love in which the self is entirely lost in God. Bernard admits that he does not know whether this state can be fully attained in this life, but he describes it with extraordinary precision—the self dissolved like a drop of water in wine, like iron glowing in fire, like air flooded with sunlight. The self does not cease to exist, but it loses all self-referential awareness. This is Western theosis at its most radical, and Bernard’s analogies—water in wine, iron in fire—became the standard vocabulary for mystical union across all subsequent Western theology.

§10.27 · SBO III, p. 142
Manuscript
Latin

Sic affici deificari est. Quomodo stilla aquae modica multo infusa vino deficere a se tota videtur.

English

To lose yourself in some way, as if you did not exist, and to have no awareness of yourself at all, and to be emptied out of yourself and almost annihilated—this belongs to heavenly intercourse, not to human feeling.

§10.28 · SBO III, p. 143
Manuscript
Latin

Quomodo stilla aquae modica multo infusa vino deficere a se tota videtur, dum et saporem vini induit et colorem; et quomodo ferrum ignitum et candens igni simillimum fit, pristina propriaque forma exutum; et quomodo solis luce perfusus aer in eandem transformatur luminis claritatem.

English

To be so affected is to be deified. As a small drop of water mixed with a great quantity of wine seems to lose itself entirely, taking on the taste and colour of the wine; as iron heated in the fire loses its own appearance and glows like fire; as air suffused with sunlight is transformed into the very clarity of the light, so that it seems not merely illuminated but to be the light itself—so it will be necessary for all human feeling toward the saints to melt, in some ineffable way, and to be poured entirely into the will of God.

§10.28 (cont.) · SBO III, p. 143
Manuscript
Latin

Alioquin quomodo omnia in omnibus erit Deus, si in homine de homine quicquam supererit? Manebit quidem substantia, sed in alia forma, alia gloria, alia potentia.

English

For how shall God be all in all, if anything of the human remains in the human? The substance will indeed remain, but in another form, another glory, another power.

§10.29 · SBO III, p. 144
Manuscript
Latin

Quando hoc erit? Quis hoc videbit? Quis possidebit? Quando veniam et apparebo ante faciem Dei?

English

When will this happen? Who will see this? Who will possess it? “When shall I come and appear before the face of God?” O Lord my God, “my heart has said to you: my face has sought you; your face, O Lord, will I seek.” Do you think that I shall see your holy temple?

§10.30 · SBO III, p. 144
Manuscript
Latin

Nec puto id posse contingere donec illud impleatur: Diliges Dominum Deum tuum ex toto corde tuo et ex tota anima tua et ex tota virtute tua.

English

I do not think that can fully happen until the word is fulfilled: “Love the Lord your God with your whole heart and your whole soul and your whole strength.” Not until the heart is no longer compelled to think of the body, nor the soul to give it life and feeling, and the body’s strength, freed from weakness, is made strong in the power of God. For it is impossible to gather all these faculties and turn them toward the face of God so long as the care of this frail and wretched body demands their attention.

Translator's Notes

  1. §10.28: The three analogies—water in wine, iron in fire, air in sunlight—became the standard Western vocabulary for describing mystical union without pantheistic absorption. Each analogy preserves the ontological distinction between the creature and God (the water does not become wine, the iron does not become fire) while insisting that the transformation is real and total in terms of experience and quality. Meister Eckhart, John of the Cross, and the entire Western mystical tradition would build on these images.
  2. §10.28 (cont.): Bernard’s careful qualification—“the substance will indeed remain, but in another form”—is theologically precise. The human being is not annihilated in the fourth degree of love; the substance persists. But its form, glory, and power are entirely transformed. This is the Western equivalent of the Eastern patristic formula: the human nature remains, but it is entirely permeated by the divine energies.
  3. §10.30: Bernard’s doubt about whether the fourth degree is fully attainable in this life is characteristic of his realism. Unlike some later mystics who claimed to have achieved permanent union with God, Bernard maintains an eschatological reserve: full theosis awaits the resurrection, when the body itself is transformed and no longer draws the soul’s attention away from God.
