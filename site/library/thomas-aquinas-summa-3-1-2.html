Summa Theologiae III.1.2: Sharers in His Divinity

Thomas Aquinas, 1225–1274 AD

Introduction

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) is the preeminent theologian of the Western scholastic tradition. His Summa Theologiae, left unfinished at his death, represents the most ambitious attempt in Christian history to synthesize the whole of theology into a single systematic framework. The Third Part (Tertia Pars) treats the Incarnation and the sacraments, beginning with the question of why God became man.

Question 1, Article 2 asks whether the Incarnation was necessary for human salvation. Thomas answers that it was not strictly necessary—God could have redeemed humanity by other means—but that it was supremely fitting. Among the reasons for this fittingness, Thomas cites the principle that the Son of God became man so that human beings might become “sharers in his divinity.” This language, drawn from 2 Peter 1:4 and mediated through the liturgical tradition, shows that even the most rigorously philosophical theologian of the medieval West preserved the patristic logic of the exchange.

§III.1.2 · Summa Theologiae III, q. 1, a. 2, resp.
Manuscript
Latin

Unigenitus Dei Filius, suae divinitatis volens nos esse participes, naturam nostram assumpsit, ut homines deos faceret factus homo.

English

The only-begotten Son of God, wanting to make us sharers in his divinity, assumed our nature, so that having become man he might make men gods.

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.1.2: Thomas is here quoting a text attributed to Augustine but actually deriving from the Offertory prayer of the Roman Mass (“O God, who wonderfully created and yet more wonderfully restored the dignity of human nature”). The phrase ut homines deos faceret factus homo (“so that having become man he might make men gods”) preserves the exact structure of the patristic exchange formula. Thomas’s use of participes (“sharers” or “partakers”) rather than a stronger term reflects his careful metaphysical framework: human beings participate in divinity by grace, not by a change of nature. This participatory ontology, developed through Aristotelian categories, is Thomas’s distinctive contribution to the theosis tradition.
