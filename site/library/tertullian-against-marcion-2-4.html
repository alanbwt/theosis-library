Against Marcion II.4: Image and Likeness

Tertullian, c. 155–220 AD

Introduction

Tertullian (c. 155–220) is the father of Latin theology, the first major Christian author to write in Latin rather than Greek. His Against Marcion, written in five books, is his longest and most systematic work, directed against the Gnostic teacher Marcion who rejected the Old Testament God as an inferior demiurge distinct from the Father of Jesus Christ. Tertullian defends the unity of the Creator and the Redeemer and, in doing so, articulates a theology of creation that grounds human dignity in the divine image.

Book II, Chapter 4 addresses the creation of humanity. Against Marcion’s denigration of the material world, Tertullian insists that the human being was made “in the image and likeness of God” by God’s own hands—a phrase he takes literally as referring to the Son and the Spirit. The dignity of the human form derives from its divine origin and its divine destiny.

§II.4 · Adversus Marcionem II.4 (CCSL 1)
Manuscript
Latin

Factus in imaginem Dei et similitudinem, a manibus Dei formatus est, id est Filii et Spiritus, quibus dixit: Faciamus hominem ad imaginem et similitudinem nostram. Et finxit Deus hominem de limo terrae, et insufflavit in faciem eius flatum vitae, et factus est homo in animam vivam.

English

Having been made in the image of God and the likeness, man was formed by the very hands of God—that is, by the Son and the Spirit—to whom he said: Let us make man in our image and likeness. And God formed man from the dust of the earth, and breathed into his face the breath of life, and man became a living soul.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II.4: Tertullian’s identification of the “hands of God” with the Son and the Spirit is an exegetical move with far-reaching theological consequences. It means that the Trinity is already active in creation, not merely in redemption. The distinction between imago (image) and similitudo (likeness) would be developed by later Latin theologians into a framework where the image is given at creation but the likeness is achieved through moral and spiritual growth—a framework that parallels the Eastern distinction between the image as given and deification as the fulfillment of human nature.
