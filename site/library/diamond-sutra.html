Diamond Sutra: Beyond Form and Non-Form

Gautama Buddha (attributed), c. 563-483 BCE

Introduction

The Diamond Sūtra (Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, “The Perfection of Wisdom that Cuts Like a Diamond”) is one of the most celebrated texts in the entire Buddhist canon. The earliest printed book in the world with a date—a Chinese scroll from 868 CE, now in the British Library—is a copy of this sutra. Sections 5 and 6 contain the Diamond Sūtra’s most distinctive teaching: the Buddha cannot be recognized through physical characteristics. Wherever there are material marks, there is delusion.

For the theosis question, the Diamond Sūtra poses a devastating challenge. If the awakened one cannot be identified through any form, characteristic, or quality, then what exactly is enlightenment? The sutra insists that the Tathāgata is not a body, not an appearance, not a set of attributes. He is recognized precisely by the absence of marks. This is the via negativa taken to its logical extreme—beyond anything Pseudo-Dionysius or Meister Eckhart dared.

§Section 5: Seeing the Truth · Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, §5
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Tat kiṃ manyase Subhūte lakṣaṇa-saṃpadā tathāgato draṣṭavyaḥ? No hīdaṃ Bhagavan, na lakṣaṇa-saṃpadā tathāgato draṣṭavyaḥ. Tat kasmād dhetoḥ? Yaiṣā Bhagavān lakṣaṇa-saṃpadā bhāṣitā, saivālakṣaṇa-saṃpat. Bhagavān āha: Yāvat Subhūte lakṣaṇa-saṃpadas tāvan mṛṣā. Yadā sarva-lakṣaṇāny alakṣaṇāni paśyasi tadā tathāgataṃ paśyasi.

English

“Subhūti, what do you think? Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of bodily characteristics?” “No, World-Honoured One. The Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of bodily characteristics. Why? Because the bodily characteristics spoken of by the Tathāgata are not bodily characteristics.” The Buddha said to Subhūti: “Wherever there are material characteristics there is delusion. If you see all characteristics as non-characteristics, then you see the Tathāgata.”

§Section 6: Rare is True Faith · Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, §6
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Āha āyuṣmān Subhūtir Bhagavantam etad avocat: Asti Bhagavan ke cit sattvā bhavisyanti anagata-adhvani pascime kāle pascime samaye pascimāyāṃ pañcaśatyāṃ saddharma-vipralope vartamāne ya imeṣv evaṃrūpeṣu sūtrānta-padeṣu bhāṣyamāṣu bhavat-saṃjñām utpanādāsyānti? Bhagavān āha: Mā Subhūte evaṃ vocaḥ. Bhavisyanti ke cit sattvāḥ śīlavāntaḥ guṇavantaḥ prajñāvantaḥ tatra praśraddadhāsyānti. Na te ekam buddhaṃ dvāv api pañca api buddhān paricaryāṃ kṛtvā, apramāṇaiḥ buddha-śata-sahasraiḥ kuśala-mūlāny avropāyānti.

English

Subhūti said to the Buddha: “World-Honoured One, will there be living beings in the future who, hearing these words, will have true faith in them?” The Buddha said: “Subhūti, do not speak thus. In the last five hundred years before the final end, there will be those who keep the precepts and cultivate merit, who will be able to give rise to a thought of faith in these words and accept them as truth. Know that such persons have not planted good roots under one Buddha, two Buddhas, three, four, or five Buddhas, but have planted good roots under measureless tens of thousands of Buddhas.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Section 5: The phrase “wherever there are material characteristics there is delusion” (yāvat lakṣaṇa-saṃpadas tāvan mṛṣā) is the Diamond Sūtra’s most famous line and its most radical claim. Lakṣaṇa means “mark” or “characteristic”—it is the same term used to describe the thirty-two marks of a great man (mahāpuruṣa-lakṣaṇa) that supposedly identify a Buddha. The sutra is saying that even the traditional physical marks of Buddhahood are delusion. This is not iconoclasm but ontology: any attempt to fix awakening in a form betrays it.
  2. §Section 6: The reference to the “last five hundred years” reflects the Buddhist cosmological scheme of dharma-decline (saddharma-vipralopa), in which the teaching gradually degenerates over time. The claim that even in the worst age there will be those who understand this teaching—because they have cultivated merit under “measureless tens of thousands of Buddhas”—links the present moment to an inconceivably vast karmic past. Understanding the Diamond Sūtra is not an intellectual achievement but the fruition of aeons of spiritual practice.
