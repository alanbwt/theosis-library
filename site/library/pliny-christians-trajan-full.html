Introduction

Pliny the Younger’s letter to Emperor Trajan (c. 112 CE) and Trajan’s reply constitute the earliest Roman administrative correspondence about Christians. Pliny, governing Bithynia, asks how to handle accused Christians and describes their practices.

§Pliny, Epistulae X.96.7 · Pliny, Epistulae X.96.7
Manuscript
Greek

Affirmabant autem hanc fuisse summam vel culpae suae vel erroris, quod essent soliti stato die ante lucem convenire carmenque Christo quasi deo dicere secum invicem.

English

They declared that the sum total of their guilt or error amounted to this: they were accustomed to meet on a fixed day before dawn and sing responsively a hymn to Christ as to a god, and to bind themselves by oath not to commit any wickedness.

§Trajan’s reply, X.97 · Pliny, Epistulae X.97
Manuscript
Greek

Actum quem debuisti, mi Secunde, in excutiendis causis eorum qui Christiani ad te delati fuerant secutus es. Conquirendi non sunt; si deferantur et arguantur, puniendi sunt.

English

You have followed the right course of procedure, my dear Pliny. These people must not be sought out; if they are denounced and proved guilty, they must be punished, but with this reservation: whoever denies that he is a Christian and proves it by worshipping our gods shall be pardoned.

Translator's Notes

  1. §X.96.7: Pliny’s description of Christians singing “a hymn to Christ as to a god” (carmenque Christo quasi deo) is the earliest pagan testimony to Christian worship. The phrase “as to a god” (quasi deo) reveals Roman uncertainty about whether Christ was being worshipped as a deity or merely honored.
