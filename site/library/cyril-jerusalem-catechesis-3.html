Catechetical Lecture 3: You Have Become Christs

Cyril of Jerusalem, c. 313-386 AD

Introduction

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 313–386) was bishop of Jerusalem during one of the most turbulent periods in early Christian history. His Catechetical Lectures were delivered to baptismal candidates during Lent, preparing them for initiation at Easter. They are among the most important witnesses to early Christian liturgical practice and baptismal theology.

Lecture 3 concerns baptism and the anointing (chrismation) that follows it. Cyril makes an extraordinary claim: through the anointing with holy oil, the baptized person becomes a “Christ”—an anointed one. The name “Christian” is not merely a social label but an ontological description: Christians are those who have been anointed with the same Holy Spirit that anointed Jesus, and who therefore participate in Christ’s divine nature. This is theosis through sacramental participation, grounded not in mystical experience or philosophical argument but in the concrete ritual of oil on skin.

§3.1 · PG 33, col. 425
Manuscript
Greek

Εἰς Χριστὸν βαπτισθέντες καὶ Χριστὸν ἐνδυσάμενοι, συμμόρφους τοῦ Υἱοῦ τοῦ Θεοῦ γεγένησθε.

English

Baptized into Christ and having put on Christ, you have been made conformable to the Son of God. For God, having predestined us for adoption, made us share the form of Christ’s glorious body. Having become partakers of Christ, you are properly called “Christs.”

§3.2 · PG 33, col. 428
Manuscript
Greek

Τούτου τοῦ χρίσματος Χριστιανοὶ καλεῖσθε

English

Of this anointing you are called Christians, being born again through water and the Holy Spirit. For Christ was not anointed by human beings with oil or material ointment, but the Father, having appointed him Saviour of the whole world, anointed him with the Holy Spirit.

§3.3 · PG 33, col. 429
Manuscript
Greek

Καὶ ὑμῖν ὁμοίως μετὰ τὸ ἀναβῆναι ἐκ τῆς κολυμβήθρας

English

And to you in like manner, after you had come up from the pool of the sacred waters, there was given the chrism, the antitype of that with which Christ was anointed; and this is the Holy Spirit. For as Christ after his baptism, and the descent of the Holy Spirit upon him, went forth and vanquished the adversary, so likewise with you.

§3.4 · PG 33, col. 432
Manuscript
Greek

Μετὰ τὸ χρῖσμα οὖν Χριστοὶ καλεῖσθε

English

After the anointing, you are now called “Christs,” being made worthy of this name by the anointing. For before you were deemed worthy of this grace, you had no real right to this title, but were advancing on your way toward being Christians.

§3.5-6 · PG 33, col. 433
Manuscript
Greek

Τοῦτο τὸ χρῖσμα τηρήσατε ἄσπιλον

English

Keep this chrism unspotted: for it teaches you concerning all things, if it remain in you, as you have heard the blessed John say, speaking much concerning this chrism. For this holy thing is a spiritual safeguard of the body and salvation of the soul. The ointment is applied to your forehead, and to the organs of sense: the body is anointed with the visible ointment, and the soul is sanctified by the Holy and life-giving Spirit.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3.1: The claim that baptized Christians are “properly called Christs” (the plural is deliberate) is among the boldest theosis statements in patristic literature. Cyril is not speaking metaphorically. The Greek Christos means “anointed one”; Christians who have been anointed with chrism share in the same anointing as Christ and therefore share in his identity. This is sacramental theosis: the ritual effects an ontological change.
  2. §3.3: The word “antitype” (αντίτυπν) is a technical term in early Christian typological thinking. The chrism given to the baptized is the “antitype”—the corresponding reality—of the Holy Spirit that descended on Christ at the Jordan. This language implies that the sacramental anointing is not merely a symbol of the Spirit but participates in the same reality.
  3. §3.5-6: Cyril’s reference to 1 John 2:27 (“the anointing teaches you concerning all things”) connects the physical rite of chrismation to the Johannine theology of interior knowledge. The anointing is both external (oil on skin) and internal (the Spirit teaching the soul). This dual nature of the sacrament is characteristic of the Jerusalem liturgical tradition, which placed great emphasis on the material elements as vehicles of divine transformation.
