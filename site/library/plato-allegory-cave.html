Republic VII: The Allegory of the Cave

Republic VII: The Allegory of the Cave
Plato, c. 428-348 BCE
Codex Parisinus Graecus 1807

Introduction

The Allegory of the Cave is the most influential image in the history of Western philosophy. Plato describes prisoners chained since childhood in an underground cave, watching shadows cast on a wall by objects passing before a fire. They take the shadows for the whole of reality. When one prisoner is freed and dragged into the sunlight, he is blinded — but gradually comes to see the real world, and finally the sun itself, which Plato identifies with the Form of the Good. The allegory maps the soul's journey from ignorance to knowledge, from the visible world to the intelligible.

§514a-b · Rep. 514a-b
Greek

ἀπείκασον τοιούτῳ πάθει τὴν ἡμετέραν φύσιν παιδείας τε πέρι καὶ ἀπαιδευσίας. ἰδὲ γὰρ ἀνθρώπους οἷον ἐν καταγείῳ οἰκήσει σπηλαιώδει.

English

Compare our nature, in respect of education and its lack, to such an experience as this. Picture human beings living in an underground cave-like dwelling.

§515c-516a · Rep. 515c-516a
Greek

εἴ τις αὐτὸν βίᾳ... ἕλκοι... πρὸς τὸ τοῦ ἡλίου φῶς, ἆρ᾿ οὐχὶ ὀδυνᾶσθαί τε ἂν καὶ ἀγανακτεῖν ἑλκόμενον;

English

If someone were to drag him by force up the steep and rough path and did not let him go until he had been dragged out into the light of the sun — would he not be pained and resentful at being so dragged?

§516b-c · Rep. 516b-c
Greek

τελευταῖον δὴ οἶμαι τὸν ἥλιον... αὐτὸν καθ᾿ αὑτὸν ἐν τῇ αὑτοῦ χώρᾳ δύναιτ᾿ ἂν κατιδεῖν καὶ θεάσασθαι οἷός ἐστιν.

English

Last of all, I think, he would be able to look upon the sun itself, by itself, in its own place, and see it as it truly is.
