§56.11.1-5 · Saṃyutta Nikāya
Manuscript
Greek

evaṃ me sutaṃ | bhagavā bārāṇasīyaṃ viharati isipatāne migadāye

English

The Blessed One addressed the group of five monks: “These two extremes ought not to be practiced by one who has gone forth. What two? Devotion to sensual pleasure, which is low and vulgar, and devotion to self-mortification, which is painful. Avoiding both, the Tathāgata has realized the Middle Way, which leads to peace, to direct knowledge, to awakening, to Nibbāna.”

§56.11.5-8 · Saṃyutta Nikāya
Manuscript
Greek

idaṃ kho pana bhikkhave dukkhaṃ ariya-saccaṃ | jāti pi dukkhā jarā pi dukkhā

English

Now this, monks, is the noble truth of suffering: birth is suffering, aging is suffering, illness is suffering, death is suffering. Union with what is displeasing is suffering; separation from what is pleasing is suffering; not to get what one wants is suffering.

Translator's Notes

  1. §56.11: The Middle Way is not a compromise between extremes but a third position that transcends both. The Buddha rejected not only hedonism but also the asceticism he himself had practiced for six years. Experience, not theory, drove the revision.
