Various History II.26: The Most Just of Men

Aelian, c. 175-235 AD

Introduction

Claudius Aelianus (Aelian), a Roman author writing in Greek around 200 CE, includes a remarkable passage on the Hyperboreans in his Various History (II.26). Writing at the height of the Roman Empire, Aelian draws on much earlier sources to present the Hyperboreans as the most just of all peoples, blessed with extraordinary longevity—they live to one thousand years—and possessed of a remarkable custom: when they have lived long enough, they garland themselves with flowers and cast themselves voluntarily from a cliff into the sea.

Aelian’s account is distinctive for its emphasis on justice as the defining Hyperborean virtue. While other sources focus on their piety, their music, or their freedom from disease, Aelian insists that the Hyperboreans are above all “the most just of men.” For the theosis tradition, this is a crucial addition. Perfection is not merely freedom from suffering or closeness to the gods—it is also moral perfection, the achievement of complete justice in human relations. The Hyperboreans’ thousand-year lifespan and their voluntary death suggest a people who have mastered time itself: they live not because they cannot die but because they choose to live, and they die not because they must but because they are complete.

Greek

Δικαιότατοι μὲν ἀνθρώπων οἱ Ὑπερβόρεοι. οἰκοῦσι δὲ γῆν εὔκαρπον καὶ ἐπὶ πλεῖστον βιοῦσιν. Μυθολογεῖται δὲ περὶ αὐτῶν πολλά.

English

The Hyperboreans are the most just of men and inhabit a land that is productive of every kind of fruit. They live to an exceedingly great age, and all manner of legend is told about them. They say that among them Boreas has sovereignty, and that they are beloved of Apollo. The air there is always serene and the climate exceedingly mild. They dwell in groves and forests. Sacrificing hecatombs of asses to the god, they rejoice continuously, singing hymns and celebrating Apollo, who takes the greatest pleasure in the honor they render him.

English

They live to a thousand years. But when they have enjoyed every happiness for this great length of time, and when they are weary of living, they adorn themselves with garlands and throw themselves from a certain rock into the sea. They consider this the finest manner of death. Their women too are of extraordinary beauty, and the race is untouched by disease. They practice justice not by law but by nature, since they have no need of courts or legislation, being righteous (dikaioi, δίκαιοι) of their own accord.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II.26 (justice): The opening declaration “the most just of men” (δικαιότατοι ἀνθρώπων) places justice at the center of the Hyperborean ideal. Aelian specifies that they practice justice “by nature, not by law”—they have no courts because they have no disputes. This corresponds to the Aristotelian and Stoic ideal of natural virtue, and it parallels the Christian concept of the prelapsarian state, in which humanity’s moral sense was uncorrupted and law was unnecessary.
  2. §II.26 (thousand years): The thousand-year lifespan echoes the extreme ages attributed to the antediluvian patriarchs in Genesis (Methuselah lived 969 years). Whether there is any direct connection between the Hyperborean and biblical traditions of extreme longevity is debated, but the structural parallel is clear: in the time before the world’s decline, humans lived far longer than they do now. Both traditions treat shortened lifespan as a symptom of humanity’s distance from the divine.
  3. §II.26 (voluntary death): The custom of voluntary death by leaping from a cliff into the sea, after garlanding oneself with flowers, echoes Pliny’s account almost exactly. The image is deliberately beautiful: death comes not as a horror but as the final festival. The garlands transform the act from despair to celebration. For the theosis tradition, this represents the ultimate mastery over mortality—the Hyperboreans have so thoroughly transcended the fear of death that their departure from life is an act of gratitude, not grief. They have received everything the gods intended for them, and they return themselves to the elements with the same grace with which they lived.
