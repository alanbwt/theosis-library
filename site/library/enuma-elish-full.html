Introduction

        
The Enuma Elish ('When on High') is the Babylonian national creation epic, inscribed on seven clay tablets and recited annually during the New Year festival (Akitu) in Babylon. The text describes the creation of the world from primordial chaos through divine combat — the storm god Marduk defeats the sea-dragon Tiamat and fashions the cosmos from her body.
The parallels to Genesis 1 are unmistakable: creation from a watery chaos, the separation of the waters above from the waters below, the creation of humanity as the final act. The differences are equally significant: in Genesis, God creates by speaking; in the Enuma Elish, Marduk creates by violence. The text was central to the religion of Babylon for over a millennium.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · enuma-elish-full
          
When on high the heaven had not been named, and below the earth had not been called by name, when Apsu, the first one, their begetter, and Tiamat, the one who bore them all, had mixed their waters together — before any of the gods had been brought into being, before they were called by name, and their destinies had not been determined — then it was that the gods were formed. Tiamat grew restless and planned war against the younger gods. She created eleven terrible monsters. The gods were afraid. Only Marduk would face her. He armed himself with the winds and the thunderbolt. He spread his net and caught Tiamat. He drove the evil wind into her body and shot an arrow through her heart. He split her skull with his mace. He cut her body in half like a dried fish: one half he set up as the sky, the other he laid down as the earth. From her eyes flowed the Tigris and Euphrates. From the blood of Kingu, the rebel god, Ea created humanity to do the work the gods refused.
