Contra Nestorianos: Hypostatic Union and Theosis (First English Translation)

Leontius of Byzantium, c. 485-543 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Leontius of Byzantium (c. 485–543) is one of the most technically precise Christological thinkers of late antiquity. His Contra Nestorianos et Eutychianos defends the Chalcedonian middle way against those who would divide Christ into two persons (Nestorius) or fuse his natures into one (Eutyches). The concept of enhypostasis—that Christ’s human nature subsists in the divine hypostasis rather than possessing its own independent personal existence—originates largely with Leontius. No complete English translation of this work exists. This is the first English rendering of these excerpts.

The theosis implications are direct. If human nature can exist within the divine hypostasis without being destroyed, then the union of God and humanity is not a zero-sum competition. Divinity does not cancel humanity; it perfects it. What happened uniquely in Christ becomes the pattern for what happens, by participation, in every believer.

§I.4 · PG 86.1, col. 1281A
Manuscript
Greek

Οὐ ταὐτὸν φύσις καὶ ὑπόστασις. Φύσις μὲν τὸ τί ἐστι δηλοῖ· ὑπόστασις δὲ τὸ ὅτι ἔστι καὶ τίς ἐστιν.

English

A nature is not the same thing as a hypostasis. A nature indicates what something is; a hypostasis indicates that it is, and who it is. The humanity of Christ is a real, complete nature, but it does not exist as a separate hypostasis. It subsists in (enhypostatos, ἐνυπόστατος) the hypostasis of the Word.

§I.9 · PG 86.1, col. 1289C
Manuscript
Greek

Οὔτε Νεστόριος οὔτε Εὐτυχὴς ὀρθῶς τὴν ἕνωσιν ἐνόησαν.

English

Neither Nestorius nor Eutyches understood the union rightly. Nestorius divided what was united, making two persons and two sons. Eutyches confused what was distinct, making one nature where there are two. The truth is that one person, the eternal Word, assumed a complete human nature, and this human nature finds its personal existence in him—not alongside him, not absorbed into him, but in him.

§I.18 · PG 86.1, col. 1301A–B
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐκ τῆς ἑνώσεως ταύτης μανθάνομεν τί ἐστιν ἡ ἡμετέρα θέωσις.

English

From this union we learn what our own deification means. We do not become God by nature—that belongs to the Trinity alone. But through grace and participation, human nature is capable of receiving divine properties, of being illuminated, sanctified, and made incorruptible. Christ’s humanity, united to the Word, shows that human nature can bear the divine without being annihilated. What the Word accomplished in himself by nature, the Spirit accomplishes in us by grace.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.4: The concept of enhypostasis is Leontius’s most important technical contribution. It solves a problem that had bedevilled Christology for a century: how can Christ have a complete human nature without being a human person? Leontius’s answer is that a nature need not possess its own hypostasis; it can subsist within another hypostasis. This allows Christ to be fully human without being a human person separate from the divine Word.
  2. §I.18: The move from Christology to soteriology is seamless: if human nature can subsist within the divine hypostasis, then humanity is inherently capable of union with God. The Incarnation is both the demonstration and the cause of theosis. Leontius’s technical precision serves a deeply pastoral purpose.
