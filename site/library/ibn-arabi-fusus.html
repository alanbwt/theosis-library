Fusus al-Hikam: Unity of Being

Ibn Arabi, 1165-1240 AD

Introduction

The Fusus al-Hikam (Bezels of Wisdom) of Muhyi al-Din Ibn Arabi, written in 1229, is the most philosophically dense work in the Sufi tradition. Each of its twenty-seven chapters is a “bezel”—a gem setting—devoted to the wisdom embodied by a particular prophet, from Adam to Muhammad. But the real subject of the book is wahdat al-wujud, the Unity of Being: the doctrine that there is only one reality, and it is God. Everything that exists—every stone, every person, every galaxy—is a self-disclosure (tajalli) of the divine.

Ibn Arabi did not invent this idea; he systematized it. The Quran says “Wherever you turn, there is the face of God” (2:115). The Upanishads say “All this is Brahman” (sarvam khalvidam brahma). Plotinus says the One overflows into all things. Ibn Arabi’s contribution was to show how these insights work as a coherent metaphysics: God is the only being, and creation is the mirror in which God sees Himself. The human being, as the most perfect mirror, is the purpose of creation—the eye through which God beholds God.

§The Bezel of Divine Wisdom in the Word of Adam · Fusus al-Hikam, Chapter 1 (Fass Hikmah Ilahiyyah fi Kalimah Adamiyyah)
Manuscript
Arabic

Lamma shaʼa al-Haqq subhanahu min haythu asmaʼuhu al-husna allati la yablughuha al-ihsaʼ an yara aʻyanaha—wa in shiʼta qulta an yara ʻaynihi—fi kawnin jamiʻ yahtawi ʻala al-amr kullihi.

English

God wished to see His own essence in a comprehensive object that, having been endowed with existence, epitomizes the whole divine order, so that He might behold His mystery in it. For the seeing of a thing by itself is not like the seeing of itself in another thing, which serves it as a mirror. So the whole affair began and ended with Adam. He is the mirror, and God is the meaning reflected in the mirror. The cosmos was a polished but lifeless mirror before humanity. When God created Adam, the mirror came alive.

§The Unity of Being · Fusus al-Hikam, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Arabic

Fa-huwa al-marʼah allati yara fiha suratihi, wa huwa al-mirʼah allati yara al-Haqq fiha asmaaʼahu. Wa ma huwa illa ʻaynu dhatihi.

English

The Reality is at once the Seer and the Seen, the Observer and the Observed. The cosmos is His form, and He is the spirit of the cosmos. He is the meaning and the cosmos is His garment. So the cosmos is the outward and God is the inward. God is also the outward, and the cosmos is also the inward. The matter has no beginning and no end—it has always been thus.

§The Perfect Human · Fusus al-Hikam, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Arabic

Fa-l-insan jamaʻa bayna al-surah wa-l-khalifah. Wa huwa al-barzakh bayna al-ʻalami wa-l-Haqq.

English

The human being unites in himself the form of the Real and the form of the cosmos. He is the isthmus (barzakh) between the world and the Real, the line where the two seas meet. He is the pupil of the eye of the world, and through him the world gains its sight. Without him, the world would be as a body without a soul.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Bezel of Divine Wisdom in the Word of Adam: Ibn Arabi’s metaphor of the mirror is precise and deliberate. A mirror does not create the image it reflects; the image is the reality showing itself through the mirror. Likewise, creation does not have independent existence—it is God’s self-disclosure. The human being is the “polished mirror” because humanity alone is created in the divine image (surah), a concept Ibn Arabi derives from both the Quran and the hadith “God created Adam in His image.” This parallels Genesis 1:27 and the Kabbalistic doctrine of Adam Kadmon, the primordial human who contains all divine attributes.
  2. §The Perfect Human: The concept of al-insan al-kamil (the Perfect Human) is Ibn Arabi’s most influential contribution to Islamic thought. The Perfect Human is not merely a saint or prophet but the ontological bridge between God and creation. The term barzakh (isthmus, barrier between two seas) comes from Quran 55:19–20 and 25:53. Ibn Arabi transforms it into a metaphysical principle: the Perfect Human stands where the divine and created orders meet, partaking of both, reducible to neither. This is theosis stated in Islamic terms: the human being is the meeting point of God and world.
