On Abstinence I: The Soul's Return

Porphyry, c. 234-305 AD

Introduction

Porphyry of Tyre (c. 234–305 CE), Plotinus’s most important student and the editor of the Enneads, wrote On Abstinence from Animal Food (De Abstinentia) as a letter to a fellow philosopher named Firmus Castricius, who had lapsed from vegetarianism. The work is far more than a dietary treatise. Book I develops a comprehensive argument that the soul’s purification and return to the divine requires the systematic withdrawal of attention from the body and its appetites. Abstinence from meat is not an end in itself but a discipline of detachment—one practice within a larger program of ascetic return.

Porphyry’s position represents the ascetic pole of the theosis tradition. Where the Hermetica emphasize knowledge and the Enneads emphasize contemplative union, Porphyry insists that practical discipline of the body is a necessary precondition. The soul cannot ascend while entangled in bodily passions. This is not hatred of the body but a recognition that attention directed toward material pleasures is attention diverted from the intelligible world. The passage excerpted here lays out the philosophical foundations of this ascetic soteriology.

§I.29 · Porphyry, De Abstinentia I.29
Manuscript
Greek

τέλος γὰρ τῆς πορείας ἡ τῶν ὄντως Ὄντων θεωρία, τουτέστι τῶν νοητῶν

English

For the purpose of our journey is the contemplation of that which truly exists, which is the intelligible. The soul must withdraw from all that is foreign to it, and the body and its affections are foreign to the soul. We must therefore become strangers to the body and its life, if we wish to achieve contact with the intelligible and truly real.

§I.30 · Porphyry, De Abstinentia I.30
Manuscript
Greek

ἐπεὶ ἑκάστῳ τὸ μὲν οἰκεῖον τὸ δὲ ἀλλότριον

English

Since to each thing there is something proper and something foreign, and what is proper nourishes and preserves while what is foreign corrupts and destroys—we must distinguish what is proper to the soul and what is foreign. What is proper to the soul is the contemplation of true being and the intelligible order. What is foreign is attachment to the body, to sensation, and to the passions that arise from the body.

§I.31–32 · Porphyry, De Abstinentia I.31–32
Manuscript
Greek

δεῖ οὖν τὸν φιλόσοφον ἀφαιρεῖν ἑαυτὸν ἀπὸ πάντων τῶν συναπτόντων πρὸς τὸ σῶμα

English

The philosopher, then, must separate himself from all that connects him to the body—not by violence but by the gradual withdrawal of desire. For the passions are bonds that bind the soul to the corporeal. And each pleasure is a nail that fastens the soul to the body, making the soul believe that what the body tells it is true. The soul that has been purified of these attachments and freed from the body’s influence turns naturally toward the intelligible, as a thing returns to its proper place when the impediment is removed.

§I.34 · Porphyry, De Abstinentia I.34
Manuscript
Greek

τέλος γὰρ οὐ τὸ ἀναμάρτητον ἀλλὰ τὸ θεὸν γενέσθαι.

English

The end is not to be without sin but to become God. It is not merely moral perfection that we seek but likeness to the divine—and not merely likeness but, insofar as is possible for human nature, union with it. The return of the soul to God is the natural completion of its journey, as the return of a stream to the sea.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.29: The phrase “become strangers to the body” echoes the Hermetic language of Corpus Hermeticum XIII.1, where Tat must become “a stranger to the world” before receiving the teaching on rebirth. For Porphyry, estrangement from the body is not world-hatred but a recognition that the soul’s proper home is the intelligible realm. The Platonic source is Phaedo 67c–d, where philosophy is defined as the “practice of death”—the separation of soul from body.
  2. §I.31–32: The image of pleasures as “nails” fastening the soul to the body comes directly from Plato’s Phaedo 83d. Porphyry extends the metaphor: each attachment is a point of fixation that prevents the soul from moving freely. The withdrawal must be gradual—not violence against the body but the slow loosening of attachment. This moderate asceticism distinguishes the Neoplatonic tradition from the more extreme practices of some Gnostic and early Christian groups.
  3. §I.34: The declaration that “the end is not to be without sin but to become God” (θεὸν γενέσθαι) is Porphyry’s sharpest formulation of the Neoplatonic theosis claim. Moral perfection is necessary but insufficient. The goal exceeds ethics entirely. Compare this with the later Christian distinction between praxis (moral purification) and theoria (contemplative union with God)—Porphyry’s framework anticipates the structure that Evagrius Ponticus and the Cappadocian Fathers would develop.
