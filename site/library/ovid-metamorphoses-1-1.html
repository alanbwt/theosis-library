Introduction

The Metamorphoses opens with Ovid’s account of creation from Chaos. Before sea, earth, or sky existed, there was only formless matter—a raw, undifferentiated mass that a god or higher nature separated into the ordered cosmos.

Greek original and English translation.
§I.1–31 · Ovid, Metamorphoses I.1–31 (Tarrant, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

In nova fert animus mutatas dicere formas corpora; di, coeptis (nam vos mutastis et illas) adspirate meis. Ante mare et terras et quod tegit omnia caelum unus erat toto naturae vultus in orbe, quem dixere chaos.

English

My mind leads me to speak of forms changed into new bodies. Gods, since you also changed these, favor my undertaking. Before the sea and lands and the sky that covers all, nature throughout the whole world wore one face, which they called Chaos—a raw, disordered mass, nothing but inert weight, discordant seeds of ill-matched things heaped together. Some god, or kindlier nature, settled this strife.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1–31: Ovid’s cosmogony draws on Hesiod, Empedocles, and the Platonic Timaeus. The phrase deus et melior natura (“a god or kindlier nature”) deliberately leaves the creative principle ambiguous, balancing between theistic and naturalistic accounts of cosmic order.
