Introduction

The Liber Linteus (Linen Book of Zagreb) is the longest surviving Etruscan text, a ritual calendar written on linen wrappings later reused to wrap an Egyptian mummy. It is the most substantial document in the still only partially deciphered Etruscan language.

[Etruscan text on linen, Archaeological Museum, Zagreb; partially deciphered]

English

[The text is a ritual calendar prescribing offerings and ceremonies. Partially deciphered passages indicate:] On the day of offering to the god Nethuns, wine shall be poured. For the rites of the month of Celi, a black sheep shall be sacrificed. The ceremonies must be performed at the proper time according to the sacred calendar.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Column 1: Etruscan remains one of the great unsolved problems of linguistics. Though the alphabet (derived from Greek) is easily read, the language has no known relatives. The Liber Linteus survives only because its linen was reused in Ptolemaic Egypt—a remarkable accident of preservation across two civilizations.
