Introduction

In Meditations VI.15, Marcus Aurelius reflects on the power of opinion to shape experience. Things do not touch the soul directly; our disturbance comes entirely from internal judgments, which we have the power to revise.

§VI.15 · Marcus Aurelius, Meditations VI.15 (Dalfen, Teubner)
Manuscript
Greek

τὰ μέν ἐπείγεται εἰς τὸ εἶναι, τὰ δὲ ἐπείγεται εἰς τὸ μὴ εἶναι

English

Some things are rushing toward being, others rushing away from being, and of that which is coming to be, some part has already perished. Flux and change renew the cosmos continually, just as the unbroken course of time makes eternity ever new. In such a river, where nothing stands firm, what is there among these things rushing past to value greatly?

Translator's Notes

  1. §VI.15: The river imagery echoes Heraclitus’s doctrine of flux, which Marcus explicitly references elsewhere in the Meditations. For Marcus, impermanence is not cause for despair but for detachment: one should value only what is stable—namely, virtue and rational judgment.
