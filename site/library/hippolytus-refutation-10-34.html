Refutation X.34: Companion of the Deity

Hippolytus of Rome, c. 170–235 AD

Introduction

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235) was a theologian and antipope whose Refutation of All Heresies is one of the most comprehensive catalogues of heterodox teachings from the early church. The work surveys dozens of philosophical and religious systems—from the Greek pre-Socratics to the Gnostic schools of Valentinus, Basilides, and Marcion—before concluding with a positive statement of orthodox Christian teaching.

Book X, Chapter 34 is part of this concluding positive statement. Having spent nine books dismantling rival systems, Hippolytus sets forth what he considers the true Christian promise: that believers shall become companions of the Deity and co-heirs with Christ. The language is striking in its directness. Hippolytus does not merely promise moral transformation or post-mortem reward but genuine participation in the divine life.

§X.34 · Refutatio Omnium Haeresium X.34 (ed. Marcovich)
Manuscript
Greek

Έσῃ σύνθρονος τῷ θεῷ καὶ συγκληρονόμος Χριστοῦ, οὐκέτι ἐπιθυμίαις ἢ πάθεσιν ἢ νόσοις δουλαγωγούμενος. Γέγονας γὰρ θεός.

English

You shall be a companion of the Deity and a co-heir with Christ, no longer enslaved by desires or passions or wasted by disease. For you have become god. Whatever sufferings you endured as a man, God gave to you because you were a man; but whatever belongs to God, God has promised to bestow upon you, because you have been deified.

Translator's Notes

  1. §X.34: The term synthronos (σύνθρονος), rendered “companion,” literally means “sharing the same throne.” It implies not merely proximity to God but participation in divine sovereignty. The blunt declaration “you have become god” (γέγονας θεός) is among the most direct statements of deification in pre-Nicene literature. Hippolytus is writing in a polemical context against Gnostic systems that promised divinization through secret knowledge; his counter-claim is that genuine deification comes through orthodox faith and the sacramental life of the church.
