Tao Te Ching 1 + 25: The Tao That Cannot Be Spoken

Lao Tzu (attributed), c. 6th c. BCE

Introduction

The opening chapter of the Tao Te Ching is one of the most famous passages in world literature and one of the most resistant to translation. Lao Tzu begins with a paradox that undermines the entire enterprise of reading his book: “The Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao.” The word Tao (道) means “way” or “path,” but Lao Tzu immediately insists that whatever the Tao truly is, it cannot be captured in language. Chapter 25 extends this logic by describing the Tao as preceding heaven and earth, standing alone and unchanging, cycling without ceasing—the mother of all things. Lao Tzu confesses he does not know its name and provisionally calls it “Tao.”

These two chapters together establish the Taoist parallel to the apophatic theology of the Christian and Neoplatonic traditions. The Tao, like the God of Pseudo-Dionysius or the One of Plotinus, is beyond all naming and all predication. The theosis question in Taoism takes a distinctive form: not union with a personal God, but return to the nameless source from which all things emerge.

§Chapter 1, lines 1–2 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 1

Chinese

道可道，非常道。名可名，非常名。

English

The Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao. The name that can be named is not the eternal name.

§Chapter 1, lines 3–4 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Chinese

無名天地之始，有名萬物之母。

English

The nameless is the origin of heaven and earth. The named is the mother of the ten thousand things.

§Chapter 1, lines 5–8 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Chinese

故常無欲，以觀其妙。常有欲，以觀其徼。此兩者同出而異名，同謂之玄。玄之又玄，衆妙之門。

English

Therefore, always without desire, one observes its subtlety. Always with desire, one observes its manifestations. These two emerge together but differ in name. Together they are called the mystery. Mystery upon mystery—the gateway of all wonders.

§Chapter 25, lines 1–4 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 25
Manuscript
Chinese

有物混成，先天地生。寂公寥公，獨立而不改，周行而不殆，可以為天下母。

English

There was something formless and complete, born before heaven and earth. Silent and void, it stands alone and does not change, goes round and does not weary. It is capable of being the mother of the world.

§Chapter 25, lines 5–7 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 25
Manuscript
Chinese

吾不知其名，字之曰道。強為之名曰大。大曰逝，逝曰遠，遠曰反。

English

I do not know its name. I style it “Tao.” Forced to give it a name, I call it “Great.” Great means going forth. Going forth means reaching far. Reaching far means returning.

§Chapter 25, lines 8–10 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 25
Manuscript
Chinese

故道大，天大，地大，王亦大。域中有四大，而王居其一焉。人法地，地法天，天法道，道法自然。

English

Therefore the Tao is great, heaven is great, earth is great, and the king is also great. In the realm there are four great things, and the king is one of them. Humankind follows earth, earth follows heaven, heaven follows the Tao, and the Tao follows its own nature.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1, line 1: The opening line is one of the most debated sentences in Chinese philosophy. The character 道 (dao) appears three times, functioning first as a noun (“the Way”), then as a verb (“to speak” or “to walk”), then as a noun again. This triple use creates a self-referential paradox: the passage about the ineffability of the Tao is itself an instance of speaking the Tao.
  2. §Chapter 25: The phrase “the Tao follows its own nature” (道法自然) is crucial. The Tao does not answer to a higher principle; it is self-so (ziran, 自然). This distinguishes Taoist cosmology from traditions that posit a personal creator. The Tao is not a being that chooses to create; it is the spontaneous unfolding of reality itself.
