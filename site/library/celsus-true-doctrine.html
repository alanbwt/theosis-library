True Doctrine: How Can a Mortal Body Be God?

Celsus, c. 2nd c. AD

Introduction

Celsus was a second-century pagan philosopher (c. 170–180 CE) who wrote the first systematic intellectual attack on Christianity: the Alethes Logos (True Doctrine or True Word). The original text is lost, but substantial fragments survive because Origen of Alexandria quoted them extensively in his point-by-point rebuttal, Contra Celsum (c. 248 CE). Celsus was philosophically sophisticated, well-read in Jewish and Christian scripture, and unsparing in his criticism.

Celsus attacks the central claim of Christian theosis from a Platonic perspective: how can a mortal body be God? The divine, in Platonic thought, is immaterial, unchanging, and impassible. A god who becomes flesh, suffers, and dies on a cross is a contradiction in terms. Celsus does not deny the possibility of approaching the divine—he is a Platonist who believes the soul can ascend through philosophy—but he insists that the Christian path through a crucified carpenter is philosophically absurd and socially degrading.

§I.1 · Celsus, True Doctrine (preserved in Origen, Contra Celsum I.28)
Manuscript
Greek

φασὶν ὅτι κατέβη ὁ θεὸς… εἰ δέ τι μετέβαλεν ἑαυτοῦ, ἐπὶ τὸ χεῖρον μετέβαλεν.

English

The Christians say that God came down to earth to dwell among men. But this means that God abandoned his heavenly throne. If he changed even the smallest part of himself, he would be changed for the worse, for God cannot change to anything but what is less than God. And if he did not really change but only seemed to change, he is a deceiver.

§I.2 · Celsus, True Doctrine (preserved in Origen, Contra Celsum VI.73)
Manuscript
Greek

θεὸς ἀγαθὸς καὶ καλὸς καὶ εὐδαίμων… θνητοῦ μὲν φύσις μεταβάλλειν, ἀθανάτου δὲ τὸ αὐτὸ ἔχειν.

English

God is good and beautiful and happy, and exists in the most beautiful state. If he then descends to men, he must undergo a change—a change from good to bad, from beautiful to ugly, from happiness to misery, from what is best to what is worst. Who would choose such a change? It is the nature of a mortal thing to change and be remoulded, but the nature of an immortal thing to remain the same forever.

§I.3 · Celsus, True Doctrine (preserved in Origen, Contra Celsum I.67)
Manuscript
Greek

πῶς θνητὸν σῶμα θεός;

English

How can a mortal body be God? Was the body that was born of Mary truly God’s body? Did God eat, drink, and sleep? The corpse on the cross—was that the Lord of the universe? What god or spirit or sensible man, foreseeing that such things would happen to him, would not avoid them if he could?

§I.4 · Celsus, True Doctrine (preserved in Origen, Contra Celsum III.44)
Manuscript
Greek

συνάγουσι σκυτοτόμους καὶ γναφεῖς καὶ τοὺς ἀπαιδευτοτάτους.

English

The Christians gather together cobblers and washerwomen and the most illiterate rustics, and say to them: come, you who are sinners, you who are fools, you who are wretched—come to the kingdom of God! What other cult openly admits that it seeks out the worst of humanity?

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1–2: Celsus’s argument rests on the Platonic axiom of divine immutability: God, being perfect, cannot change, because any change from perfection is a change for the worse. This was the strongest philosophical objection to the Incarnation and forced Christian theologians to develop the doctrine of the communicatio idiomatum (communication of attributes)—the idea that Christ’s divine nature did not change when he assumed human nature, but the two natures coexisted in one person.
  2. §I.4: Celsus’s social critique reveals what most disturbed educated pagans about Christianity: not just its theology but its demographics. Philosophy was for the educated elite; Christianity claimed to offer divine transformation to slaves, women, and the uneducated. This was offensive not merely as snobbery but as a philosophical principle: if theosis requires moral and intellectual excellence (as Plato, Aristotle, and the Stoics all assumed), then a religion that offers it to the ignorant is selling counterfeit goods.
