Emerald Tablet: As Above, So Below

Hermes Trismegistus (attributed), c. 1st-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The Emerald Tablet (Tabula Smaragdina) is a brief Hermetic text attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, the legendary sage who was identified with both the Greek Hermes and the Egyptian Thoth. The earliest known version appears in an Arabic work attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan (8th century CE), though the text claims far greater antiquity. Its most famous dictum—“As above, so below”—became the foundational axiom of Western esotericism, alchemy, and Hermetic philosophy.

The Tablet’s theological vision is one of cosmic correspondence: the macrocosm (the universe) and the microcosm (the human being) mirror each other because both proceed from the same source. This principle of correspondence is not merely analogical but ontological—the same divine power operates at every level of reality. For the theosis library, the Emerald Tablet represents the Hermetic stream of the universal insight that humanity and divinity share a common nature, and that the transformation of the lower into the higher is not only possible but is the purpose of existence.

§1–3 · Tabula Smaragdina (Latin version of Chrysogonus Polydorus, 1541)
Manuscript
Latin/Arabic

Verum, sine mendacio, certum et verissimum: quod est inferius est sicut quod est superius, et quod est superius est sicut quod est inferius, ad perpetranda miracula rei unius. Et sicut omnes res fuerunt ab uno, meditatione unius, sic omnes res natae fuerunt ab hac una re, adaptatione.

English

True, without falsehood, certain, and most true: that which is above is like that which is below, and that which is below is like that which is above, to accomplish the miracles of the One Thing. And as all things have proceeded from this One Thing, by the meditation of the One Mind, so all things are born from this One Thing by adaptation.

§4–6 · Tabula Smaragdina
Manuscript
Latin/Arabic

Pater eius est Sol, mater eius Luna. Portavit illud ventus in ventre suo. Nutrix eius terra est. Pater omnis telesmi totius mundi est hic. Vis eius integra est, si versa fuerit in terram.

English

Its father is the Sun; its mother is the Moon. The Wind carried it in its womb. Its nurse is the Earth. The father of all perfection of the whole world is here. Its power is complete when it is turned toward the earth.

§7–9 · Tabula Smaragdina
Manuscript
Latin/Arabic

Separabis terram ab igne, subtile a spisso, suaviter, cum magno ingenio. Ascendit a terra in caelum, iterumque descendit in terram, et recipit vim superiorum et inferiorum. Sic habebis gloriam totius mundi. Ideo fugiet a te omnis obscuritas.

English

Separate the earth from the fire, the subtle from the gross, gently, and with great skill. It ascends from the earth to the heaven and descends again to the earth, and receives the power of things above and below. Thus you will have the glory of the whole world. Therefore all darkness will flee from you.

§10–13 · Tabula Smaragdina
Manuscript
Latin/Arabic

Haec est totius fortitudinis fortitudo fortis, quia vincet omnem rem subtilem omnemque solidam penetrabit. Sic mundus creatus est. Hinc erunt adaptationes mirabiles, quarum modus est hic. Itaque vocatus sum Hermes Trismegistus, habens tres partes philosophiae totius mundi.

English

This is the strong force of all forces, overcoming every subtle thing and penetrating every solid thing. Thus was the world created. From this are wonderful adaptations, the means of which is here. Therefore I am called Hermes Trismegistus, having the three parts of the wisdom of the whole world. What I have said about the operation of the Sun is complete.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1–3: The axiom “as above, so below” (quod est inferius est sicut quod est superius) is the principle of cosmic correspondence that underwrites all Hermetic thought. It assumes a universe structured by analogy: what happens at the divine level is reflected at the human level, and vice versa. This principle is the philosophical foundation for both alchemy (the transformation of base metals mirrors the transformation of the soul) and astrology (celestial movements correspond to earthly events).
  2. §7–9: The ascending and descending movement—from earth to heaven and back—describes the alchemical process but also the path of spiritual transformation. The adept must first purify (separate the subtle from the gross), then ascend to the divine, then return to the world bearing divine power. The parallel to the Christian pattern of kenosis (divine descent) and theosis (human ascent) is unmistakable, and the Hermetic tradition may have directly influenced early Christian mystical theology through figures like Clement of Alexandria.
