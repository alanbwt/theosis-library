Introduction

Cicero’s De Officiis (On Duties) is a treatise on moral obligation addressed to his son Marcus, drawing primarily on Stoic ethics. It became the most influential moral philosophy text in the Latin West through the Renaissance.

§1.1–4 · Cicero, De Officiis 1.1–4
Manuscript
Greek

Quamquam te, Marce fili, annum iam audientem Cratippum idque Athenis, abundare oportet praeceptis institutisque philosophiae

English

Although you, my son Marcus, having listened for a year to Cratippus at Athens, ought to be well furnished with the precepts of philosophy, I wish you to learn from my writings also. The foundation of justice is good faith—that is, fidelity and truth in promises and agreements.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.4: Cicero’s fides (good faith) as the foundation of justice bridges Stoic ethics and Roman law. The concept became central to Western contract theory and influenced both Aquinas and the natural law tradition.
