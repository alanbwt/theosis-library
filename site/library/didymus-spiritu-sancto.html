De Spiritu Sancto: Theosis Through the Spirit (First English Translation)

Didymus the Blind, c. 313-398 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Didymus the Blind (c. 313–398), head of the Catechetical School of Alexandria for over half a century, composed De Spiritu Sancto (On the Holy Spirit) in Greek, but the original was lost. It survives only in Jerome’s Latin translation, completed around 387. Despite its importance for pneumatology and the theology of deification, no complete English translation has been published. This is the first English rendering of these excerpts. The passages selected here address the Spirit’s role in the theosis of the believer.

Didymus argues that the Holy Spirit is not a creature but fully divine, and that the Spirit’s indwelling is what transforms human nature into a participation in the divine. His theology anticipates the later Byzantine distinction between divine essence and divine energies: the Spirit communicates God’s life without dissolving the boundary between Creator and creature.

§Ch. 18 · Jerome, De Spiritu Sancto (PG 39, col. 1045A)
Manuscript
Latin

Spiritus Sanctus non est in his quae facta sunt, sed in his quae sunt. Dat enim esse his qui sanctificantur, non tamquam ipse ab alio acceperit esse. Qui deificat, ipse inter deificatos esse non potest; qui sanctificat, supra sanctificationem est.

English

The Holy Spirit is not among the things that are made, but among the things that are. For he gives being to those who are sanctified, not as one who himself received being from another. He who deifies (deificat) cannot himself be among the deified; he who sanctifies is above sanctification.

§Ch. 22 · Jerome, De Spiritu Sancto (PG 39, col. 1049B)
Manuscript
Latin

Cum Spiritus descendit in animam, templum Dei eam facit. Quod terrenum erat, caeleste efficitur; quod mortale erat, immortalitatis indumentum accipit. Non enim visitat tantum Spiritus, sed inhabitat, et inhabitando naturam vasis in sui similitudinem transformat.

English

When the Spirit descends upon the soul, he makes it a temple of God. What was earthly becomes heavenly; what was mortal puts on the garment of immortality. For the Spirit does not merely visit but takes up dwelling, and by dwelling he transforms the nature of the vessel into something like his own.

§Ch. 28 · Jerome, De Spiritu Sancto (PG 39, col. 1057A)
Manuscript
Latin

Per Spiritum divinae naturae participes efficimur, ut Petrus apostolus dicit. Non quod essentia Deus efficiamur—absit—sed quod per gratiam accipiamus quod Deus natura possidet. Spiritus signaculum est quo imago Dei, in lapsu corrupta, in nobis restauratur et perficitur.

English

Through the Spirit we become partakers of the divine nature, as the Apostle Peter says. Not that we become God in essence—far from it—but that we receive through grace what God possesses by nature. The Spirit is the seal (signaculum) by which the image of God, damaged in the fall, is restored and perfected in us.

§Ch. 34 · Jerome, De Spiritu Sancto (PG 39, col. 1063C)
Manuscript
Latin

Quemadmodum ferrum in ignem positum non desinit esse ferrum, sed ignis calore et lumine rutilat, ita anima a Spiritu inhabitata non desinit esse anima, sed divino igne ardet. Fit quasi tota lux, tota calor, tota Deus—et tamen manet quod erat natura, dum fit quod non erat gratia.

English

Just as iron placed in fire does not cease to be iron but glows with the fire’s heat and light, so the soul indwelt by the Spirit does not cease to be a soul but burns with divine fire. It becomes, as it were, all light, all warmth, all God—yet remains what it was by nature, even as it becomes what it was not by grace.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 18: The axiom “he who deifies cannot himself be among the deified” (qui deificat, ipse inter deificatos esse non potest) is a foundational principle for Nicene pneumatology. Athanasius uses a similar argument in his Letters to Serapion: if the Spirit makes us divine, the Spirit must be divine. Didymus makes this the centrepiece of his case against the Pneumatomachians (Spirit-fighters) who denied the Spirit’s full divinity.
  2. §Ch. 34: The iron-in-fire analogy became one of the most enduring images in theosis theology. It appears in Origen, Maximus the Confessor, John of Damascus, and later in the Western tradition in Thomas Aquinas. Didymus’s version is notable for its precision: the soul “remains what it was by nature, even as it becomes what it was not by grace.” This formulation preserves the distinction between nature and grace that would become central to later debates.
