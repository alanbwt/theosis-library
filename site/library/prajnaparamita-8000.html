Prajnaparamita: The Perfection of Wisdom

Nagarjuna (attributed), c. 150-250 AD

Introduction

The Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā (“Perfection of Wisdom in Eight Thousand Lines”) is the oldest surviving Prajñāpāramitā text, likely composed between 100 BCE and 100 CE, and the source from which the Heart Sūtra, Diamond Sūtra, and the entire Perfection of Wisdom literature ultimately derive. Where the later condensations strip the teaching to its essential formulas, the Eight Thousand preserves the original dialogical form: Subhūti and the Buddha in sustained conversation about the nature of wisdom, emptiness, and the bodhisattva path.

The Perfection of Wisdom is not a wisdom about something. It is the wisdom that sees through all distinctions—self and other, samsara and nirvana, being and non-being—and finds them empty of inherent existence. For the theosis question, this text insists that the very categories we use to frame the question are the obstacle. There is no “self” that becomes divine, no “divine” that is separate from what already is. The perfection of wisdom is the dissolution of the framework within which theosis makes sense—and yet what remains, the sutra insists, is not nothing.

§The Bodhisattva's Practice · Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Bhagavān āha: Iha Subhūte bodhisattvo mahāsattvaḥ prajñāpāramitāyāṃ sthitvā anutpatti-sthitvā dānaṃ dadāti. Na rūpe tiṣṭhati, na vedanāyāṃ, na saṃjñāyāṃ, na saṃskāreṣu, na vijñāne tiṣṭhati. Dānaṃ dadāti Subhūte kvacid astiṭhitvā, iyaṃ dāna-pāramitā ity ucyate.

English

The Lord said: “Here, Subhūti, a bodhisattva, a great being, having stood in the perfection of wisdom by way of not taking his stand anywhere, should give a gift. He should not stand in form, nor in feeling, nor in perception, nor in mental formations, nor in consciousness. He should give a gift, Subhūti, without standing anywhere, and that is called the perfection of giving.”

§The Wisdom Beyond Grasping · Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Subhūtir āha: Bhagavan kiṃ prajñāpāramitā bhūtā vā abhūtā vā? Bhagavān āha: Na bhūtā nābhūtā. Ākāśavat Subhūte prajñāpāramitā avibhaktā abhedyakā. Na śakyate grahītuṃ, na dhārayituṃ, na nirśdeṣṭuṃ, na nimittīkartuṃ, nābhinirvṛttā, na niruddhā.

English

Subhūti said: “World-Honoured One, is the perfection of wisdom a real thing or is it not a real thing?” The Lord said: “It is neither a real thing nor is it not a real thing. Like space, Subhūti, the perfection of wisdom has no distinction, no differentiation. It cannot be seized, cannot be held, cannot be pointed out, cannot be made a sign of, is not something produced, not something stopped.”

§The Non-Arising of Dharmas · Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā, Chapter 3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Subhūte bodhisattvo mahāsattvaḥ prajñāpāramitāyāṃ caran na bodhisattvaṃ paśyati, na bodhisattva-śabdaṃ, na prajñāpāramitāṃ, na prajñāpāramitā-śabdaṃ. Na paśyati carāmīti, na na carāmīti. Tat kasmād dhetoḥ? Svabhāvena śūnyā bodhisattvāḥ, śūnyā ca prajñāpāramitā. Śūnyaṃ śūnye na carati.

English

“Subhūti, the bodhisattva who courses in the perfection of wisdom does not see a bodhisattva, does not see the word ‘bodhisattva,’ does not see a perfection of wisdom, does not see the word ‘perfection of wisdom.’ He does not see ‘I course,’ nor ‘I do not course.’ And why? Because the bodhisattva in his very nature is empty, and so is the perfection of wisdom. An empty thing does not course in an empty thing.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Bodhisattva's Practice: The phrase “standing in the perfection of wisdom by way of not taking his stand anywhere” (prajñāpāramitāyāṃ sthitvā anutpatti-sthitvā) is a deliberate paradox. To practise perfect wisdom is to not stand, not grasp, not fixate on any dharma whatsoever—including the perfection of wisdom itself. This recursive structure is characteristic of the entire Prajñāpāramitā literature: every teaching undermines itself, not out of nihilism but because any teaching that became a fixed object of attachment would cease to be wisdom.
  2. §The Wisdom Beyond Grasping: The comparison of the perfection of wisdom to space (ākāśa) is significant. In Indian philosophy, space is the standard example of something that exists but is not a “thing.” It cannot be grasped, divided, created, or destroyed. It is the condition of all things appearing but is not itself a thing among things. The analogy suggests that wisdom is not a possession or an attainment but the very medium in which all experience occurs.
  3. §The Non-Arising of Dharmas: The final line—“an empty thing does not course in an empty thing” (śūnyaṃ śūnye na carati)—is the logical conclusion of the perfection of wisdom. If the bodhisattva is empty of inherent existence, and the perfection of wisdom is empty of inherent existence, then there is no agent practising and no practice being performed. This is not a recipe for quietism but a description of the freedom that arises when the illusion of a separate, substantial self performing spiritual practice is seen through.
