Introduction

The Letter to Herodotus is Epicurus’s summary of his physics: atoms and void are the fundamental constituents of reality, nothing comes from nothing, and the universe is infinite. It served as the principal textbook of Epicurean natural philosophy.

§38–41 · Diogenes Laertius X.38–41
Manuscript
Greek

οὐδὲν γίνεται ἐκ τοῦ μὴ ὄντος

English

Nothing comes into being from what does not exist; for if it did, anything would come from anything with no need of seeds. And if that which disappears were destroyed into what does not exist, all things would have perished, since what they dissolved into would not exist. The universe always was such as it is now and always will be such; for there is nothing into which it can change.

Translator's Notes

  1. §38–41: The principle that nothing comes from nothing (ex nihilo nihil fit) is shared by virtually all Greek natural philosophers. Epicurus uses it to establish that atoms are eternal and indestructible, which in turn grounds his argument that death is mere dissolution, not annihilation.
