Quran 4:171: Do Not Say Trinity

Quran (divine revelation), c. 610-632 AD

Introduction

Surah an-Nisa (The Women), verse 171, addresses the “People of the Book” directly and commands them to cease speaking of a Trinity. The verse simultaneously affirms Jesus as “the Messiah,” “a messenger of God,” and “His word which He directed to Mary, and a spirit from Him,” while insisting that none of these exalted titles make him divine. Jesus is honoured in the highest possible terms—and then the line is drawn absolutely.

This verse is particularly significant for the theosis debate because it engages with the very language Christians use to argue for Christ’s divinity. The Quran accepts that Jesus is God’s “Word” (kalima) and a “spirit from Him” (ruh minhu)—titles that in Christian theology point toward the Logos doctrine and the Incarnation. But in the Quranic framework, these are descriptions of Jesus’s prophetic mission, not his ontological nature. The bridge that Christianity builds from “Word of God” to “God the Word” is precisely what the Quran dismantles.

Arabic

يا أهل الكتاب لا تغلوا في دينكم ولا تقولوا على الله إلا الحق إنما المسيح عيسى ابن مريم رسول الله وكلمته ألقاها إلى مريم وروح منه فآمنوا بالله ورسله ولا تقولوا ثلاثة انتهوا خيرا لكم إنما الله إله واحد سبحانه أن يكون له ولد له ما في السماوات وما في الأرض وكفى بالله وكيلا

English

O People of the Book, do not commit excess in your religion and do not say about God except the truth. The Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, was only a messenger of God and His word which He directed to Mary and a spirit from Him. So believe in God and His messengers. And do not say “Three.” Cease—it is better for you. Indeed, God is but one God. Exalted is He above having a son. To Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth. And sufficient is God as disposer of affairs.

Translator's Notes

  1. §4:171: The command la taghlu (“do not commit excess”) is the operative term. In the Islamic reading, the Christian doctrine of the Incarnation and theosis represents ghuluw—theological excess, going beyond what is warranted. Jesus is to be honoured as a messenger, but elevating him to divinity crosses the line from devotion into idolatry. The Sufi tradition would later develop its own vocabulary for intimate nearness to God (qurb, fana), but always within the boundary that this verse establishes.
  2. §4:171: The phrase “His word which He directed to Mary” (kalimatuhu alqaha ila Maryam) parallels John 1:1 (“the Word was God”) while arriving at the opposite conclusion. In Christianity, the Logos is a divine person co-eternal with the Father. In the Quran, the kalima is a creative command—God says “Be” and it is (cf. 3:59). Jesus is the product of God’s word, not the Word itself.
