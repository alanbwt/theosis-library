Rig Veda 10.90: Purusha Sukta (The Cosmic Person)

Vedic Rishis, c. 1500-1200 BCE

Introduction

The Purusha Sukta (Rig Veda 10.90) is one of the most consequential hymns in all of Indian literature. It describes the sacrifice of the cosmic person, Purusha, whose body becomes the entire universe. The moon is born from his mind, the sun from his eye, the wind from his breath, the earth from his feet. All of reality—every animal, every element, every social class—is a portion of this one divine being.

For the theosis question, the implications are total. If the universe is literally the body of Purusha, then every being is divine tissue. There is no creation “out of nothing” here; there is creation out of God. The hymn also provides the earliest Vedic basis for the four varṇas (social classes), deriving them from different parts of Purusha’s body. This made the hymn both cosmologically radical and socially foundational. Later Vedanta philosophy draws heavily on the Purusha Sukta to argue that the apparent multiplicity of the world is an illusion (māyā)—there is only one being, and everything is That.

§10.90.1 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verse 1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

sahasrāśīrṣā puruṣaḥ sahasrākṣaḥ sahasrapāt | sa bhūmiṃ viśvato vṛtvātyatiṣṭhad daśāṅgulam

English

The Purusha (cosmic person) has a thousand heads, a thousand eyes, a thousand feet. He pervades the earth on every side and extends ten fingers’ breadth beyond it.

§10.90.2 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verse 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

puruṣa eved aṃ sarvaṃ yad bhūtaṃ yac ca bhavyam | utāmṛtatvasyēśāno yad annenātirohati

English

Purusha is all this—what has been and what will be. He is the lord of immortality, which he outgrows through food.

§10.90.3 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verse 3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

etāvānasya mahimā ato jyāyāṃś ca pūruṣaḥ | pādo’sya viśvā bhūtāni tripādasyāmṛtaṃ divi

English

Such is his greatness, and yet Purusha is greater still. One quarter of him is all beings. Three quarters are the immortal in heaven.

§10.90.4 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verse 4
Manuscript
Sanskrit

tripād ūrdhva ud ait puruṣaḥ pādo’syēhābh avat punaḥ | tato viṣvaṅ vy akrāmat sāśanānaśane abhi

English

Three quarters of Purusha rose upward. One quarter remained here. From this he spread in all directions, into what eats and what does not eat.

§10.90.5–6 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verses 5–6
Manuscript
Sanskrit

tasmād virāḷ ajāyata virājo adhi pūruṣaḥ | sa jāto aty aricyata paścād bhūmim atho puraḥ | yat puruṣeṇa haviṣā devā yajñam atanvata | vasanto asyāsīd ājyaṃ grīṣma idhśmaḥ śarad dhaviḥ

English

From him Viraj was born, and from Viraj came Purusha. As soon as he was born, he extended beyond the earth, both behind and in front. When the gods performed the sacrifice with Purusha as the offering, spring was its clarified butter, summer the fuel, and autumn the oblation.

§10.90.11–12 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verses 11–12
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yat puruṣaṃ vy adadhuḥ katidhā vy akalpayan | mukhaṃ kim asya kau bāhū kā ūrū pādā ucyete | brāhmaṇo’sya mukham āsīd bāhū rājanyaḥ kṛtaḥ | ūrū tad asya yad vaiśyaḥ padabhyāṃ śūdro ajāyata

English

When they divided Purusha, into how many parts did they apportion him? What was his mouth? What were his arms? What were his thighs and feet called? The Brahmin was his mouth, the Rajanya (warrior) was made his arms. His thighs became the Vaishya (merchant), from his feet the Shudra (servant) was born.

§10.90.13–14 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verses 13–14
Manuscript
Sanskrit

candramā manaso jātaś cakṣoḥ sūryo ajāyata | mukhād indraś cāgniś ca prāṇād vāyur ajāyata | nābhyā āsīd antarikṣaṃ śīrṣṇo dyauḥ sam avartata | padabhyāṃ bhūmir diśaḥ śrotrāt tathā lokā̅āṃ akalpayan

English

The moon was born from his mind. From his eye the sun was born. From his mouth Indra and Agni, from his breath Vayu (the wind) was born. From his navel came the atmosphere. From his head the sky evolved. From his feet the earth, from his ear the directions. Thus they fashioned the worlds.

§10.90.16 · Rig Veda, Maṇḍala 10, Sūkta 90, Verse 16
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yajñen a yajñam ayajanta devās tāni dharmāṇi prathamāny āsan | te ha nākaṃ mahimānaḥ sacanta yatra pūrve sādhyāḥ santi devāḥ

English

With the sacrifice the gods sacrificed the sacrifice. These were the first laws. These powers reached the firmament, where the ancient gods and perfected beings dwell.

Translator's Notes

  1. §10.90.2: The statement puruṣa eved aṃ sarvam (“Purusha is indeed all this”) is one of the most direct identity claims in the Vedas. It does not say Purusha created all this, or rules all this, but is all this. The implication is that every being in the universe is divine substance. This is the cosmological foundation for the later Upanishadic teaching that ātman (the individual self) is identical with Brahman (the cosmic self).
  2. §10.90.3: The image of three quarters of Purusha remaining transcendent while one quarter becomes the manifest world anticipates the later Vedantic distinction between nirguṇa Brahman (Brahman without qualities, the transcendent absolute) and saguṇa Brahman (Brahman with qualities, the personal God who interacts with creation). The visible universe is real but partial—a fraction of the divine totality.
  3. §10.90.11–12: These verses became the scriptural foundation for the varṇa system (the four social classes). By grounding social hierarchy in cosmic anatomy, the hymn makes caste appear to be built into the structure of reality itself. Later reformers—the Buddha, Kabir, Gandhi—would challenge this reading, but the verses remain one of the most debated passages in Hindu scripture.
