Introduction

Chapter 4, "In the World of Men," addresses how to survive in a world of dangerous rulers without compromising one's inner freedom. It introduces the concept of the "fasting of the mind" as the key to navigating political life.

§Chapter 4: Fasting of the Mind · Zhuangzi, Inner Chapters, Chapter 4
Manuscript
Greek

堕肢体，黎聪明，离形去知，同于大通

English

Yan Hui said: "I am making progress." Confucius asked: "What do you mean?" Yan Hui said: "I have forgotten benevolence and righteousness." Confucius said: "That is good, but it is not yet enough." Another day Yan Hui said: "I have forgotten rites and music." Confucius said: "That is good, but it is not yet enough." Another day Yan Hui said: "I sit and forget everything." Confucius was startled and asked: "What do you mean, sit and forget?" Yan Hui said: "I smash my limbs and body, drive out perception and intellect, cast off form, do away with understanding, and make myself identical with the Great Thoroughfare. This is what I mean by sitting and forgetting."

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 4: "Sitting and forgetting" (zuowang) became one of the foundational practices of Taoist meditation and later influenced Chan Buddhist sitting practice (zuochan). The progressive shedding of Confucian virtues—benevolence, righteousness, rites, music—is deliberately provocative: Zhuangzi uses Confucius's own favorite student to subvert Confucian values.
