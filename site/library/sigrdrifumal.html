Introduction

The Sigrdrífumál (“Sayings of Sigrdrífa”) presents the Valkyrie’s teachings to the hero Sigurðr after he wakes her from an enchanted sleep. She instructs him in rune-lore, wisdom, and ethical conduct—a key source for Norse magical tradition.

§Stanzas 5–7 · Poetic Edda, Sigrdrífumál 5–7
Manuscript
Greek

Sigrúnar þú skalt kunna, ef þú vilt sigr hafa, ok rísta á hjalti hjǫrs— sumar á véttrímum, sumar á valbjǫstum, ok nefna tysvar Tý.

English

Victory-runes you must know if you will have victory, and carve them on the sword’s hilt—some on the blade guards, some on the flat of the blade—and name Tyr twice. Ale-runes you must know if you would that another’s wife not betray your trust.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanzas 5–7: The rune-categories—victory-runes, ale-runes, birth-runes, wave-runes—suggest a systematic magical curriculum. The instruction to “name Tyr twice” when carving victory-runes connects runic magic to the god of law and combat. Archaeological evidence confirms rune-carving on weapons was a genuine practice.
