Code of Ur-Nammu: The Oldest Legal Code

Anonymous (Sumerian), c. 2600 BCE

Introduction

The Code of Ur-Nammu (c. 2100–2050 BCE) is the oldest surviving legal code in human history, predating the more famous Code of Hammurabi by roughly three centuries. Composed in Sumerian during the Third Dynasty of Ur, the code survives in fragmentary form on clay tablets discovered at Nippur. Its prologue establishes the king’s authority as divinely granted and frames justice as a cosmic obligation—the king acts as the earthly representative of the gods Nanna and Utu.

The theological significance is considerable. The prologue presents law not as human invention but as divine order mediated through kingship. The concept of cosmic justice (nig-si-sa) that pervades the code is the Sumerian ancestor of later Near Eastern ideas about divine law—ideas that flow directly into the Torah, the Quranic shari’a, and the Greek concept of dike. The specific laws, with their preference for fines over corporal punishment, reveal a surprisingly humane legal philosophy.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Prologue: The concept of nig-si-sa (literally “the thing that is straight”) is the Sumerian equivalent of cosmic justice or right order. It is not merely human fairness but a metaphysical principle: the universe has a moral structure, and the king’s role is to align human society with it. This concept flows into the Akkadian kittum and misharum, the Egyptian ma’at, and ultimately into the Hebrew tsedek.
  2. §Laws 19–22: The preference for monetary compensation over bodily punishment (“an eye for an eye”) is striking. Hammurabi’s later code, written for a more stratified society, prescribes talion (like-for-like punishment) in many cases where Ur-Nammu prescribes fines. This suggests that the oldest known legal tradition was in some respects more humane than its successors.
