Olympian 3: Heracles and the Hyperborean Olive

Pindar, c. 518-438 BCE

Introduction

Pindar’s Third Olympian Ode, composed around 476 BCE, celebrates Theron of Acragas and his chariot victory at Olympia. Within this victory song, Pindar embeds the mythic account of how Heracles traveled to the land of the Hyperboreans to bring back the olive tree, which would provide the wreaths for Olympic victors. The olive thus becomes a living link between the paradise of the Hyperboreans and the sacred games of Greece.

The passage is significant for the theosis tradition because it establishes the Hyperboreans as custodians of sacred nature. The olive tree—symbol of peace, wisdom, and divine favor throughout the Mediterranean world—originates in their land. Heracles, the hero who would himself be deified after death, is the one who makes the journey. The pattern is clear: a mortal who will become divine travels to a land of beings who already live in a divine state, and brings back a gift that sanctifies human achievement. The Olympic wreath is, in this reading, a fragment of paradise placed upon a mortal brow.

§Olympian 3.16–27 · Pindar, Olympian 3.16–27
Manuscript
Greek

παῖς ὅ γε Διός, ὅτ’ ἐπὶ ταὺταν ἴκετο Δανουβίου σκιοειδέας ἀπὸ παγᾶς, Ἡρακλέης ἐλαίας χρυσέας, πείθετο ὁ θυμὸς Ἀθαναίᾳ, φυτεῦσαι παρὰ τὸν Οὐλυμπίας κάλλιστον ἀγώνων μναμεῖον.

English

For the son of Zeus, when he went to the shaded sources of the Danube, Heracles of the golden olive, was persuaded in his heart by Athena to bring the most beautiful memorial of the contests at Olympia—the tree of fair leaves. For the precinct of Zeus lay bare and open to the piercing rays of the sun, and the land did not yet flourish with trees. So his spirit urged him to travel to the land of the Istrians, where once the horse-driving daughter of Leto received him when he came from the mountain ridges and winding glens of Arcadia.

§Olympian 3.28–34 · Pindar, Olympian 3.28–34
Manuscript
Greek

τὰν ὅ γ’ Αὐρυσθέος ἀνάγκαισι πατρός, ἐκέλευσεν ἄγειν τὰν χρυσόκερων ἔλαφον, τάν ποτε Ταπγέτα ἀντίθεισ’ Ὀρθωσίᾳ ἐπέγραψεν ἱράν. τὰν μεταδιώκων ἴδε καὶ κείναν χθόνα, πνοιᾶς ὄπιθεν Βορέα ψυχροῦ.

English

Under the compulsion of Eurystheus and by the decrees of his father, he was compelled to bring the doe with the golden horns that Taygeta had once consecrated as a sacred thing to Artemis. Pursuing her, he saw also that land beyond the blowing of the cold north wind. There he stood and marveled at the trees. And sweet desire for them seized him, to plant them around the turning-post of the twelve-lap horse-race.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3.16–27: The phrase “Heracles of the golden olive” (Ἡρακλέης ἐλαίας χρυσέας) epitomizes the hero by the gift he brought back from Hyperborea. The golden olive is not merely a tree but a sacramental object: it connects the divine perfection of the Hyperborean land to the human striving of the Olympic games. Every victory wreath woven from its leaves carries a trace of paradise.
  2. §3.28–34: The phrase “that land beyond the blowing of the cold north wind” (πνοιᾶς ὄπιθεν Βορέα ψυχροῦ) gives the literal etymology of “Hyperborea”—hyper (“beyond”) + Boreas (“the North Wind”). Heracles arrives there almost by accident, pursuing the golden-horned deer of Artemis. His response upon seeing the trees is not conquest but wonder and “sweet desire” (γλυκὺς ἵμερος)—the Hyperborean paradise does not need to be conquered, only received. This is the posture of theosis: the divine gift is given to those who approach it with awe rather than force.
