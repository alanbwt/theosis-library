Introduction

Fragment 8 of Parmenides’s poem On Nature contains the most rigorous argument for monism in ancient philosophy. The goddess demonstrates that Being is ungenerated, imperishable, whole, and complete—a sphere of pure presence that admits no change or division.

§Fragment 8 (lines 1–21) · DK 28 B8 (Simplicius, In Phys. 145.1–28)
Manuscript
Greek

μόνος δ᾽ ἔτι μῦθος ὁδοῖο λείπεται ὡς ἔστιν· ταύτῃ δ᾽ ἐπὶ σήματ᾽ ἔασι μάλα πολλά· ὡς ἀγένητον ἐὸν καὶ ἀνώλεθρόν ἐστιν, οὖλον μουνογενές τε καὶ ἀτρεμὲς ἠδὲ τέλειον

English

Only one path of inquiry remains: that it is. Along this path are very many signs: that Being is ungenerated and imperishable, whole, of one kind, unshaken, and complete. Nor was it ever, nor will it be, since it is now, all together, one, continuous.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragment 8: Parmenides’s argument that Being is “ungenerated” (ἀγένητον) and “imperishable” (ἀνώλεθρον) directly influenced Plato’s doctrine of the Forms and, through Neoplatonism, the Christian theological concept of divine immutability.
