Introduction

The Epistle to Diognetus (c. 150–200 CE) is an anonymous apologetic letter describing Christians as a “third race” who are in the world but not of it. It contains the famous analogy: “What the soul is to the body, Christians are to the world.”

§5.1–6 · Epistle to Diognetus
Manuscript
Greek

Χριστιανοὶ γὰρ οὔτε γῇ οὔτε φωνῇ οὔτε ἔθεσι διακεκριμένοι

English

Christians are not distinguished from the rest of humanity by country, language, or customs. They do not dwell in cities of their own, nor use a different language, nor practice an extraordinary way of life. Yet while they dwell in both Greek and barbarian cities, following local customs in dress and food and the rest of life, the constitution of their own citizenship is wonderful and confessedly beyond expectation.

§6.1–3 · Epistle to Diognetus
Manuscript
Greek

ὅπερ ἐστὶν ἐν σώματι ψυχή, τοῦτ' εἰσὶν ἐν κόσμῳ Χριστιανοί

English

In a word, what the soul is to the body, Christians are to the world. The soul is dispersed through all the members of the body, and Christians are scattered through all the cities of the world. The soul dwells in the body but is not of the body; Christians dwell in the world but are not of the world.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6.1: The analogy of Christians as the soul of the world is one of the boldest ecclesiological claims in early Christianity. It implies that the church is not merely one institution among others but the animating principle of civilization itself—a claim that would be developed by Augustine and become the basis for Christendom’s self-understanding.
