Quran 2:115: Wherever You Turn, There Is the Face of God

Quran (divine revelation), c. 610-632 AD

Introduction

Quran 2:115 is one of the most philosophically charged verses in the entire Quran. In a single line it asserts divine omnipresence: wherever you turn, there is the face (wajh) of God. Orthodox Islamic theology reads this as a statement about God’s knowledge and sovereignty—He is aware of all directions, all places, all moments. But the Sufi mystics read it differently. For them, “the face of God” is not a metaphor for divine awareness but a literal description of reality: every phenomenon, every direction, every grain of sand is a manifestation of the divine presence.

This verse became a cornerstone of Sufi metaphysics, particularly in the tradition of Ibn Arabi’s wahdat al-wujud (Unity of Being). If God’s face is everywhere, then there is nowhere that God is not. The implications are identical to those found in the Hindu Isha Upanishad (“All this is pervaded by the Lord”) and the Stoic doctrine of the Logos permeating all matter. The verse stands as Islam’s clearest assertion of divine immanence.

Arabic

Wa lillahi al-mashriqu wal-maghrib, fa-aynama tuwallu fa-thamma wajhu Allahi, inna Allaha wasiʻun ʻalim.

English

To God belongs the East and the West. Wherever you turn, there is the face of God. Indeed, God is all-encompassing, all-knowing.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:115: The Arabic wajh (وجه) means “face” but also “essence” or “countenance.” Orthodox exegetes (such as al-Tabari) interpret it as God’s direction (qiblah) or His sovereignty. The Sufis, however, take the word at full metaphysical weight: the “face” of God is His self-disclosure in every phenomenon. Ibn Arabi cites this verse repeatedly in the Futuhat al-Makkiyya to argue that the cosmos is nothing other than God’s self-manifestation. The tension between transcendence (tanzih) and immanence (tashbih) is the central dialectic of Islamic mystical theology, and this verse sits at its heart.
