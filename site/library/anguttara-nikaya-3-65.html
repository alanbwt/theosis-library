Introduction

The Kālāma Sutta is Buddhism’s charter of free inquiry. The Buddha tells the Kālāmas not to accept teaching based on tradition, authority, or reasoning alone, but to test it against direct experience.

§3.65.1-3 · Aṅguttara Nikāya
Manuscript
Greek

kālāmā kesamuttiyā bhagavantaṃ etad avocuṃ

English

The Kālāmas said: “Lord, there are ascetics and brahmins who come to Kesamutta. They explain their own doctrines, but disparage the doctrines of others. Then other ascetics come and do the same. We are uncertain: which of these speaks truth?”

§3.65.4-6 · Aṅguttara Nikāya
Manuscript
Greek

mā anussrāvena mā paramparāya mā iti-kirāya

English

The Buddha said: “Do not go by oral tradition, by lineage of teaching, by hearsay, by a collection of texts, by logic, by inference, by the seeming competence of a speaker. When you know for yourselves that these qualities are wholesome, blameless, and lead to welfare and happiness—then you should enter upon and abide in them.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §3.65: The Kālāma Sutta is often cited as evidence that Buddhism is empirical and anti-dogmatic. This is partially correct but oversimplified. The Buddha’s criterion is not pure empiricism but moral pragmatism: test teachings by whether they lead to wholesome or unwholesome outcomes.
