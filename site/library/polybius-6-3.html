Histories VI.3-9: The Cycle of Constitutions

Polybius, c. 200-118 BCE

Introduction

Book VI of Polybius’s Histories contains the most systematic theory of constitutional cycles in ancient literature. Writing in the second century BCE as a Greek hostage in Rome, Polybius sought to explain why Rome had conquered the entire Mediterranean world in barely fifty years. His answer was Rome’s constitution—but before describing it, he lays out his theory of anacyclosis (ἀνακύκλωσις), the natural cycle through which all simple constitutions rotate.

The cycle proceeds by internal degeneration: monarchy becomes tyranny when kings’ descendants grow corrupt; aristocracy overthrows tyranny but degenerates into oligarchy when the aristocrats pursue wealth over virtue; democracy overthrows oligarchy but degenerates into mob rule when the people lose all restraint. Mob rule produces chaos, from which a strong man emerges—and the cycle begins again. The only escape from this iron wheel is the mixed constitution, which combines monarchic, aristocratic, and democratic elements in a self-correcting balance. Rome, Polybius argues, achieved this balance—and that is why Rome rules the world.

§VI.3–4 (The Natural Origin of Government) · Polybius, Histories VI.3–4
Manuscript
Greek

τὴν μὲν οὖν ἀρχὴν τῆς πολιτείας οὕτως ἔχουσαν εὑρήσομεν.

English

The origin of the state is this: when, after floods, plagues, crop failures, or similar catastrophes, the human race has been reduced and then gradually increases again, people naturally come together; and the one who surpasses the rest in bodily strength and courage will lead and rule. This is called monarchy—a government established by nature, not by design. Just as we see happening among bulls, boars, and roosters, among whom the strongest invariably takes the lead.

§VI.5–6 (Monarchy to Tyranny) · Polybius, Histories VI.5–6
Manuscript
Greek

χρόνου δὲ προβαίνοντος καὶ τῆς ἡγεμονίας κατὰ διαδοχὴν παραγινομένης εἰς τοὺς ἐξ αὐτῶν.

English

When in time the leadership passes by inheritance to the children and grandchildren of the original king, they no longer find the position a novelty, since they were born to it. Turning to excess in food, dress, and drink, and to the pursuit of forbidden pleasures, they provoke envy and offense among their subjects. Thus kingship degenerates into tyranny. For tyranny is not a different thing from kingship in its origin—it is kingship corrupted. And the destruction of tyranny is the beginning of aristocracy.

§VI.7–8 (Aristocracy to Oligarchy to Democracy) · Polybius, Histories VI.7–8
Manuscript
Greek

οἱ δὲ καταλύσαντες τοὺς μονάρχους κατ’ ἀρχὰς μὲν δικαιότατοι καὶ φρονιμώτατοι.

English

The aristocrats who overthrew the tyrant are at first the most just and prudent of men, and the people gladly submit to their authority. But in time the aristocrats’ descendants, who have never experienced oppression and have no knowledge of equality, begin to use their inherited power for personal enrichment. Some turn to greed, others to drunkenness, others to the violation of women. Aristocracy thus degenerates into oligarchy. And when the people can no longer bear the oligarchs’ outrages, they rise up and establish a democracy.

§VI.9 (Democracy to Mob Rule and the Cycle Renewed) · Polybius, Histories VI.9
Manuscript
Greek

οἱ μὲν γὰρ τὴν ἰσηγορίαν καὶ παρρησίαν περὶ πλείστου ποιούμενοι.

English

The generation that established democracy cherishes equality and freedom above all. But when a new generation has grown up and freedom has lost its value because it was never in question, some seek to rise above their fellows and to acquire distinction—especially the wealthy. Unable to achieve this through their own merit, they squander their fortunes corrupting and debauching the masses. And when they have made the people greedy and hungry for bribes, democracy is destroyed in its turn, and gives way to violence and the rule of the strong hand. The people, habituated to taking from others, choose a champion—and the cycle of constitutions begins again.

Translator's Notes

  1. §VI.3–9: The theory of anacyclosis (ἀνακύκλωσις) draws on both Platonic and Aristotelian precedents—Plato’s degeneration of constitutions in Republic VIII–IX and Aristotle’s classification in Politics III—but Polybius gives the sequence a deterministic, cyclical character that neither predecessor quite intended. For Polybius, the degeneration is not merely possible but inevitable for any simple constitution. Only the mixed constitution breaks the cycle, and even that is not permanent—Polybius hints that Rome’s mixed constitution will eventually decay as well.
  2. §VI.9: Polybius’s account of democratic collapse is strikingly specific: democracy dies not from external conquest but from internal corruption, as wealthy citizens bribe the populace, the populace becomes dependent on handouts, and a demagogue rises to power on the back of popular resentment. The parallel with Tacitus’s account of Augustus—who “seduced the army with gifts, the people with grain”—is exact, though Polybius was writing a century before Augustus.
