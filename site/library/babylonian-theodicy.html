Introduction

The Babylonian Theodicy (c. 1000 BCE) is an acrostic dialogue between a sufferer and a friend on the question of divine justice. Often called the “Babylonian Job,” it confronts the same problem: why do the righteous suffer while the wicked prosper?

English

The sufferer spoke: ‘O sage, I would learn from you. Where are the rewards of the pious? The godless man who has wealth is well-fed; he who devotes himself to his god is impoverished.’ The friend replied: ‘The mind of the gods is remote, like the center of the heavens. The plan of a god is deep waters; who can comprehend it?’

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanza 24: Unlike Job, the Babylonian Theodicy offers no theophany or divine answer. The friend’s final concession—that the gods made humans with a tendency toward injustice—is startlingly modern: the problem is not cosmic but built into human nature. The text predates the biblical Book of Job by centuries.
