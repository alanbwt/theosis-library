Introduction

Iliad 24:486–551 presents Priam’s supplication to Achilles for Hector’s body. The aged king kisses the hands that killed his son, and the two enemies weep together—the Iliad’s final word on the shared humanity beneath war.

§24:486–512 · Homer, Iliad 24.486–512
Manuscript
Greek

λάθε δ’ εἰσελθὼν Πρίαμος μέγας ἄγχι δ’ ἱστάμενος χερσὶν Ἀχιλῆος λάβε γούνατα

English

Great Priam entered unseen, and clasping Achilles’ knees, he kissed the terrible, man-slaying hands that had killed so many of his sons. He said, ‘Remember your father, O Achilles, who is the same age as I am, on the wretched threshold of old age. But he at least has hope, that he will see his dear son returning from Troy. But I am utterly wretched.’

Translator's Notes

  1. §24:486: This scene is the moral culmination of the Iliad. Achilles, moved by Priam’s invocation of his own father Peleus, recognizes their shared mortality. The poem that began with wrath ends with compassion—the first great meditation on empathy in Western literature.
