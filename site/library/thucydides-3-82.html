Introduction

Thucydides 3.82–83 describes the moral collapse during the revolution at Corcyra (427 BCE). His analysis of how civil war corrupts language itself—redefining recklessness as courage and prudence as cowardice—remains the most penetrating account of political degeneration ever written.

§3.82 · Thucydides, History
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ τὴν εἰωθυῖαν ἀξίωσιν τῶν ὀνομάτων ἐς τὰ ἔργα ἀντήλλαξαν

English

Revolution brought upon the cities of Greece many terrible calamities, such as have been and always will be as long as human nature remains the same. Words changed their ordinary meaning and were twisted to justify actions. Reckless daring was held to be loyal courage; prudent delay was the excuse of a coward; moderation was the disguise of an unmanly nature; the ability to see all sides of a question was accounted inability to act on any.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3.82: The observation that revolution corrupts language—that words lose their meaning when power becomes the only value—anticipates Orwell by twenty-four centuries. Thucydides’s claim that such events will recur “as long as human nature remains the same” is the foundation of political realism.
