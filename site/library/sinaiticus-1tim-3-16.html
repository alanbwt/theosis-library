1 Timothy 3:16: The Mystery of Godliness

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

First Timothy 3:16 preserves one of the earliest christological hymns in the New Testament. The verse is structured in three couplets of contrasting pairs—flesh and spirit, angels and nations, world and glory—mapping the trajectory of the Incarnation from manifestation to cosmic vindication. The opening declaration, “Great is the mystery of godliness,” uses the term eusebeia (εὐσέβεια), which in the Pastoral Epistles denotes not merely piety but the entire divine economy of salvation.

The hymn’s first line—“he was manifested in the flesh”—is one of the clearest pre-creedal affirmations of the Incarnation. Codex Sinaiticus reads ὃς (“who”), referring to a person manifested in flesh, rather than the later Byzantine reading θεός (“God”), which makes the incarnational claim explicit. The hymn compresses the entire Christ-event into six lines: incarnation, vindication, angelic witness, global proclamation, worldwide faith, and heavenly ascension.

English

And confessedly great is the mystery of godliness (eusebeia, εὐσέβεια): He who was manifested in the flesh, vindicated in the Spirit, seen by angels, proclaimed among the nations, believed in throughout the world, taken up in glory.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:16: Codex Sinaiticus reads ὃς (“who”) rather than θεός (“God”). The later reading θεός appears to be a scribal alteration: in Greek uncial script, Ο΃ (the abbreviation for θεός) differs from Ο΃ (ὃς) by only a single horizontal stroke. The Sinaiticus reading, supported by Codex Alexandrinus’s original hand and the earliest patristic citations, is almost certainly original. The theology is not diminished: the relative pronoun “who” refers to a person whose manifestation “in the flesh” presupposes prior existence in a non-fleshly mode.
  2. §3:16: The six-line structure of the hymn follows a pattern of earthly-heavenly alternation: flesh/Spirit, angels/nations, world/glory. This chiastic or antithetical structure is characteristic of early Christian hymnic material (compare Philippians 2:6–11, Colossians 1:15–20). The hymn traces the full arc of the Christ-event from incarnation to glorification, compressing the entire drama of redemption into liturgical form.
