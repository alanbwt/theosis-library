Introduction

Luke 2 presents the nativity narrative, situating Jesus’ birth within Roman imperial history under the census of Augustus. The Sinaiticus text preserves this account with minor orthographic variants from later manuscripts.

§2:1–3 · Codex Sinaiticus, Luke 2:1–3
Manuscript
Greek

ἐγένετο δὲ ἐν ταῖς ἡμέραις ἐκείναις ἐξῆλθεν δόγμα παρὰ Καίσαρος Αὐγούστου ἀπογράφεσθαι πᾶσαν τὴν οἰκουμένην

English

In those days a decree went out from Caesar Augustus that all the world should be registered. This was the first registration when Quirinius was governing Syria. And all went to be registered, each to his own city.

§2:10–14 · Codex Sinaiticus, Luke 2:10–14
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ εἶπεν αὐτοῖς ὁ ἄγγελος · μὴ φοβεῖσθε · ἰδοὺ γὰρ εὐαγγελίζομαι ὑμῖν χαρὰν μεγάλην

English

And the angel said to them, ‘Fear not, for behold, I bring you good news of great joy that will be for all the people. For unto you is born this day in the city of David a Savior, who is Christ the Lord.’

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:11: The title “Christ the Lord” (Χριστὸς Κύριος) appears only here in Luke’s birth narrative. The combination is unusual in the Septuagint tradition and signals a messianic claim embedded within the political language of Roman lordship.
