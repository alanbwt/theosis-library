Bodhicaryavatara 8: The Exchange of Self and Other

Shantideva, c. 685-763 AD

Introduction

The Bodhicaryavatara (“A Guide to the Bodhisattva’s Way of Life”) by the eighth-century Indian monk Shantideva is one of the most beloved texts in Mahayana Buddhism. Chapter 8, on meditation (dhyana), contains the practice of “exchanging self and other” (paramatmaparivartana)—the systematic replacement of self-cherishing with concern for all beings. The logic is as rigorous as it is radical: all suffering arises from the habit of placing oneself at the centre; all happiness arises from placing others there instead.

Shantideva’s chapter addresses the theosis question from an unexpected angle. Buddhism denies the existence of a permanent self (atman) that could be divinized. But the Bodhisattva path offers a functional equivalent: the dissolution of the boundary between self and other, not through metaphysical identification with the absolute, but through the cultivation of universal compassion. The Bodhisattva does not become God; the Bodhisattva becomes the servant of all sentient beings, which in the Mahayana framework amounts to the same transformation.

§8.120 · Bodhicaryavatara 8.120
Manuscript
Sanskrit

लोके यानि दुःखानि सर्वाण्यात्मसुखेच्छया । लोके यानि सुखानि सर्वाण्यन्यसुखेच्छया ।

English

All the suffering in the world arises from seeking happiness for oneself. All the happiness in the world arises from seeking happiness for others.

§8.121–122 · Bodhicaryavatara 8.121–122
Manuscript
Sanskrit

भाषणेन किं बहुना पश्यतामिमावयोः । स्वार्थं यः करोत्येव बालः परार्थं यः करोति स मुनिः ।

English

What need is there to say more? Look at the difference between the two: the fool who works for his own benefit, and the sage who works for the benefit of others. One who does not exchange his own happiness for the suffering of others will certainly not achieve Buddhahood—and even in the cycle of existence, there will be no joy.

§8.129–130 · Bodhicaryavatara 8.129–130
Manuscript
Sanskrit

आत्मनः सुखं यत् किंचिद् यत् सर्वं परसुखेच्छया । आत्मनो दुःखं यत् किंचिद् यत् सर्वमात्मसुखेच्छया ।

English

Whatever happiness there is in the world, all comes from wanting others to be happy. Whatever suffering there is in the world, all comes from wanting oneself to be happy. But why say more? The childish work for their own benefit; the Buddhas work for the benefit of others. Just look at the difference between them!

§8.131 · Bodhicaryavatara 8.131
Manuscript
Sanskrit

आत्मनः सुखं परदुःखेन यो न व्यतिवर्तयेत् । संसारे’पि सिद्धिर्नास्ति किमुत बोधिसुखम् ।

English

If one does not truly exchange one’s own happiness for the suffering of others, there is no attaining Buddhahood, and there is no happiness even in worldly existence.

Translator's Notes

  1. §8.120: This verse has become the most quoted line from the Bodhicaryavatara, cited by the Dalai Lama as the essence of Mahayana ethics. Its logic depends on the Buddhist analysis of suffering (dukkha): self-cherishing is not merely selfish but metaphysically confused, because the “self” it cherishes does not ultimately exist. The exchange of self and other is therefore not a sacrifice but a correction of a fundamental error.
  2. §8.121–122: The contrast between the “fool” (bala) and the “sage” (muni) echoes the earliest Buddhist literature, but Shantideva gives it a distinctly Mahayana inflection. In the Theravada tradition, the sage seeks personal liberation (nibbana). In Shantideva’s Mahayana framework, even personal liberation is a form of self-cherishing if it does not extend to all sentient beings.
