Introduction

Genjōkōan is the opening fascicle of Dōgen’s Shōbōgenzō. It establishes the core Soto Zen teaching: practice and enlightenment are one, not sequential.

Greek original and English translation.
§Genjokoan 1-3 · Shōbōgenzō
Manuscript
Greek

万法の仏法なる時節、迷惟あり修行あり

English

When all things are the Buddha-dharma, there is delusion and realization, practice, birth and death, buddhas and sentient beings. When the myriad things are without self, there is no delusion, no realization, no buddhas, no sentient beings. The Buddha Way leaps free of abundance and deficiency.

§Genjokoan 4-5 · Shōbōgenzō
Manuscript
Greek

仏道をならふといふは、自己をならふなり

English

To study the Buddha Way is to study the self. To study the self is to forget the self. To forget the self is to be actualized by the myriad things. When actualized by the myriad things, your body and mind as well as the bodies and minds of others drop away.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Genjokoan: Dōgen’s statement “to study the self is to forget the self” inverts the Western philosophical project. Where Descartes finds certainty in the self’s presence, Dōgen finds awakening in the self’s absence. The “dropping away of body and mind” (shinjin datsuraku) is his term for enlightenment.
