Introduction

The Divan-i Shams-i Tabrizi of Rumi (c. 1260 CE) is a vast collection of lyric poems inspired by Rumi’s transformative encounter with the wandering dervish Shams of Tabriz. The poems express ecstatic love, spiritual intoxication, and the annihilation of self in the Beloved.

§Ghazal 116 · Rumi, Divan-i Shams
Manuscript
Greek

آنچه با گل گفتند تا شکفته شد

English

What was said to the rose to make it open was said to me here in my chest. What was told the cypress to make it tall and straight, what was whispered to the jasmine. Whatever made sugarcane sweet, whatever was said to the inhabitants of the city of Sheba, whatever is the reason candles and moths play together—it all comes back to me, and I hear it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ghazal 116: Rumi’s poetics locate the divine word not in scripture alone but in the creative force that pervades all of nature. What made the rose open is the same word spoken to the human heart. This “cosmic revelation” theology resonates with the Quranic concept of ayat (signs): all of creation is a text written by God, readable by those whose hearts are awake.
