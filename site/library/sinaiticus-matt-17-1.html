Matthew 17:1-8: The Transfiguration

Matthew (attributed), c. 70-90 AD

Introduction

The Transfiguration of Christ on the mountain (Matthew 17:1–8) is the single most important biblical passage for the Eastern Orthodox doctrine of theosis. Jesus takes Peter, James, and John up a high mountain, and before their eyes his face shines like the sun and his garments become white as light. Moses and Elijah appear with him. A cloud overshadows them, and the voice of the Father speaks. The disciples fall on their faces in terror.

The theological claim is precise: divinity, which was always present in Christ, becomes visible. The Greek term for this transformation is metemorphothe (μετεμορφώθη)—the same root that Paul uses in Romans 12:2 when he commands believers to “be transformed.” Gregory Palamas built his entire theology of the uncreated light on this passage: the light the disciples saw was not a created effect but the eternal energy of God itself, and it is the same light that transforms the saints in prayer. The text is from Codex Sinaiticus.

Translator's Notes

  1. §17:1–2: The verb metemorphothe (μετεμορφώθη) is the passive form of metamorphoo—to transform, to change form. It is the same word Paul uses in Romans 12:2 (“be transformed by the renewing of your mind”) and 2 Corinthians 3:18 (“we are being transformed into the same image from glory to glory”). The Orthodox tradition reads this connection as deliberate: the transformation that was visible in Christ on the mountain is the same transformation happening invisibly in every believer. Gregory Palamas argued in the fourteenth century that the light on Tabor was not created light but the uncreated energy of God—and that the saints, through prayer, participate in this same light.
  2. §17:7–8: The ending is theologically crucial: after the vision fades, the disciples see “no one but Jesus only.” Moses (the Law) and Elijah (the Prophets) have vanished. The voice of the Father has spoken. What remains is the incarnate Christ—not glowing, not transfigured, but in his ordinary human form. The implication: the divine glory they witnessed is always present in the human Jesus, whether visible or not. The Transfiguration reveals what was always true rather than producing something new.
