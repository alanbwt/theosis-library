The Legend of Sargon: The First Emperor

        
Sargon of Akkad, c. 2334-2279 BCE

        
Nineveh Library tablets

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The birth legend of Sargon of Akkad (c. 2334-2279 BCE) describes how the founder of the world's first empire was born in secret, placed in a basket on the river, rescued by a water-drawer, and rose from humble origins to conquer all of Mesopotamia. The parallel to the Moses birth narrative (Exodus 2:1-10) is one of the most discussed connections between Mesopotamian and biblical literature.
Sargon's empire, centered at Akkad, was the first in history to unite multiple city-states under a single ruler. His legend was copied and recopied for nearly two thousand years, making it one of the most enduring stories from the ancient Near East.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · sargon-birth-legend
          
Sargon, the mighty king, king of Agade, am I. My mother was a high priestess, my father I knew not. My mother, the high priestess, conceived me and in secret she bore me. She set me in a basket of rushes, with bitumen she sealed my lid. She cast me into the river which did not rise over me. The river bore me up and carried me to Akki, the drawer of water. Akki, the drawer of water, took me as his son and reared me. Akki, the drawer of water, appointed me as his gardener. While I was a gardener, the goddess Ishtar loved me, and for fifty-four years I exercised kingship.
