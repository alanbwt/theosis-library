Enneads V.1.6: The Emanation of the One

Plotinus, 204-270 AD

Introduction

Enneads V.1 is Plotinus’s treatise On the Three Primary Hypostases, and section 6 provides the clearest account of the central Neoplatonic doctrine: emanation. The One, being absolutely perfect and self-sufficient, does not create by deliberate act but “overflows” by the sheer superabundance of its being. This overflow produces Nous (Intellect or Mind), which in turn overflows to produce Soul, which in turn produces the material world. Each level of reality is a diminished image of the one above it, like light spreading from a central source and growing dimmer at the periphery.

This passage is essential for the theosis question because it establishes the metaphysical framework within which the soul’s return to the divine makes sense. If reality is a series of emanations from the One, then the soul’s ascent is simply the reversal of emanation—a tracing of the path back to its source. The soul does not need to acquire something foreign; it needs to remember and return to where it came from. The doctrine influenced every subsequent tradition of mystical ascent, from Proclus and Pseudo-Dionysius through the Kabbalistic sefirot to Sufi cosmology.

§V.1.6 [1–10] · Plotinus, Enneads V.1.6
Manuscript
Greek

πῶς οὖν ἐκ τοῦ ἑνὸς νοῦς; ὃτι τέλειον τὸ ἓν, τῷ μηδὲν ζητεῖν μηδὲ ἔχειν μηδὲ δεῖσθαι, οἷον ὑπερερρύη

English

How then does Intellect come from the One? Since the One is perfect, seeking nothing, having nothing, needing nothing, it overflowed, as it were, and its superabundance made something other than itself. That which was generated turned back to the One and was filled, and looking toward it became Intellect. Its halt and turning toward the One constituted being; its gaze upon the One constituted Intellect.

§V.1.6 [10–20] · Plotinus, Enneads V.1.6
Manuscript
Greek

στᾶσα γὰρ πρὸς αὐτὸ καὶ πρὸς αὐτὸ ἰδοῦσα νοῦς ἅμα καὶ ὂν ἐγένετο

English

Since it stood in relation to the One and gazed upon it, it became at once both Intellect and Being. Standing in the place of the One, it necessarily produces things similar, pouring out a great power. And this too was a form of that which poured it out, as the One was before it. This activity from the substance of Intellect is Soul, which came to be while Intellect stayed in its place—just as Intellect came to be while the One stayed in its place. But Soul does not stay in its place; it produces an image of itself, which is the sense world.

§V.1.6 [20–30] · Plotinus, Enneads V.1.6
Manuscript
Greek

ἕκαστον τὸ εἰς τελειότητα ἐλθὸν γεννᾷ

English

Each thing that has reached its perfection produces something. That which is eternally perfect produces eternally, and what it produces is eternally less than itself. What then must we say about the Most Perfect? That nothing comes from it except that which is greatest after it. That which is greatest after it is Intellect, and Intellect is second. For Intellect sees the One, and needs only the One. But the One has no need of Intellect. And what is produced by that which is above Intellect must be Intellect. And Intellect is the greatest of all things after the One, because all other things are below it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §V.1.6 [1–10]: The word “overflowed” (ὑπερερρύη) is Plotinus’s central metaphor for emanation. The One does not choose to create, does not plan, does not deliberate. It produces by the necessity of its own perfection, as a spring produces water or the sun produces light. This is neither creation ex nihilo (as in the Abrahamic traditions) nor craftsmanship (as in Plato’s Timaeus). It is a third model: spontaneous, necessary, and involuntary generation from superabundance.
  2. §V.1.6 [10–20]: The three-level hierarchy—One, Intellect, Soul—became the template for all subsequent Neoplatonic metaphysics. Proclus would elaborate it into a system of triadic processions; Pseudo-Dionysius would Christianize it into his celestial hierarchies; the Kabbalists would develop it into the ten sefirot. The key structural principle is that each level is produced by the overflow of the one above it and produces the one below it by its own overflow. The soul’s task in theosis is to reverse this process: to ascend from Soul to Intellect to the One.
  3. §V.1.6 [20–30]: The principle that “each thing that has reached its perfection produces something” is Plotinus’s answer to the problem of why there is something rather than nothing. The One does not produce out of deficiency or desire but out of fullness. This has direct consequences for the theosis question: the soul’s descent into matter is not a punishment or a mistake but a natural consequence of the superabundance of being. And the return is equally natural.
