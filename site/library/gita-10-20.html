Bhagavad Gita 10.20: I Am the Self in the Heart of All

Vyasa (attributed), traditional

Introduction

Chapter 10 of the Bhagavad Gita is known as the Vibhuti Yoga—the yoga of divine manifestations. In it, Krishna reveals to Arjuna the extent of his presence in the world. He is the Self seated in the heart of all beings. He is the beginning, the middle, and the end of all that exists. Among the Adityas he is Vishnu, among lights he is the sun, among stars he is the moon. The list goes on for forty verses, and its cumulative effect is overwhelming: there is nothing in which the divine is not present.

Verse 10.20 is the thesis statement. Before enumerating his specific manifestations, Krishna declares the general principle: “I am the Self seated in the heart of all beings.” This is not metaphor. It is the Gita’s restatement of the Upanishadic teaching that the innermost Self (ātman) of every creature is identical with the divine. The theosis question is answered in a single line: divinity is not something to be achieved but something already present, waiting to be recognized.

§10.20 · Bhagavad Gita 10.20
Manuscript
Sanskrit

aham ātmā guḍākeśa sarvabhūtāśayasthitaḥ | aham ādiś ca madhyaṃ ca bhūtānām anta eva ca

English

I am the Self, O Gudakesha (Arjuna), seated in the heart of all beings. I am the beginning, the middle, and the end of all beings.

§10.21–22 · Bhagavad Gita 10.21–22
Manuscript
Sanskrit

ādityānām ahaṃ viṣṇur jyotiṣāṃ ravir aṃśumān | marīcir marutām asmi nakṣatrāṇām ahaṃ śaśī | vedānāṃ sāmavedo’smi devānām asmi vāsavaḥ | indriyāṇāṃ manaś cāsmi bhūtānām asmi cetanā

English

Among the Adityas, I am Vishnu. Among lights, I am the radiant sun. Among the Maruts, I am Marichi. Among the stars, I am the moon. Among the Vedas, I am the Sama Veda. Among the gods, I am Indra. Among the senses, I am the mind. In beings, I am consciousness.

§10.39 · Bhagavad Gita 10.39
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yac cāpi sarvabhūtānāṃ bījaṃ tad aham arjuna | na tad asti vinā yat syān mayā bhūtaṃ carācaram

English

And whatever is the seed of all beings, that I am, O Arjuna. There is no being, moving or unmoving, that can exist without me.

§10.41–42 · Bhagavad Gita 10.41–42
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yad yad vibhūtimat sattvaṃ śrīmad ūrjitam eva vā | tat tad evāvagaccha tvaṃ mama tejo’ṃśasambhavam | athavā bahunaitena kiṃ jñātena tavārjuna | viṣṭabhyaāham idaṃ kṛtsnam ekāṃśena sthito jagat

English

Whatever being is glorious, prosperous, or powerful, know that it springs from a fraction of my splendour. But what need is there for all this detail, Arjuna? I stand sustaining this entire world with a single fragment of myself.

Translator's Notes

  1. §10.20: The compound sarvabhūtāśayasthitaḥ means “seated in the inner chamber (āśaya) of all (sarva) beings (bhūta).” This echoes the Katha Upanishad’s image of the Self hidden in the cave of the heart. But where the Upanishad speaks of the Self in the third person, Krishna speaks in the first person: aham, “I.” The philosophical abstraction of the Upanishads becomes, in the Gita, a personal declaration from God.
  2. §10.42: The closing image—“I stand sustaining this entire world with a single fragment of myself” (ekāṃśena sthito jagat)—recalls the Purusha Sukta’s teaching that one quarter of the cosmic person became all of creation while three quarters remained transcendent. The visible universe, in all its vastness, is a fraction of the divine. This is not humility but scale: the divine so exceeds creation that the entire cosmos is a minor expression of it.
