Gospel of Thomas: The Kingdom Within (Sayings 3, 22, 50, 77, 108)

Εὐαγγέλιον Θωμᾶ
Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Gospel of Thomas is a collection of 114 sayings attributed to Jesus, preserved in Coptic in Nag Hammadi Codex II. It contains no narrative, no miracles, no crucifixion, no resurrection. Only words. The text claims that whoever finds the interpretation of these sayings “will not taste death.” Unlike the canonical Gospels, Thomas locates salvation not in an event but in understanding—a direct act of gnosis that reveals what has always been present.

The five sayings selected here represent the core of Thomas’s theology of the divine within. Saying 3 places the kingdom inside the seeker. Saying 22 dissolves the binary oppositions—male and female, inner and outer, above and below—that structure ordinary consciousness. Saying 50 gives the disciples an origin story: they came from the light. Saying 77 identifies Jesus with a universal divine presence in all matter. Saying 108 collapses the distance between the seeker and the source entirely. Together they form a coherent theology of immanent divinity that stands in sharp tension with the orthodox insistence on transcendence.

§Saying 3 · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 2, plate 33
Manuscript
Coptic/Greek

Ⲑⲁⲟⲉ ⲓⲉ · ⲣⲉⲓⲁⲓⲉⲥ ⲓⲉⲓ · λέγει Ἰησοῦς · ἐὰν οἱ ὡγούμενοι λέγωσιν ὑμῖν · ἤ βασιλεία ἐντὸς ὑμῶν ἐστιν καὶ ἐκτὸς ὑμῶν

English

Jesus said: If those who lead you say to you, “See, the kingdom is in the sky,” then the birds of the sky will precede you. If they say to you, “It is in the sea,” then the fish will precede you. Rather, the kingdom is within you and outside you. When you come to know yourselves, then you will be known, and you will realize that you are the children of the living Father. But if you do not know yourselves, then you dwell in poverty, and you are that poverty.

§Saying 22 · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 2, plate 37
Manuscript
Coptic/Greek

λέγει Ἰησοῦς · ὅταν ποιήσητε τὰ δύο ἕν · καὶ τὸ ἔσωθεν ὡς τὸ ἔξωθεν · καὶ τὸ ἄνω ὡς τὸ κάτω

English

Jesus said to them: When you make the two one, and when you make the inside like the outside and the outside like the inside, and the above like the below, and when you make the male and the female one and the same, so that the male not be male nor the female female—then you will enter the kingdom.

§Saying 50 · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 2, plate 41
Manuscript
Coptic/Greek

λέγει Ἰησοῦς · ἐὰν λέγωσιν ὑμῖν · πόθεν ἐλήλυθατε · λέγετε αὐτοῖς · ἐκ τοῦ φωτὸς ἐλήλυθαμεν

English

Jesus said: If they say to you, “Where did you come from?” say to them, “We came from the light, the place where the light came into being on its own accord and established itself and became manifest through their image.” If they say to you, “Is it you?” say, “We are its children, and we are the elect of the living Father.” If they ask you, “What is the sign of your Father in you?” say to them, “It is movement and repose.”

§Saying 77 · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 2, plate 46
Manuscript
Coptic/Greek

λέγει Ἰησοῦς · ἐγώ εἰμι τὸ φῶς τὸ ἐπί πᾶσιν · ἐγώ εἰμι τὸ πᾶν · σχίσον ξύλον · ἐγώ ἐκεῖ εἰμι

English

Jesus said: I am the light that is over all things. I am all. From me all came forth, and to me all attained. Split a piece of wood: I am there. Lift up the stone, and you will find me there.

λέγει Ἰησοῦς · ὁ πίνων ἐκ τοῦ στόματός μου · γενήσεται ὡς ἐγώ · καὶ ἐγώ αὐτὸς γενήσομαι

Translator's Notes

  1. §Saying 3: The kingdom “within you and outside you” collapses the spatial metaphor entirely. The Coptic nhytn (“within you”) is unambiguously interior—this is not a community reading (“among you”) as some scholars propose for Luke 17:21. Thomas means it literally: the divine is inside the individual. The second clause, “outside you,” prevents this from becoming mere subjectivism. The kingdom is not a psychological state. It is everywhere, and the obstacle is not its absence but the seeker’s failure to recognize it.
  2. §Saying 22: The dissolution of opposites—inside/outside, above/below, male/female—echoes the Valentinian concept of the syzygy, the paired aeons of the Pleroma. But Thomas lacks Valentinian cosmological machinery. Here the unity is achieved not through emanation but through direct perception. The saying also resonates with Paul’s declaration in Galatians 3:28 (“there is neither male nor female”), though the theological context is entirely different.
  3. §Saying 50: This saying reads like a catechism for the soul’s ascent—a set of passwords for passing through the archons (cosmic rulers) who guard the gates between the material world and the divine realm. The concept of “movement and repose” as the sign of the Father appears in several Gnostic systems and may reflect an understanding of divinity as simultaneously dynamic and still, the unmoved mover that is also the source of all motion.
  4. §Saying 77: This is the most radical immanence claim in any early Christian text. The declaration “I am the light that is over all things” places Jesus in the role of the cosmic light found in John 1:4–5. But the second half—“split a piece of wood, I am there”—goes further than anything in the canonical tradition. The divine is not merely present in creation; it is materially located in every object. This is closer to Stoic pantheism than to anything in orthodox Christianity.
  5. §Saying 108: The most direct theosis statement in the Thomas collection. “He who will drink from my mouth will become like me. I myself shall become he.” This is not imitation or moral conformity. It is ontological identification: the seeker becomes Jesus, and Jesus becomes the seeker. The orthodox tradition would eventually develop its own theosis doctrine (“God became man so that man might become God”), but it always maintained a distinction between Creator and creature. Thomas dissolves that distinction entirely.
