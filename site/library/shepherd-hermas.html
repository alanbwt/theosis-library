Shepherd of Hermas: Vision 1

Hermas, c. 100-160 AD

Introduction

The Shepherd of Hermas was one of the most widely read texts in the early Church, quoted by Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and Origen, and nearly included in the New Testament canon. Written in Rome, probably between 100 and 160 AD, it consists of a series of visions, mandates, and parables delivered to a freedman named Hermas by an elderly woman (representing the Church) and later by an angel in the form of a shepherd. Vision 1 introduces the central theme: Hermas is a sinner in need of repentance, and repentance is still possible—but the window is closing.

The Shepherd matters for the theosis tradition because it presents transformation not as an intellectual process but as a moral and ecclesial one. The Church appears to Hermas as an old woman who grows younger as her members repent—the corporate body is being renewed, becoming what it was always meant to be. This image of collective transformation, the whole Church growing toward its perfected state, represents a strand of theosis thinking that is often overshadowed by the individualistic mysticism of later centuries.

Ὁ θρέψας με ἐπώλησέν τινι Ῥόδῃ εἰς Ῥώμην.

English

The one who raised me sold me to a certain Rhoda in Rome. After many years, I saw her again and began to love her as a sister. After some time I saw her bathing in the river Tiber, and I gave her my hand and led her out of the river. Seeing her beauty, I reflected in my heart, saying: “I should be happy if I had a wife of such beauty and character.” I reflected on this and nothing more.

Manuscript
Greek

μετὰ χρόνον τινα πορευομένου μου εἰς Κύμας

English

After a certain time, as I was journeying to Cumae and glorifying the creations of God—how great and beautiful and powerful they are—as I walked I fell asleep. And a spirit took me and carried me through a pathless region through which no person could travel, for the place was precipitous and broken up by streams of water. When I had crossed that river, I came to level ground and knelt down and began to pray to the Lord and to confess my sins.

§Vision 1.1.9–2.1 · Shepherd of Hermas, Vision 1.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

προσευχομένου μου ἀνοίγη ὁ οὐρανὸς

English

While I was praying, the heaven was opened and I saw that woman whom I had desired. She greeted me from heaven, saying: “Hermas, hail!” And looking at her I said: “Lady, what are you doing here?” She answered me: “I was taken up to accuse you of your sins before the Lord.” I said to her: “Are you now accusing me?” “No,” she said, “but hear the words which I am about to say to you. God, who dwells in the heavens and created out of nothing the things which are, and increased and multiplied them for the sake of his holy Church, is angry with you because you sinned against me.”

§Vision 1.2.2–4 · Shepherd of Hermas, Vision 1.2
Manuscript
Greek

ἀπεκρίθην αὐτῇ καὶ εἶπον

English

I answered her and said: “Lady, if I sinned against you, in what way did I sin? When did I speak an indecent word to you? Did I not always regard you as a goddess? Did I not always respect you as a sister? Why do you charge me falsely, Lady, with these wicked and unclean things?” She laughed and said to me: “The desire for evil entered your heart. Or do you not think that it is an evil deed for a just man when an evil desire enters his heart? It is indeed a sin, and a great one,” she said.

English

After she had spoken these words the heavens were shut. I was overwhelmed with sorrow and fear, and said to myself: “If this sin is recorded against me, how can I be saved?” While I was considering these things and debating them in my heart, I saw before me a great white chair made of snow-white wool. Translator's Notes

  1. §Vision 1.1.1–4: The autobiographical opening is unusual in early Christian literature. Hermas presents himself not as an apostle or prophet but as an ordinary freedman—a former slave—who struggles with everyday temptation. The sin in question is remarkably mild by modern standards: he thought a woman was beautiful and briefly wished he could marry someone like her. That this is treated as a serious sin reflects the rigorous moral standards of the early Roman church.
  2. §Vision 1.3.1–4: The elderly woman who appears is later identified as the Church. She appears old because, Hermas is told, she was created before all things—the Church is the purpose of creation. In subsequent visions she grows younger as the community repents. This image of the Church as a person undergoing transformation—aging in reverse, becoming what she was always meant to be—is one of the most vivid corporate theosis images in early Christianity.
