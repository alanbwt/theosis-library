Odyssey, Book I: The Counsel of the Gods

Odyssey, Book I: The Counsel of the Gods
Homer, c. 8th century BCE
Venetus A (Marcianus Graecus 454)

Introduction

Where the Iliad begins with wrath, the Odyssey begins with wandering. Homer's second epic follows Odysseus — polytropos, the man of many turns — on his decade-long journey home from Troy. Book I opens on Olympus, where Zeus delivers one of the most important theological statements in Greek literature: humans bring suffering upon themselves beyond what fate decrees. Athena then descends to Ithaca to set the homecoming in motion.

§1.1-10 · Od. 1.1-10
Greek

Ἄνδρα μοι ἔννεπε, Μοῦσα, πολύτροπον, ὃς μάλα πολλὰ πλάγχθη, ἐπεὶ Τροίης ἱερὸν πτολίεθρον ἔπερσε· πολλῶν δ᾿ ἀνθρώπων ἴδεν ἄστεα καὶ νόον ἔγνω, πολλὰ δ᾿ ὅ γ᾿ ἐν πόντῳ πάθεν ἄλγεα ὃν κατὰ θυμόν, ἀρνύμενος ἥν τε ψυχὴν καὶ νόστον ἑταίρων.

English

Tell me, Muse, of the man of many turns, who wandered far and wide after he sacked the sacred city of Troy. Many were the peoples whose cities he saw and whose minds he learned, and many the sorrows he suffered at sea in his heart, struggling for his own life and the homecoming of his companions.

§1.32-43 · Od. 1.32-43
Greek

ὢ πόποι, οἷον δή νυ θεοὺς βροτοὶ αἰτιόωνται. ἐξ ἡμέων γάρ φασι κάκ᾿ ἔμμεναι· οἱ δὲ καὶ αὐτοὶ σφῇσιν ἀτασθαλίῃσιν ὑπὲρ μόρον ἄλγε᾿ ἔχουσιν.

English

How mortals blame the gods! They say that evils come from us, but they themselves by their own recklessness bring suffering beyond what is fated.
