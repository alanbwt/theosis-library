Platform Sutra: Your Nature Is Originally Pure

Huineng, 638-713 AD

Introduction

The Platform Sūtra (Liùzǔ Tánjīng) is the autobiography and collected teachings of Huineng (638–713 CE), the Sixth Patriarch of Chan Buddhism and the most important figure in the Zen tradition. It is the only Chinese Buddhist text honoured with the title sūtra—a designation otherwise reserved for the words of the Buddha himself. Huineng was an illiterate firewood seller from the southern provinces who reportedly attained enlightenment upon hearing a single line of the Diamond Sūtra. His teaching is radical in its simplicity: your own nature is originally pure; your own nature is originally complete; there is nothing to add and nothing to remove.

For the theosis question, the Platform Sūtra represents the most direct possible answer. You do not become a Buddha. You already are one. The obscurations are not real barriers but misunderstandings. Enlightenment is not a distant attainment but an immediate recognition of what was always the case. This is the “sudden” school of Chan, as opposed to the “gradual” school, and the debate between them—whether awakening is instantaneous recognition or progressive cultivation—mirrors the debate between Eastern and Western Christian theologies of deification.

§The Original Purity of Mind · Liùzǔ Tánjīng, Section 1
Manuscript
Chinese

Shī yún: Shànzhī, rénxìng běnlái qīngjìng. Wànfǎ jiē zài zìxìng. Ruò sī yīqiē èshì, jí xíng èxíng. Ruò sī yīqiē shànshì, jí xíng shànxíng. Shì zhī wànfǎ jiē zài zìxìng. Zìxìng chángqīng, rú rì yuè chángmíng. Zhǐ wèi yúngài, shàngmíng xiààn. Hūrán fēngchuī yúnsàn, shàngxià jùmíng, wànxìng jiē xiàn.

English

The Patriarch said: “Good friends, the self-nature of human beings is originally pure. The ten thousand dharmas are all within the self-nature. If one thinks of all the evil things, then evil actions are performed. If one thinks of all the good things, then good actions are performed. Thus all dharmas are within the self-nature. The self-nature is always pure, just as the sun and moon are always bright. It is only because of clouds that there is darkness above and brightness below. When a sudden wind blows the clouds away, all is bright, and the ten thousand forms appear.”

§Huineng's Verse · Liùzǔ Tánjīng, Section 1
Manuscript
Chinese

Huìnéng yǒu jì yīn: Pútí běn wú shù, míngjìng yì fēi tái. Běnlái wú yī wù, héchù rě chénāi?

English

Huineng composed this verse: “Bodhi originally has no tree. The bright mirror also has no stand. Originally there is not a single thing—where can dust alight?”

§Sudden Awakening · Liùzǔ Tánjīng, Section 2
Manuscript
Chinese

Shī yún: Shànzhī, wǒ cǐ fǎmén, cóng shàngyǐlái, xiānjīn jīn dài, jiē lì wúnàn wéizōng, wúxìang wéitǐ, wúzhù wéiběn. Wúxìang zhě hé? Yú xiàngér lí xiàng. Wúnàn zhě hé? Yú niànér wú niàn. Wúzhù zhě, rén zhī běnxìng.

English

The Patriarch said: “Good friends, in this teaching of mine, from ancient times up to the present, all have set up no-thought as the main doctrine, non-form as the substance, and non-abiding as the root. What is non-form? Non-form is to be apart from form while in the midst of forms. No-thought is to be without thought while in the midst of thoughts. Non-abiding is the original nature of human beings.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Original Purity of Mind: The term zìxìng (self-nature, Sanskrit svabhāva) is used here in a way that would alarm a strict Madhyamaka Buddhist, for whom svabhāva is precisely what all things lack. Huineng uses it to mean not an inherent essence but the original, luminous quality of awareness before it is obscured by conceptual overlay. The Chinese Chan tradition was less concerned with doctrinal consistency across schools than with the direct pointing at experience. The cloud-and-sun metaphor echoes the Tathāgatagarbha tradition’s image of adventitious defilements covering an originally pure nature.
  2. §Huineng's Verse: This verse is the most famous poem in the Chan/Zen tradition. It was composed in competition with Shenxiu, the head monk, whose verse ran: “The body is the Bodhi tree, the mind is like a bright mirror stand. At all times we must strive to polish it and must not let dust alight.” Shenxiu’s verse represents the gradualist position: enlightenment requires constant practice. Huineng’s response negates the entire framework. There is no mirror to polish because there is no mirror. The Fifth Patriarch recognized Huineng’s verse as the deeper understanding and secretly transmitted the robe and bowl to him.
  3. §Sudden Awakening: The triad of no-thought (wúnàn), non-form (wúxìang), and non-abiding (wúzhù) became the technical foundation of Huineng’s teaching. Crucially, none of these mean absence. “No-thought” does not mean the cessation of thinking but thinking without attachment—thoughts arise and pass without being grasped. “Non-form” does not mean the rejection of forms but freedom from fixation on them. “Non-abiding” means the mind rests nowhere, clings to nothing. This is not withdrawal from the world but a particular way of being fully present in it.
