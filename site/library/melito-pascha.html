On Pascha: He Who Hung the Earth

Melito of Sardis, d. c. 180 AD

Introduction

Melito of Sardis composed On Pascha (Peri Pascha) around 160–170 AD as a homily for the Easter vigil. Lost for centuries, the full text was recovered only in 1940 from a papyrus codex. It is the most rhetorically powerful Christological text of the second century—a sustained meditation on the Passover narrative of Exodus as a type fulfilled in the death and resurrection of Christ. The climax is a series of paradoxes that identify the crucified Jesus with the Creator God: “He who hung the earth hangs there. He who fixed the heavens is fixed there. He who fastened all things is fastened on the tree.”

Melito’s significance for the theosis tradition lies in his unflinching identification of the suffering human being on the cross with the God who made the universe. If the one who dies on the cross is the same one who created the cosmos, then the boundary between divine and human has been crossed in the most radical way possible—not in mystical contemplation or philosophical abstraction, but in flesh and blood and death. The incarnation is not merely a doctrine; it is, in Melito’s telling, the most dramatic event in the history of being.

§1–5 · Peri Pascha, sections 1–5

Greek

Ἡ μὲν γραφὴ τῆς Ἑβραϊκῆς ἐξόδου ἀνέγνωσται

English

The Scripture of the Hebrew Exodus has been read, and the words of the mystery have been explained: how the sheep was sacrificed, and how the people were saved, and how Pharaoh was scourged through the mystery. Understand, therefore, beloved: the mystery of the Pascha is new and old, eternal and temporal, corruptible and incorruptible, mortal and immortal.

§46–47 · Peri Pascha, sections 46–47
Manuscript
Greek

οὗτός ἐστιν τὸ πάσχα τῆς σωτηρίας ἡμῶν

English

He is the Pascha of our salvation. It is he who in many people endured many things: it is he who was murdered in Abel, bound in Isaac, exiled in Jacob, sold in Joseph, exposed in Moses, slaughtered in the lamb, persecuted in David, dishonoured in the prophets.

§66–71 · Peri Pascha, sections 66–71
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ τὸν οὐρανὸν ποιήσας καὶ τὴν γῆν καὶ τὸν ἄνθρωπον ἐν ἀρχῇ πλάσας

English

He who made the heaven and the earth, and fashioned humanity in the beginning, is proclaimed through the law and the prophets, is made flesh in a virgin, is hung upon the tree, is buried in the earth, is raised from the dead, and is exalted to the heights of heaven. He is the silent lamb, the slaughtered lamb, born of Mary the fair ewe. He was seized from the flock and dragged to slaughter, and sacrificed in the evening, and buried at night.

§95–97 · Peri Pascha, sections 95–97
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ κρεμάσας τὴν γῆν κρεμαται · ὁ πήξας τοὺς οὐρανοὺς πέπηγεν · ὁ στηρίξας τὰ πάντα ἐπὶ ξύλου ἐστερεώθη · ὁ δεσπότης ὕβρισται · ὁ θεὸς πεφόνευται

English

He who hung the earth hangs there. He who fixed the heavens is fixed there. He who fastened all things is fastened on the tree. The Master is insulted. God is murdered. The King of Israel is destroyed by an Israelite hand. O fearful murder! O unheard-of injustice! The Master is disguised, his body naked, and he is not even deemed worthy of a covering that his nakedness might not be seen. For this reason the stars turned and fled, and the day was darkened.

§100–103 · Peri Pascha, sections 100–103
Manuscript
Greek

ἀλλ’ ἀνέστη ἐκ νεκρῶν καὶ ἀνηλθεν εἰς τὰ ὕψη τῶν οὐρανῶν

English

But he rose from the dead, and mounted up to the heights of heaven. When the Lord had clothed himself with humanity, and had suffered for the sake of the sufferer, and had been bound for the sake of the imprisoned, and had been judged for the sake of the condemned, and had been buried for the sake of the one who was buried, he rose from the dead and cried out: Who is he who contends with me? Let him stand before me. I set the condemned free. I gave the dead life. I raised up the one who had been entombed. Who is my adversary? I am the Christ. I am the one who destroyed death and triumphed over the enemy and trampled Hades underfoot and bound the strong one and carried off humanity to the heights of heaven. I am the Christ.

Translator's Notes

  1. §46–47: This passage is a striking example of “prosopological exegesis”—the identification of Christ as the hidden subject behind the Old Testament narratives. Christ was not merely prefigured by Abel, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Moses, and David; he was present in their suffering. This reading of the Hebrew Bible as Christological through and through became standard in patristic interpretation and has deep implications for the theosis question: if Christ was always already present in human suffering, then the divine has always been entangled with the human.
  2. §95–97: The phrase “God is murdered” (ὁ θεὸς πεφόνευται) is the earliest and most uncompromising statement of theopaschism—the doctrine that God suffered on the cross. Later theology would qualify this: technically it is the human nature of Christ that suffers, not the divine nature. But Melito refuses the qualification. The rhetorical power of the passage depends on the full force of the paradox: the one who made the cosmos is the one who dies on a piece of wood.
