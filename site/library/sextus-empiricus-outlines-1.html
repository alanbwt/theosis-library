Introduction

Sextus Empiricus (c. 160–210 CE) systematized Pyrrhonian skepticism in the Outlines of Pyrrhonism. Book I defines the skeptical disposition: the ability to set opposing appearances and judgments against one another, leading to suspension of judgment (epoche) and tranquility (ataraxia).

§I.8–12 · Sextus Empiricus, Pyrrhoniae Hypotyposes I.8–12 (Mutschmann-Mau)
Manuscript
Greek

ἔστι δὲ ἡ σκεπτικὴ δύναμις ἀντιθετικὴ φαινομένων τε καὶ νοουμένων

English

Skepticism is the ability to set out oppositions among things that appear and things that are thought in any way whatever, from which we come first to suspension of judgment and after that to tranquility. The skeptic’s aim is tranquility in matters of opinion and moderation of feeling in matters forced upon us.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.8–12: The skeptical epoche (ἐποχή, suspension of judgment) is not a dogmatic denial of knowledge but a therapeutic practice: by ceasing to assert or deny, the skeptic finds the tranquility that dogmatic philosophers seek but never achieve through their doctrines.
