Res Gestae Divi Augusti: The Divine Augustus

Augustus, 63 BCE-14 AD

Introduction

The Res Gestae Divi Augusti (The Deeds of the Divine Augustus) is the political autobiography that Augustus Caesar (63 BCE–14 CE) composed shortly before his death and ordered inscribed on bronze tablets at his mausoleum. Copies were erected throughout the empire; the most complete surviving text is the Monumentum Ancyranum in modern Ankara. The document records Augustus’s military victories, political offices, building projects, and acts of generosity—and its very title declares him divus, divine.

Roman emperor worship represents a form of theosis radically different from anything in the Christian, Platonic, or Hindu traditions. Here, a human being becomes a god not through mystical transformation, philosophical contemplation, or divine grace, but through the exercise of supreme political power. The Senate voted Augustus divine honours after his death; his successors often received them during their lifetimes. This is deification by decree: the state creates gods. The early Christians lived in a world where this was the dominant public understanding of how humans become divine, and their counter-claim—that a crucified criminal was the true God—was made in direct opposition to it.

§1 · Res Gestae Divi Augusti, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Latin

Annos undeviginti natus exercitum privato consilio et privata impensa comparavi, per quem rem publicam a dominatione factionis oppressam in libertatem vindicavi.

English

At the age of nineteen, on my own initiative and at my own expense, I raised an army by means of which I restored liberty to the Republic, which had been oppressed by the tyranny of a faction. For which service the Senate enrolled me in its order by laudatory resolutions and gave me the rank of a consul in the taking of votes, and gave me the imperium.

§2 · Res Gestae Divi Augusti, Chapter 34
Manuscript
Latin

In consulatu sexto et septimo, postquam bella civilia exstinxeram, per consensum universorum potitus rerum omnium, rem publicam ex mea potestate in senatus populique Romani arbitrium transtuli. Quo pro merito meo senatus consulto Augustus appellatus sum.

English

In my sixth and seventh consulships, after I had extinguished civil wars, and at a time when with universal consent I was in complete control of affairs, I transferred the Republic from my power to the dominion of the Senate and People of Rome. For this service I was named Augustus by decree of the Senate, and the doorposts of my house were publicly wreathed with laurels, and a civic crown was fixed over my door.

§3 · Res Gestae Divi Augusti, Chapter 35
Manuscript
Latin

Post id tempus auctoritate omnibus praestiti, potestatis autem nihilo amplius habui quam ceteri qui mihi quoque in magistratu conlegae fuerunt. Clupeus aureus in curia Iulia positus.

English

After that time I surpassed all in authority, although I possessed no more official power than others who were my colleagues in the several magistracies. A golden shield was placed in the Curia Julia, which, as attested by the inscription on it, was given to me by the Senate and People of Rome on account of my courage, clemency, justice, and piety.

§4 · Inscription formula (various imperial dedications)
Manuscript
Latin

Divo Augusto, divi Iuli filio, patri patriae. Senatus populusque Romanus.

English

To the divine Augustus, son of the divine Julius, father of the fatherland. The Senate and People of Rome.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1: The opening sentence establishes the pattern of the entire document: Augustus acts on his own initiative, at his own expense, and through his own power. The implicit theology is that divinity is achieved through supreme agency—the man who saves the state earns divine honours. This is the opposite of the Christian understanding, where divinity is received as a gift, not earned through achievement.
  2. §2–3: The title Augustus (from augere, “to increase” or “to make sacred”) carried religious connotations from the beginning. The name suggests one who has been set apart, made holy, elevated beyond the merely human. The four virtues inscribed on the golden shield—virtus (courage), clementia (clemency), iustitia (justice), and pietas (piety)—constitute the Roman civic path to deification: the emperor becomes divine by perfecting the virtues of a Roman citizen to a superhuman degree.
  3. §4: The formula Divo Augusto, divi Iuli filio (“To the divine Augustus, son of the divine Julius”) creates a genealogy of divinity through political succession. Julius Caesar was declared divus by the Senate after his assassination; Augustus then became “son of a god” (divi filius) while still alive. The Christian title for Jesus—“Son of God”—was heard in the Roman world against this background. The Gospel of Mark opens with “Jesus Christ, the Son of God”; Roman audiences would have heard this as a direct counter-claim to the emperor’s title.
