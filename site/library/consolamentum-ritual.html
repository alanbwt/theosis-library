Ritual of the Consolamentum: Becoming a Perfect

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Consolamentum was the central and only sacrament of the Cathar church. The ritual text survives in two versions: one in Occitan (the Rituel Cathare, preserved in a manuscript from Lyon) and one in Latin (preserved in Inquisition records from Florence). The ceremony consisted of the laying on of hands and the placement of the Gospel of John upon the head of the candidate, through which the Holy Spirit was believed to be transmitted directly, making the recipient a “Perfect” (Perfectus or Perfecta)—a fully initiated member of the Cathar elect.

The consolamentum is the medieval continuation of Gnostic theosis. The Cathars believed that through this rite, the divine spark imprisoned in the body was reconnected to its source in the good God. The “Perfect” were understood to have already begun their return to the heavenly world. They lived lives of extreme asceticism—no meat, no sex, no property, no oaths—because they understood themselves as angels temporarily clothed in flesh, awaiting liberation. The ritual itself is spare and austere, consisting mainly of prayers, renunciations, and the transmission of the Lord’s Prayer.

§Opening Prayer · Lyon MS, Rituel Cathare, f. 1r
Manuscript
Latin

Oremus Patrem et Filium et Spiritum Sanctum. Oremus Dominum Deum, qui habet potestatem dimittendi peccata et liberandi nos a vinculis maligni.

English

Let us pray to the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. Let us pray to the Lord God, who has the power to forgive sins and to free us from the bonds of the evil one. Let us pray that the Holy Spirit may descend upon this servant of God and make this person a dwelling place of the Most High.

§The Renunciation · Lyon MS, f. 1v
Manuscript
Latin

Dicit senior: Vis accipere baptismum Spiritus Sancti, consolamentum Bonorum Christianorum?

English

The elder says: “Do you wish to receive the baptism of the Holy Spirit, the consolamentum of the Good Christians? Do you renounce the world and all that is in it? Do you promise to eat no meat, to take no oaths, to lie with no man or woman, and to live in truth and in the faith of the Good Christians unto the end of your days?” And the candidate answers: “I do.”

§Transmission of the Lord's Prayer · Lyon MS, f. 2r
Manuscript
Latin

Dicit senior: Tradimus vobis orationem sanctam quam Dominus Iesus Christus docuit discipulis suis.

English

The elder then says: “We give to you the holy prayer which the Lord Jesus Christ taught to his disciples. Henceforth you may say this prayer at all times, by day and by night, alone and in company, and you must never eat or drink without first saying this prayer. If you fail in this, you must do penance.” And they deliver to the candidate the Pater Noster.

§The Laying on of Hands · Lyon MS, f. 2v
Manuscript
Latin

Tunc senior ponit Evangelium Iohannis super caput candidati, et omnes Boni Christiani ponunt manus dexteras super candidatum.

English

Then the elder places the Gospel of John upon the head of the candidate, and all the Good Christians present place their right hands upon the candidate, and the elder says: “Holy Father, receive this your servant, and send upon this person your grace and your Holy Spirit.” And they say the Pater Noster together, and the elder reads the prologue of the Gospel of John: “In the beginning was the Word.”

§The Kiss of Peace · Lyon MS, f. 3r
Manuscript
Latin

Cum oratio et lectio finitae sunt, senior dat osculum pacis candidato, et omnes Boni Christiani similiter.

English

When the prayer and the reading are finished, the elder gives the kiss of peace to the candidate, and all the Good Christians do likewise. And from that hour the candidate is a Good Christian, a Perfect, having received the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands, which is the true baptism that Christ gave to his apostles.

§Closing Admonition · Lyon MS, f. 3r-v
Manuscript
Latin

Dicit senior: Accepistis Spiritum Sanctum a Deo et ab Ecclesia Bonorum Christianorum et a nobis.

English

The elder says: “You have received the Holy Spirit from God and from the Church of the Good Christians, and from us. Henceforth you are a servant of God and of Christ. You must not lie, nor swear oaths, nor touch a woman or a man in lust, nor kill any living thing, nor eat meat or eggs or cheese, nor travel alone. And if you are faithful in these things, your soul shall return to the heavenly Father when it departs this body of death.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Renunciation: The renunciations—no meat, no oaths, no sexual contact—define the Cathar “Perfect” as a person who has already begun to separate from the material world. The prohibition against oaths was particularly threatening to medieval feudal society, which was held together by oaths of fealty. This alone was enough to make the Cathars enemies of the social order.
  2. §The Laying on of Hands: The Cathars rejected water baptism, insisting that the true baptism was the baptism of the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands, as described in Acts 8:17 and 19:6. The placement of the Gospel of John on the candidate’s head reflects the Cathars’ particular reverence for the Fourth Gospel, whose Prologue (“In the beginning was the Word”) they considered the key to all Christian truth.
  3. §Closing Admonition: The promise that “your soul shall return to the heavenly Father when it departs this body of death” is the Cathar theosis statement. Salvation is not resurrection of the body (which is a prison) but liberation of the soul. This directly contradicts the Nicene Creed’s affirmation of “the resurrection of the body,” which is why the Catholic Church classified Catharism as heresy rather than schism.
