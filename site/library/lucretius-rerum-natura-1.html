De Rerum Natura I: The Gods Live Unconcerned

Lucretius, c. 99-55 BCE

Introduction

Titus Lucretius Carus (c. 99–55 BCE) wrote De Rerum Natura (On the Nature of Things) as a verse exposition of Epicurean philosophy. In lines 62–79 of Book I, he celebrates his master Epicurus as the first human being brave enough to challenge the tyranny of religion. While others cowered before the gods, Epicurus looked at the heavens and declared that the universe operates by natural law, not divine will. The gods exist, but they live in perfect tranquility between the worlds, entirely unconcerned with human affairs.

This is the materialist rejection of theosis in its purest ancient form. If the gods have no interest in humanity—if they neither created us nor sustain us nor judge us—then the entire project of becoming divine is based on a misunderstanding. There is no divine plan for human beings, no cosmic drama of fall and redemption, no ladder of ascent. The atoms combine and disperse. Death is the end. The only “salvation” available is the philosophical liberation from the fear of the gods and the fear of death.

Latin original and English translation.
§I.62–67 · Lucretius, De Rerum Natura I.62–67
Manuscript
Latin

Humana ante oculos foede cum vita iaceret / in terris oppressa gravi sub religione, / quae caput a caeli regionibus ostendebat / horribili super aspectu mortalibus instans, / primum Graius homo mortalis tollere contra / est oculos ausus primusque obsistere contra.

English

When human life lay foully prostrate upon the earth, crushed beneath the weight of religion—which showed its face from the regions of heaven, looming over mortals with horrible aspect—a man of Greece was the first to dare to raise his mortal eyes against it, the first to take his stand against it.

§I.68–73 · Lucretius, De Rerum Natura I.68–73
Manuscript
Latin

quem neque fama deum nec fulmina nec minitanti / murmure compressit caelum, sed eo magis acrem / inritat animi virtutem, effringere ut arta / naturae primus portarum claustra cupiret. / ergo vivida vis animi pervicit, et extra / processit longe flammantia moenia mundi.

English

Neither the stories of the gods, nor thunderbolts, nor heaven with its threatening rumble could restrain him. Rather, they only spurred the keen courage of his mind, so that he desired to be the first to burst through the tight bars of nature’s gates. And so the living force of his mind prevailed, and he journeyed far beyond the flaming walls of the world.

§I.74–79 · Lucretius, De Rerum Natura I.74–79
Manuscript
Latin

atque omne immensum peragravit mente animoque, / unde refert nobis victor quid possit oriri, / quid nequeat, finita potestas denique cuique / quanam sit ratione atque alte terminus haerens. / quare religio pedibus subiecta vicissim / obteritur, nos exaequat victoria caelo.

English

He traversed the immeasurable universe in mind and spirit, and returned to us victorious, reporting what can come into being and what cannot, by what principle each thing has its power limited and its deep-set boundary. Therefore religion is now cast beneath our feet and trampled, and his victory raises us to heaven.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.62–67: The unnamed “man of Greece” is Epicurus (341–270 BCE). Lucretius presents him as a liberator and culture hero—the man who freed humanity from the slavery of religious terror. The Latin religio here does not mean “religion” in the modern sense but rather superstitious fear of the gods and their punishments. Lucretius is attacking not belief in general but the specific terror that drives humans to sacrifice, appeasement, and desperate appeals for divine favour.
  2. §I.74–79: The final line—nos exaequat victoria caelo, “his victory raises us to heaven”—is deliberately paradoxical. Epicurus achieves a kind of deification by proving that deification is unnecessary. By liberating humans from fear of the gods, he “raises them to heaven” in the only sense that matters: psychological freedom. This is the Epicurean inversion of theosis—not ascending to become like the gods, but realizing that the gods are irrelevant.
