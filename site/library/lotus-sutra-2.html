Lotus Sutra Chapter 2: All Beings Can Become Buddhas

Gautama Buddha (attributed), c. 563-483 BCE

Introduction

The Lotus Sūtra (Saddharma Puṇḍarīka Sūtra) is the single most influential text in East Asian Buddhism, revered as the “king of sutras” by the Tiantai, Tendai, and Nichiren traditions. Chapter 2, “Skillful Means” (Upāya-kauśalya), contains the Lotus Sūtra’s most revolutionary claim: there is only one vehicle (ekāyana), not three. The earlier Buddhist distinction between the paths of the śrāvaka (hearer), the pratyekabuddha (solitary realizer), and the bodhisattva was merely a pedagogical device. In reality, all beings are destined for complete and perfect Buddhahood.

This is Buddhism’s strongest universalist claim and its most direct answer to the theosis question. Not some beings, not the spiritually gifted, not monks alone—all sentient beings without exception will become Buddhas. The three vehicles were taught as skillful means to accommodate beings of different capacities, but the ultimate truth is that there is only one destination.

§The One Vehicle · Saddharma Puṇḍarīka Sūtra, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Śāriputra ekaṃ yānam eva tathāgatā buddha-yānaṃ sattvānāṃ dharmaṃ deśayanti. Nāsti dvitīyaṃ vā tṛtīyaṃ vā yānam. Śāriputra daśa-dig-lokānāṃ sarva-buddhānāṃ dharmo ’pi evam eva.

English

“Śāriputra, the Thus-Come Ones use only the one Buddha-vehicle to preach the Dharma to living beings. There is no other vehicle, neither a second nor a third. Śāriputra, the Dharma of all the Buddhas of the ten directions is also like this.”

§Skillful Means · Saddharma Puṇḍarīka Sūtra, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Śāriputra ekārtham eva tathāgatā loke utpadyante: sattvānāṃ buddha-jñāna-darśanāya, buddha-jñāna-darśanāvabodhāya, buddha-jñāna-darśanādhigamāya, buddha-jñāna-darśana-mārga-praveśāya.

English

“Śāriputra, the Buddhas appear in the world solely for the one great purpose: to cause living beings to open the Buddha-knowledge, to show living beings the Buddha-knowledge, to cause living beings to awaken to the Buddha-knowledge, and to cause living beings to enter the path of the Buddha-knowledge.”

§The Universal Prophecy · Saddharma Puṇḍarīka Sūtra, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Śāriputra daśasu dig-lokeṣu ekaṃ yāna-dharmaṃ, na dvitīyaṃ na tṛtīyaṃ, anyātra tathāgatānām upāya-deśanāt. Saṃvr̩ti-nāma-pada-vyavaśāt sattvān ākarṣanti. Buddha-jñāna-deśanārtham utpadyante loke. Mama putrāḥ sarva-sattvāḥ, teṣāṃ mahāyānaṃ dāsyāmi.

English

“Śāriputra, in the Buddha-lands of the ten directions there is only the one-vehicle Dharma, not two, not three—except where the Buddhas preach by skillful means. It is by means of provisional names and words that they draw living beings to them. They appear in the world to preach the Buddha-wisdom. All beings are my children, and I shall give them all the great vehicle.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §The One Vehicle: The concept of ekāyana (one vehicle) directly challenged the sectarian structure of early Buddhism, which distinguished between the paths of hearers (śrāvakas), solitary realizers (pratyekabuddhas), and bodhisattvas. The Lotus Sūtra’s claim that these are merely skillful means (upāya) was revolutionary and controversial. Some Theravada scholars rejected it as a later Mahayana innovation. The Lotus Sūtra’s response is that the Buddha always intended the one vehicle but taught the three because beings were not yet ready for the full truth.
  2. §Skillful Means: The fourfold formula—open, show, awaken, enter—became a central organizing principle in Tiantai Buddhist hermeneutics. Zhiyi (538–597 CE), the founder of the Tiantai school, interpreted these four as stages of the Buddha’s pedagogical activity and built an entire system of doctrinal classification around them. The key word is buddha-jñāna-darśana (“Buddha-knowledge and vision”): the sutra claims not that beings can learn about the Buddha’s knowledge but that they can attain it directly.
  3. §The Universal Prophecy: The phrase “all beings are my children” (mama putrāḥ sarva-sattvāḥ) employs paternal language that resonates with the theistic traditions. The Buddha speaks here not as a teacher among equals but as a cosmic father. This is the Lotus Sūtra at its most theistic—and most controversial. The claim that all beings will receive the great vehicle amounts to a doctrine of universal salvation, which stands in tension with the karma-based gradualism of earlier Buddhist teaching.
