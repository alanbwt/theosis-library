Natural History IV.89-91: Beyond the North Wind

Pliny the Elder, 23-79 AD

Introduction

Pliny the Elder’s Natural History (completed 77 CE) includes in Book IV.89–91 a notable Latin account of the Hyperboreans, treated not as pure myth but as a geographical curiosity worthy of inclusion in what was intended as a comprehensive encyclopedia of the natural world. Pliny synthesizes Greek sources—Hecataeus, Pindar, and others—into a compact summary, adding his characteristic Roman interest in practical details: the climate, the length of the day, the agricultural conditions, and the manner of death.

Pliny’s account is significant because it represents the moment when the Hyperborean tradition passes from Greek poetry into Roman scientific prose. The details are striking: the sun shines for six continuous months, the people live to extreme old age, and when they have had enough of life, they leap from a certain cliff into the sea after garlanding themselves and feasting. This voluntary, joyful death is perhaps the most provocative element of the Hyperborean tradition for the theosis question. The Hyperboreans do not fear death because they have already lived fully in the divine presence. Their departure from life is not tragedy but completion—the final act of a soul that has achieved everything mortality can offer.

§IV.89 · Pliny, Natural History IV.89
Manuscript
Latin

Pone eos montes ultraque Aquilonem gens felix, si credimus, quos Hyperboreos appellavere, annoso degit aevo, fabulosis celebrata miraculis. Ibi creduntur esse cardines mundi extremique siderum ambitus, semenstri luce et una die solis aversi, non, ut imperiti dixere, ab aequinoctio verno in autumnum.

English

Beyond these mountains and beyond the north wind there is a happy race, if we can believe it, called the Hyperboreans. They reach extreme old age and are famous for legendary marvels. There, they believe, are the hinges on which the world turns and the extreme limits of the revolutions of the stars. Sunlight lasts for six months, and there is a single day when the sun does not hide itself, as the ignorant would claim, from the spring equinox to the autumn.

§IV.90 · Pliny, Natural History IV.90
Manuscript
Latin

Semel in anno, solstitio, oriuntur iis soles, brumaque semel occidunt. Regio aprica, felici temperie, omni adflatu noxio carens. Domus iis nemora lucique, et deorum cultus viritim gregatimque; discordia ignota et aegritudo omnis. Mors non nisi satietate vitae, epulatis delibutoque senio luxu, ex certa rupe in mare salientibus: hoc beatissimum funeris genus.

English

The sun rises for them once in the year, at the summer solstice, and sets once, at the winter solstice. It is a genial region, with a delightful climate, and free from every harmful blast. The homes of the inhabitants are the woods and groves; the worship of the gods is conducted by individuals and by communities; discord and all manner of sickness are unknown. Death comes to them only when they have had enough of life: after a feast, in old age, sated with luxury, they leap from a certain rock into the sea. This is the happiest form of burial.

§IV.91 · Pliny, Natural History IV.91
Manuscript
Latin

Quidam illos non in Europa sed in ipsa Asiae parte prima litoris posuere, quod ibi similis mos genusque Attacorum sit, alii medios inter utrumque solem antipodum occasus exortusque—quod fieri nequit tanto mari interveniente. Qui de iis scripserunt maxime celebres sunt Hecataeus, Hellanicus, Pindarus, Simonides, Damastes, Aristeas Proconnesius, Apollonides, Callimachus, Diodorus Erythraeus.

English

Some have placed them not in Europe but on the nearest part of the shores of Asia, because there is a people there of similar customs and location called the Attacori; others have placed them midway between the two suns, the setting of one and the rising of the other in the antipodes—but this is impossible, since an immense sea lies between. Those who have written about them most are Hecataeus, Hellanicus, Pindar, Simonides, Damastes, Aristeas of Proconnesus, Apollonides, Callimachus, and Diodorus of Erythrae.

Translator's Notes

  1. §IV.89: Pliny’s conditional “if we can believe it” (si credimus) is characteristic of his method: he reports traditions without fully endorsing them, but includes them because they are widely attested. The detail about six months of sunlight is astronomically accurate for locations within the Arctic Circle, lending the account an unexpected credibility. The “hinges on which the world turns” (cardines mundi) places the Hyperboreans at the cosmic axis—the still point around which the heavens revolve.
  2. §IV.90: The description of voluntary death by leaping into the sea after feasting is one of the most memorable images in the Hyperborean tradition. The Latin phrase mors non nisi satietate vitae (“death only from fullness of life”) captures the essential idea: the Hyperboreans do not die from disease or decay but from satisfaction. They have lived enough. This challenges the assumption that immortality is the highest good—for the Hyperboreans, a complete life freely concluded is superior to an endless one. The theological implications are profound: theosis may not mean living forever but living so fully in the divine presence that death becomes a willing return rather than a catastrophe.
  3. §IV.91: Pliny’s list of authors who wrote about the Hyperboreans is invaluable because most of their works are now lost. Aristeas of Proconnesus, for instance, wrote the Arimaspea, a poem about his journey to the far north, which survives only in fragments. The list demonstrates that the Hyperborean tradition was not a marginal curiosity but a major subject of Greek geographical and mythological writing over several centuries.
