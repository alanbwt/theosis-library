Introduction

Hymns to Cybele and Attis survive in fragments from Catullus, Julian’s Oration V, and inscriptions. The cult of the Great Mother centered on the death and resurrection of the youth Attis, with ecstatic rites including self-mutilation by the galli priests.

§Catullus 63, Lines 1–12 · Catullus, Carmen 63.1–12
Manuscript
Greek

Super alta vectus Attis celeri rate maria, Phrygium ut nemus citato cupide pede tetigit, adiitque opaca silvis redimita loca deae, stimulatus ibi furenti rabie, vagus animis, devolvit ili acuto sibi pondera silice.

English

Over the deep seas Attis was borne in his swift ship, and when with eager haste he reached the Phrygian grove and entered the goddess’s dark-forested domain, there, goaded by raging madness and bereft of reason, he cast off the weight of his manhood with a sharp flint.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Catullus 63: Catullus’s poem captures the horror and fascination that Cybele’s cult evoked in Roman observers. The self-castration of the galli was the most extreme form of sacrificial dedication in ancient religion. The underlying theology of dying to the flesh to be reborn in the divine has obvious Christian parallels.
