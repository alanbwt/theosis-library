Bhagavad Gita 4.7-8: Whenever Dharma Declines

Vyasa (attributed), traditional

Introduction

In Bhagavad Gita 4.7–8, Krishna reveals to Arjuna the doctrine of the avatāra: whenever righteousness (dharma) declines and unrighteousness rises, the divine manifests in bodily form to restore the cosmic order. This is one of the most consequential theological statements in Hinduism, and it stands in striking contrast to the Christian doctrine of a single, unrepeatable incarnation.

For the theosis question, the implications are profound. If God incarnates repeatedly—not once, but whenever the need arises—then the boundary between divine and human is porous. Incarnation is not a unique emergency measure but a recurring feature of how the divine relates to the world. The later Hindu tradition extended this logic: if God can enter human form, and if the Self of every being is already divine, then the distance between the avatar and the devotee is one of realization, not of nature.

§4.7 · Bhagavad Gita 4.7
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yadā yadā hi dharmasya glānir bhavati bhārata | abhyutthānam adharmasya tadātmānaṃ sṛjāmy aham

English

Whenever there is a decline of dharma (righteousness) and a rise of adharma (unrighteousness), O Bharata, then I manifest myself.

§4.8 · Bhagavad Gita 4.8
Manuscript
Sanskrit

paritrāṇāya sādhūnāṃ vināśāya ca duṣkṛtām | dharmasamsthāpanārthāya sambhavāmi yuge yuge

English

For the protection of the good, for the destruction of the wicked, and for the establishment of dharma, I come into being age after age.

Translator's Notes

  1. §4.7: The phrase tadātmānaṃ sṛjāmy aham (“then I create myself” or “then I manifest myself”) is theologically loaded. The verb sṛj means “to release, to emit, to create.” Krishna does not say he is born (jāyate) in the ordinary sense; he says he creates or projects himself into the world. The incarnation is a voluntary act, not a necessity—a descent of grace, not a fall.
  2. §4.8: The phrase yuge yuge (“age after age”) is the key difference between the Hindu and Christian doctrines of incarnation. In Christianity, the Incarnation is a once-for-all event: God became man in Christ, and this will not be repeated. In Hinduism, divine incarnation is cyclical. Vishnu has incarnated as Rama, as Krishna, as the Buddha (in some lists), and will incarnate again as Kalki at the end of the current age. This cyclical view means that no single incarnation holds a monopoly on divine presence.
