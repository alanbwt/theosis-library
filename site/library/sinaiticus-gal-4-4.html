Galatians 4:4-7: Adoption as Sons

Πρὸς Γαλάτας
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

Galatians 4:4–7 contains the Pauline doctrine of adoption in its most concentrated form. The passage moves from the sending of the Son to the sending of the Spirit, and from the legal metaphor of redemption to the intimate cry of “Abba, Father.” The Greek term huiothesia (υἱοθεσία), rendered “adoption as sons,” was a technical legal term in the Roman world for the formal act by which a person not born into a family was granted full rights of inheritance and status.

For the theosis tradition, these verses are foundational. The logic is compact but explosive: the Son was sent so that we might receive adoption; adoption means sharing in the Son’s own relationship to the Father; and the proof of this shared relationship is the Spirit crying “Abba”—the same address Jesus himself used. If believers share Christ’s filial relationship to God, they share, in some real sense, in his divine sonship. This is not metaphor. It is, for the Fathers, ontological participation.

Greek

English

But when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of a woman, born under the law, in order that he might redeem those under the law, so that we might receive adoption as sons (huiothesia, υἱοθεσία). And because you are sons, God sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, “Abba, Father!” So you are no longer a slave but a son, and if a son, then an heir through God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §4:5: The term huiothesia (υἱοθεσία) is uniquely Pauline in the New Testament, appearing five times in his letters and nowhere else. It is a compound of huios (son) and thesis (placing)—literally, a “placing as son.” In Roman law, adoption conferred full legal status: the adopted son received the father’s name, inheritance rights, and social standing. Paul uses this legal framework to make a theological claim that goes beyond law: believers are placed into the same filial relationship that the Son has with the Father. The distinction between Christ as Son by nature and believers as sons by adoption was essential to later orthodox theology, but Paul’s language here strains against it. The same Spirit, the same cry, the same inheritance.
  2. §4:6: The Aramaic Abba (Αββα) is preserved untranslated in the Greek text, immediately followed by the Greek equivalent ho pater (ὁ πατήρ). This bilingual formula appears also in Romans 8:15 and in Mark 14:36, where Jesus uses it in Gethsemane. The retention of the Aramaic term in Greek-speaking communities suggests it carried special weight—this was understood to be Christ’s own form of address to God. That the Spirit enables believers to use this same address is, for Paul, evidence that they have entered into Christ’s own relationship with the Father.
