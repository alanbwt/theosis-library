Milindapanha: The Chariot Analogy

Nagasena (attributed), c. 2nd c. BCE

Introduction

The Milindapañha (“Questions of King Milinda”) records a dialogue between the Greek king Menander I (Pali: Milinda), who ruled a Hellenistic kingdom in northwest India around 150 BCE, and the Buddhist monk Nāgasena. It is one of the most remarkable documents of cross-cultural philosophical exchange in the ancient world—a Greek king trained in Platonic and Aristotelian logic interrogating a Buddhist monk about the nature of the self. The chariot analogy, presented early in the dialogue, is Nāgasena’s opening move: just as a chariot is not any one of its parts, nor the collection of its parts, nor something apart from its parts, so a “person” is a conventional designation for a collection of aggregates. There is no permanent self.

This is the anti-theosis position stated with maximum clarity. If there is no enduring self, there is nothing to deify. There is no soul to ascend to God, no divine spark to be fanned into flame, no atman to discover its identity with Brahman. The Milindapañha represents early Buddhism’s refusal of the entire framework within which the theosis question is posed. And yet Nāgasena does not say that persons do not exist—he says they exist as conventional designations. The question is whether that is enough.

§The Meeting · Milindapañha, Book 2, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Pali

Atha kho rājā Milindo āyasmantaṃ Nāgasenaṃ etad avoca: Kena bho tvaṃ ñāyasi? Ko nāma tvaṃ bhante? Nāgaseno ahaṃ mahārāja ñāyāmi, Nāgasenoti maṃ sabrahmacarino samājapenti. Api ca kho mahārāja mātāpitaro nāmaṃ karonti Nāgasenoti vā; tathāpi kho mahārāja saṅkhā samannā paññatti voharamattaṃ Nāgasenoti, na hettha puggalo upalabbhati.

English

Then King Milinda said to the Venerable Nāgasena: “How is your reverence known? What is your name, venerable sir?” “I am known as Nāgasena, O King, and it is by that name that my fellow monks address me. But although parents give such a name as Nāgasena, it is nevertheless just a name, a designation, a conceptual term, a conventional usage. There is no permanent individual (puggala) to be found therein.”

§The Chariot Analogy · Milindapañha, Book 2, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Pali

Atha kho āyasmā Nāgaseno rājānaṃ Milindaṃ etad avoca: Katham pana tvaṃ mahārāja idhagato? Pattiyā udha yānena? Rathenahaṃ bhante āgato. Yadi tvaṃ mahārāja rathena āgato, ācikkha me rathaṃ. Kiṃ īsā ratho? Na hidaṃ bhante. Kiṃ akkho ratho? Kiṃ cakkāni ratho? Kiṃ rathapāñjā ratho? Kiṃ yugaddaṇḍaṃ ratho? Na hidaṃ bhante. Kiṃ pana mahārāja ete sabbe ratho? Na hidaṃ bhante. Kiṃ pana mahārāja aññatra etehi ratho? Na hidaṃ bhante. Na khvāhaṃ mahārāja rathaṃ paśyāmi. Saddamattaṃ hi esa mahārāja ratho. Ko pan’ettha ratho? Musā tvaṃ mahārāja bhaṇasi, natthi ratho!

English

Then Nāgasena said to King Milinda: “Your Majesty, how did you come here—on foot or in a vehicle?” “I came in a chariot, venerable sir.” “If you came in a chariot, explain to me what the chariot is. Is the pole the chariot?” “No, venerable sir.” “Is the axle the chariot? Are the wheels the chariot? Is the frame the chariot? Is the yoke the chariot?” “No, venerable sir.” “Is the chariot all of these parts taken together?” “No, venerable sir.” “Is the chariot something apart from these parts?” “No, venerable sir.” “Then, Your Majesty, I can discover no chariot. The chariot is just a sound. What then is the chariot? Your Majesty has spoken a falsehood—there is no chariot!”

§The Conclusion · Milindapañha, Book 2, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Pali

Na khvāhaṃ bhante Nāgasena musā bhaṇāmi. Īsāñ ca paṭicca, akkhañ ca paṭicca, cakkāni ca paṭicca, rathapāñjañ ca paṭicca, yugaddaṇḍañ ca paṭicca, ratho ti saṅkhā samannā paññatti voharamattaṃ nāmamattaṃ. Sādhu mahārāja! Rathapassi tvaṃ. Evam eva kho mahārāja kesādiṃ paṭicca, rūpaṃ paṭicca, vedanāṃ paṭicca, saññaṃ paṭicca, saṅkhāre paṭicca, viññāṇaṃ paṭicca, Nāgasenoti saṅkhā samannā paññatti voharamattaṃ. Paramatthato pan’ettha puggalo na upalabbhati. Yathā hi aṅgasambārā hoti saddo ratho iti, evam khandhesu santesu hoti satto ti sammāti.

English

King Milinda said: “I have not spoken a falsehood, venerable Nāgasena. It is on account of the pole, the axle, the wheels, the frame, and the yoke that ‘chariot’ exists as a designation, a conceptual term, a conventional usage, a name.” “Excellent, Your Majesty! You understand the chariot. In exactly the same way, it is on account of hair, the body, feelings, perceptions, mental formations, and consciousness that ‘Nāgasena’ exists as a designation. But in the ultimate sense, no person is to be found therein. As the nun Vajirā said in the presence of the Blessed One: ‘Just as the word chariot is used when the parts are assembled, so the conventional term being is used when the aggregates are present.’”

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Meeting: The historical Menander I (Milinda) was a real Greek king who ruled in Gandhara and the Punjab region from approximately 165–130 BCE. Greek coins bearing his name survive. Whether the dialogue in the Milindapañha reflects an actual conversation is uncertain, but the text captures something genuine: the encounter between Greek logic, which assumes stable substances with definable essences, and Buddhist analysis, which dissolves every substance into a process. The term puggala (Sanskrit pudgala, “person”) is what Nāgasena denies—not the conventional existence of persons but the existence of a permanent, unchanging self.
  2. §The Chariot Analogy: The chariot analogy is the Buddhist version of the Ship of Theseus, but it goes further. Nāgasena does not merely ask whether a chariot remains the same when its parts are replaced. He asks whether the chariot is any of its parts, all of its parts, or something beyond its parts—and answers no to all three. The chariot is a conventional designation (paññatti) applied to a collection of parts in a particular arrangement. It is “just a sound” (saddamattaṃ). This is not nihilism—Nāgasena does not deny that the king arrived somehow—but it denies that there is a “chariot” existing over and above the parts.
  3. §The Conclusion: The distinction between conventional truth (sammuti-sacca) and ultimate truth (paramattha-sacca) is doing the heavy lifting here. Conventionally, there is a chariot and there is Nāgasena. Ultimately, there are only aggregates in dependence on which the designation arises. This two-truth framework became fundamental to all subsequent Buddhist philosophy. For the theosis question, it means: conventionally, we can speak of persons becoming enlightened or even of beings possessing Buddha-nature. Ultimately, there is no person and no Buddha-nature—only the aggregates arising and passing away. Whether this destroys the theosis question or merely relocates it is one of Buddhism’s most profound internal debates.
