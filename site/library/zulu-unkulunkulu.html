Introduction

Unkulunkulu ("the Old, Old One") is the creator deity in Zulu cosmology. This creation narrative explains the origin of humanity, death, and the relationship between the visible and invisible worlds.

Translation

§The Coming of Unkulunkulu · Zulu Oral Tradition, as recorded by Henry Callaway

Unkulunkulu broke off from the reed bed. He came out of the reed and created all things: people, cattle, water, and fire. He said to the chameleon: "Go and tell the people that they shall not die." The chameleon set out but walked slowly, stopping to eat along the way. Then Unkulunkulu sent the lizard with a different message: "Go and tell the people that they shall die." The lizard ran quickly and arrived first. And so death came into the world, because the chameleon was slow.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Coming of Unkulunkulu: The "perverted message" motif—in which death enters the world through a failed or delayed communication—is found across sub-Saharan Africa in dozens of variants. It presents death not as punishment for sin (as in Genesis) but as the result of accident or delay. This distinction gives African cosmologies a fundamentally different moral framework for understanding mortality.
