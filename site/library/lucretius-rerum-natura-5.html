Introduction

Book V of De Rerum Natura argues that the world was not created by divine design. Lucretius catalogs the imperfections of nature—hostile environments, disease, the helplessness of human infants—as evidence against providential creation.

§V.195–215 · Lucretius, De Rerum Natura V.195–215 (Bailey, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Nequaquam nobis divinitus esse creatam naturam mundi, quae tanta est praedita culpa.

English

If you would learn to understand that the nature of the world was by no means made for us by divine power—so great are the faults with which it stands endowed. First, of all that the sky covers with its vast canopy, greedy mountains and forests of wild beasts possess a portion; rocks hold a part, and waste marshes and the sea which keeps the shores of lands far sundered. Nearly two-thirds of the earth heat and perpetual frost snatch from mortals.

Translator's Notes

  1. §V.195–215: Lucretius’s argument from imperfection is one of the earliest systematic critiques of the design argument (teleology). It directly challenges Stoic providential theology and anticipates modern arguments against intelligent design.
