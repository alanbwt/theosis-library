Sermon 52: The Eye Through Which I See God

Meister Eckhart, c. 1260-1328 AD

Introduction

Sermon 52 (Beati pauperes spiritu, “Blessed are the poor in spirit”) is Meister Eckhart’s most radical work, and the sermon that brought him closest to condemnation. Preached around 1300 AD, it redefines spiritual poverty not as humility or detachment but as the complete annihilation of the self—including the annihilation of the desire for God. True poverty, Eckhart argues, means having no will, no knowledge, and no self at all. In this state of absolute emptiness, the soul returns to the “ground” (Grunt) where God and the soul are one, prior to all distinction.

Eckhart’s language pushes beyond anything the Greek Fathers dared to say. Where Athanasius wrote “God became man so that man might become God,” Eckhart claims that in the deepest ground of reality, the distinction between God and the soul was never real to begin with. “The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me.” Several propositions from this sermon were condemned by Pope John XXII in 1329. Eckhart submitted to the Church’s judgment but died before the condemnation was published.

§Opening · Sermon 52 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Beati pauperes spiritu, quia ipsorum est regnum caelorum. Sæligkeit ûf tet den munt der wîsheit und sprach: sælic sint die armen des geistes, wan daz hîmelrîche ist ir.

English

“Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.” Blessedness opened its mouth of wisdom and spoke: “Blessed are the poor in spirit, for the kingdom of heaven is theirs.” All the angels and all the saints and everything that was ever born must keep silence here, for they must attend to the speech of the only-begotten Son. I say that blessedness consists in the very thing that the poorest and most destitute person can have.

§On the Will · Sermon 52 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Vrâget mich ieman, waz daz sî ein arm mensche, der niht enhât, sô antwurte ich und spriche alsô:

English

If someone asks me what a poor person is who has nothing, I answer thus: as long as a person has his own will, and considers it his will to fulfil the all-lovely will of God, that person does not have the poverty of which I speak. For that person still has a will with which he wills to satisfy the will of God, and that is not true poverty. For true poverty demands this: that a person be so free of all will and of all God’s commands that he would be as free of every divine will as he was when he did not yet exist.

§On Knowledge · Sermon 52 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Ander stünde, der ist ein arm mensche, der niht enweiz.

English

In the second place, a poor person is one who knows nothing. We have sometimes said that a person should live as if he did not live, neither for himself, nor for truth, nor even for God. But now I say further: a person must be so free and so emptied that he does not even know or experience that God is at work in him. Only then does he possess poverty.

§On Having Nothing · Sermon 52 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Daz dritte ist daz ergest, daz der mensche niht enhât.

English

The third poverty, and the most extreme, is that a person has nothing. Many people have said that perfection consists in having nothing of earthly goods, and this is true in one sense. But I do not mean it so. I have said before: a poor person is not one who wills to fulfil God’s will, but one who lives in such a way that he is free of his own will and of God’s will, as he was when he was not yet. Of this poverty the highest is that a person so stands empty and free that he does not have even a place in which God might work. For as long as he has that, he still has distinction.

§The Ground of God and Soul · Sermon 52 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Dô ich stuont in mîner êrsten sache, dô enhete ich keinen got, und dô was ich sache mîn selbes.

English

When I stood in my first cause, I had no God, and I was my own cause. I willed nothing, I desired nothing, for I was free being and the enjoyment of myself in the likeness of God. Then I willed myself, and I willed all things. And that is what God is. But when I return to God and to the ground where God himself rests in his own nature, that breaking-through is greater than the emanation. For in this breaking-through I receive what God and I have in common. There I am what I was, and there I neither increase nor decrease, for there I am the immovable that moves all things.

§The Eye of God · Sermon 52 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Daz ouge, dâ inne ich got sihe, daz ist daz selbe ouge, dâ inne mich got sihet: mîn ouge und gotes ouge daz ist éin ouge und éin gesiht und éin bekennen und éin minne.

English

The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me: my eye and God’s eye are one eye, one seeing, one knowing, one love.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Opening: Eckhart’s sermons were preached in Middle High German to lay audiences, then recorded and copied by his listeners. The Latin headings are Eckhart’s own; the German text may reflect some scribal variation. The Pfeiffer edition, while not a critical text by modern standards, remains the most widely referenced.
  2. §The Ground of God and Soul: The claim “when I stood in my first cause, I had no God” was among the propositions condemned in the papal bull In agro dominico (1329). Eckhart’s point is not atheism but a metaphysical claim: prior to creation and distinction, there is a “ground” (Grunt) in which God and the soul are indistinguishable. This is not pantheism (everything is God) but what scholars call “panentheism” or “mystical identity”—the claim that at the deepest level of reality, Creator and creature share a common ground that precedes the distinction between them.
  3. §The Eye of God: This is perhaps the most famous sentence in Western mystical literature. It collapses the subject-object distinction between the human soul and God. In the Dominican tradition from which Eckhart writes, this is understood not as a claim about human nature in general but about the state of the soul that has achieved perfect detachment (Abgeschiedenheit)—which, Eckhart insists, is a gift of grace, not an achievement of effort.
