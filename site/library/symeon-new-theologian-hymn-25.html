Hymn 25: Members of Christ

Symeon the New Theologian, 949–1022 AD

Introduction

Symeon the New Theologian (949–1022) is the last of the three saints honored with the title “Theologian” in the Orthodox tradition, after John the Evangelist and Gregory of Nazianzus. A Byzantine monk and mystic, Symeon wrote with an autobiographical intensity unprecedented in Eastern Christian literature. His Hymns of Divine Love are ecstatic poems describing direct encounters with the uncreated light of God—experiences that Symeon insisted were available to every baptized Christian, not only to monastics or saints of the past.

Hymn 25 contains one of Symeon’s most daring formulations of the mutual indwelling between Christ and the believer. Drawing on Pauline language about the body of Christ, Symeon pushes beyond metaphor into ontological claim: the union between Christ and the Christian is so complete that the boundaries between the two become, in some real sense, permeable. We become members of Christ, and Christ becomes our members.

Greek

Μέλη Χριστοῦ γινόμεθα, καὶ μέλη γίνεται ημῶν ὁ Χριστός· Χριστὸς γίνεται χείρ μου, Χριστὸς γίνεται πούς μου.

English

We become members of Christ—and Christ becomes our members. Christ becomes my hand, Christ becomes my foot, and I, wretched one, am the hand and foot of Christ. I move my hand, and my hand is wholly Christ, for God’s divinity is united to me inseparably.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Hymn 25: Symeon’s language about becoming Christ’s members draws on 1 Corinthians 6:15 and 12:27, but his application is far more radical than Paul’s ecclesiological usage. Where Paul speaks of the church as the body of Christ, Symeon speaks of the individual believer’s body as literally becoming Christ. The phrase “my hand is wholly Christ” pushes theosis language to its extreme: not merely moral transformation or spiritual elevation but a real, physical participation in the divine. Symeon’s contemporary critics found this language dangerously close to pantheism; his defenders argued that it was the natural consequence of the Incarnation taken seriously.
