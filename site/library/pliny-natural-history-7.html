§7.1–2 · Pliny, Natural History 7.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

Mundum et terras, gentesque in iis memorabiles indicavimus. Nunc reliqua animalia et primum hominem, cuius causa videtur cuncta alia genuisse natura

English

The world and the lands that form its principal ornament we have now described. Now let us treat of the animals, and first of man, for whose sake great Nature seems to have created all other things—though at a harsh price, so that it is difficult to judge whether she has been more a kind parent or a cruel stepmother.

Translator's Notes

  1. §7.1: Pliny’s question whether Nature is parens melior an tristior noverca (a kind parent or a cruel stepmother) encapsulates the Roman ambivalence about the human condition. Unlike Stoic optimism, Pliny sees humanity’s biological weakness—naked, helpless at birth—as evidence of nature’s indifference.
