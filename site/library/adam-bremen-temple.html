Introduction

Adam of Bremen’s description of the temple at Uppsala (c. 1075 CE) in his Gesta Hammaburgensis is the most detailed account of a functioning Norse pagan sanctuary. He describes a great temple containing images of Thor, Odin, and Freyr.

Greek original and English translation.
§Gesta IV.26–27 · Adam of Bremen, Gesta Hammaburgensis IV.26–27
Manuscript
Greek

In hoc templo, quod totum ex auro paratum est, statuas trium deorum veneratur populus, ita ut potentissimus eorum Thor in medio solium habeat triclinio; Wodan et Fricco hinc et inde locum possident.

English

In this temple, entirely decked out in gold, the people worship the statues of three gods: the mightiest of them, Thor, occupies a throne in the middle of the chamber; Wodan and Fricco have places on either side. Thor, they say, presides over the air and governs thunder and lightning, winds and rains.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Gesta IV.26–27: Adam’s account places Thor—not Odin—in the central position, reflecting the reality that Thor was the most widely worshipped deity among ordinary Scandinavians. The golden temple has never been archaeologically confirmed, but recent excavations at Gamla Uppsala have found ritual structures consistent with his account.
