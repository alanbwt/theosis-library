Instructions of Shuruppak: The Oldest Wisdom Text

Šuruppak
Anonymous (Sumerian), c. 2600 BCE

Introduction

The Instructions of Shuruppak is the oldest known wisdom text in world literature, dating to roughly 2600 BCE in its earliest form, with copies surviving from the Abu Salabikh tablets and later Old Babylonian recensions. It takes the form of a father’s advice to his son: Shuruppak, son of Ubar-Tutu, speaks to his son Ziusudra—the same Ziusudra who would later survive the great flood in Sumerian tradition.

The precepts range from the practical to the profound. Some are proverbs about daily life; others touch on justice, speech, and the proper relationship between humans and gods. The text is remarkable for how recognizable its moral concerns remain across nearly five millennia.

English

In those days, in those far-off days, in those nights, in those far-off nights, in those years, in those far-off years—in those ancient days, when the things that were needful had been brought into being, in those ancient days, when the things that were needful had been given their proper nourishment—when bread had been baked in the shrines of the land, and bread had been tasted in the houses of the land—Shuruppak gave instructions to his son. Shuruppak, the son of Ubar-Tutu, gave instructions to his son Ziusudra:

“My son, I would give you instruction. Take my instruction.”

English

“Do not buy a donkey that brays. It will split your very yoke.

Do not locate a field on a road. Do not place your house near a public square.

Do not vouch for someone. That man will have a hold on you.

Do not curse with powerful means. The curse will recoil upon you.

Do not speak arrogantly to your mother. That causes hatred for you.

Do not question the words of your mother and your personal god.

The elder brother is indeed like a father. The elder sister is indeed like a mother. Listen to your elder brother. Listen to your elder sister.

Do not pass judgment when you drink beer.

You should not steal anything. You should not kill yourself with a knife.

Do not laugh with or sit alone in a room with a girl who is married.”

§Closing · Instructions of Shuruppak, closing lines
Manuscript
Sumerian

gi₆ gíd-da šà-bi zu-àm

dumu-ğu₁₀ na de₅-ga-àm na ḫé-àm Ziú-sud-rà na de₅-ga-àm na ḫé-àm inim um-ma-ke₄ nig̲ kú-zu-àm Utu-ùr ḫé-em-tà-a-è

na-rí Šuruppak dumu Ubar-Tutu-ka-kam

English

“The night is long. Think upon it.

My son, you should heed my instructions. Ziusudra, you should heed my instructions. The instructions of an old man are precious. May Utu let you attain them.”

These are the instructions of Shuruppak, the son of Ubar-Tutu.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Opening Address: The elaborate temporal preamble (“in those days, in those far-off days”) is a standard opening formula for Sumerian literary compositions. It situates the text in a mythic past when the fundamental structures of civilization were being established. The mention of bread baked in shrines connects wisdom with the basic acts of sustaining life.
  2. §Key Precepts: The precepts mix what seems like folk wisdom (“do not buy a braying donkey”) with genuine moral instruction (“do not steal,” “do not speak arrogantly to your mother”). The prohibition against passing judgment while drinking beer is notable: beer was the staple drink of Sumerian civilization, and the tavern was a central social institution. The warning against vouching for others reappears in the biblical book of Proverbs nearly two millennia later.
  3. §Closing: The closing invocation of Utu (the sun god and god of justice) connects the father’s practical advice to the cosmic order. Wisdom is not merely useful; it is aligned with divine justice. The identification of Ziusudra as the son of Shuruppak links this wisdom text to the Sumerian flood tradition: the man who will be chosen to survive the destruction of humanity is the man who received his father’s wisdom.
