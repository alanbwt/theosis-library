Masnavi: I Died as a Mineral and Became a Plant

Jalal ad-Din Rumi, 1207-1273 AD

Introduction

The Masnavi-ye Ma’navi (Spiritual Couplets) of Jalal al-Din Rumi is the longest mystical poem in the Persian language—over 25,000 verses across six books. It opens with the Song of the Reed, a lament for separation from the divine source that became the most famous passage in all of Sufi literature. The reed flute, cut from the reed bed, cries out for the homeland it has lost. Every human being, Rumi says, is that reed: torn from God, longing to return.

Book III contains an equally remarkable passage that anticipates evolutionary thought by six centuries. Rumi traces consciousness through mineral, plant, animal, and human stages—each death a promotion to a higher form of being. But the progression does not stop at the human. Beyond humanity lies the angelic, and beyond the angelic lies the return to God. This is theosis described not as a single event but as a cosmic process: the universe itself is evolving toward divinity.

§The Song of the Reed (Book I, Opening) · Masnavi-ye Ma’navi, Daftar I, lines 1–18
Manuscript
Persian

Beshnow az ney chon hekayat mikonad, az jodayiha shekayat mikonad. K-az neyestan ta mara bebrideh-and, dar nafiram mard-o zan nalideh-and.

English

Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale, complaining of separations—saying, “Ever since I was parted from the reed-bed, my lament has caused man and woman to moan. I want a bosom torn by severance, that I may unfold the pain of love-desire. Everyone who is left far from their source wishes back the time when they were united with it. In every company I uttered my wailing note; I consorted with the unhappy and with them that rejoice. Every one became my friend from their own opinion; none sought out my secrets from within me.”

§The Evolution of the Soul (Book III) · Masnavi-ye Ma’navi, Daftar III, lines 3901–3906
Manuscript
Persian

Az jamadi mordam-o nami shodam, az nami mordam ze hayvani zadam. Mordam az heyvani-o adam shodam, pas che tarsam key ze mordan kam shodam?

English

I died as a mineral and became a plant, I died as a plant and rose to animal, I died as an animal and I was a human. What should I fear? When have I become less by dying? Yet once more I shall die as a human, to soar with angels blessed above. And even from angelhood I must pass on: all except God perishes. When I have sacrificed my angel-soul, I shall become what no mind has ever conceived. Oh, let me not exist! For non-existence proclaims in organ tones: to Him we shall return.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Song of the Reed: The ney (reed flute) is Rumi’s central metaphor for the human soul. The reed is hollow—it produces music only because it is empty, because something has been removed. Rumi’s point is that human longing for God is itself the evidence of divine origin: you would not ache for a home you had never known. This parallels the Neoplatonic doctrine of emanation and return found in Plotinus, and the Gnostic myth of the divine spark trapped in matter, longing for the Pleroma.
  2. §The Evolution of the Soul: This passage is often cited as an anticipation of Darwinian evolution, but Rumi’s vision is metaphysical, not biological. Each “death” is a transcendence of a lower mode of being. The progression—mineral, plant, animal, human, angel, God—maps onto the Neoplatonic chain of being and the Hindu concept of transmigration through increasingly subtle bodies. The final line, “to Him we shall return” (inna ilayhi raji’un), quotes Quran 2:156 and frames the entire evolutionary arc as a return journey to the divine source. Rumi’s insight is that annihilation (fana) is not loss but the ultimate promotion.
