Introduction

The first chapter of the Zhuangzi, "Free and Easy Wandering" (Xiaoyao You), opens with the image of a colossal fish that transforms into a bird spanning thousands of miles. It challenges conventional notions of scale, perspective, and freedom.

§Chapter 1: Free and Easy Wandering · Zhuangzi, Inner Chapters, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Greek

北冥有鱼，其名为鲲

English

In the northern ocean there is a fish called Kun. The Kun is so enormous that no one knows how many thousands of li it measures. It transforms into a bird called Peng. The back of the Peng measures thousands of li across. When it rises in flight, its wings are like clouds filling the sky. This bird, when the seas begin to move, journeys to the southern ocean—the Pool of Heaven. A cicada and a small dove laugh at it, saying: "When we fly up with all our might, we sometimes cannot even reach the elm tree. What is the use of going ninety thousand li to the south?"

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: The cicada and dove represent the narrow perspective that judges everything by its own limited experience. Zhuangzi's point is not that the Peng is "better" than the cicada but that each creature is bound by its own nature—and that true freedom (xiaoyao) requires transcending all such relative standpoints entirely.
