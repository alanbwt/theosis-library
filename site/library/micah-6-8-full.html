Introduction

Micah 6:1–8 presents God’s covenant lawsuit against Israel, culminating in the famous summary of prophetic religion: to do justice, love mercy, and walk humbly with God. It distills the prophetic tradition to its ethical essence.

§6:6–8 · Septuagint, Micah 6:6–8
Manuscript
Greek

ἐν τίνι καταλάβω τὸν Κύριον ἀντιλήμψομαι θεοῦ μου ὑψίστου

English

With what shall I come before the Lord, and bow myself before God on high? Shall I come before him with burnt offerings, with calves a year old? Will the Lord be pleased with thousands of rams? He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?

Translator's Notes

  1. §6:8: This verse is often called the “summary of the prophets.” The three requirements—justice (κρίμα), mercy (ἔλεος), and humble walk—correspond to the teachings of Amos, Hosea, and Isaiah respectively, compressing the entire prophetic tradition into a single sentence.
