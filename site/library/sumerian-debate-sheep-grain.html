Introduction

The Debate between Sheep and Grain is a Sumerian literary composition in which two divine beings argue their respective superiority. It represents one of the oldest literary genres: the disputational dialogue exploring the relative value of pastoral and agricultural life.

§Opening · Sumerian Debate between Sheep and Grain
Manuscript
Greek

[Sumerian cuneiform, multiple tablets from Nippur]

English

On the mountain of heaven and earth, when Anu had caused the gods to be born, Grain had not yet been born, nor had the loom been fashioned. Sheep had not yet appeared, nor were kids numerous. Grain said: ‘I am superior to you! The bread I provide sustains the life of the land.’ Sheep replied: ‘Without my wool, there would be no cloth for king or commoner.’

Translator's Notes

  1. §Opening: The genre of the Sumerian debate poem—where two elements of civilization argue their relative value—is among the oldest literary forms (c. 2000 BCE). Similar debates exist between copper and silver, summer and winter, and hoe and plow. They reflect a culture consciously analyzing the foundations of its own prosperity.
