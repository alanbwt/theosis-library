Didache 1-6: The Two Ways

Anonymous (Didache), c. 50-120 AD

Introduction

The Didache (“Teaching”), also known as The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, is the earliest surviving Christian document outside the New Testament, likely composed between 50 and 120 AD. Its opening section (chapters 1–6) presents the “Two Ways” doctrine: there is a Way of Life and a Way of Death, and every person must choose between them. The ethical instruction is practical, concrete, and severe. It draws on Jewish moral teaching (the Two Ways pattern appears also in the Dead Sea Scrolls) and on the words of Jesus, though it cites them in forms that do not always match the canonical Gospels.

The Didache matters for the theosis tradition because it shows what the earliest Christians actually taught converts. Before the philosophical theology of the Fathers, before the creeds and councils, there was this: a stark moral catechism rooted in the conviction that the path to God runs through the transformation of daily conduct. The Way of Life is not a doctrine to be believed but a pattern to be lived. In this respect the Didache is closer to the Desert Fathers than to the Scholastics.

§1.1–2 · Didache 1.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

Ὀδοὶ δύο εἰσίν, μία της ζωῆς καὶ μία τοῦ θανάτου, διαφορὰ δὲ πολλὴ μεταξὺ τῶν δύο ὁδῶν.

English

There are two ways, one of life and one of death, and there is a great difference between the two ways. The way of life is this: first, you shall love God who made you; second, your neighbour as yourself; and whatever you do not wish to happen to you, do not do to another.

§1.3–4 · Didache 1.3–4
Manuscript
Greek

τούτων δὲ τῶν λόγων ἡ διδαχή ἐστιν αὕτη · εὐλογεῖτε τοὺς καταρωμένους ὑμῖν

English

Now the teaching of these words is this: bless those who curse you, and pray for your enemies, and fast for those who persecute you. For what credit is it if you love those who love you? Do not even the gentiles do the same? But you shall love those who hate you, and you shall not have an enemy.

§2.1–3 · Didache 2.1–3
Manuscript
Greek

Δευτέρα δὲ ἐντολὴ της διδαχης · οὐ φονεύσεις

English

The second commandment of the teaching: you shall not murder; you shall not commit adultery; you shall not corrupt children; you shall not be sexually immoral; you shall not steal; you shall not practise magic; you shall not practise sorcery; you shall not murder a child by abortion, nor kill one that is born.

§3.1–6 · Didache 3.1–6
Manuscript
Greek

τέκνον μου, φεῦγε ἀπὸ παντὸς πονηροῦ καὶ ἀπὸ παντὸς ὁμοίου αὐτοῦ

English

My child, flee from every evil and from everything like it. Do not be prone to anger, for anger leads to murder. Do not be jealous or quarrelsome or hot-tempered, for from all these things murders are born. My child, do not be lustful, for lust leads to sexual immorality. Do not use foul language or be an ogler, for from all these things adulteries are born.

§4.1–4 · Didache 4.1–4
Manuscript
Greek

τέκνον μου, τοῦ λαλοῦντός σοι τὸν λόγον τοῦ θεοῦ μνησθήσῃ νυκτὸς καὶ ἡμέρας

English

My child, remember night and day the one who speaks the word of God to you, and honour him as the Lord. For wherever the lordly rule is spoken of, there the Lord is. Seek out daily the faces of the saints, that you may rest in their words. Do not cause division, but rather make peace between those who quarrel.

§5.1–2 · Didache 5.1–2
Manuscript

Ἡ δὲ τοῦ θανάτου ὁδός ἐστιν αὕτη · πρῶτον πάντων πονηρά ἐστι καὶ κατάρας μεστή

English

But the way of death is this: first of all, it is evil and full of cursing—murders, adulteries, lusts, sexual immoralities, thefts, idolatries, magic arts, sorceries, robberies, false testimonies, hypocrisies, duplicity, deceit, arrogance, malice, stubbornness, greed, foul speech, jealousy, audacity, pride, boasting.

§6.1–2 · Didache 6.1–2
Manuscript

Ὅρα μή τις σε πλανήσῃ ἀπὸ ταύτης της ὁδοῦ της διδαχης

English

See that no one leads you astray from this way of the teaching, since such a person teaches you apart from God. For if you are able to bear the entire yoke of the Lord, you will be perfect; but if you are not able, do what you can.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1–2: The Two Ways pattern has deep roots in Jewish moral instruction. It appears in Deuteronomy 30:19 (“I have set before you life and death, blessing and cursing”), in the Community Rule of Qumran (the Two Spirits doctrine), and in the Epistle of Barnabas (chapters 18–20), which shares extensive material with the Didache. Whether the Didache borrowed from Barnabas, or both drew on a common Jewish source document, is unresolved.
  2. §6.1–2: The concession “if you are not able, do what you can” is striking in its pastoral realism. The Didache does not demand perfection as a prerequisite for belonging to the community; it acknowledges human limitation while still holding up the “entire yoke of the Lord” as the ideal. This tension between the absolute demand and the pastoral accommodation will characterize the entire subsequent history of Christian ethics.
