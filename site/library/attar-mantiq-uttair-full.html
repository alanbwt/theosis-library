Introduction

Farid ud-Din Attar’s Conference of the Birds (c. 1177 CE) is a Sufi allegorical poem in which thirty birds journey through seven valleys to seek the Simorgh, the divine king of birds. When they arrive, they discover that si morgh means “thirty birds”—the divine was themselves all along.

§The Valley of Unity · Attar, Conference of the Birds

وادی توحید

English

When the traveler arrives at the Valley of Unity, he is stripped of all desire and will. In this valley, the many become one, and all differences disappear. If you see many, you see falsely; if you see one, you see the truth. One and many are the same in this valley; past and future are erased. The lover and the beloved are a single flame.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Valley of Unity: The pun on Simorgh / si morgh (“thirty birds”) encapsulates the Sufi doctrine of fana (annihilation of the self in God). The seekers discover that they themselves, purified through the journey, are the divine they sought. This parallels the Upanishadic tat tvam asi (“thou art that”) and represents the most radical expression of mystical union in Islamic literature.
