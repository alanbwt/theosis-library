Introduction

The brief and enigmatic chapter De Anima III.5 introduces the “active intellect” (nous poietikos)—a mind that is separable, impassive, and immortal. This passage generated more commentary than perhaps any other in Aristotle.

Greek original and English translation.
§430a10–25 · Aristotle, De Anima 430a10–25 (Ross, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἔστιν ὁ μὲν τοιοῦτος νοῦς τῷ πάντα γίνεσθαι, ὁ δὲ τῷ πάντα ποιεῖν

English

Since in every class of things, as in nature as a whole, there is something which is the matter and something which is the cause and agent, these distinct elements must likewise be found within the soul. And in fact mind as described is what it is by virtue of becoming all things, while there is another which is what it is by virtue of making all things. This is a sort of positive state like light. And this mind is separable, impassive, and unmixed, being in its essential nature activity.

Translator's Notes

  1. §430a10–25: Whether the active intellect is individual to each person or a single cosmic mind shared by all humans was debated for two millennia. Alexander of Aphrodisias identified it with God; Averroes held it was a single intellect for all humanity; Thomas Aquinas argued each person has their own.
