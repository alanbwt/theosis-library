Wisdom 2:23-24: Created for Incorruption

Solomon (attributed), traditional

Introduction

Wisdom of Solomon 2:23–24 states with startling directness the anthropological premise of all theosis theology: “God created man for incorruption, and made him in the image of his own eternity.” The Greek term aphtharsian (ἀφθαρσίαν), “incorruption,” is not merely the absence of decay but a positive quality—participation in God’s own imperishable nature. Humanity was made not simply to live long but to share in the deathless mode of divine existence.

The passage then identifies the source of death: “through the envy of the devil, death entered the world.” Death is an intruder, not part of the original design. This claim became foundational for the Greek patristic understanding of salvation as the restoration of the original divine intention. If humanity was created for incorruption and lost it through the devil’s envy, then salvation is not a legal transaction but a healing—a return to what God always intended human beings to be.

For God created man for incorruption (aphtharsia, ἀφθαρσία), and made him in the image of his own eternity (aidiotētos, ἀϊδιότητος).

§2:24 · Septuagint (LXX)
Manuscript
Greek

φθόνῳ δὲ διαβόλου θάνατος εἰσῆλθεν εἰς τὸν κόσμον · πειράζουσιν δὲ αὐτὸν οἱ τῆς ἐκείνου μερίδος ὄντες ·

English

But through the envy of the devil, death entered the world, and those who belong to his portion experience it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:23: The phrase “image of his own eternity” (εἰκόνα τῆς ἰδίας ἀϊδιότητος) goes beyond Genesis 1:26–27, which says humanity was made in God’s “image and likeness” without specifying the content of that image. The Wisdom author identifies the image specifically with God’s eternity (aidiotēs)—his everlastingness. This became a key text for Athanasius and the Cappadocians, who argued that humanity’s destiny is participation in the eternal, incorruptible life of God.
  2. §2:24: The identification of the serpent of Genesis 3 with “the devil” (διάβολος) is first made explicit here in the Wisdom of Solomon. The Hebrew text of Genesis never identifies the serpent as Satan. This interpretive move, which became standard in both Jewish and Christian tradition, transforms the Fall from a story about human disobedience into a cosmic drama involving supernatural malice directed against God’s plan for human divinization.
