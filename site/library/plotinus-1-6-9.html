Enneads I.6.9: Cut Away Everything

Plotinus, 204-270 AD

Introduction

Enneads I.6 is Plotinus’s treatise On Beauty, and its final section (I.6.9) is one of the most celebrated passages in the history of philosophy. The treatise begins with a question—what makes things beautiful?—and ends with a mystical imperative: strip away everything until only the inner light remains. Plotinus argues that physical beauty is merely a trace of intelligible Beauty, which is itself a trace of the Good (the One). To see true Beauty, the soul must turn inward, sculpting itself as a sculptor works marble, cutting away everything that is not the statue.

The passage stands at the intersection of aesthetics and soteriology. Beauty is not decoration but a path of ascent. The soul that recognizes beauty in the sensible world is awakened to the beauty of virtue, then to the beauty of Mind (Nous), and finally to the One itself. The command “cut away everything” is both an aesthetic and an ascetic imperative—the return to the divine requires the removal of all that is extraneous, all that the soul has accumulated in its descent into matter.

§I.6.9 [1–5] · Plotinus, Enneads I.6.9
Manuscript
Greek

τί οὖν χρὴ ποιεῖν; τί μηχανᾶσθαι; πῶς ἄν τις θεάσαιτο κάλλος ἀμήχανον

English

What then must we do? What device can we employ? How can one see the “inconceivable beauty” which stays within the holy sanctuary and does not come out where the profane may see it? Let whoever can, follow and come within, and leave outside the sight of his eyes and not turn back to the former splendours of bodies.

§I.6.9 [5–15] · Plotinus, Enneads I.6.9
Manuscript
Greek

ὃς γὰρ ἂν διώκῃ τὰ σωμάτων καλά... καταδὺς εἰς τὸν βυθὸν ἀφανὴς γένηται

English

For when one sees the beauties of body, one must not run toward them but know that they are images, traces, shadows—and flee toward that of which they are likenesses. For if someone should pursue them, wishing to grasp them as real, he will be like the one who wished to grasp the beautiful image floating on the water—as a certain myth, I think, signified—and sinking into the deep current, disappeared. In the same way, the one who clings to beautiful bodies and does not let go will sink, not in body but in soul, into the dark depths, hateful to the intellect, where, blind, he will dwell in Hades, consorting with shadows there too.

§I.6.9 [15–25] · Plotinus, Enneads I.6.9
Manuscript
Greek

φεύγωμεν δὴ φίλην ἐς πατρίδα... πατρὶς δὲ ἡμῖν ὅθεν ἤλθομεν καὶ πατὴρ ἐκεῖ.

English

“Let us flee then to our dear fatherland”—this is the truest counsel. But what is this flight? And how do we escape? We shall set sail, as Odysseus did from Circe or Calypso—as the poet says, I think, with a hidden meaning—and was not content to stay though he had delights of the eyes and much sensible beauty to enjoy. Our fatherland is where we came from, and our Father is there.

§I.6.9 [25–40] · Plotinus, Enneads I.6.9
Manuscript
Greek

τίς οὖν ἡ φυγή; οὐ ποσὶν... περιελεῖν πάντα δεῖ

English

What then is our means of flight? Not by feet—for feet carry us always from land to land. Nor must you prepare a chariot drawn by horses or a ship. You must cut away all this and not look, but close your eyes and exchange this way of seeing for another, and awaken a faculty that everyone possesses but few use. And what does this inner sight see? It cannot, when just awakened, look at the brilliance before it. The soul must be trained first to look at beautiful practices, then beautiful works—not those which the arts produce, but those of men who are called good. Then look at the soul of those who produce the beautiful works.

§I.6.9 [40–55] · Plotinus, Enneads I.6.9
Manuscript
Greek

ἄναγε ἐπὶ σαυτὸν καὶ ἴδε· κἂν μήπω σεαυτὸν ἴδῃς καλόν, οἷα ἀγαλματοποιός...

English

Go back into yourself and look. If you do not yet see yourself as beautiful, then, just as the sculptor of a statue that must be beautiful cuts away here and polishes there, makes this line lighter and that one purer until he has given the statue a beautiful face—so you too must cut away what is excessive, straighten what is crooked, purify what is dark to make it bright, and never stop “working on your statue” until the divine splendour of virtue shines in you, until you see “self-mastery enthroned upon its holy seat.”

§I.6.9 [55–65] · Plotinus, Enneads I.6.9
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ τοίνυν γέγονας τοῦτο... θαρρήσας σεαυτῷ. ἤδη ἀναβέβηκας.

English

If you have become this, and seen it, and been with yourself in purity, with nothing hindering you from becoming one in this way, with nothing else mixed in with yourself, but being entirely yourself alone, true light—not measured by size, not bounded by shape into smallness, not expanded to size by unboundedness, but everywhere unmeasured, because greater than all measure and more than all quantity—if you see that you have become this, then you have become sight itself. Trust yourself then. You have already ascended and need no one to show you. Concentrate your gaze and see. This alone is the eye that sees the great beauty.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.6.9 [5–15]: The “certain myth” Plotinus alludes to is the story of Narcissus, who drowned pursuing his own reflection in the water. Plotinus inverts the myth: the danger is not self-love but love of material beauty. The soul that pursues physical beauty as though it were real is Narcissus reaching into the water—it will drown not in water but in matter. This is Plotinus’s answer to the Hermetic problem of the Fall: the soul descends not through sin or ignorance but through love misdirected toward images rather than their source.
  2. §I.6.9 [15–25]: The Homeric allusion is to Odyssey V.203–224, where Odysseus refuses Calypso’s offer of immortality and chooses to return home. For Plotinus, this is an allegory of the soul’s situation: it is offered the pleasures of the sensible world (Calypso’s island) but its true home is elsewhere. The “fatherland” is the intelligible world, and the “Father” is the One. The flight is not spatial but a turning of attention.
  3. §I.6.9 [40–55]: The sculptor metaphor is perhaps the most famous image in Plotinus and one of the most influential in the history of mysticism. The soul does not add divinity to itself; it removes everything that is not divine. This is a via negativa of the self: just as the sculptor reveals the statue already present in the marble by removing excess stone, the soul reveals its innate divinity by stripping away accumulated material. The metaphor assumes that beauty—and divinity—are already present within; they need only to be uncovered.
