Divine Names 2.11: Theosis as Union with God

Περὶ Θείων Ὀνομάτων
Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, c. 500 AD

Introduction

The Divine Names is one of four treatises attributed to Dionysius the Areopagite, the Athenian convert mentioned in Acts 17:34. The actual author wrote around 500 CE and drew heavily on the Neoplatonist Proclus. Despite the pseudonymity, the corpus exerted enormous influence on both Eastern and Western theology — from Maximus the Confessor to Thomas Aquinas.

In chapter 2, section 11, Pseudo-Dionysius provides one of the most concise definitions of theosis in the entire tradition. It is the attaining of likeness to God and union with God, so far as is possible. The qualification matters: theosis has a limit set by the infinite distance between Creator and creature, even as it bridges that distance through grace.

§2.11 · De Divinis Nominibus 2.11 (Suchla ed., PTS 33; PG 3.649C–652A)
Manuscript
Greek

Θέωσις εστιν η προς Θεον ως εφικτον αφομοίωσίς τε και ένωσις. Κοινός δε πάσης ιεραρχίας σκοπός η προς Θεόν τε και τα θεια συνεχής αγάπη, ενθέως τε και ενιαίως ιερουργουμένη.

English

Theosis is the attaining of likeness to God and union with God, so far as is possible. This is the common goal of every hierarchy: the continuous love of God and of things divine, a love that is sacredly worked out in a divine and unified manner; and before this, the complete and irrevocable turning away from what is contrary, the knowledge of beings as they truly are, the vision and understanding of sacred truth, the inspired participation in the uniform perfection and in the One himself, so far as may be, the feast of contemplation which nourishes the intellect and deifies everyone who is raised up to it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2.11: The opening definition — Θέωσις εστιν η προς Θεον ως εφικτον αφομοίωσίς τε και ένωσις — echoes Plato’s Theaetetus 176b (“becoming like God so far as is possible”), a passage that shaped the entire Greek philosophical tradition of homoiosis theo. But Pseudo-Dionysius adds ένωσις (“union”), which goes beyond Platonic assimilation into a properly Christian and mystical category. The phrase ως εφικτον (“so far as is possible”) preserves the apophatic reserve that characterizes the entire Dionysian corpus: God always exceeds what the creature can attain.
