On the Making of Man 16: The Mirror of the Divine

Περὶ Κατασκευῆς Ἀνθρώπου
Gregory of Nyssa, c. 335–395 AD

Introduction

Gregory of Nyssa’s On the Making of Man (De Hominis Opificio), composed around 379 CE, is a treatise on theological anthropology — what it means for humanity to be created in the image of God. It was written as a supplement to his brother Basil’s Hexaemeron (On the Six Days of Creation), which had treated the natural world but left the creation of humanity unfinished.

Chapter 16 contains Gregory’s central insight: the human soul is a mirror. It does not possess divinity in itself, but when it is turned toward God, it reflects the divine nature and thereby participates in it. This mirror metaphor becomes one of the most important images in the Eastern Christian tradition of theosis. The soul is not God, but it can become God-like by reflecting what it contemplates.

§16 · De Hominis Opificio 16 (Forbes ed.; PG 44.184A–B)
Manuscript
Greek

Καθαρότης, απάθεια, κακου παντός αλλοτρίωσις η θεότης εστίν. Ει ουν ταυτα εν σοί, Θεός εν σοι πάντως εστίν. Ει ακήρατος κακου παντος η διάνοια, πάθους ελευθέρα, παντός μολυσμου καθαρεύουσα, μακάριος ει της οξυδερκίας· ότι το τοις κεκαθαρμένοις αθέατον, οιον εν κατόπτρω και εικόνι, της θείας φύσεως την εικόνα εν τη ιδία καθαρότητι εποπτεύων.

English

The Godhead is purity, freedom from passion, and separation from all evil. If therefore these things are in you, God is indeed in you. If your mind is untouched by any evil, free from passion, purified from all stain, then you are blessed because of your clear-sightedness. For by looking at your own purity, you perceive the image of the divine nature as in a mirror and a reflection. And if we may use a somewhat bold expression, it is the nature of your mind that is the image of the beauty above. Just as in the case of mirrors, what appears in the material substance takes its form from the face that presents itself, so the nature that was formed after the divine is made like it, and if it looks upon the Good it is stamped with the image of the Good, while if it turns to the evil it takes upon itself the form of that which is contrary to the Good.

Translator's Notes

  1. §16: The mirror analogy (κάτοπτρον) echoes Paul’s language in 1 Corinthians 13:12 and 2 Corinthians 3:18, but Gregory develops it into a full theory of the image of God. The soul’s likeness to God is not a fixed property but a dynamic relationship: it depends on which direction the mirror faces. This has profound implications for theosis. Divinization is not the acquisition of something new but the restoration of the soul’s original orientation. Gregory’s anthropology is thus optimistic — the capacity for theosis is built into human nature — but also demanding, because the mirror must be actively kept clean and turned toward the light.
