Corpus Hermeticum XI: All Things in God

Hermes Trismegistus (attributed), c. 1st-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The eleventh treatise of the Corpus Hermeticum, sometimes titled Mind to Hermes, is a sustained meditation on the nature of God and the cosmos. Its central claim is radical panentheism: God is not separate from any being, but contains all things within himself while simultaneously exceeding them. “All things are in God, and God is in all things.” The treatise pushes this logic to its conclusion: if God contains all things and is everywhere, then there is nothing outside God, and the apparent multiplicity of the world is an expression of divine unity.

For the theosis question, this treatise provides the metaphysical ground: if God is already in all things and all things are already in God, then deification is not the acquisition of something foreign but the recognition of what is already the case. The soul’s return to God is not a journey across distance but an awakening to presence. The text anticipates Plotinus’s insight that the One is “nowhere and everywhere” and the Upanishadic formula tat tvam asi—“that thou art.”

§1–2 · Corpus Hermeticum XI.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

χθὲς, ὦ Ἀσκληπιέ, τὸν τέλειον λόγον ἀπέδωκα

English

Yesterday, Asclepius, I delivered the perfect discourse. Now I think it necessary to follow it up by discussing the nature of sensation. Sensation and thought differ in this: sensation belongs to matter, thought to essence. But to me, thought does not differ from sensation, at least in humans; for thought is sensible and sensation is thoughtful. In God, however, thought and sensation are one.

§4–5 · Corpus Hermeticum XI.4–5
Manuscript
Greek

ἐὰν μὴ σεαυτὸν ἐξισώσῃς τῷ θεῷ, τὸν θεὸν νοῆσαι οὐ δύνασαι· τὸ γὰρ ὅμοιον τῷ ὁμοίῳ νοεῖται.

English

If you do not make yourself equal to God, you cannot understand God; for like is known by like. Grow to immeasurable size. Be free from every body, transcend all time. Become eternity and thus you will understand God. Believe that nothing is impossible for you. Consider yourself immortal and able to understand everything: all arts, all sciences, the nature of every living creature.

§7–8 · Corpus Hermeticum XI.7–8
Manuscript
Greek

πάντα γὰρ ἐν τῷ θεῷ, καὶ ὁ θεὸς ἐν πᾶσιν. οὐδενὸς ἀπολείπεται ὁ θεός.

English

Look at what I now show you. There are many forms of every kind of living thing. Can you see where they all are? In God. And where is God? Not in heaven above, nor below the earth, nor in any other place. He is in all these things. For all things are in God, and God is in all things. God is not separate from any being.

§14 · Corpus Hermeticum XI.14
Manuscript
Greek

οὐ νοῦς ἐστιν ὁ θεός, αἴτιος δὲ τοῦ εἶναι νοῦν

English

God is not Mind, but the cause of Mind; not Spirit, but the cause of Spirit; not Light, but the cause of Light. We must honour God by these two names, which belong to him alone and to no other. For none of the other beings called gods, none of men or demons, can be even slightly good apart from God alone.

§20–21 · Corpus Hermeticum XI.20–21
Manuscript
Greek

τοιαύτη ἡ καλὴ εἰκὼν τοῦ θεοῦ

English

Such is the beautiful image of God, if indeed you can contemplate it. But you cannot contemplate it with these eyes of yours. The image of God cannot be seen with hands or feet, but with mind and heart. If you can contemplate this, then believe me: you will find the path of ascent. Or rather, the image itself will lead you on the way.

Translator's Notes

  1. §4–5: The command “make yourself equal to God” (ἐξισώσῃς τῷ θεῷ) is perhaps the most audacious statement in the entire Hermetic corpus. It is not presented as blasphemy but as the prerequisite for knowledge. The logic is epistemological: like can only be known by like. If you wish to know the infinite, you must become infinite. This principle appears in Empedocles, in Plotinus, and in the Christian mystical tradition—Meister Eckhart’s “The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me.”
  2. §7–8: The formula “all things are in God, and God is in all things” is technically panentheistic rather than pantheistic. Pantheism identifies God with the totality of things; panentheism holds that God contains all things but also exceeds them. The distinction matters: in pantheism, there is nowhere to ascend to, because God is simply the sum of what is. In panentheism, God exceeds the world, and the soul can rise beyond the material to that excess.
  3. §14: The via negativa here—God is not Mind but the cause of Mind—places the Hermetic God beyond even the highest philosophical categories. This is remarkably close to Plotinus’s description of the One as beyond being, beyond thought, beyond predication. It also anticipates the apophatic theology of Pseudo-Dionysius and the Cappadocian Fathers.
