2 Corinthians 3:18: Transformed from Glory to Glory

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Second Corinthians 3:18 is arguably the single most important verse in the New Testament for the doctrine of theosis. In one sentence Paul describes a process of progressive transformation: believers, with unveiled faces, behold the glory of the Lord and are “being transformed into the same image from glory to glory.” The verb metamorphoumetha (μεταμορφούμεθα) is in the present passive—the transformation is ongoing, and it is something done to us rather than something we achieve.

The verse operates within the contrast between Moses’ veiled glory and the unveiled glory available in Christ. Moses’ face shone after encountering God on Sinai, but he veiled it. In Christ, the veil is removed, and the glory that transforms is not reflected but absorbed. The phrase “from glory to glory” (ἀπὸ δόξης εἰς δόξαν) implies an ascending process without terminal point—a claim that Gregory of Nyssa would develop into his doctrine of epektasis, the soul’s eternal progress into the infinite God.

Greek

ἡμεῖς δὲ πάντες ἀνακεκαλυμμένῳ προσώπῳ τὴν δόξαν κυρίου κατοπτριζόμενοι · τὴν αὐτὴν εἰκόνα μεταμορφούμεθα ἀπὸ δόξης εἰς δόξαν · καθάπερ ἀπὸ κυρίου πνεύματος ·

English

And we all, with unveiled face, beholding as in a mirror the glory of the Lord, are being transformed (metamorphoumetha, μεταμορφούμεθα) into the same image, from glory to glory, just as from the Lord, the Spirit.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:18: The verb katoptrizomenoi (κατοπτριζόμενοι) can mean either “beholding as in a mirror” or “reflecting as a mirror.” The ambiguity is likely intentional: the believer simultaneously beholds the divine glory and reflects it, and in the process is transformed into what is beheld. This is the logic of theosis in a single verb.
  2. §3:18: The phrase “from the Lord, the Spirit” (ἀπὸ κυρίου πνεύματος) is grammatically ambiguous: it may identify the Lord with the Spirit, or it may mean “from the Lord of the Spirit” or “from the Lord who is Spirit.” The verse played a significant role in later trinitarian debates about the relationship between the Son and the Spirit.
