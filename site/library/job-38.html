Job 38: Where Were You When I Laid the Earth?

Moses (attributed), traditional

Introduction

The Book of Job reaches its climax not with an answer but with a question. After thirty-seven chapters of human argument about the meaning of suffering—Job’s friends insisting that suffering implies guilt, Job insisting on his innocence and demanding an audience with God—God finally speaks from the whirlwind. But instead of explaining Job’s suffering, God responds with a devastating series of rhetorical questions: “Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?” The message is not that Job is guilty. The message is that Job is small.

This is the anti-theosis of divine majesty. The gap between God and the human being is not a problem to be solved through mystical ascent or incarnational theology. It is simply the way things are. God is the architect of creation; Job is a creature who cannot even comprehend the architecture. The speech does not invite Job to participate in the divine nature. It invites him to be silent before it.

On what were its bases sunk, or who laid its cornerstone, when the morning stars sang together and all the sons of God shouted for joy?”

English

“Or who shut in the sea with doors when it burst out from the womb, when I made clouds its garment and thick darkness its swaddling band, and prescribed limits for it and set bars and doors, and said, ‘Thus far shall you come, and no farther, and here shall your proud waves be stayed’?”

Translator's Notes

  1. §38:4–7: The “sons of God” (bene elohim, בְּנֵי אֱלֹהִים) who shout for joy at creation are the angelic beings of the divine council—a feature of ancient Israelite theology that later monotheism would suppress. Even these divine beings are spectators at creation, not participants in it. The gap between Creator and creature applies to angels as much as to humans.
  2. §38:8–11: The sea in ancient Near Eastern mythology was a chaos monster (Tiamat in Babylonian myth, Yamm in Ugaritic). God’s setting of boundaries for the sea echoes the creation-through-combat motif while transforming it: God does not fight the sea but simply commands it. The image of “swaddling” the sea like an infant domesticates the cosmic threat. The theological point is that God’s power is not struggled for but simply exercised—a power that no human being can approach.
