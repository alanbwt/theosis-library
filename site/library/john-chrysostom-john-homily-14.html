Homily 14 on John: Gods by Grace

John Chrysostom, c. 349–407 AD

Introduction

John Chrysostom (c. 349–407), Archbishop of Constantinople and the most prolific preacher of the patristic era, delivered his Homilies on the Gospel of John during his time as a presbyter in Antioch. These homilies combine close textual exegesis with pastoral exhortation, and they remain among the most influential commentaries on the Fourth Gospel in the Christian tradition.

Homily 14, commenting on John 1:12–13, addresses the extraordinary claim that believers receive “the right to become children of God.” Chrysostom presses the logic of this passage further than most of his contemporaries: if we are made children of God, then we are called to participate in the divine nature itself—not by our own capacity, but by the gift of grace. His formulation that we are “called to become gods” stands as one of the most direct articulations of theosis in the Antiochene tradition.

Greek

Καλούμεθα θεοὶ γενέσθαι, οὐ φύσει ἀλλὰ χάριτι.

English

We are called to become gods, not by nature but by grace. For just as he became man by nature who was God, so he makes gods by grace those who were men.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Homily 14.1: Chrysostom’s qualifying phrase “not by nature but by grace” (οὐ φύσει ἀλλὰ χάριτι) is characteristic of the Antiochene school’s careful distinction between the Creator and the creature. Unlike the Alexandrian tradition, which could sometimes speak of deification in terms that blurred this boundary, Chrysostom consistently insists that human participation in divinity is a gift, not an ontological upgrade. The symmetry of his formulation—God became man by nature, man becomes god by grace—mirrors the “wonderful exchange” (admirabile commercium) that would become central to both Eastern and Western soteriological language.
