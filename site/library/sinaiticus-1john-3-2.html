1 John 3:1-3: We Shall Be Like Him

Ἰωάννου Αʹ
John the Elder (attributed), c. 90-100 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

First John 3:1–3 makes one of the most direct eschatological promises in the New Testament: when Christ appears, the faithful will become like him, because they will see him as he is. The passage moves from present identity (we are already children of God) to future transformation (we shall be like him), linking the two through the act of seeing. This is the scriptural basis for what the later Latin tradition called the visio beatifica—the beatific vision—and what the Greek Fathers understood as the consummation of theosis.

The Greek phrase homoioi auto esometha (ὅμοιοι αὐτῷ ἐσόμεθα, “we shall be like him”) does not specify in what respect the likeness obtains. The ambiguity is generative: it leaves open whether the likeness is moral, ontological, or both. The text follows Codex Sinaiticus.

Greek

ἴδετε ποταπὴν ἀγάπην δέδωκεν ἡμῖν ὁ πατήρ · ἵνα τέκνα θεοῦ κληθῶμεν · καὶ ἐσμέν · διὰ τοῦτο ὁ κόσμος οὐ γινώσκει ἡμᾶς · ὅτι οὐκ ἔγνω αὐτόν · ἀγαπητοί · νῦν τέκνα θεοῦ ἐσμεν · καὶ οὔπω ἐφανερώθη τί ἐσόμεθα · οἴδαμεν ὅτι ἐὰν φανερωθῆ · ὅμοιοι αὐτῷ ἐσόμεθα · ὅτι ὀψόμεθα αὐτὸν καθώς ἐστιν · καὶ πᾶς ὁ ἔχων τὴν ἐλπίδα ταύτην ἐπ’ αὐτῷ · ἁγνίζει ἑαυτόν · καθὼς ἐκεῖνος ἁγνός ἐστιν ·

English

See what kind of love the Father has given to us, that we should be called children of God—and so we are. The reason the world does not know us is that it did not know him. Beloved, we are now children of God, and what we shall be has not yet been revealed. We know that when he appears, we shall be like him (homoioi auto, ὅμοιοι αὐτῷ), for we shall see him as he is. And everyone who has this hope in him purifies himself, just as he is pure.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:2: The phrase homoioi auto esometha (ὅμοιοι αὐτῷ ἐσόμεθα) uses homoios (like, similar) rather than the stronger isos (equal). The distinction matters. The theosis tradition consistently maintained that human deification is by grace and participation, not by nature or essence—humans become like God without becoming God in the absolute sense. The causal link in the verse is remarkable: we shall be like him because (ὅτι) we shall see him as he is. Likeness results from vision. This became the foundation of the beatific vision tradition in both East and West: to see God is to be transformed by what one sees. Gregory of Nyssa, Augustine, and Thomas Aquinas all built on this verse, though they drew different conclusions about the nature and finality of the vision.
  2. §3:1: Codex Sinaiticus includes the phrase kai esmen (καὶ ἐσμέν, “and so we are”), which some later manuscripts omit. The inclusion is significant: it insists that divine filiation is not merely a title or a future hope but a present reality. We are already children of God. The tension between the present reality (“we are now children of God”) and the unrevealed future (“what we shall be has not yet been revealed”) is the eschatological structure of theosis: already begun, not yet consummated.
