Introduction

Romans 7:14–25 is Paul’s anguished meditation on the divided self: “The good that I want to do, I do not do.” Whether this describes pre-conversion or ongoing Christian experience has been debated since Augustine.

§7:14–25 · Codex Sinaiticus, Romans 7:14–25
Manuscript
Greek

οἴδαμεν γὰρ ὅτι ὁ νόμος πνευματικός ἐστιν · ἐγὼ δὲ σάρκινός εἰμι πεπραμένος ὑπὸ τὴν ἁμαρτίαν

English

For we know that the law is spiritual, but I am of the flesh, sold under sin. For I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate. Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death? Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord!

Translator's Notes

  1. §7:24: The cry “Wretched man that I am!” (ταλαίπωρος ἐγὼ ἄνθρωπος) became central to Augustine’s theology of original sin and Luther’s doctrine of simul justus et peccator—simultaneously righteous and sinner.
