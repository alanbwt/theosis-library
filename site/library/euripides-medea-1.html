Introduction

The Medea of Euripides (431 BCE) dramatizes Medea’s revenge against Jason, who has abandoned her for a new wife. The play explores the destructive power of betrayed love and the ambiguous boundary between human and divine violence.

§Lines 1–15 · Euripides, Medea
Manuscript
Greek

εἴθ' ὤφελ' Ἀργοῦς μὴ διαπτάσθαι σκάφος

English

Would that the hull of the Argo had never flown through the dark-blue Symplegades to the land of Colchis, and that the pine in the glens of Pelion had never been felled and furnished oars for the hands of the heroes who went to fetch the golden fleece for Pelias! For then my mistress Medea would not have sailed to the towers of Iolcus, her heart struck with love for Jason.

§Lines 230–251 · Euripides, Medea
Manuscript
Greek

πάντων δ' ὅσ' ἔμψυχ' ἐστὶ καὶ γνώμην ἔχει

English

Of all creatures that have breath and sense, we women are the most wretched. First we must buy a husband at an extravagant price, and then accept a master for our body—this evil is even more painful than the first. And the great contest is this: whether we take a bad one or a good one.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 230–251: Medea’s speech on the condition of women is the most explicit feminist critique in surviving Greek literature. Her analysis of marriage as economic transaction and bodily subjection resonated far beyond its dramatic context. Euripides’s willingness to give voice to the marginalized was noted—and criticized—in antiquity.
