Introduction

Catullus 101 is an elegy for the poet’s dead brother, written during a journey to his grave site in the Troad. Its final words—ave atque vale (hail and farewell)—are among the most famous in Latin literature.

§Poem 101 (complete) · Catullus, Carmen 101
Manuscript
Greek

Multas per gentes et multa per aequora vectus advenio has miseras, frater, ad inferias

English

Carried through many nations and over many seas, I come, brother, to these wretched funeral rites, to present you with the last gift due to the dead, and to speak in vain to your silent ashes, since fortune has taken you yourself from me. Alas, poor brother, unfairly taken from me. But now, meanwhile, receive these offerings, drenched in a brother’s tears. Hail and farewell forever, brother.

Translator's Notes

  1. §101: The poem’s power lies in its restraint—Catullus, usually voluble and passionate, here achieves grief through formal simplicity. Tennyson’s “Frater Ave atque Vale” pays direct homage. The address to silent ashes (mutam cinerem) confronts the absolute finality of death without consolation.
