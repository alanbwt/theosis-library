Prometheus Bound: The Suffering of the Fire-Bringer

Prometheus Bound: The Suffering of the Fire-Bringer
Aeschylus, c. 525-456 BCE
Mediceus Laurentianus 32.9

Introduction

Prometheus Bound stages the ultimate confrontation between divine authority and the rebel who defies it for the sake of humanity. Chained to a crag at the edge of the world by order of Zeus, Prometheus endures eternal torment for having stolen fire and given mortals the arts of civilization. The play raises questions that have never been settled: Is it just to punish those who act for the good of others? Can a tyrant, even a divine one, be truly sovereign if he rules through fear?

§88-100 · PV 88-100
Greek

ὦ δῖος αἰθὴρ καὶ ταχύπτεροι πνοαί, ποταμῶν τε πηγαί, ποντίων τε κυμάτων ἀνήριθμον γέλασμα, παμμῆτόρ τε γῆ, καὶ τὸν πανόπτην κύκλον ἡλίου καλῶ· ἴδεσθέ μ᾿ οἷα πρὸς θεῶν πάσχω θεός.

English

O divine sky and swift-winged winds, O river springs and numberless laughter of the sea-waves, O Earth, mother of all, and all-seeing circle of the sun — behold me! See what I, a god, suffer at the hands of gods.

§436-445 · PV 436-445
Greek

τέχνας δέ σφιν πάσας ἐμηχανησάμην... ἐγὼ δ᾿ αὐτοῖσι νοῦν ἐνέθηκα, κἀφρένωσα τὸ πρίν.

English

All human arts are from Prometheus... I gave them understanding and a portion of reason, they who before were like shapes in a dream.

§250-256 · PV 250-256
Greek

ἑκὼν ἑκών ἥμαρτον, οὐκ ἀρνήσομαι· θνητοῖσι δ᾿ ἀρήγων αὐτὸς ηὑρόμην πόνους.

English

Willingly, willingly I transgressed — I will not deny it. By helping mortals I found suffering for myself.
