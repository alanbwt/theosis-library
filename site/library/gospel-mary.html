Gospel of Mary: The Soul Ascending Past the Powers

Anonymous (Gospel of Mary), c. 2nd c. AD

Introduction

The Gospel of Mary survives in two fragmentary Greek papyri (third century) and a longer Coptic translation in the Berlin Codex (fifth century). Roughly half the text is missing. What remains is among the most striking documents in the Gnostic corpus: after the risen Jesus departs, the male disciples are afraid and despairing, and it is Mary Magdalene who comforts them and reveals a private teaching she received from the Saviour in a vision.

Pages 7 through 10 contain the heart of the text: Mary’s account of the soul’s ascent past the cosmic powers. The soul encounters four powers—Darkness, Desire, Ignorance, and Wrath—and must defeat each one through knowledge of its true nature. This is theosis as escape: the soul recognizes that it does not belong to the material world, that the powers have no legitimate authority over it, and that its true home is in the silence of the divine. The passage is also remarkable for what follows it—Peter’s challenge to Mary’s authority, and Levi’s rebuke of Peter—which makes this text central to debates about gender, authority, and whose experience of the divine counts.

Translator's Notes

  1. §7:1-7: The saying “Where the mind is, there is the treasure” inverts Matthew 6:21 (“Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also”). In the Gnostic reading, the mind (nous) itself is the treasure—the divine faculty within the human being that enables direct contact with God.
  2. §8:1-10: The soul’s declaration “In a world I was set free from a world, and in a type from a heavenly type” is one of the most compressed statements of Gnostic soteriology in existence. It suggests that liberation happens simultaneously on multiple levels: freedom from the material world and from the cosmic archetypes that structure it.
  3. §9:13-29: The seven forms of the fourth power correspond loosely to the seven planets of ancient cosmology, which in Gnostic thought are the seven archons who imprison the soul. The soul must pass through all seven to return to the Pleroma. This schema appears in many Gnostic texts, most notably in the Apocryphon of John.
