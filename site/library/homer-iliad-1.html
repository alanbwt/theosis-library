Iliad, Book I: The Wrath of Achilles

        
Iliad, Book I: The Wrath of Achilles

        
Homer, c. 8th century BCE

        
Venetus A (Marcianus Graecus 454)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
Book I of the Iliad establishes the central conflict of the entire epic in 611 lines. After the famous proem (the first seven lines that begin all of Western literature), Homer narrates: the plague sent by Apollo because Agamemnon dishonored his priest Chryses; the assembly where Achilles confronts Agamemnon; Agamemnon's seizure of Briseis; Achilles' withdrawal from battle; and Thetis's prayer to Zeus, which sets the divine machinery in motion. The story of the Iliad — the wrath of Achilles and its consequences — flows entirely from this opening book.

            §1.1-7 (Proem) · Il. 1.1-7
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Μῆνιν ἄειδε, θεά, Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος οὐλομένην, ἣ μυρί᾿ Ἀχαιοῖς ἄλγε᾿ ἔθηκε, πολλὰς δ᾿ ἰφθίμους ψυχὰς Ἄϊδι προΐαψεν ἡρώων, αὐτοὺς δὲ ἑλώρια τεῦχε κύνεσσιν οἰωνοῖσί τε πᾶσι, Διὸς δ᾿ ἐτελείετο βουλή, ἐξ οὗ δὴ τὰ πρῶτα διαστήτην ἐρίσαντε Ἀτρεΐδης τε ἄναξ ἀνδρῶν καὶ δῖος Ἀχιλλεύς.

          


          

            
English

            
Sing, goddess, the wrath of Achilles, son of Peleus — the accursed wrath that brought countless sorrows upon the Achaeans, and hurled many mighty souls of heroes down to Hades, making them prey for dogs and all the birds — and the will of Zeus was being fulfilled — from the moment when first they parted in strife: Atreus' son, lord of men, and godlike Achilles.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.8-21 (The Plague Begins) · Il. 1.8-21
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Τίς τ᾿ ἄρ σφωε θεῶν ἔριδι ξυνέηκε μάχεσθαι; Λητοῦς καὶ Διὸς υἱός· ὁ γὰρ βασιλῆϊ χολωθεὶς νοῦσον ἀνὰ στρατὸν ὦρσε κακήν, ὀλέκοντο δὲ λαοί, οὕνεκα τὸν Χρύσην ἠτίμασεν ἀρητῆρα Ἀτρεΐδης.

          


          

            
English

            
Which of the gods set them to quarrel and fight? The son of Leto and Zeus — for he was angry with the king, and sent a plague upon the army, an evil sickness, and the people were dying — because Atreus' son had dishonored the priest Chryses. For he came to the swift ships of the Achaeans to ransom his daughter, bringing untold ransom and holding in his hands the wreaths of Apollo who shoots from afar, on a golden staff. He begged all the Achaeans, but most of all the two sons of Atreus, the marshalers of the host.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.43-53 (Apollo Descends) · Il. 1.43-53
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Ὣς ἔφατ᾿ εὐχόμενος, τοῦ δ᾿ ἔκλυε Φοῖβος Ἀπόλλων, βῆ δὲ κατ᾿ Οὐλύμποιο καρήνων χωόμενος κῆρ, τόξ᾿ ὤμοισιν ἔχων ἀμφηρεφέα τε φαρέτρην· ἔκλαγξαν δ᾿ ἄρ᾿ ὀϊστοὶ ἐπ᾿ ὤμων χωομένοιο.

          


          

            
English

            
So he spoke in prayer, and Phoebus Apollo heard him, and came down from the peaks of Olympus, his heart raging, with his bow on his shoulder and the close-covered quiver. The arrows clanged on his shoulders as he moved in his anger. He came as the night comes down. He sat opposite the ships and let an arrow fly: terrible was the twang of the silver bow. First he attacked the mules and the swift dogs, then he loosed his sharp arrows against the men themselves. The pyres of the dead burned everywhere and never ceased.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.121-129 (Achilles vs Agamemnon) · Il. 1.121-129
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Τὸν δ᾿ ἠμείβετ᾿ ἔπειτα ποδάρκης δῖος Ἀχιλλεύς· Ἀτρεΐδη κύδιστε φιλοκτεανώτατε πάντων, πῶς γάρ τοι δώσουσι γέρας μεγάθυμοι Ἀχαιοί;

          


          

            
English

            
Then swift-footed Achilles answered him: 'Most glorious son of Atreus, most greedy for gain of all men, how can the great-hearted Achaeans give you a prize? We know nothing of any common store laid up. Whatever we took from the cities we have already distributed.'

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.188-200 (Athena Restrains Achilles) · Il. 1.188-200
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Ὣς φάτο· Πηλεΐωνι δ᾿ ἄχος γένετ᾿, ἐν δέ οἱ ἦτορ στήθεσσιν λασίοισι διάνδιχα μερμήριξεν, ἢ ὅ γε φάσγανον ὀξὺ ἐρυσσάμενος παρὰ μηροῦ τοὺς μὲν ἀναστήσειεν, ὁ δ᾿ Ἀτρεΐδην ἐναρίζοι, ἦε χόλον παύσειεν ἐρητύσειέ τε θυμόν.

          


          

            
English

            
So he spoke. Grief came to the son of Peleus, and the heart in his shaggy breast was divided two ways: should he draw the sharp sword from his thigh and force the others aside and kill the son of Atreus, or should he restrain his wrath and curb his anger? While he debated this in his mind and his heart, and was drawing his great sword from its sheath, Athena came down from the sky. White-armed Hera had sent her, who loved both men alike in her heart and was concerned for them. Athena stood behind him and seized the son of Peleus by his golden hair, appearing to him alone — none of the others saw her.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.348-356 (Achilles Withdraws) · Il. 1.348-356
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Πάτροκλος δὲ φίλῳ ἐπεπείθεθ᾿ ἑταίρῳ, ἐκ δ᾿ ἄγαγε κλισίης Βρισηΐδα καλλιπάρῃον, δῶκε δ᾿ ἄγειν· τὼ δ᾿ αὖτις ἴτην παρὰ νῆας Ἀχαιῶν· ἡ δ᾿ ἀέκουσ᾿ ἅμα τοῖσι γυνὴ κίεν.

          


          

            
English

            
Patroclus obeyed his dear comrade. He brought out of the hut Briseis of the lovely cheeks and gave her over to the heralds to lead away. The two of them went back beside the ships of the Achaeans. The woman went with them unwilling. Achilles in tears withdrew apart from his companions and sat down by the shore of the gray sea, looking out over the wine-dark deep. He stretched out his hands and prayed long to his mother.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.494-516 (Thetis Petitions Zeus) · Il. 1.494-516
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Καὶ τότε δὴ πρὸς Ὄλυμπον ἴσαν θεοὶ αἰὲν ἐόντες πάντες ἅμα, Ζεὺς δ᾿ ἦρχε· Θέτις δ᾿ οὐ λήθετ᾿ ἐφετμέων παιδὸς ἑοῦ, ἀλλ᾿ ἥ γ᾿ ἀνεδύσετο κῦμα θαλάσσης.

          


          

            
English

            
Then the immortal gods went to Olympus together, and Zeus led them. But Thetis did not forget the requests of her son. She rose from the sea, and came at dawn to great Olympus and the heavens. She found wide-seeing Zeus sitting alone on the topmost peak of many-ridged Olympus. She sat down before him, and laid her left hand on his knees, and her right she put under his chin. She spoke in supplication: 'Father Zeus, if ever I have helped you among the gods by word or deed, fulfill this prayer for me. Honor my son, who is doomed to a short life among all others. He has been dishonored by Agamemnon, who has taken his prize. So honor him for me, you Olympian, all-wise Zeus. Give victory to the Trojans until the Achaeans honor my son and exalt him with recompense.'
