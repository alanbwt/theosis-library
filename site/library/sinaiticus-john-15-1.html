John 15:1-8: I Am the Vine, You Are the Branches

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

The vine and branches discourse of John 15:1–8 is the most sustained organic metaphor for divine-human union in the New Testament. Jesus does not say the disciples are like branches; he says they are branches, and he is the vine. The relationship is not analogy but identity—the same life flows through vine and branch. The Greek verb meno (μένω), “to remain” or “abide,” appears eleven times in these eight verses, hammering home the necessity of continuous indwelling.

The passage is both promise and warning. Those who abide in the vine bear fruit; those who do not are cut off and burned. The Fathers read this as a description of the mechanics of theosis: participation in the divine life is not a static achievement but an ongoing relationship that requires active cooperation. Separation from the vine is not merely moral failure but ontological catastrophe—a severing from the source of life itself.

Translator's Notes

  1. §15:1: The “I am” (ego eimi, ἐγώ εἰμι) formula echoes the divine name revealed at the burning bush (Exodus 3:14, LXX: ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν). This is the seventh and final “I am” saying in John’s Gospel. The vine metaphor draws on a rich Old Testament tradition in which Israel is God’s vineyard (Isaiah 5:1–7, Psalm 80:8–16). Jesus claims not merely to tend the vine but to be it.
  2. §15:4–5: The verb meno (μένω) is the key theological term in this passage. It means to remain, abide, or dwell. In Johannine theology, it describes the permanent mutual indwelling of Father and Son (14:10), of Christ and the believer (here), and of the Spirit in the community (14:17). The concept is not static but dynamic—it implies continuous, active participation in the life of the vine.
