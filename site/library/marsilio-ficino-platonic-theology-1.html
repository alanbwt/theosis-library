Introduction

Marsilio Ficino's Platonic Theology (1482) argues for the immortality of the soul through a synthesis of Platonic philosophy and Christian doctrine. Ficino's translations and commentaries single-handedly revived Platonism in the Latin West.

Translation

§Book 1, Chapter 1 · Platonic Theology, Book 1, Chapter 1

The soul of man is all things together. It is the center of nature, the middle term of all things, the chain of the world, the face of all, and the bond and juncture of the universe. When the soul ascends to the mind, it approaches the divine. When it descends to the body, it touches the animal. Alone among all creatures, the human soul occupies every degree of being: it unites in itself the highest and the lowest, the eternal and the temporal. This is why Plato rightly called it a great miracle.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Book 1, Chapter 1: Ficino's doctrine of the soul as copula mundi (bond of the world) positions humanity at the center of a cosmic hierarchy, capable of participating in every level of reality. This anthropology deeply influenced Pico della Mirandola and, through him, the entire Renaissance conception of human dignity. Ficino's synthesis of Plato and Christ shaped European intellectual life for two centuries.
