Introduction

Diogenes of Sinope (c. 412–323 BCE), the most famous Cynic philosopher, taught through provocative action rather than systematic writing. These anecdotes from Diogenes Laertius capture his radical rejection of convention and his pursuit of virtue through nature alone.

§Lives VI.20–32 · Diogenes Laertius, Vitae Philosophorum VI.20–32
Manuscript
Greek

ἅψας λύχνον μεθ᾽ ἡμέραν περιῆει λέγων ἄνθρωπον ζητῶ

English

He lit a lamp in broad daylight and went about saying, “I am looking for an honest man.” When asked where he was from, he said, “I am a citizen of the world.” Seeing a child drinking from his hands, he cast away the cup from his wallet, saying, “A child has beaten me in plainness of living.” Alexander the Great stood over him and said, “Ask of me any favor you like.” He replied, “Stand out of my sunlight.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lives VI.20–32: Diogenes’s declaration “I am a citizen of the world” (κοσμοπολίτης) is the first recorded use of the term. It was adopted by the Stoics as a core political concept and remains foundational to cosmopolitan thought.
