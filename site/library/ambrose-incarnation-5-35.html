On the Incarnation V.35: The Imperfect Made Perfect

Ambrose of Milan, c. 339–397 AD

Introduction

Ambrose of Milan (c. 339–397) was one of the four original Doctors of the Latin Church, a bishop whose theological writings, hymns, and pastoral influence shaped Western Christianity decisively. His treatise On the Incarnation of the Lord (De Incarnationis Dominicae Sacramento) was written against Apollinarian and Arian Christologies that diminished either the humanity or the divinity of Christ.

Chapter V, section 35 addresses the purpose of the Incarnation. Ambrose argues that Christ assumed imperfect human nature not to be diminished by it but to bring it to perfection. The logic is therapeutic and transformative: the physician must take on the disease in order to heal it. This medical metaphor for the Incarnation was widespread in patristic thought, but Ambrose gives it particular force by insisting that the end result is not merely restoration but elevation beyond the original state.

§V.35 · De Incarnationis Dominicae Sacramento V.35 (CSEL 79)
Manuscript
Latin

Suscepit imperfectum ut perficeret. Formam servi accepit ut servo libertatem Domini conferret. Descendit ut ascenderemus; infirmitatem nostram suscepit ut nobis suam virtutem donaret.

English

He assumed the imperfect to make it perfect. He took upon himself the form of a servant so that he might confer on the servant the freedom of the Lord. He descended that we might ascend; he took on our weakness that he might give us his strength.

Translator's Notes

  1. §V.35: Ambrose’s rhetoric follows the classical pattern of antithesis: imperfect/perfect, servant/Lord, descent/ascent, weakness/strength. Each pair describes the same exchange from a different angle. The term virtutem, rendered “strength,” carries a broader range of meaning in Latin than in English, encompassing power, excellence, and moral virtue. Ambrose’s formulation influenced his most famous catechumen, Augustine of Hippo, though Augustine would develop the exchange motif in a more grace-centered direction that emphasized human inability and divine initiative.
