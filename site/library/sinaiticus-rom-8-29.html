Romans 8:29-30: Conformed to the Image of His Son

Πρὸς Ῥωμαίους
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

Romans 8:29–30 contains what theologians call the golden chain of salvation: foreknowledge, predestination, calling, justification, glorification. Each link is forged in the divine will, and the chain terminates not in mere forgiveness but in conformity to the image of the Son. The Greek symmorphous tes eikonos (συμμόρφους τῆς εἰκόνος) is striking: God’s purpose is not that believers would merely imitate Christ but that they would be formed together with his image. This is the Pauline foundation for theosis.

The passage appears in a letter written to a community Paul had not yet visited, which makes its theological ambition all the more remarkable. Paul is laying out, in compressed form, the entire arc of salvation history as he understands it. The text here follows Codex Sinaiticus, the earliest complete New Testament manuscript, produced in the fourth century.

§8:29–30 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

οὕς προέγνω καὶ προώρισεν συμμόρφους τῆς εἰκόνος τοῦ υἱοῦ αὐτοῦ · εἰς τὸ εἶναι αὐτὸν πρωτότοκον ἐν πολλοῖς ἀδελφοῖς · οὓς δὲ προώρισεν · τούτους καὶ ἐκάλεσεν · καὶ οὓς ἐκάλεσεν · τούτους καὶ ἐδικαίωσεν · οὓς δὲ ἐδικαίωσεν · τούτους καὶ ἐδόξασεν ·

English

For those whom he foreknew, he also predestined to be conformed to the image (eikon, εἰκών) of his Son, so that he might be the firstborn among many brothers. And those whom he predestined, he also called; and those whom he called, he also justified; and those whom he justified, he also glorified.

Translator's Notes

  1. §8:29: The word symmorphous (σύμμορφος) is a compound of syn (with, together) and morphe (form, shape). It does not mean mere resemblance but a sharing in form. Paul’s choice of eikon (εἰκών) rather than homoioma (likeness) is deliberate: the image of the Son is not a copy but a participation in the original. This verse became a cornerstone of patristic theosis theology. Athanasius, Cyril of Alexandria, and Maximus the Confessor all cite it as evidence that the purpose of salvation is not merely forensic acquittal but ontological transformation into the divine likeness.
  2. §8:30: The chain of aorist verbs—foreknew, predestined, called, justified, glorified—presents the entire process as already completed from God’s perspective. The use of the aorist edoxasen (ἐδόξασεν, “glorified”) for something that has not yet occurred in human experience is one of the boldest theological claims in the Pauline corpus. Glorification is spoken of as a fait accompli. For the theosis tradition, this is decisive: deification is not speculative but is already accomplished in the divine will.
