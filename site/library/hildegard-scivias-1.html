Introduction

Hildegard of Bingen's Scivias ("Know the Ways") is the first of her three visionary works, completed around 1151. It opens with a divine command to write down the visions she has received since childhood.

Translation

§Declaration: The Voice from Heaven · Scivias, Book 1, Vision 1

And behold, in the forty-third year of my earthly course, as I gazed with great fear and trembling attention at a heavenly vision, I saw a great splendor in which resounded a voice from heaven, saying to me: "O fragile human, ashes of ashes and filth of filth! Say and write what you see and hear. But since you are timid in speaking, simple in expounding, and unlearned in writing, speak and write these things not according to human speech or human invention, but according to what you see and hear in the heavens above, in the marvelous works of God."

Translator's Notes

  1. §Vision 1: Hildegard's claim to be "unlearned" (indocta) served both as genuine humility and as a rhetorical strategy: by presenting herself as a mere vessel for divine communication, she bypassed the restrictions on women's theological authority. Pope Eugenius III authenticated her visions at the Synod of Trier in 1148, making her the first woman officially sanctioned to write theology.
