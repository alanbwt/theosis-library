2 Enoch 22: Enoch Transformed into an Angel

Anonymous (Enochic), c. 3rd c. BCE

Introduction

2 Enoch (also called the Slavonic Apocalypse of Enoch or the Book of the Secrets of Enoch) survives only in Old Church Slavonic manuscripts, though it was almost certainly composed in Greek, possibly drawing on a Hebrew or Aramaic original. Its date is debated—proposals range from the first century BCE to the early medieval period—but the theological content of chapter 22 places it firmly within the tradition of Jewish angelomorphic transformation.

Chapter 22 describes the most explicit deification of a human being in the Jewish pseudepigraphical tradition. Enoch stands before the face of God, and the Lord commands the archangel Michael to strip him of his earthly garments, anoint him with holy oil, and clothe him in garments of glory. Enoch looks at himself and discovers that he has become “like one of the glorious ones, and there was no observable difference.” He has been transformed from a human being into an angel—or, more precisely, into a being indistinguishable from the highest angels. This is not metaphor. The text presents it as an ontological change.

Translator's Notes

  1. §22:1-3: The stripping of earthly garments and clothing in garments of glory is a motif found across Jewish mystical literature, from the priestly vestments of the high priest (which symbolized the cosmos) to the heavenly garments in the Hekhalot literature. The anointing with “sweet ointment” parallels the anointing of the high priest in Exodus 30:22-33, but here it effects an ontological transformation rather than a consecration to office.
  2. §22:4-6: The declaration “I was like one of the glorious ones, and there was no observable difference” is the most unambiguous statement of human deification in the Jewish pseudepigraphical corpus. “The glorious ones” are the angels. Enoch has not merely been elevated or honoured; he has become indistinguishable from angelic beings. This tradition feeds directly into the later Metatron traditions in 3 Enoch, where Enoch is identified as the “lesser YHWH.”
  3. §22:7-8: Vreveil (also spelled Vrevoil or Pravuil) is the angelic scribe, a figure paralleling Thoth in Egyptian tradition and Nabu in Mesopotamian. The transmission of heavenly books to Enoch makes him not merely an angel but a possessor of divine knowledge—the same claim that Gnostic texts make for their initiates. The number 366 books may correspond to the days of the solar calendar, suggesting that Enoch’s knowledge encompasses all of time itself.
