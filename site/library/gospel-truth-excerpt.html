Gospel of Truth: Knowledge as Return to the Father

Εὐαγγέλιον Ἀληθείας
Valentinus, c. 100–160 AD

Introduction

The Gospel of Truth is one of the most literary and philosophically sophisticated texts in the Nag Hammadi library. Church father Irenaeus attributes a “Gospel of Truth” to the Valentinian school, and many scholars identify this text with that reference, possibly written by Valentinus himself in the mid-second century. If so, it is the only surviving work by the most influential Gnostic theologian—a man who nearly became Bishop of Rome.

Unlike the cosmogonic myths of the Apocryphon of John, the Gospel of Truth operates at a high level of abstraction. Its central argument is that the material world originated from ignorance of the Father, and that salvation consists in the Father being known. There is no demiurge here, no archons, no dramatic fall of Sophia. There is only a single elegant thesis: ignorance produces anguish and terror, which solidify into matter; knowledge dissolves matter back into the Father’s fullness.

§Ignorance and the Origin of the World (16.31–17.36) · Nag Hammadi Codex I, tractate 3, plates 16–17
Manuscript
Coptic

ἐπεὶ τὸ ὑστέρημα ἐγένετο ἐπεὶ ὁ πατὴρ οὐκ ἐγινώσκετο · ὅταν οὖν ὁ πατὴρ γινώσκηται · τὸ ὑστέρημα οὐκέτι ἔσται

English

Since the deficiency came about because the Father was not known, therefore when the Father is known, from that moment on the deficiency will no longer exist. As in the case of a person’s ignorance—when he comes to have knowledge, his ignorance vanishes of itself; as darkness vanishes when light appears—so also the deficiency vanishes in the perfection. So from that moment on, the form is no longer apparent, but it will vanish in the fusion of unity, for now their works lie scattered. In time, unity will perfect the spaces. It is within unity that each one will attain himself; within knowledge he will purify himself from multiplicity into unity, consuming matter within himself like fire, and darkness by light, death by life.

§Knowledge and Return to the Father (21.3–22.20) · Nag Hammadi Codex I, tractate 3, plates 21–22
Manuscript
Coptic

εὗρεν αὐτοὺς ἐν ἑαυτῷ · καὶ αὐτοὶ εὗρον αὐτὸν ἐν ἑαυτοῖς · τὸν ἀκατάληπτον · τὸν ἀνεννόητον · τὸν πατέρα · τὸν τέλειον

English

He discovered them in himself, and they discovered him in themselves—the incomprehensible, inconceivable one, the Father, the perfect one, the one who made all things. Within him are all things, and all things have need of him. Although he retained their perfection within himself, which he did not give to the all, the Father was not jealous. What jealousy indeed could there be between himself and his members? For if the aeon had thus received their perfection, they could not have come to the Father. He retains within himself their perfection, granting it to them as a return to him, and a knowledge that is uniquely his. It is he who fashioned the all, and the all is within him, and the all had need of him.

As in the case of a person whom some do not know: he wishes them to know him and to love him—for what did the all have need of, if not the knowledge of the Father?—so he became a guide, restful and at leisure. In the middle of a school he came and spoke the word as a teacher. Those who were wise in their own estimation came to test him. But he confounded them, because they were foolish. They hated him because they were not truly wise.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ignorance and the Origin of the World: The formula “since the deficiency came about because the Father was not known, from the moment the Father is known, the deficiency will no longer exist” is the most concise statement of Valentinian soteriology in any surviving text. Salvation is not a moral achievement, not a sacrificial transaction, not even a gift of grace in the Pauline sense. It is an epistemological event: the Father becomes known, and ignorance—together with everything ignorance produced, including the material world itself—ceases to exist. The metaphors of fire consuming matter and light consuming darkness are not metaphors for destruction but for reabsorption. Matter returns to the fullness from which it fell.
  2. §Knowledge and Return to the Father: The mutual discovery—“he discovered them in himself, and they discovered him in themselves”—is the most intimate statement of the God-soul relationship in Gnostic literature. It echoes the Gospel of Thomas Saying 3 (“when you come to know yourselves, then you will be known”) but places it within a Valentinian framework of emanation and return. The image of the Father as a teacher who enters “the middle of a school” is striking for its ordinariness—a reminder that Valentinus, unlike many Gnostic teachers, did not rely on elaborate mythology but on philosophical argument and parable.
