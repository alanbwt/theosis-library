Introduction

Livy’s Ab Urbe Condita opens with the arrival of Aeneas in Italy and the legendary founding of Rome. The preface establishes Livy’s purpose: to trace the moral rise and decline of the Roman people from their divine origins to the present day.

§Praefatio 1–9 · Livy, Ab Urbe Condita, Praefatio 1–9 (Ogilvie, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Facturusne operae pretium sim si a primordio urbis res populi Romani perscripserim nec satis scio nec, si sciam, dicere ausim.

English

Whether the task I have undertaken of writing a complete history of the Roman people from the very beginning of its existence will reward me for the labor spent on it, I neither know nor would I venture to say. The subjects to which I would ask each of my readers to devote his earnest attention are these: what life and morals were, through what men and by what policies the empire was established and enlarged—then let him note how, with the gradual relaxation of discipline, morals first gave way.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Praefatio: Livy’s moral framework—that Rome rose through virtue and declined through corruption—became the standard model for understanding Roman history. Augustine would later repurpose this narrative in De Civitate Dei, arguing that Rome’s true decline was spiritual rather than political.
