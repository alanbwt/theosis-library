Meditations II: Impermanence

        
Meditations II: Impermanence

        
Marcus Aurelius, 121-180 AD

        
Codex Vaticanus Graecus 1950

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
Book 2 of the Meditations was written, as Marcus notes at the end, 'among the Quadi at the Granua' — meaning during his military campaign on the Danube frontier. It is full of his most famous reflections: the morning meditation on dealing with difficult people, the urgency of using time wisely, the equality of all lives in the face of death, and the definition of philosophy as guarding the inner divinity. The text is in Greek, even though Marcus was Roman emperor — Greek was the language of philosophy.

            §2.1 · Med. 2.1
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Ἕωθεν προλέγειν ἑαυτῷ· συντεύξομαι περιέργῳ, ἀχαρίστῳ, ὑβριστῇ, δολερῷ, βασκάνῳ, ἀκοινωνήτῳ.

          


          

            
English

            
Begin the morning by saying to yourself: I shall meet today inquisitive, ungrateful, violent, treacherous, envious, unsocial people. They are like this because they cannot tell good from evil. But I, having seen the nature of the good — that it is beautiful, and the nature of the bad — that it is ugly, and the nature of the wrongdoer himself — that he is akin to me, not through blood or seed, but a sharer in the same intelligence and the same portion of divinity — I cannot be harmed by any of them. None can involve me in what is base. Nor can I be angry with my kinsman or hate him. We were born to work together, like feet, hands, eyelids, like the rows of upper and lower teeth. To work against one another is contrary to nature.

          

        

        
        


          

            §2.4 · Med. 2.4
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Μέμνησο ἀπὸ πόσου ταῦτα ἀναβάλλῃ καὶ ὁποσάκις προθεσμίας λαβὼν παρὰ τῶν θεῶν οὐ χρᾷ αὐταῖς.

          


          

            
English

            
Remember how long you've been putting this off, how many times the gods have given you another period of grace and you have not used it. The time has come now to recognize the universe of which you are a part, and the controlling power of which you are an offshoot, and that there is a limit to the time given you. If you do not use this time to clear away the clouds from your mind, it will go and you will go, and the opportunity will not return.

          

        

        
        


          

            §2.5 · Med. 2.5
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Πάσης ὥρας φρόντιζε στιβαρῶς ὡς Ῥωμαῖος καὶ ἄρρην τὸ ἐν χερσὶ μετὰ τῆς ἀκριβοῦς καὶ ἀπλάστου σεμνότητος.

          


          

            
English

            
Every hour, focus your mind, with steadfast attention, like a Roman and a man, on doing what is in front of you with precise and unaffected dignity, and with affection, freedom, and justice — and give yourself respite from all other thoughts. You will give yourself this if you perform every action of your life as if it were your last, free from all randomness, from all passionate aversion to the dictates of reason, from hypocrisy, self-love, and dissatisfaction with your portion. You see how few are the things which one needs to grasp in order to live a smooth and reverent life. The gods will require nothing more from him who keeps these things.

          

        

        
        


          

            §2.11 · Med. 2.11
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Ὡς ἤδη δυνατοῦ ὄντος ἐξιέναι σου τοῦ βίου, οὕτως ἕκαστα ποιεῖν καὶ λέγειν καὶ διανοεῖσθαι.

          


          

            
English

            
Act in every way, speak in every way, think in every way, as one who is ready to depart from life right now. To leave the company of men, if there are gods, is nothing terrible — for the gods will not involve you in evil. If, however, there are no gods, or if they have no concern for human affairs, what is life to me in a world without gods or without providence? But there are gods, and they do care for human things; and they have put it wholly in a man's power not to fall into real evils.

          

        

        
        


          

            §2.14 · Med. 2.14
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Κἂν τρισχίλια ἔτη βιώσεσθαι μέλλῃς καὶ τοσαυτάκις μύρια, ὅμως μέμνησο, ὅτι οὐδεὶς ἄλλον ἀποβάλλει βίον, ἢ τοῦτον ὃν ζῇ, οὐδὲ ἄλλον ζῇ ἢ ὃν ἀποβάλλει.

          


          

            
English

            
Even if you were going to live three thousand years, or thirty thousand, remember that no one loses any other life than the one he is living, nor lives any other than the one he is losing. The longest and the shortest amount to the same thing. For the present is the same for all, though that which perishes is not the same; and so what is lost appears to be a mere moment. No one can lose either the past or the future — for how can one be deprived of what one does not have? Two things, therefore, must be borne in mind. The first, that all things from eternity are of like form and recurring in cycles, and that it makes no difference whether one watches the same things for a hundred years or for two hundred or for an infinite period. The second, that the longest-lived and the shortest-lived lose the same thing. For the present is the only thing of which a man can be deprived, since this is the only thing he has.

          

        

        
        


          

            §2.17 · Med. 2.17
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Τοῦ ἀνθρωπίνου βίου ὁ μὲν χρόνος στιγμή, ἡ δὲ οὐσία ῥέουσα, ἡ δὲ αἴσθησις ἀμυδρά.

          


          

            
English

            
Of human life: time is a point, substance is in flux, sensation is dim, the composition of the whole body is rapid in decay, the soul is a whirligig, fortune is hard to predict, fame undiscerning. In a word, all that belongs to the body is a stream, and what belongs to the soul is a dream and a vapor; life is warfare and a stranger's sojourn, and after-fame is oblivion. What then can guide a man? One thing only: philosophy. And philosophy consists in keeping the inner divinity unspoiled and free from harm — superior to pleasures and pains, doing nothing without purpose, never being false, never being hypocritical, not depending on another whether he does or does not do something. And awaiting death with a cheerful mind, as being nothing else than a dissolution of the elements of which every living being is composed.
