Fragments 14-16: Gods Made in Our Image

Xenophanes, c. 570-475 BCE

Introduction

Xenophanes of Colophon (c. 570–475 BCE) launched the first sustained philosophical critique of anthropomorphic religion in the Western tradition. Writing a century before Plato, he observed that every people creates gods in its own image: the Ethiopians make their gods dark-skinned, the Thracians make theirs red-haired and blue-eyed. The logical conclusion is devastating: if cattle and horses had hands, they would draw gods that looked like cattle and horses. The gods of popular religion are projections of human nature, not revelations of divine truth.

For the theosis question, Xenophanes cuts both ways. On one hand, his critique undermines the popular understanding of human-divine kinship: if the gods are merely human inventions, then “becoming like the gods” is circular narcissism. On the other hand, Xenophanes also posited a “one greatest god,” unlike mortals in form and thought, who “shakes all things by the thought of his mind.” This god is so radically other that any human attempt to approach or resemble it is inherently anthropomorphic—and therefore false.

§Fragment 14 · Xenophanes, Fragment 14 (DK 21 B14)
Manuscript
Greek

ἀλλ’ οἱ βροτοὶ δοκέουσι γεννᾶσθαι θεούς, τὴν σφετέρην δ’ ἐσθῆτα τ’ ἔχειν φωνήν τε δέμας τε.

English

But mortals suppose that gods are born, and that they wear clothes like their own, and have a voice and a body.

§Fragment 15 · Xenophanes, Fragment 15 (DK 21 B15)
Manuscript
Greek

ἀλλ’ εἰ χεῖρας ἔχον βόες ἵπποι τ’ ἠδὲ λέοντες, ἢ γράψαι χείρεσσι καὶ ἔργα τελεῖν ὅπερ ἄνδρες, ἵπποι μέν θ’ ἵπποισι βόες δέ τε βουσὶν ὁμοίας καί κε θεῶν ἰδέας ἔγραφον καὶ σώματ’ ἐποίουν τοιαῦθ’ οἷόν περ καὐτοὶ δέμας εἶχον.

English

But if cattle and horses and lions had hands, or could draw with their hands and produce works of art as men do, horses would draw the forms of gods like horses, and cattle like cattle, and they would make the gods’ bodies in the shape of their own.

§Fragment 16 · Xenophanes, Fragment 16 (DK 21 B16)
Manuscript
Greek

Αἰθίοπές τε θεοὺς σφετέρους σιμούς τε μέλανάς τε, Θρῇκές τε γλαυκούς τε καὶ πυρρούς.

English

The Ethiopians say that their gods are snub-nosed and dark, the Thracians that theirs have blue eyes and red hair.

§Fragment 23 · Xenophanes, Fragment 23 (DK 21 B23)
Manuscript
Greek

εἷς θεός, ἔν τε θεοῖσι καὶ ἀνθρώποισι μέγιστος, οὔτι δέμας θνητοῖσιν ὁμοίιος οὐδὲ νόημα.

English

One god, greatest among gods and men, in no way similar to mortals either in body or in thought.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragment 15: This is one of the most famous arguments in the history of philosophy of religion. Feuerbach, Freud, and Durkheim all developed elaborate theories of religion as projection, but Xenophanes stated the essential insight twenty-five centuries earlier. The argument does not prove that God does not exist—it proves that human representations of God are unreliable. This is compatible with both atheism and negative theology.
  2. §Fragment 23: Xenophanes’s “one god” (heis theos) is radically unlike mortals “in body or in thought” (demas and noema). This is not merely a denial of physical resemblance but of cognitive resemblance—God does not think the way we think. The implications for theosis are severe: if the divine mind is utterly unlike the human mind, then the mystical traditions that promise union of the human intellect with the divine intellect are based on a false analogy. This fragment anticipates both the Islamic tanzih tradition and the Maimonidean via negativa.
