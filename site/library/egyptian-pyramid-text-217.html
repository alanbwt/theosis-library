Pyramid Text Utterance 217: The King Becomes a Star

Anonymous (Egyptian), c. 1550 BCE

Introduction

Pyramid Text Utterance 217 is among the oldest religious texts in human history, carved into the walls of the pyramid of Unas at Saqqara around 2350 BCE. It describes the dead pharaoh’s ascent to the sky, where he takes his place among the imperishable stars and is recognised as a god among gods. This is not metaphor or hope. It is state theology rendered as cosmic fact: the king is deified, and the text is the instrument of that deification.

The Egyptian hieroglyphic text presents deification as a journey—the king crosses the sky, is ferried over the celestial waters, and is welcomed by the gods who recognise him as one of their own. The theological claim is staggering in its directness: a human being becomes a star, becomes Osiris, sits on the throne of the gods. This is theosis at its most ancient and literal, predating the Greek philosophical tradition by two millennia. The Church Fathers who argued that humans could “become God” were making a claim that Egypt had carved in stone a thousand years before Moses.

English

Translator's Notes

  1. §§154–155: The systematic identification of the king’s body parts with those of Atum—the creator god—is not metaphorical. In Egyptian theology, the king literally becomes the god through ritual identification. Each limb is named and claimed. This is the logic of deification at its most concrete: not that the soul ascends to heaven while the body rots, but that the body itself is transfigured, limb by limb, into divine substance. The later Christian language of the resurrection body (“it is sown a natural body, it is raised a spiritual body,” 1 Corinthians 15:44) is tame by comparison.
  2. §§156–157: The king “as a star which goes across the sea” refers to the circumpolar stars—the imperishable ones (ikhmw-sk) that never set below the horizon. To become a star is to achieve the Egyptian definition of immortality: permanent visibility in the sky. The text inscribed in the pyramid is not a record of what will happen; it is a performative utterance that makes it happen. The carving of the words is the deification.
