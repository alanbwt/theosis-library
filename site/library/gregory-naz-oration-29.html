Third Theological Oration 19: On the Son and Theosis

Λόγος Θεολογικός Γ'
Gregory of Nazianzus, c. 329–390 AD

Introduction

Gregory of Nazianzus delivered his five Theological Orations in Constantinople in 380 CE, the year before the Council of Constantinople would affirm the Nicene faith as imperial orthodoxy. Oration 29, the Third Theological Oration, defends the full divinity of the Son against the Eunomians, who argued that the Son was unlike (anomoios) the Father in essence.

In section 19, Gregory turns the soteriological argument: if the Son is not fully God, then humanity has not been fully united to God in the Incarnation, and theosis is impossible. The logic is the same as Athanasius’s famous principle — what is not assumed is not healed — but Gregory gives it sharper rhetorical form. Christ must be fully divine for human divinization to be real.

Greek

Θνητός, αλλα Θεός· εκ γένους Δαβίδ, αλλα πλάστης Αδάμ· σαρκοφόρος, αλλα και ασώματος· μητρί μεν εγνωσθη, παρθένω δέ· εδέθη, αλλα και έλυσεν· επράθη, και λίαν ευτελώς, τριάκοντα αργυρίων· αλλα λυτρουται κόσμον, και μεγάλης τιμής, του ιδίου αίματος.

English

He was mortal, but God. He was of the line of David, but the maker of Adam. He was clothed in flesh, but free from the things of the flesh. He had a mother, but a virgin mother. He was bound, yet he loosed. He was sold, and very cheaply — for thirty pieces of silver — yet he redeemed the world, and at great cost, for the price was his own blood. As a sheep he was led to the slaughter, but he is the Shepherd of Israel and now of the whole world. As a lamb he was silent, yet he is the Word, proclaimed by the voice of one crying in the wilderness. He was bruised and wounded, but he heals every disease and every infirmity. He was lifted up and nailed to the tree, but by the tree of life he restores us. He died, but he gives life, and by his death destroys death. He was buried, but he rose again. He descended into Hades, but he brought up souls from there. He ascended to heaven, and shall come again to judge the living and the dead. If the one set of facts makes you stumble, the other set should lift you up.

Translator's Notes

  1. §29.19: Gregory’s rhetorical method is antithetical parallelism: each pair of clauses juxtaposes the human and the divine in Christ. The implied argument for theosis is structural. If Christ holds together mortality and divinity, humanity and God-making, suffering and healing, then the Incarnation is itself the bridge. Gregory does not state the theosis principle abstractly here; he performs it. Every antithesis enacts the union of human and divine that is theosis. The closing challenge — “if the one set of facts makes you stumble, the other set should lift you up” — is addressed to the Eunomians who emphasize Christ’s human limitations to deny his divinity.
