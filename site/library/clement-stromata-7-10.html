Stromata VII.10: The True Gnostic Has Become God

Clement of Alexandria, c. 150–215 AD

Introduction

Clement of Alexandria (c. 150–215) stands at the intersection of Greek philosophy and Christian theology. His Stromata (“Miscellanies”) is a sprawling, deliberately unsystematic work that weaves together Platonic, Stoic, and biblical thought into a vision of the Christian life as a progressive ascent toward God. Clement writes for educated Greek converts who need to see that the faith is not anti-intellectual but is, in fact, the fulfillment of philosophy.

Book VII, Chapter 10 contains Clement’s most provocative claim about the end of this ascent. The “true gnostic”—Clement’s term for the mature Christian who has moved beyond mere faith to genuine knowledge of God—has “already become god.” The language is deliberately bold. Clement is reclaiming the term gnosis from the heterodox Gnostic movements by arguing that authentic knowledge of God transforms the knower into the image of what is known.

§VII.10 · Stromata VII.10.56 (ed. Stählin)
Manuscript
Greek

Ἀληθῶς γνωστικὸς θεὸς ἤδη γέγονεν, ἐξομοιωθεὶς τῷ θεῷ καθ᾽ ὅσον οἷόν τε τῇ ἀνθρωπίνῃ φύσει.

English

The true gnostic has already become god, being assimilated to God as far as is possible for human nature. For likeness to God is the end of contemplation, and it is through contemplation that the soul becomes divine.

Translator's Notes

  1. §VII.10: Clement’s use of the term gnostikos (γνωστικός) is deliberately polemical. By the late second century, various Gnostic schools claimed that gnosis was the exclusive possession of a spiritual elite who possessed a divine spark. Clement counters by defining the true gnostic as any Christian who has progressed from faith (pistis) to knowledge (gnosis) through moral discipline and contemplation. The phrase “already become god” (θεὸς ἤδη γέγονεν) echoes Plato’s language in the Theaetetus (176b) about “becoming like God as far as possible,” but Clement’s claim is stronger: not merely likeness but actual transformation.
