Titus 3:4-7: Washing of Regeneration

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Titus 3:4–7 contains the most concentrated baptismal theology in the Pauline corpus. The passage describes salvation as “the washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit” (loutron palingenesias kai anakainōseōs pneumatos hagiou). The term palingenesia (παλιγγενεσία) means literally “birth again” or “re-genesis”—a new beginning that recapitulates the original creation. In Stoic philosophy, the word described the cosmic renewal after the world-conflagration; here Paul applies it to the individual believer.

The passage insists that this transformation comes not from human works of righteousness but from divine mercy, through the Spirit “poured out richly” upon believers through Jesus Christ. The result is that the reborn are made “heirs according to the hope of eternal life.” The logic is thoroughly theotic: God’s own Spirit is poured into human beings, regenerating them from within, making them heirs of the divine life they now carry.

English

But when the kindness and love for humanity (philanthrōpia, φιλανθρωπία) of God our Savior appeared, he saved us, not from works done in righteousness that we had performed, but according to his mercy, through the washing of regeneration (palingenesia, παλιγγενεσία) and renewal of the Holy Spirit, which he poured out upon us richly through Jesus Christ our Savior, so that, having been justified by his grace, we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:4: The term philanthrōpia (φιλανθρωπία), “love for humanity,” is a distinctively Hellenistic term. In classical usage it described the benevolence of rulers toward their subjects. Applied to God, it transforms the concept: divine philanthropy is not patronage from above but the Creator’s intimate love for the creature. The Greek Fathers, especially John Chrysostom, made this term central to their understanding of the Incarnation as an act of divine love.
  2. §3:5: The word palingenesia (παλιγγενεσία) appears only twice in the New Testament: here and in Matthew 19:28, where Jesus uses it to describe the cosmic renewal at the end of the age. The Stoics used the term for the cyclical rebirth of the cosmos after its destruction by fire. Paul’s application to individual baptism implies that what happens in the baptismal font is a microcosm of cosmic regeneration—each believer’s rebirth participates in the renewal of all things.
