Conference 10: Continuous Prayer

John Cassian, c. 360-435 AD

Introduction

John Cassian (c. 360–435) was the critical link between Eastern and Western monasticism. Born in the Roman province of Scythia Minor (modern Romania), he trained in the monasteries of Egypt, then moved to Marseilles where he founded two monasteries and composed his two great works: the Institutes and the Conferences. The Conferences purport to record the teachings of the great Egyptian desert fathers, though they are clearly shaped by Cassian’s own theological vision.

Conference 10, attributed to Abba Isaac, concerns prayer. Its central teaching is that continuous prayer—the unceasing repetition of a single verse (“O God, make speed to save me; O Lord, make haste to help me,” Psalm 69:2)—can transform the monk’s entire being, so that prayer is no longer an activity but a state. The monk who achieves this state is, in Cassian’s language, united with God through the prayer itself. This teaching would profoundly influence the later Hesychast tradition of the Jesus Prayer and, through Benedict of Nursia, the entire Western monastic tradition.

§10.6 · CSEL 13, p. 289
Manuscript
Latin

Finis ergo monachi et perfectionis culmen est iugis et inperturbata orationis perseverantia.

English

And so the end of the monk and the perfection of the heart is unceasing and unbroken perseverance in prayer. And insofar as it is granted to human frailty, it strives toward immovable tranquillity of mind and perpetual purity, for the sake of which we seek all bodily labours and all contrition of heart.

§10.7 · CSEL 13, p. 291
Manuscript
Latin

Quicquid ergo ad hunc finem id est ad orationem iugem perducere potest omni virtute sectandum est.

English

Therefore whatever can lead to this end—that is, to unceasing prayer—must be pursued with all our power. This is the reason for all fasting, all vigils, all meditation on Scripture, all stripping away of possessions and renunciation of the world: so that the heart may be purified and made capable of receiving the presence of God.

§10.10 · CSEL 13, p. 296
Manuscript
Latin

Non inmerito ergo hic versiculus de toto scripturarum corpore tamquam formula est decerptus.

English

And so this verse has rightly been selected from the whole body of Scripture as a formula. For it takes up all the emotions that can arise in human nature and can be adapted to every condition and every attack. It contains the invocation of God against every danger. It contains the humility of a pious confession. It contains the watchfulness of concern and of continual fear.

§10.11 · CSEL 13, p. 298
Manuscript
Latin

Hic versiculus in adversis orandus est ut erui mereamur, in prosperis ut conservemur et non extollamur.

English

This verse should be poured out in unceasing prayer, in adversity that we may be delivered, in prosperity that we may be preserved and not puffed up. Let the meditation of this verse be turned over unceasingly in your breast. Let it not leave you when you are doing any work, when you are going on a journey, when you are eating. Falling asleep, let this verse be in your thoughts; waking up, let it meet you.

§10.14 · CSEL 13, p. 303

English

When the soul has been established in this state of tranquillity and has been freed from the meshes of all carnal desires, and the purpose of the heart has been firmly fixed on the one supreme good, then the monk will fulfil that apostolic command: “Pray without ceasing.” For the soul that has learned to pray in this manner will at last begin to pray in reality, when every intention and every thought and every word is a prayer to God.

Then shall the prayer of the Lord be fulfilled in us: “That they all may be one, as you, Father, are in me and I in you, that they also may be one in us.” And this will happen when that perfect love of God, by which he first loved us, passes into the affection of our heart, and that prayer of our Lord shall be fulfilled: that the love with which he loved us may be in us, and we in him.

Translator's Notes

  1. §10.6: Cassian’s identification of the “end of the monk” with unceasing prayer, and his subordination of all ascetic practices (fasting, vigils, poverty) to this single goal, had an enormous influence on Western monasticism. Benedict’s Rule echoes this priority, and it is through Cassian that the Eastern monastic ideal of continuous prayer entered the Western tradition.
  2. §10.10: The single verse (“O God, make speed to save me; O Lord, make haste to help me”—Deus in adiutorium meum intende; Domine ad adiuvandum me festina) functions in Cassian’s system exactly as the Jesus Prayer (“Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me”) functions in the later Hesychast tradition: a short formula repeated continuously until it becomes the permanent background of consciousness. Cassian may represent an earlier form of the same practice.
  3. §10.15: The quotation from John 17:21 (“that they all may be one, as you, Father, are in me and I in you”) is the locus classicus for theosis through prayer. Cassian reads the High Priestly Prayer as a promise that human beings can participate in the same unity that exists between Father and Son. This is not merely moral agreement or emotional closeness but ontological participation in the divine life.
