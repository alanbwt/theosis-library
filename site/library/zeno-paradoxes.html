The Paradoxes of Motion

The Paradoxes of Motion
Zeno of Elea, c. 490-430 BCE
Aristotle, Physics VI.9 239b

Introduction

Zeno of Elea devised his paradoxes to defend Parmenides' doctrine that reality is one and changeless. By showing that the concept of motion leads to logical contradictions, Zeno forced subsequent philosophers to grapple with infinity, continuity, and the relationship between mathematics and physical reality. These paradoxes remained unresolved until the development of calculus two millennia later.

§1 · Aristotle, Physics 239b14
Greek

ἐν δὲ τῷ Ἀχιλλεῖ ὅτι τὸ βραδύτατον οὐδέποτε καταληφθήσεται θέον ὑπὸ τοῦ ταχίστου· ἔμπροσθεν γὰρ ἀναγκαῖον ἐλθεῖν τὸ διῶκον ὅθεν ὥρμησε τὸ φεῦγον.

English

In the Achilles, the slowest runner will never be overtaken by the swiftest; for the pursuer must first reach the point from which the pursued started, so that the slower must always be some distance ahead.

§2 · Aristotle, Physics 239b30
Greek

ἡ δὲ λεγομένη Ζήνωνος [ἀπόδειξις] ψευδῶς ἀξιοῖ μὴ ἐνδέχεσθαι τὰ ἄπειρα διελθεῖν ἢ ἅψασθαι τῶν ἀπείρων καθ᾿ ἕκαστον ἐν πεπερασμένῳ χρόνῳ.

English

The so-called proof of Zeno falsely assumes that it is impossible to traverse an infinite number of things or to touch each of an infinite number in a finite time.

§3 · Aristotle, Physics 239b5
Greek

εἰ γὰρ αἰεί, φησίν, ἠρεμεῖ πᾶν ὅταν ᾖ κατὰ τὸ ἴσον, ἔστι δ᾿ αἰεὶ τὸ φερόμενον ἐν τῷ νῦν, ἀκίνητον τὴν φερομένην εἶναι ὀϊστόν.

English

If everything, he says, is at rest whenever it occupies a space equal to itself, and if the moving thing is always in the now, then the flying arrow is motionless.
