Introduction

Dhul-Nun al-Misri (d. 859) was an Egyptian Sufi credited with introducing ma'rifa (gnosis, direct experiential knowledge of God) into Islamic mysticism. His sayings survive in later Sufi biographical anthologies.

Translation

§On Gnosis · Attributed in al-Qushayri's Risala and Attar's Tadhkirat al-Awliya

The gnostic (arif) grows ever more humble the closer he draws to God. Three kinds of knowledge exist: knowledge of God's oneness, which belongs to all believers; knowledge of proof and demonstration, which belongs to the wise and the learned; and knowledge of the attributes of divine unity, which belongs to the saints (awliya) of God—those who behold God with their hearts, so that what is hidden from others is manifest to them.

Translator's Notes

  1. §On Gnosis: Dhul-Nun's three-tiered epistemology—common faith, rational demonstration, and direct vision—parallels the Neoplatonic hierarchy of opinion, discursive reason, and noetic intuition. The claim that saints "behold God with their hearts" became the experiential basis of the Sufi tradition and was sharply debated by rationalist theologians.
