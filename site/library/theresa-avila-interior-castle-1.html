Introduction

Teresa of Avila's Interior Castle (1577) maps the soul's journey to God through seven concentric "mansions," from the outer courtyard of initial prayer to the innermost chamber of spiritual marriage. It is the most systematic work of mystical psychology in the Christian tradition.

Translation

§First Mansions, Chapter 1 · Interior Castle, First Mansions, Chapter 1

I began to think of the soul as if it were a castle made of a single diamond or of very clear crystal, in which there are many rooms, just as in heaven there are many mansions. If we consider it carefully, the soul of the just person is nothing less than a paradise in which, as God tells us, He takes His delight. What do you think that room must be like, in which a King so powerful, so wise, so pure, so full of all good things, takes His delight? Nothing can be compared to the great beauty and capacity of a soul. Our intellects, however sharp, can hardly comprehend it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §First Mansions: Teresa's castle metaphor draws on John 14:2 ("In my Father's house are many mansions") but applies it to the interior landscape of the soul rather than the afterlife. The seven mansions correspond to progressive stages of prayer: vocal prayer, meditation, the prayer of quiet, the prayer of union, and finally spiritual marriage. Teresa insisted that the mystical life was available to all Christians, not just cloistered religious.
