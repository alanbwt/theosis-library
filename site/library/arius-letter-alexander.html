Arius: The Son Is a Creature

Arius, c. 256-336 AD

Introduction

Arius of Alexandria (c. 256–336) was a presbyter whose teaching provoked the most consequential theological crisis in Christian history. In his letter to Alexander, Bishop of Alexandria, Arius states his position with brutal clarity: the Son is a creature. He had a beginning. “There was when he was not.” The Father alone is unbegotten, alone eternal, alone without origin. The Son, however exalted, is a made thing—the first and greatest of all created beings, but created nonetheless.

The implications for theosis are devastating. If Christ is not fully God, then the entire logic of deification collapses. Athanasius would later argue that only God can make humans divine—a creature cannot bridge the gap between creation and the Creator. The Council of Nicaea (325) condemned Arius’s position, but the debate raged for another half-century. This letter preserves the Arian argument in Arius’s own words, before the councils rewrote the terms of the debate.

§1 · Arius, Letter to Alexander of Alexandria (c. 320)
Manuscript
Greek

ἕνα οἶδαμεν θεόν, μόνον ἀγέννητον, μόνον ἀΐδιον, μόνον ἄναρχον, μόνον ἀληθινόν, μόνον ἀθανασίαν ἔχοντα, μόνον σοφόν, μόνον ἀγαθόν, μόνον δυνάστην, μόνον κριτὴν πάντων.

English

We acknowledge one God, alone unbegotten, alone eternal, alone without beginning, alone true, alone possessing immortality, alone wise, alone good, alone sovereign, alone judge of all.

§2 · Arius, Letter to Alexander of Alexandria (c. 320)
Manuscript
Greek

πρὸ χρόνων αἰωνίων γεννήσαντα υἱὸν μονογενῆ, δι’ οὗ τοὺς αἰῶνας καὶ τὰ πάντα πεποίηκεν.

English

He begot an only-begotten Son before eternal times, through whom he made the ages and all things. He begot him not in appearance but in truth, bringing him into existence by his own will, unchangeable and unalterable.

§3 · Arius, Letter to Alexander of Alexandria (c. 320)
Manuscript
Greek

κτίσμα τοῦ θεοῦ τέλειον, ἀλλ’ οὐχ ὡς ἓν τῶν κτισμάτων· γέννημα, ἀλλ’ οὐχ ὡς ἓν τῶν γεγεννημένων.

English

A perfect creature of God, but not as one of the creatures; an offspring, but not as one of the things begotten. He is not a part of the Unbegotten, nor is he made from any pre-existing substance.

§4 · Arius, Letter to Alexander of Alexandria (c. 320)
Manuscript
Greek

θελήματι τοῦ πατρὸς πρὸ χρόνων καὶ πρὸ αἰώνων υὑποστάς.

English

He was brought into being by the will of the Father before times and ages, and received life and being from the Father. The Father gave him his glories. The Son does not possess being together with the Father as something unbegotten. God was alone, and the Word was not yet, nor the Wisdom.

§5 · Arius, Letter to Alexander of Alexandria (c. 320)
Manuscript
Greek

τρεῖς εἰσὶν ὑποστάσεις… ἦν ποτε ὅτε οὐκ ἦν ὁ υἱός.

English

And so there are three hypostases. God, being the cause of all things, is absolutely alone, without beginning. But the Son, begotten timelessly by the Father, created and established before the ages, did not exist before he was begotten. He is not eternal or co-eternal or co-unbegotten with the Father. There was when the Son was not.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1: The repeated monon (μόνον, “alone”) is the rhetorical key to the entire letter. Every divine attribute is reserved exclusively for the Father. The Son can participate in none of them by nature—only by gift. This is the foundation of the Arian position.
  2. §3: The word ktisma (κτίσμα, “creature”) is the term that provoked the crisis. Arius insists the Son is a “perfect creature”—supreme among created things, but created nonetheless. Athanasius would later argue that this makes salvation impossible: a creature cannot deify other creatures.
  3. §5: The phrase ἦν ποτε ὅτε οὐκ ἦν (“there was when he was not”) became the slogan of Arianism and was specifically condemned at Nicaea. Arius avoids saying “there was a time when he was not,” since the Son was created before time itself. The formulation is philosophically careful: he concedes the Son’s pre-temporal existence while denying his co-eternity with the Father.
