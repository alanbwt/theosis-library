Thanksgiving Hymns (1QH): Purified from Sin

Anonymous (Enochic), c. 3rd c. BCE

Introduction

The Thanksgiving Hymns (Hodayot, 1QH) are a collection of devotional poems from Qumran, some likely composed by the sect’s founder, the Teacher of Righteousness. Column 11.19–36 contains one of the most theologically dense passages in the scrolls: the speaker confesses his utter unworthiness as a creature of clay and dust, then marvels that God has nonetheless purified him, cleansed him of sin, and raised him into the company of the angels. The language oscillates between radical self-abasement and ecstatic transformation.

This passage is arguably the closest thing in pre-Christian Judaism to the later patristic doctrine of theosis. The speaker does not merely ask for forgiveness—he claims to have been purified and placed among the “heavenly beings.” The tension between human nothingness and divine elevation that runs through these hymns will reappear, in different theological idioms, in Paul, the Desert Fathers, and the Byzantine mystics.

§11.19–21 · 1QH Column XI, lines 19–21

אודכה אדוני כי פדיתה נפשי משחת

English

I thank you, O Lord, for you have redeemed my soul from the pit, and from the Sheol of Abaddon you have raised me up to everlasting height. I walk on limitless level ground, and I know there is hope for the one whom you have shaped from dust for the eternal council.

§11.21–24 · 1QH Column XI, lines 21–24
Manuscript
Hebrew

ותטהר רוח נעוה מפשע רב להתיצב במעמד עם צבא קדושים

English

You have cleansed a perverse spirit of great sin, that it may stand in the station with the host of the holy ones, and enter into community with the congregation of the sons of heaven. You have cast for man an eternal lot with the spirits of knowledge, to praise your name in the community of jubilation.

§11.24–27 · 1QH Column XI, lines 24–27
Manuscript
Hebrew

ואני יצר החמר מה אני מגובל במים

English

And I, a creature of clay, what am I? Kneaded with water, what is my worth? What strength have I? For I stood in the realm of wickedness, and with the wretched was my lot. The soul of the poor one sojourned amid great tumult, and calamitous disasters accompanied my steps.

To raise the worm of the dead from the dust, to the council of your truth, and from a perverse spirit to your understanding; that he might take his stand before you with the everlasting host and the spirits of knowledge, to be renewed with all that is and shall be, and to rejoice together with those who know in a common jubilation.

Translator's Notes

  1. §11.21–24: The claim that a purified human can “stand in the station with the host of the holy ones” and “enter into community with the congregation of the sons of heaven” is the most explicit statement of angelic fellowship in the Dead Sea Scrolls. This is not a future eschatological hope but a present reality: the speaker has already been elevated. The theological parallel to Paul’s claim that Christians are already “seated with Christ in the heavenly places” (Ephesians 2:6) is striking.
  2. §11.33–36: The image of raising “the worm of the dead from the dust” combines the most extreme language of human lowliness (the speaker is not merely mortal but a worm among the dead) with the most exalted destiny (joining the eternal council of divine beings). This paradox—that the lowest creature can be raised to the highest station—is the essential structure of theosis theology in all its forms.
