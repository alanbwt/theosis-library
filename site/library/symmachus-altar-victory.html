Introduction

Quintus Aurelius Symmachus’s Relatio III (384 CE) is the most eloquent surviving plea for Roman pagan tolerance. As prefect of Rome, Symmachus petitioned Emperor Valentinian II to restore the Altar of Victory to the Senate, arguing that many paths lead to the same divine mystery.

§Relatio III.10 · Symmachus, Relatio III.10
Manuscript
Greek

Eadem spectamus astra, commune caelum est, idem nos mundus involvit. Quid interest qua quisque prudentia verum requirat? Uno itinere non potest perveniri ad tam grande secretum.

English

We gaze up at the same stars, the sky is common to us all, the same universe surrounds us. What difference does it make by what method each person seeks the truth? We cannot arrive at so great a mystery by one road alone.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Relatio III.10: Symmachus’s argument—“one cannot arrive at so great a mystery by one road alone” (uno itinere non potest perveniri ad tam grande secretum)—became the classic formulation of pagan pluralism. Ambrose’s rebuttal would argue that Christ is the one road, making the exchange a defining moment in the transition from pagan to Christian Rome.
