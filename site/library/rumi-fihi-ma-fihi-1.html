Introduction

Rumi’s Fihi Ma Fihi (“In It What Is In It,” c. 1270 CE) is a collection of his prose discourses, recorded by disciples. Where the Masnavi is structured poetry, these are spontaneous teachings on love, God, the spiritual path, and the nature of reality.

§Discourse 1 · Rumi, Fihi Ma Fihi
Manuscript
Greek

هر نبی و هر ولی را مسلکیست

English

Every prophet and every saint has a way, but all lead to God. All ways are really one. When people come to the sea, they bring different-shaped containers. One brings a barrel, another a jar, another a cup. But the sea is the same sea, and the water that fills each vessel is the same water. The difference is in the containers, not in the water.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Discourse 1: The sea-and-containers metaphor expresses Rumi’s universalist theology: all authentic spiritual paths lead to the same divine reality, differing only in the human vessels that carry the teaching. This perennialist position, while not universally accepted in Islamic orthodoxy, represents a major strand of Sufi thought that has profoundly influenced modern interfaith dialogue.
