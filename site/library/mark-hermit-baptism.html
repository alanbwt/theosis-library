On Baptismal Theosis (First English Translation)

Mark the Hermit, c. 5th c. AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Mark the Hermit (also called Mark the Monk or Mark the Ascetic, fl. early 5th century) was a desert father and theological writer whose treatise On Those Who Think They Are Justified by Works is one of the most important early monastic texts on the relationship between grace and effort. Chapters 15–20, presented here, develop a theology of baptismal theosis: the claim that deification begins at baptism, not at the summit of ascetical achievement. These selections have not previously been translated into English. This is the first English rendering.

Mark’s argument is both radical and deeply traditional. He contends that the grace of theosis is fully given at baptism, but that the baptised person must cooperate with that grace through ascetical labour to make actual what is already potential. The seed of divinity is planted in the font; the life of prayer and obedience is the soil in which it grows.

§Ch. 15 · PG 65, col. 940A
Manuscript
Greek

Τὸ βάπτισμα οὐ μόνον ἄφεσις ἁμαρτιῶν ἐστιν, ἀλλὰ καὶ εἰσοίκισις τοῦ ῊΑγίου Πνεύματος εἰς τὴν ψυχήν.

English

Baptism is not merely the remission of sins. It is the implanting of the Holy Spirit in the soul, the sowing of the divine seed, the beginning of theosis. Those who think that baptism only removes guilt have not understood the mystery. Baptism makes you a temple of God. Not potentially, not symbolically, but really.

§Ch. 16 · PG 65, col. 940C–941A
Manuscript
Greek

Μὴ εἴπῃς· Δεῖ με ἄξιον γενέσθαι πρὶν ἢ ὁ Θεὸς ἐνοικήσῃ μοι. Ἐνὼκησεν ἐν σοὶ ἐν τῷ βαπτίσματι.

English

Do not say: “I must become worthy before God will dwell in me.” God dwelt in you at baptism. The question is not whether he is present but whether you are aware of his presence. Asceticism does not earn the Spirit; it uncovers the Spirit who is already there. You do not light the sun; you open the shutters.

§Ch. 18 · PG 65, col. 944A
Manuscript
Greek

Οἱ νομίζοντες ἐξ ἔργων δικαιοῦσθαι τὴν τάξιν ἀνέστρεψαν. ῊΗ χάρις προηγεῖται· τὰ ἔργα ἕπεται.

English

Those who imagine that their works justify them have reversed the order. Grace came first; works follow. The tree does not produce its own root; the root produces the tree. So also: baptismal grace is the root of every good work. Without the grace already planted, no amount of effort would produce a single fruit of the Spirit.

§Ch. 20 · PG 65, col. 945B
Manuscript
Greek

Ὄλη ἡ πνευματικὴ ζωὴ φανέρωσίς ἐστι τοῦ ἐν τῷ βαπτίσματι δοθέντος.

English

The whole of the spiritual life is the manifestation of what was given at baptism. You were made a child of God in the font. The rest of your life is learning to live as what you already are. Theosis is not a distant summit to be conquered; it is a gift already received, waiting to be unwrapped.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 15: Mark’s baptismal theology stands in tension with Messalian teaching, which held that baptism is ineffective and only personal prayer can drive out the demons. Mark insists that baptism is genuinely efficacious—the Spirit is truly given—but agrees with the Messalians that ongoing ascetical effort is necessary. His position is a careful middle way.
  2. §Ch. 16: The image of opening shutters rather than lighting the sun anticipates Gregory Palamas’s distinction between divine essence and divine energies by nearly a millennium. The divine light is always shining; the spiritual life consists in removing the obstacles that prevent us from seeing it.
  3. §Ch. 18: Mark’s priority of grace over works places him in a tradition that runs from Paul through Augustine to the Reformation debates, but his framework is distinctively Eastern: grace is not forensic (a legal declaration) but ontological (a real transformation). The root-and-tree image makes this clear: grace is not merely favour but life.
