Introduction

Aeschylus’ Prometheus Bound dramatizes the punishment of the Titan who stole fire for humanity, chained to a rock by Zeus’s command. Prometheus’s defiance of tyrannical divine authority became the archetype of resistance and the cost of bringing knowledge to mortals.

§Lines 436–471 · Aeschylus, Prometheus Bound 436–471
Manuscript
Greek

ἀλλ’ ὡν βροτοῖς παθήματα ἀκούσατε

English

Listen to the miseries that beset mankind—how I found them witless and gave them the use of their wits. First of all, though they had eyes to see, they saw to no avail; they had ears but did not understand. I gave them fire, and from it they learned all the arts. And number, too, I invented for them, chief of all devices, and the combining of letters, and memory, the Muses’ mother.

Translator's Notes

  1. §442: Prometheus’s catalog of gifts to humanity—fire, number, writing, medicine, astronomy—constitutes a Greek theory of civilization’s origin. The Romantic poets (Shelley, Byron, Goethe) read Prometheus as a Christ-figure who suffers for humanity’s salvation through knowledge.
