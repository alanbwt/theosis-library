Introduction

The Epistle to Philemon is Paul’s shortest letter, a personal appeal on behalf of the runaway slave Onesimus. It tests whether the theological claim that “in Christ there is neither slave nor free” has practical consequences. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§1:8–12 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

διο πολλην εν χριστω παρρησιαν εχων επιτασσειν σοι

English

Accordingly, though I am bold enough in Christ to command you to do what is required, yet for love’s sake I prefer to appeal to you—I, Paul, an old man and now a prisoner also for Christ Jesus—I appeal to you for my child, Onesimus, whose father I became in my imprisonment. Formerly he was useless to you, but now he is indeed useful to you and to me. I am sending him back to you, sending my very heart.

§1:15–17 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ταχα γαρ δια τουτο εχωρισθη προς ωραν

English

For this perhaps is why he was parted from you for a while, that you might have him back forever, no longer as a bondservant but more than a bondservant, as a beloved brother—especially to me, but how much more to you, both in the flesh and in the Lord. So if you consider me your partner, receive him as you would receive me.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:16: Paul asks Philemon to receive Onesimus “no longer as a slave but as a beloved brother.” The Greek adelphos agapetos (αδελφός αγαπητός) redefines the social relationship through a theological one. Paul stops short of demanding manumission, but the logic of his argument points in that direction.
