Introduction

In Nicomachean Ethics I.7, Aristotle presents the “function argument”: if the human being has a distinctive function (ergon), then human good consists in performing that function well. Since reason is what distinguishes humans, the good life is activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

§1097b–1098a · Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics 1097b22–1098a20 (Bywater, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

τὸ ἀνθρώπινον ἀγαθὸν ψυχῆς ἐνέργεια γίνεται κατ᾽ ἀρετήν

English

If we say that the function of man is a certain kind of life, and this is an activity of the soul in accordance with reason, and the function of a good man is to do this well and nobly, then the human good turns out to be activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1097b–1098a: The function argument (ergon argument) is the foundation of Aristotelian ethics. The term ἐνέργεια (“activity”) is crucial: happiness is not a state or possession but an ongoing activity of the soul living in accordance with its highest capacities.
