Introduction

The Dyskolos (The Grouch) of Menander (316 BCE) is the only substantially complete play of New Comedy. It tells of Knemon, a misanthropic farmer, and the young Sostratos who falls in love with Knemon’s daughter. The play represents the shift from political to domestic drama.

§Lines 1–25 · Menander, Dyskolos
Manuscript
Greek

τῆς Ἀττικῆς νομίζετ' εἶναι τὸν τόπον Φυλήν

English

Pan: Imagine that the place is Phyle in Attica, and the Nymphs’ shrine from which I come belongs to the Phylasians and those who farm these rocks—a very famous shrine. The farm on my right here belongs to Knemon, a man who is a real hermit, bad-tempered toward everyone, and he hates crowds—crowds, I say? He’s lived a long life and has never willingly spoken to anyone.

§Lines 713–730 · Menander, Dyskolos
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ τοιοῦτοι πάντες ἦσαν

English

Knemon: If everyone were like me, there’d be no law courts, no prisons, no war. Each man would have enough and be content. But perhaps you find this unpleasant? Then carry on as you are. The peevish old man won’t be in your way.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 713–730: Knemon’s utopian speech—if everyone were self-sufficient, there would be no conflict—expresses a Cynic philosophical ideal. Menander studied under Theophrastus and was a contemporary of Epicurus. The play’s gentle humanism influenced Roman comedy (Terence, Plautus) and, through them, all subsequent Western comedy.
