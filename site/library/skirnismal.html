Introduction

The Skírnismál (“Sayings of Skírnir”) narrates the god Freyr’s lovesickness for the giantess Gerðr and his servant Skírnir’s mission to win her consent. When persuasion fails, Skírnir resorts to runic curses—making this a key source for Norse magical practice.

§Stanzas 36–37 · Poetic Edda, Skírnismál 36–37
Manuscript
Greek

Þurs ríst ék þér ok þríá stafi: ergi ok æði ok óþola. Svá ék þat af ríst sem ék þat á reist, ef gǫrask þess þf.

English

I carve for you “Thurs” and three runes more: lust, and madness, and unbearable desire. But I can scratch them off, as I scratched them on, if there be need of this. Gerðr spoke: “Hail rather, lad, and take the crystal cup full of ancient mead.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanzas 36–37: The rune-curse sequence is the most detailed description of magical stave-carving in the Poetic Edda. The “Thurs” rune (þurisaz) was associated with giants, chaos, and compulsion. That Gerðr yields only to magical coercion raises uncomfortable questions about consent in Norse mythology.
