Introduction

The Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam (11th–12th century CE) is a collection of quatrains (ruba’i) that meditate on the transience of life, the inscrutability of fate, and the consolation of wine. Whether to read them as hedonist philosophy or Sufi allegory remains debated.

§Selected Quatrains · Omar Khayyam, Rubaiyat
Manuscript
Greek

یک تکه نان بیک جرعه آبی و یک نفس

English

A loaf of bread beneath the bough, a flask of wine, a book of verse—and you beside me singing in the wilderness—wilderness itself is paradise enough. The moving finger writes, and having writ, moves on; nor all your piety nor wit shall lure it back to cancel half a line, nor all your tears wash out a word of it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Selected Quatrains: Edward FitzGerald’s 1859 English translation made these quatrains world-famous but is extremely free—more FitzGerald than Khayyam. The original Persian quatrains are terser and more philosophically acute. The “moving finger” verse (bar lawh-i budan) engages with Islamic theological debates about predestination (qadar) and human free will.
