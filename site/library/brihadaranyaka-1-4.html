Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.4: Aham Brahmasmi (I Am Brahman)

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is the oldest and longest of the principal Upanishads, composed perhaps as early as the 8th century BCE. Its opening chapter (1.4) contains one of the most dramatic passages in all of philosophical literature: the Self (ātman) exists alone, and its first act is to look around and say aham brahmāsmi—“I am Brahman.” This is not a human being claiming to be God. It is the universal Self recognizing its own nature before the world has yet been created.

The passage then narrates how the One becomes many—through loneliness, through desire for a companion, through the splitting of itself into male and female. The entire world of apparent multiplicity arises from the Self’s act of self-division. But the key teaching is in the recognition: whoever knows “I am Brahman” becomes this whole world. Even the gods cannot prevent it, for that person has become their own Self. This is theosis stated not as aspiration but as ontological fact.

§1.4.1 · Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.4.1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

ātmā vā idam eka evāgra āsīt puruṣavidhaḥ so’nuvekṣya nānyad ātmano’paśyat so’ham asmīty agre vyāharat tato’haṃnāmābhavat tasmād apy etarhy āmantraṇaḥ puruṣo’ham ayam ity evagre uktvāthānyan nāma prabrute yad asya bhavati

English

In the beginning, this world was the Self (atman) alone, in the form of a person. Looking around, he saw nothing other than himself. He first said: “I am” (aham asmi). From that the name “I” (aham) arose. Therefore even today, when addressed, a person first says “It is I,” and then speaks whatever other name he has.

§1.4.2 · Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.4.2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

so’bibhet tasmād ekākī bibheti sa hayaṃ aīkṣata yan mad anyan nāsti kasmān nu bibhemīti tata evāsya bhayaṃ vīyayāya kasmād dhy abheṣyad dvitīyād vai bhayaṃ bhavati

English

He was afraid. Therefore one who is alone is afraid. But he thought: “Since there is nothing other than myself, of what am I afraid?” Then his fear departed. For what should he have feared? One fears only a second.

§1.4.3 · Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.4.3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

sa vai naiva reme tasmād ekākī na ramate sa dvitīyam aicchat sa haitāvān āsa yathā strīpumāṃsau sampariṣvaktau sa imam evātmānaṃ dvaidhāpātayat tataḥ patiś ca patnī cābhavatām tasmād idam ardhabṛgalam iva svaḥ iti ha smāha yājñavalkyas tasmād ayaṃ ākāśaḥ striyā pūryata eva

English

He had no delight. Therefore one who is alone has no delight. He desired a second. He was as large as a man and woman in close embrace. He caused that Self to fall into two pieces. From that arose husband and wife. Therefore, as Yajnavalkya used to say, this body is one half of oneself, like one half of a split pea. Therefore this space is filled by a wife.

§1.4.10 · Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.4.10
Manuscript
Sanskrit

brahma vā idam agra āsīt tad ātmānam evāvet aham brahmāsmīti tasmāt tat sarvam abhavat tad yo yo devānāṃ pratyaābudhyata sa eva tad abhavat tathārṣīṇāṃ tathā manuṣyāṇāṃ tad dhaitat paśyan ṛṣir vāmadevaḥ pratipede’haṃ manur abh avaṃ sūryaś ceti tad idam apy etarhi ya evaṃ vedaāhaṃ brahmāsmīti sa idaṃ sarvaṃ bhavati tasya ha na devāś canābhūtyā īśate ātmā hy eṣāṃ sa bhavati

English

Brahman was this in the beginning. It knew only itself: “I am Brahman” (aham brahmāsmi). Therefore it became all. And whoever among the gods awakened to this also became That. The same is true among sages and among human beings. Seeing this, the sage Vamadeva declared: “I was Manu and the sun.” Even now, whoever knows “I am Brahman” becomes this whole world. Even the gods cannot prevent it, for he becomes their very Self.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.4.1: The Self’s first utterance—so’ham asmi, “I am”—parallels the divine self-identification in Exodus 3:14 (ehyeh asher ehyeh, “I am that I am”). Both traditions locate the ground of being in self-awareness. The difference is that in the Hebrew tradition, this is God speaking to Moses across an unbridgeable gap. In the Upanishad, this is the Self recognizing itself—and every being who achieves that same recognition becomes what the Self always was.
  2. §1.4.2: The logic of fear is striking: fear requires a second, an other. If there is only one Self, there is nothing to fear. This anticipates the Advaita Vedanta argument that all suffering arises from the illusion of duality (dvaita). When the illusion is dissolved, so is fear, so is suffering. The parallel with Epicurus’s argument about death is notable: what is not other than you cannot harm you.
  3. §1.4.10: The phrase aham brahmāsmi (“I am Brahman”) is the second of the four mahāvākyas (great sayings) of Vedanta, alongside tat tvam asi (“You are That”) from the Chandogya, prajñānam brahma (“Consciousness is Brahman”) from the Aitareya, and ayam ātmā brahma (“This Self is Brahman”) from the Mandukya. Together they form the core declaration of Advaita Vedanta: the individual self and the cosmic absolute are identical.
