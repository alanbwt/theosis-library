John 3:1-8: Born Again from Above

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

The encounter between Jesus and Nicodemus in John 3:1–8 is the New Testament’s most direct statement about the necessity of radical transformation. Nicodemus—a Pharisee, a ruler, a man of learning—comes to Jesus at night with a polite theological compliment. Jesus ignores the compliment entirely and makes a claim that leaves Nicodemus bewildered: “Unless one is born from above (anothen, ἄνωθεν), one cannot see the kingdom of God.” The Greek word anothen means both “again” and “from above,” and the entire conversation turns on this ambiguity.

Nicodemus hears “born again” and asks the obvious biological question. Jesus answers with “born of water and Spirit”—a phrase the Church Fathers universally read as referring to baptism, and which the later theosis tradition read as describing the beginning of human transformation into divine life. The text is from Codex Sinaiticus.

English

“Do not marvel that I said to you: you must be born from above. The wind (pneuma, πνεῦμα) blows where it wills, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it goes. So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:3–4: The Greek anothen (ἄνωθεν) is deliberately ambiguous: it can mean “again” (as Nicodemus takes it) or “from above” (as Jesus intends it). The entire dialogue is constructed on this double meaning. Nicodemus understands physical rebirth; Jesus means ontological rebirth—birth from a heavenly source. The Church Fathers used this passage as the scriptural foundation for baptismal regeneration. The theosis tradition read it more radically: being “born from above” means receiving a divine nature, becoming a participant in the life of God.
  2. §3:7–8: The Greek pneuma (πνεῦμα) means both “wind” and “spirit,” and Jesus exploits the ambiguity just as he did with anothen. The Spirit, like the wind, is invisible, unpredictable, and sovereign. You cannot control it or comprehend its origin and destination. This is a direct challenge to Nicodemus’s Pharisaic assumption that relationship with God can be managed through knowledge of the law. The Spirit blows where it wills—not where the theologian expects.
