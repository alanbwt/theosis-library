Introduction

Book 1 of Herodotus’s Histories (c. 440 BCE) opens the first work of historical prose in Western literature. Herodotus announces his purpose: to preserve the memory of great deeds and to explain why Greeks and barbarians went to war.

§1.1 · Herodotus, Histories
Manuscript
Greek

Ἡροδότου Ἁλικαρνησσέος ἱστορίης ἀπόδεξις ἥδε

English

This is the display of the inquiry of Herodotus of Halicarnassus, so that the things done by men may not be forgotten in time, and that great and marvelous deeds, some displayed by the Greeks, some by the barbarians, may not lose their glory, including among other things the reason why they went to war with each other.

§1.32 (Solon and Croesus) · Herodotus, Histories
Manuscript
Greek

ὦ Κροῖσε, ἐπιστάμενόν με τὸ θεῖον πᾶν ἐὸν φθονερόν τε καὶ ταραχῶδες

English

Solon said: “Croesus, you ask me about human affairs, and I know that the divine is entirely jealous and prone to disturb us. In a long span of time there is much to see that one would rather not, and much to suffer. Call no man happy until he is dead.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1: The word historie (ἱστορίη) means “inquiry” or “investigation,” not “history” in the modern sense. Herodotus is presenting the results of his research. The even-handed inclusion of “barbarian” achievements alongside Greek ones was revolutionary and distinguishes Herodotus from later, more chauvinistic historians.
