Library of History II.47: The Island of the Hyperboreans

Diodorus Siculus, c. 90-30 BCE

Introduction

Diodorus Siculus, writing in the first century BCE, preserves in Book II, Chapter 47 of his Library of History what is arguably the most detailed and extraordinary description of Hyperborea in all of ancient literature. Drawing on the earlier account of Hecataeus of Abdera (fourth century BCE), Diodorus describes a great island in the far north, opposite the land of the Celts, where a magnificent circular temple stands dedicated to Apollo. The inhabitants play lyres and sing hymns to the god continuously, and the climate is so mild that the land yields two harvests per year.

This passage has fascinated scholars for centuries because its details are simultaneously mythic and oddly specific. The circular temple has been compared to Stonehenge; the six-month day suggests a location within the Arctic Circle; the 19-year cycle of Apollo’s return corresponds precisely to the Metonic cycle of lunar-solar alignment. For the theosis tradition, the Hyperboreans of Diodorus represent the most fully realized vision of humanity living in unbroken communion with the divine—a people whose entire existence is worship, whose land is abundant without toil, and whose god walks among them in person.

§II.47.1–2 · Diodorus Siculus, Library of History II.47.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

τῶν δὲ τὰς παλαιὰς μυθολογίας ἀναγεγραφότων Ἑκαταῖός τε καί τινες ἕτεροί φασιν ἐν τοῖς ἀντιπέραν τῆς Κελτικῆς τόποις κατὰ τὸν ὠκεανὸν εἶναι νῆσον οὐκ ἐλάττω τῆς Σικελίας.

English

Among those who have written about the ancient myths, Hecataeus and certain others say that in the regions beyond the land of the Celts there lies in the ocean an island no smaller than Sicily. This island, situated in the north, is inhabited by the Hyperboreans, who are so called because their home is beyond the point from which the north wind (Boreas) blows. The soil of this island is fertile and productive of every crop, and since it has an unusually temperate climate, it produces two harvests each year.

§II.47.3–4 · Diodorus Siculus, Library of History II.47.3–4
Manuscript
Greek

μυθολογεῖσθαι δ’ ἐν ταύτῃ γεγενῆσθαι τὴν Λητώ, καὶ διὰ τοῦτο τὸν Ἀπόλλωνα παρ’ αὐτοῖς τιμᾶσθαι μάλιστα τῶν θεῶν.

English

The following legend is told of it: Leto was born on this island, and for that reason Apollo is honored there above all other gods. The inhabitants are, as it were, priests of Apollo, since they celebrate him with continuous songs of praise every day, and honor him exceedingly. There is also on the island both a magnificent sacred precinct of Apollo and a notable temple, which is circular in form and adorned with many votive offerings. There is also a city sacred to this god, and most of its inhabitants are players on the lyre, and they continuously play their instruments in the temple and sing hymns of praise to the god, glorifying his deeds.

§II.47.5–6 · Diodorus Siculus, Library of History II.47.5–6
Manuscript
Greek

χρῆσθαι δ’ αὐτοὺς διαλέκτῳ ἰδίᾳ, καὶ φιλελλήνως διακεῖσθαι μάλιστα, καὶ τούτων Ἀθηναίοις καὶ Δηλίοις μάλιστα.

English

The Hyperboreans use a language peculiar to themselves, and have a very friendly disposition toward the Greeks, and especially toward the Athenians and the Delians, this good will having been established from ancient times. It is also said that certain Greeks visited the Hyperboreans and left behind costly votive offerings bearing inscriptions in Greek letters. Likewise, Abaris, a Hyperborean, traveled to Greece in ancient times and renewed the good will and kinship toward the Delians.

§II.47.6–7 · Diodorus Siculus, Library of History II.47.6–7
Manuscript
Greek

λέγουσι δὲ καὶ τὴν σελήνην ἐκ ταύτης τῆς νήσου φαίνεσθαι σφόδρα πλησίον τῆς γῆς, καὶ διαδήλους ἔχουσαν τινὰς γεώδεις ἐξοχάς.

English

They say also that the moon, as viewed from this island, appears to be but a little distance from the earth, and to have upon it prominences, like those of the earth, which are visible to the eye. The god is said to visit the island every nineteen years, the period in which the return of the stars to the same place in the heavens is accomplished; and for this reason the nineteen-year period is called by the Greeks the “year of Meton.” At the time of this appearance of the god, he both plays on the lyre and dances continuously through the night from the vernal equinox until the rising of the Pleiades, expressing in this manner his delight in his successes.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II.47.1–2: The comparison of the Hyperborean island to Sicily in size suggests a substantial landmass, not a small mythical isle. The double harvest points to extraordinary fertility—a land blessed beyond normal agricultural limits. This abundance without toil is the agricultural equivalent of theosis: nature itself operates at a higher level in the presence of the divine.
  2. §II.47.3–4: The circular temple has generated enormous scholarly debate. Some have identified it with Stonehenge or other megalithic sites in Britain or Scandinavia. The description of a people whose primary activity is playing lyres and singing hymns to Apollo presents a society organized entirely around worship—not as an occasional obligation but as the substance of daily life. This is the Hyperborean version of theosis: a community so suffused with divine presence that worship and ordinary life are indistinguishable.
  3. §II.47.6–7: The 19-year cycle of Apollo’s visitation corresponds exactly to the Metonic cycle, the period after which the lunar phases recur on the same days of the solar year. This astronomical precision embedded in a mythological account is remarkable. The image of Apollo dancing and playing the lyre from the vernal equinox to the rising of the Pleiades—a period of roughly six weeks—presents the god not as a distant sovereign but as a joyful participant in the life of his people. The detail that the moon appears close to the earth and shows visible features may reflect observations from a high northern latitude where atmospheric conditions can produce unusual lunar effects.
