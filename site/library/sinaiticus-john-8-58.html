John 8:58: Before Abraham Was, I Am

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

John 8:58 contains what is arguably the most audacious christological claim in the Gospels. In the midst of a heated dispute with the Judean authorities, Jesus declares: “Before Abraham was, I am.” The Greek is ego eimi (ἐγὼ εἰμί)—the same phrase used in the Septuagint rendering of Exodus 3:14, where God reveals the divine name to Moses. The listeners understood exactly what Jesus was claiming: they picked up stones to kill him for blasphemy.

The passage is central to the theosis tradition because it collapses the temporal and ontological distance between the human and the divine. Jesus does not say “Before Abraham was, I was”—a claim that would merely assert pre-existence. He says “I am,” using the present tense to claim a mode of being that transcends time altogether. The Church Fathers built their doctrine of the Incarnation on passages like this: if the divine “I am” can speak through a human voice, then the boundary between God and humanity is not impermeable.

Translator's Notes

  1. §8:58: The phrase ego eimi (ἐγὼ εἰμί) is grammatically striking. The expected form would be ego emen (“I was”), using the past tense to match “before Abraham was.” By using the present tense, Jesus claims not merely pre-existence but eternal present existence—the same timeless being that God claims in Exodus 3:14 (rendered in the Septuagint as ego eimi ho on, “I am the one who is”). The Johannine Jesus makes this ego eimi declaration seven times in the Gospel, each time with increasing force.
  2. §8:59: The immediate response—picking up stones—confirms that the audience understood ego eimi as a claim to divinity, which under Jewish law constituted blasphemy punishable by stoning (Leviticus 24:16). The narrative thus provides its own interpretation of the theological claim: this is not a metaphor or a spiritual teaching but a literal identification with the God of Israel.
