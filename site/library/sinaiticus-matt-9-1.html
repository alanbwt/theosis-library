Introduction

Matthew 9:1–8 recounts the healing of the paralytic, where Jesus claims authority to forgive sins. The scribes’ charge of blasphemy reveals the theological stakes. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§9:2–3 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

και ιδου προσεφερον αυτω παραλυτικον επι κλινης βεβλημενον

English

And behold, they brought to him a paralytic lying on a bed. And when Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralytic, “Take heart, child; your sins are forgiven.” And behold, some of the scribes said to themselves, “This man is blaspheming.”

§9:6–8 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ινα δε ειδητε οτι εξουσιαν εχει ο υιος του ανθρωπου

English

But so that you may know that the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins—then he said to the paralytic—“Rise, take up your bed, and go to your house.” And he rose and went to his house. And when the crowds saw it, they were afraid and glorified God, who had given such authority to human beings.

Translator's Notes

  1. §9:8: Matthew uniquely notes the crowds glorified God for giving such authority “to human beings” (tois anthropois)—plural. This suggests that the authority Christ exercises is, in some sense, communicable to humanity.
