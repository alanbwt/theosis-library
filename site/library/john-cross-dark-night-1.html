Introduction

John of the Cross's Dark Night of the Soul (c. 1585) is one of the greatest poems in the Spanish language and the foundational text of apophatic mysticism in the West. It describes the soul's journey through spiritual darkness to union with God.

§Stanzas 1-3 · Dark Night of the Soul, Poem
Manuscript
Greek

En una noche oscura, con ansias en amores inflamada

English

On a dark night, fired with love's urgent longings—ah, the sheer grace!—I went out unseen, my house being now all stilled. In darkness and secure, by the secret ladder, disguised—ah, the sheer grace!—in darkness and concealment, my house being now all stilled. On that glad night, in secret, for no one saw me, nor did I look at anything, with no other light or guide than the one that burned in my heart.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanzas 1-3: The "dark night" is not depression but a stage of spiritual purification in which all familiar consolations—emotional, intellectual, sensory—are stripped away so that the soul can encounter God without mediating images. John distinguishes between the "night of the senses" (detachment from sensory pleasures) and the far more radical "night of the spirit" (detachment from spiritual consolations themselves). The concept has influenced psychology, existential philosophy, and secular literature far beyond its original theological context.
