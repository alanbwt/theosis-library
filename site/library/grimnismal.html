Introduction

The Grímnismál (“Sayings of the Masked One”) is an Eddic poem in which Odin, disguised and tortured between two fires by King Geirröðr, reveals cosmic lore about the gods’ halls, Yggdrasil, and his own many names.

§Stanzas 44–45 · Poetic Edda, Grímnismál 44–45
Manuscript
Greek

Áskr Yggdrasils er æztr viða, en Skiðblaðnir skipa, Óðinn ása, en jóa Sleipnir, Bifrǫst brúa, en Bragi skálda, Hábrókr hauka, en hunda Garmr.

English

Yggdrasil’s ash is the foremost of trees, and Skiðblaðnir of ships, Odin of the Aesir, and Sleipnir of steeds, Bifrost of bridges, Bragi of poets, Hábrók of hawks, and of hounds, Garmr.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanzas 44–45: The catalogue of “foremost things” is a characteristic form of Old Norse wisdom poetry. Yggdrasil (“Odin’s horse,” from Yggr = Odin + drasill = horse) is the cosmic ash tree connecting the nine worlds. The name refers to Odin’s self-hanging on the tree—a parallel early Christian missionaries exploited.
