Apocryphon of John: The Barbelo Cosmogony

Ἀπόκρυφον Ἰωάννου
Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Apocryphon of John (Secret Book of John) is the most important Gnostic cosmogonic text to survive antiquity. It exists in four copies—two in the Nag Hammadi library, one in the Berlin Codex, and one fragmentary version—which testifies to its wide circulation and authority within Gnostic communities. The text takes the form of a post-resurrection revelation from Christ to the apostle John, disclosing the true nature of the divine realm, the origin of the material world through the error of Sophia, and the imprisonment of divine sparks in human bodies.

The two excerpts presented here cover the highest theology and the deepest anthropology in the text. The first describes the Invisible Spirit—the utterly transcendent God—and the emanation of Barbelo, the first divine thought, from whom the entire Pleroma unfolds. The second describes the implantation of the divine spark in Adam, which gives humanity its hidden connection to the world above.

§The Invisible Spirit and Barbelo (II.2.26–4.26) · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 1, plates 2–4
Manuscript
Coptic

τὸ ἀόρατον πνεῦμα · ἡ μονάς ἐστιν μοναρχία ἧς οὐδὲν ὑπεράνω ἐστιν · καὶ ἡ ἔννοια αὐτοῦ εἰργάσατο ἔργον · καὶ προῆλθεν · ἡ Βαρβηλώ

English

The Monad is a monarchy with nothing above it. It is he who exists as God and Father of everything, the invisible one who is above everything, who exists as incorruption, dwelling in the pure light into which no eye can look. He is the invisible Spirit, of whom it is not right to think of him as a god, or something similar. For he is more than a god, since there is nothing above him, for no one lords it over him. He does not exist in something inferior to him, since everything exists in him. It is he who establishes himself. He is eternal, since he does not need anything. He is total perfection. A being can have a relationship with a being, but the Invisible Spirit is not among such things. He is at rest in silence, he who is before all things.

And his thought (Ennoia) performed a deed, and she came forth—she who had appeared before him in the shine of his light. She is the first power, she who was before all of them, and who came forth from his mind: she is the forethought (Pronoia) of the All—her light shines like his light—the perfect power, the image of the invisible, virginal Spirit. She became the womb of everything, for it is she who is prior to them all: the Mother-Father, the first Human, the Holy Spirit, the triple-male, the triple-powered, the triple-named androgynous one. And the eternal aeon among the invisible ones: the first to come forth—Barbelo.

§The Divine Spark in Adam (II.19.10–20.28) · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 1, plates 19–20
Manuscript
Coptic

καὶ ἡ δύναμις τῆς μητρὸς ἐξῆλθεν ἐκ τοῦ Ἰαλδαβαώθ · εἰς τὸ σῶμα τὸ φυσικόν · καὶ ἐκίνησεν καὶ ἔλαβεν ἰσχύν · καὶ ἐφώτισεν

English

And the power of the Mother went out of Yaltabaoth into the natural body that they had fashioned after the image of the one who exists from the beginning. The body moved and gained strength, and it was luminous. And in that moment the rest of the powers became jealous, because he had come into being through all of them—and they had given their power to the man—and his intelligence was greater than that of those who had made him, and greater than that of the chief archon. And when they recognized that he was luminous, and that he could think better than they, and that he was free from wickedness, they took him and threw him into the lowest region of all matter.

But the blessed one, the Mother-Father, the beneficent and merciful one, had mercy on the power of the Mother that had been brought forth out of the chief archon. And he sent, through his beneficent Spirit and his great mercy, a helper to Adam—luminous Epinoia, she who is from him, who was called Life (Zoe). And she assists the whole creature, by toiling with him and restoring him to his fullness, and by teaching him about the descent of his seed, and teaching him about the way of ascent, which is the way he came down.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Invisible Spirit and Barbelo: The description of the Monad as “more than a god” represents the most rigorous negative theology in early Christian literature—more radical even than the Cappadocian Fathers’ apophatic tradition. The Invisible Spirit cannot be named, categorized, or thought. The emanation of Barbelo from the Spirit’s own thought (Ennoia, ἔννοια) establishes the Gnostic pattern in which the divine realm unfolds through intellectual acts rather than acts of will. Barbelo’s titles—Mother-Father, first Human, triple-male androgynous one—collapse gender and number in ways that parallel the Gospel of Thomas’s Saying 22.
  2. §The Divine Spark in Adam: This passage is the Gnostic reinterpretation of Genesis 2–3. The “power of the Mother”—the divine spark from Sophia that Yaltabaoth (the ignorant creator-god) unwittingly passed into Adam—is the engine of the entire Gnostic soteriology. Humanity is luminous not because of anything the archons intended but because they accidentally transmitted a power greater than themselves. The archons’ response—casting Adam into matter—is the Gnostic reading of the Fall: not a punishment for disobedience but a desperate attempt to contain a light that threatens the archons’ authority. Epinoia (ἐπίνοια, “afferthought” or “insight”), also called Life (Zoe), teaches Adam the way of ascent—gnosis as the reversal of the fall into matter.
