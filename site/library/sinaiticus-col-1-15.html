Colossians 1:15-20: The Christ Hymn

Πρὸς Κολοσσαεῖς
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

Colossians 1:15–20 is widely regarded as an early Christian hymn, possibly pre-Pauline, that was incorporated into the letter. Its two strophes move from creation to redemption: Christ as the image of the invisible God through whom all things were made, and Christ as the head of the body through whom all things are reconciled. The passage makes claims about Christ’s cosmic role that go well beyond anything in the Synoptic Gospels.

For the theosis tradition, two terms are decisive. First, eikon tou theou tou aoratou (εἰκὼν τοῦ θεοῦ τοῦ ἀοράτου): Christ is the visible image of the invisible God, the point where divinity becomes accessible. Second, pleroma (πλήρωμα): all the fullness of God was pleased to dwell in him. The Gnostics seized on both terms. The orthodox Fathers used them to argue that because the fullness of God dwells in Christ, and believers are in Christ, believers participate in the divine fullness. The text follows Codex Sinaiticus.

§1:15–17 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ὅς ἐστιν εἰκὼν τοῦ θεοῦ τοῦ ἀοράτου · πρωτότοκος πάσης κτίσεως · ὅτι ἐν αὐτῷ ἐκτίσθη τὰ πάντα · ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς καὶ ἐπὶ τῆς γῆς · τὰ ὁρατὰ καὶ τὰ ἀόρατα · εἴτε θρόνοι εἴτε κυριότητες εἴτε ἀρχαὶ εἴτε ἐξουσίαι · τὰ πάντα δι’ αὐτοῦ καὶ εἰς αὐτὸν ἔκτισται · καὶ αὐτός ἐστιν πρὸ πάντων · καὶ τὰ πάντα ἐν αὐτῷ συνέστηκεν ·

English

He is the image (eikon, εἰκών) of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation. For in him all things were created, in the heavens and on the earth, things visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities—all things have been created through him and for him. And he is before all things, and in him all things hold together.

§1:18–20 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ αὐτός ἐστιν ἡ κεφαλὴ τοῦ σώματος τῆς ἐκκλησίας · ὅς ἐστιν ἀρχή · πρωτότοκος ἐκ τῶν νεκρῶν · ἵνα γένηται ἐν πᾶσιν αὐτὸς πρωτεύων · ὅτι ἐν αὐτῷ εὐδόκησεν πᾶν τὸ πλήρωμα κατοικῆσαι · καὶ δι’ αὐτοῦ ἀποκαταλλάξαι τὰ πάντα εἰς αὐτόν · εἰρηνοποιήσας διὰ τοῦ αἵματος τοῦ σταυροῦ αὐτοῦ · δι’ αὐτοῦ εἴτε τὰ ἐπὶ τῆς γῆς εἴτε τὰ ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς ·

English

And he is the head of the body, the church. He is the beginning, the firstborn from the dead, so that in all things he might have first place. For in him all the fullness (pleroma, πλήρωμα) of God was pleased to dwell, and through him to reconcile all things to himself, making peace through the blood of his cross—through him, whether things on earth or things in the heavens.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:15: The term eikon tou theou tou aoratou (εἰκὼν τοῦ θεοῦ τοῦ ἀοράτου) raises the central paradox of theosis: how can the invisible God have a visible image? For Paul, Christ is that image—not a representation but the actual self-manifestation of God in created form. The term eikon carries more ontological weight than the English word “image” suggests. In Platonic usage, an image participates in the reality it reflects. If Christ is the eikon of God, and believers are being conformed to the eikon of the Son (Romans 8:29), then a chain of participation links humanity to God through Christ.
  2. §1:19: The word pleroma (πλήρωμα) became the most important technical term in Valentinian Gnosticism, where it designated the totality of divine emanations—the thirty aeons that constitute the fullness of the Godhead. Here in Colossians the usage is pre-Gnostic: the fullness of God dwells in Christ bodily. The Gnostic appropriation of this term may represent a later development from Pauline language, or both may draw on a shared Jewish-Hellenistic vocabulary. What matters for theosis is the claim itself: the totality of the divine nature is present in a single human person. If this is possible for Christ, the Fathers asked, what does it imply for those who are “in Christ”?
