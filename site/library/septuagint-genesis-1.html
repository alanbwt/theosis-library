Genesis 1:26-28: "Let Us Make Man in Our Image"

Γένεσις
Moses (attributed), traditional
Septuagint (LXX), Genesis 1 · Rahlfs, A. (ed.), Septuaginta, Stuttgart: Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, 2006

Introduction

The creation of humanity in the image (eikon, εἰκών) and likeness (homoiosis, ὁμοίωσις) of God. These three verses are the foundation text for the entire theosis tradition. The distinction between image and likeness became central to patristic theology: image is given at creation, likeness is achieved through theosis. Irenaeus of Lyon built his entire theological anthropology on this distinction. In Against Heresies (V.6.1), he argues that Adam received the image of God at creation but had not yet attained the likeness—that was to come through growth, obedience, and ultimately through union with Christ. The Fall, on this reading, did not destroy the image (which is indelible) but interrupted the journey toward likeness.

The Septuagint Greek preserves a subtlety that matters: in verse 26, God says “let us make” (poiēsomen, ποιήσωμεν) using the subjunctive—a deliberative plural that has generated centuries of debate. Who is the “us”? The text presented here follows the Septuagint Greek.

English

And God said, “Let us make humankind according to our image (eikon, εἰκών) and according to our likeness (homoiosis, ὁμοίωσις), and let them rule over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heaven and over the cattle and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps upon the earth.” And God made humankind; according to the image of God he made him; male and female he made them.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:26–27: The Septuagint uses two distinct Greek terms where English translations typically blur them into one concept. Eikon (εἰκών) means “image” in the sense of a visible representation or icon—it is the word used for a portrait, a statue, the image on a coin. Homoiosis (ὁμοίωσις) means “likeness” or “resemblance” in a more dynamic sense—it implies process, approximation, becoming similar. The Church Fathers seized on this distinction. Irenaeus (Against Heresies V.6.1) argued that the image of God is the structural capacity for reason and freedom given at creation, while the likeness is the moral and spiritual perfection that Adam had not yet achieved. Origen, Clement of Alexandria, Gregory of Nyssa, and Maximus the Confessor all developed variations of this framework. The image is the starting point; the likeness is the destination. Theosis is the journey from one to the other. Whether the Hebrew (tselem and demuth) supports this sharp a distinction is debatable, but the Greek of the Septuagint—which was the Bible of the early Church—clearly uses two different words, and the Fathers built their theology accordingly.
  2. §1:26: The plural “let us make” (poiēsomen, ποιήσωμεν) has generated an extraordinary range of interpretations. The options: (1) God is addressing the heavenly court, the angels or divine beings who attend him—this is the oldest reading and has parallels in the ancient Near Eastern divine council tradition (cf. Psalm 82). (2) It is a “plural of deliberation,” God speaking to himself in a rhetorical mode—a grammatical explanation that convinces few. (3) It is a foreshadowing of the Trinity—the standard patristic reading from the second century onward, found in Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, and Basil of Caesarea. (4) It is God addressing Wisdom (Sophia) or the Logos as co-creator—Philo takes this view, and it connects to the Wisdom tradition in Proverbs 8 and Sirach 24. For the theosis tradition, the plural matters because it suggests that the act of creating humanity was uniquely deliberate: God did not simply command (“let there be”) as with the rest of creation, but consulted, deliberated, and made humanity as a collaborative act within the divine life itself.
