Proslogion 2-3: That Than Which Nothing Greater

Anselm of Canterbury, 1033-1109 AD

Introduction

Anselm of Canterbury composed the Proslogion in 1078 AD as a meditation in the form of a prayer. Chapters 2 and 3 contain the ontological argument for the existence of God—perhaps the most debated proof in the history of philosophy. Anselm defines God as “that than which nothing greater can be conceived” (id quo nihil maius cogitari possit), then argues that such a being must exist not only in the mind but in reality, because existence in reality is greater than existence in the mind alone. Chapter 3 goes further: God not only exists but exists necessarily, because a being whose non-existence is inconceivable is greater than one whose non-existence is conceivable.

The Proslogion is relevant to the theosis tradition not because it proves God exists—that was never seriously in doubt for Anselm—but because it establishes the nature of God as absolute perfection. If God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived, then God contains all perfections. And if human beings are made in the image of God, then their telos is to participate in those perfections. The ontological argument is not merely an exercise in logic; it is a prayer. Anselm wrote it on his knees.

§Prooemium (excerpt) · Proslogion, Prooemium
Manuscript
Latin

Coepi mecum quaerere, si forte posset inveniri unum argumentum, quod nullo alio ad se probandum quam se solo indigeret, et solum ad astruendum quia Deus vere est.

English

I began to ask myself whether there might be found a single argument which would require no other for its proof than itself alone, and alone would suffice to demonstrate that God truly exists, and that there is a supreme good requiring nothing else, which all other things require for their existence and well-being.

§Chapter 2: That God Truly Exists · Proslogion, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Latin

Ergo, Domine, qui das fidei intellectum, da mihi, ut quantum scis expedire, intelligam quia es sicut credimus, et hoc es quod credimus. Et quidem credimus te esse aliquid quo nihil maius cogitari possit.

English

Therefore, Lord, you who give understanding to faith, grant me that, so far as you know it to be profitable, I may understand that you exist as we believe, and that you are what we believe you to be. And indeed we believe that you are something than which nothing greater can be conceived. Or is there no such nature, since the fool has said in his heart: there is no God? But surely this same fool, when he hears this very thing that I say—something than which nothing greater can be conceived—understands what he hears, and what he understands stands in his understanding, even if he does not understand that it exists.

§Chapter 2 (continued) · Proslogion, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Latin

Aliud enim est rem esse in intellectu, aliud intelligere rem esse.

English

For it is one thing for something to stand in the understanding, and another to understand that it exists. When a painter plans what he is about to paint, he has it in his understanding but does not yet understand it to exist, because he has not yet made it. But after he has painted it, he both has it in his understanding and understands it to exist, because he has already made it. Thus even the fool is convinced that something than which nothing greater can be conceived stands in the understanding, since he understands this when he hears it, and whatever is understood stands in the understanding.

§Chapter 2 (conclusion) · Proslogion, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Latin

Et certe id quo maius cogitari nequit, non potest esse in solo intellectu. Si enim vel in solo intellectu est, potest cogitari esse et in re, quod maius est.

English

And surely that than which a greater cannot be conceived cannot exist in the understanding alone. For if it exists solely in the understanding, it can be conceived to exist in reality also, which is greater. If therefore that than which a greater cannot be conceived exists in the understanding alone, then that than which a greater cannot be conceived is itself that than which a greater can be conceived. But this is obviously impossible. Therefore there is no doubt that something than which a greater cannot be conceived exists both in the understanding and in reality.

§Chapter 3: That God Cannot Be Conceived Not to Exist · Proslogion, Chapter 3
Manuscript
Latin

Quod utique sic vere est, ut nec cogitari possit non esse. Nam potest cogitari esse aliquid, quod non possit cogitari non esse; quod maius est quam quod non esse cogitari potest.

English

Indeed, it so truly exists that it cannot be conceived not to exist. For it is possible to conceive of something that cannot be conceived not to exist, and this is greater than that which can be conceived not to exist. Therefore, if that than which a greater cannot be conceived can be conceived not to exist, then that than which a greater cannot be conceived is not that than which a greater cannot be conceived—a contradiction. So truly therefore does something than which a greater cannot be conceived exist, that it cannot even be conceived not to exist.

§Chapter 3 (conclusion) · Proslogion, Chapter 3
Manuscript
Latin

Et hoc es tu, Domine Deus noster. Sic ergo vere es, Domine Deus meus, ut nec cogitari possis non esse.

English

And this is you, O Lord our God. So truly do you exist, O Lord my God, that you cannot be conceived not to exist. And rightly so. For if a mind could conceive of something better than you, the creature would rise above the Creator and judge the Creator—which is utterly absurd. And indeed, whatever else there is, except you alone, can be conceived not to exist. You alone, therefore, most truly of all things have being, and therefore of all things possess being to the highest degree; for whatever else exists does not exist so truly, and therefore has being to a lesser degree.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 2: The ontological argument has been accepted by Descartes, Leibniz, Gödel, and Plantinga, and rejected by Gaunilo (Anselm’s contemporary), Thomas Aquinas, Kant, and Russell. The most famous objection is Kant’s: existence is not a predicate, so saying that something exists in reality does not add a property that makes it “greater” than something that exists only in the mind. Anselm’s defenders argue that the argument works specifically for God because God is a unique case—the one being whose essence entails existence.
  2. §Chapter 3: Chapter 3 strengthens the argument by introducing the concept of necessary existence. A being that cannot be conceived not to exist is greater than one that can be conceived not to exist. Therefore, if God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived, God’s existence is not merely actual but necessary. This move anticipates the modal ontological argument developed by Plantinga in the twentieth century. For the theosis tradition, the implication is that the God in whom human beings participate is not contingent but necessarily existent—the ground of all being, not one being among others.
