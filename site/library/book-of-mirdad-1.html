Introduction

The Book of Mirdad by Mikhail Naimy (1948) is a mystical parable set in a monastery on the summit of a mountain, where the enigmatic Mirdad teaches monks about the unity of all existence. It is considered a masterpiece of Lebanese spiritual literature.

§Chapter 1 · Naimy, The Book of Mirdad, Chapter 1
Manuscript
Greek

[Original English text as published, 1948]

English

My peace I cast upon the waves. Who is there among you so valiant as to brave the seas and fetch my peace? For my peace, if it be found, shall be your anchor in the deep and your sail upon the wind. Mirdad shall speak to you of things whereof your memory retains no shadow and of which your understanding has no clue.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 1: Naimy, a close friend of Gibran, wrote in both Arabic and English. The Book of Mirdad draws on Sufi, Christian mystical, and Hindu Advaita traditions to articulate a philosophy of radical unity—all apparent dualities (good and evil, life and death) are ultimately one in the divine.
