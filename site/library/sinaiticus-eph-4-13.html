Ephesians 4:11-16: The Measure of the Fullness of Christ

Πρὸς Ἐφεσίους
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

Ephesians 4:11–16 presents the most developed ecclesiology in the Pauline corpus, but its real subject is not church structure—it is human transformation. The passage moves from the practical (apostles, prophets, teachers) to the cosmic: the goal of the entire apparatus is that believers attain “the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ.” The Greek pleroma (πλήρωμα) reappears here as in the Johannine Prologue and Colossians, but now applied to the collective body rather than to Christ alone.

For the Church Fathers, this passage was decisive. If the telos of the Christian life is to attain the fullness of Christ, then maturity is not merely moral improvement but participation in divine life. Irenaeus read this as confirmation that God became human so that humans might become God. The Sinaiticus text, from the fourth century, preserves the earliest complete witness to this passage.

§4:11–13 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ αὐτὸς ἔδωκεν τοὺς μὲν ἀποστόλους · τοὺς δὲ προφήτας · τοὺς δὲ εὐαγγελιστάς · τοὺς δὲ ποιμένας καὶ διδασκάλους · πρὸς τὸν καταρτισμὸν τῶν ἁγίων εἰς ἔργον διακονίας · εἰς οἰκοδομὴν τοῦ σώματος τοῦ Χριστοῦ · μέχρι καταντήσωμεν οἱ πάντες εἰς τὴν ἑνότητα τῆς πίστεως καὶ τῆς ἐπιγνώσεως τοῦ υἱοῦ τοῦ θεοῦ · εἰς ἄνδρα τέλειον · εἰς μέτρον ἡλικίας τοῦ πληρώματος τοῦ Χριστοῦ ·

English

And he himself gave some as apostles, some as prophets, some as evangelists, some as pastors and teachers, for the equipping of the saints for the work of ministry, for the building up of the body of Christ, until we all attain to the unity of the faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God, to a mature person, to the measure of the stature of the fullness (pleroma, πλήρωμα) of Christ.

§4:14–16 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ἵνα μηκέτι ὦμεν νήπιοι · κλυδωνιζόμενοι καὶ περιφερόμενοι παντὶ ἀνέμῳ τῆς διδασκαλίας · ἐν τῇ κυβείᾳ τῶν ἀνθρώπων · ἐν πανουργίᾳ πρὸς τὴν μεθοδείαν τῆς πλάνης · ἀληθεύοντες δὲ ἐν ἀγάπῃ αὐξήσωμεν εἰς αὐτὸν τὰ πάντα · ὃς ἐστιν ἡ κεφαλή · Χριστός · ἐξ οὗ πᾶν τὸ σῶμα συναρμολογούμενον καὶ συμβιβαζόμενον διὰ πάσης ἁφῆς τῆς ἐπιχορηγίας · κατ’ ἐνέργειαν ἐν μέτρῳ ἑνὸς ἑκάστου μέρους · τὴν αὔξησιν τοῦ σώματος ποιεῖται εἰς οἰκοδομὴν ἑαυτοῦ ἐν ἀγάπῃ ·

English

So that we may no longer be infants, tossed about by waves and carried around by every wind of teaching, by the trickery of people, by craftiness in deceitful scheming. But speaking the truth in love, we are to grow up in all things into him who is the head, Christ, from whom the whole body, joined and held together by every supporting ligament, according to the working in measure of each individual part, causes the growth of the body for the building up of itself in love.

Translator's Notes

  1. §4:13: The phrase “the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ” (εἰς μέτρον ἡλικίας τοῦ πληρώματος τοῦ Χριστοῦ) is the single most explicit statement of theosis as a communal goal in the Pauline letters. The term pleroma (πλήρωμα) here denotes the complete divine reality that indwells Christ. The destination of Christian maturity is not ethical improvement but participation in that fullness. The word teleion (τέλειον), rendered “mature,” carries the sense of completion or perfection—reaching the intended end (telos) of human existence. For the Greek Fathers, that end was nothing less than deification.
  2. §4:15–16: The body metaphor in verses 15–16 is not merely organizational. The verb auxesomen (αὐξήσωμεν), “we are to grow up,” implies an organic process rather than a mechanical one. Growth into Christ—into the head—is growth into divinity itself. The passage insists this transformation is corporate: the whole body grows together, each part contributing. Theosis in the Pauline vision is never solitary.
