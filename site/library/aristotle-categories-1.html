Introduction

The Categories opens Aristotle’s logical works with a classification of the fundamental kinds of being. Primary substance—the individual thing (this man, this horse)—is the basic subject of which everything else is predicated and in which everything else inheres.

§2a11–2b6 · Aristotle, Categories 2a11–2b6 (Minio-Paluello, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

οὐσία δέ ἐστιν ἡ κυριώτατά τε καὶ πρώτως καὶ μάλιστα λεγομένη

English

Substance, in the truest and primary and most definite sense of the word, is that which is neither predicable of a subject nor present in a subject—for instance, the individual man or horse. Those things are called secondary substances to which, as species, the primary substances belong, and also the genera of these species: for instance, the individual man belongs to the species man, and the genus of the species is animal.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2a11–2b6: The priority of individual substance over universals sets Aristotle apart from Plato, for whom the universal Form is more real than the particular. The Neoplatonists attempted to harmonize the two views, reading Aristotle’s categories as describing the sensible world while Plato’s Forms govern the intelligible.
