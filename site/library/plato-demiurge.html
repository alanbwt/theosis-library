Timaeus: The Demiurge Creates the World

Timaeus: The Demiurge Creates the World
Plato, c. 428-348 BCE
Codex Parisinus Graecus 1807

Introduction

The Timaeus is Plato's cosmology — the only Platonic dialogue continuously available in the Latin West throughout the Middle Ages, and thus one of the most influential texts in all of Western thought. It describes a divine craftsman (Demiurge) who, being perfectly good and free from envy, fashions the visible cosmos as a 'moving image of eternity.' The concept of time as an image of eternity, the World Soul, and the mathematical structure of creation all originate here.

§28c-29a · Tim. 28c-29a
Greek

τὸν μὲν οὖν ποιητὴν καὶ πατέρα τοῦδε τοῦ παντὸς εὑρεῖν τε ἔργον καὶ εὑρόντα εἰς πάντας ἀδύνατον λέγειν.

English

To find the maker and father of this universe is indeed a hard task, and having found him, it would be impossible to declare him to all people.

§29e-30a · Tim. 29e-30a
Greek

ἀγαθὸς ἦν, ἀγαθῷ δὲ οὐδεὶς περὶ οὐδενὸς οὐδέποτε ἐγγίγνεται φθόνος· τούτου δ᾿ ἐκτὸς ὢν πάντα ὅτι μάλιστα ἐβουλήθη γενέσθαι παραπλήσια ἑαυτῷ.

English

He was good, and in one who is good no envy arises concerning anything. Being free from envy, he desired that all things should come to be as much like himself as possible.

§37d · Tim. 37d
Greek

εἰκὼ δ᾿ ἐπενόει κινητόν τινα αἰῶνος ποιῆσαι, καὶ... ποιεῖ μένοντος αἰῶνος ἐν ἑνὶ κατ᾿ ἀριθμὸν ἰοῦσαν αἰώνιον εἰκόνα, τοῦτον ὃν δὴ χρόνον ὠνομάκαμεν.

English

He resolved to make a moving image of eternity, and so when he ordered the heavens he made an eternal image moving according to number — that which we have called 'time.'
