The Behistun Inscription: Darius Declares His Victories

        
The Behistun Inscription: Darius Declares His Victories

        
Darius I, 550-486 BCE

        
Rock face at Behistun, Kermanshah Province, Iran

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Behistun Inscription did for cuneiform what the Rosetta Stone did for hieroglyphs. Carved into a cliff face 100 meters above the ground on the road between Babylon and Ecbatana, it records Darius I's rise to power in three languages: Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian (Akkadian). In the 1830s-40s, Henry Rawlinson risked his life climbing the cliff to copy the inscription, then used the Old Persian (which was partially deciphered) to crack Babylonian cuneiform.
This single inscription unlocked the entire literary heritage of Mesopotamia: without it, the Epic of Gilgamesh, the Enuma Elish, the Code of Hammurabi, and every Sumerian and Akkadian text on this site would be undecipherable marks on clay tablets.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · behistun-inscription
          
I am Darius, the Great King, King of Kings, King in Persia, King of countries, son of Hystaspes, grandson of Arsames, an Achaemenid. King Darius says: My father is Hystaspes; the father of Hystaspes was Arsames; the father of Arsames was Ariaramnes; the father of Ariaramnes was Teispes; the father of Teispes was Achaemenes. King Darius says: That is why we are called Achaemenids; from antiquity we have been noble; from antiquity has our dynasty been royal. King Darius says: Eight of my dynasty were kings before me; I am the ninth. Nine in succession we have been kings. King Darius says: By the grace of Ahuramazda am I king; Ahuramazda has granted me the kingdom.
