Rig Veda 1.164.46: They Call It One by Many Names

Vedic Rishis, c. 1500-1200 BCE

Introduction

Rig Veda 1.164, known as the Riddle Hymn of Dirghatamas, is one of the most philosophically dense compositions in the oldest stratum of Indian literature (c. 1500–1200 BCE). Verse 46—“They call it Indra, Mitra, Varuna, Agni... the wise speak of what is One in many ways”—is arguably the earliest statement in human history of the principle that a single divine reality underlies the multiplicity of divine names and forms.

This verse has served as a foundational proof-text for Hindu inclusivism across three millennia. It was cited by the Upanishadic sages, by Shankara in defence of Advaita Vedanta, by Vivekananda at the Parliament of the World’s Religions in 1893, and by virtually every Hindu thinker who has argued that the diversity of religions reflects not error but the many faces of a single truth. For the theosis library, it represents the Vedic root of the insight that pervades all traditions: the divine is one, and all paths of return converge upon the same source.

इन्द्रं मित्रं वरुणमग्निमाहुर्यद् दिव्यः स सुपर्णो गरुत्मान्। एकं सद् विप्रा बहुधा वदन्त्यग्निं यमं मातरिश्वानमाहुः।।

English

They call it Indra, Mitra, Varuna, Agni, and it is the heavenly bird Garutmat. The wise speak of what is One in many ways: they call it Agni, Yama, Matarishvan.

द्वा सुपर्णा सयुजा सखाया समानं वृक्षं परिषस्वजाते। तयोरन्यः पिप्पलं स्वाद्वत्त्यनश्नन्नन्यो अभिचाकशीति।।

Two birds, companions bound together, clasp the same tree. One of them eats the sweet fruit; the other looks on without eating.

English

What can he do with the verse who does not know that imperishable one, the highest dwelling place, in which all the gods are seated? Those who know it, they sit together here.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.164.46: The phrase ekam sad vipra bahudha vadanty (“the wise speak of what is One in many ways”) became the single most cited verse in Hindu theology. It does not deny the reality of individual gods but subordinates them to a deeper unity. The question of whether this unity is personal (as in theistic Vedanta) or impersonal (as in Advaita) remained open in the Vedic period and was debated for the next three thousand years.
  2. §1.164.20: The parable of the two birds was adopted by the Mundaka Upanishad (3.1.1) and the Shvetashvatara Upanishad (4.6), where it was interpreted as the individual soul (jiva) and the supreme Self (Atman/Brahman) dwelling in the same body. The bird that eats the fruit is the soul experiencing the world through desire; the bird that watches is the eternal witness. Theosis, in the Vedantic reading, is the moment when the eating bird recognises it was always the watching bird.
