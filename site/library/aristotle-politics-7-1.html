Introduction

In Politics VII.1, Aristotle asks which life is most choiceworthy: the political life of action or the philosophical life of contemplation. He argues that the best life for both the individual and the city must be the life of virtue equipped with sufficient external goods.

Greek

περὶ μὲν οὖν τοῦ τὴν ἀρίστην εἶναι πολιτείαν

English

No one would dispute that the happiest life must possess virtue sufficiently to participate in virtuous actions, together with an adequate provision of external goods. But whether the most choiceworthy life is one of political and practical engagement, or rather one released from all external concerns—a contemplative life, which some say is the only life fitting for a philosopher—this is a question that demands investigation.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1323a–1323b: The tension between the political and the contemplative life runs through all of Aristotle’s practical philosophy. While Nicomachean Ethics X.7 appears to favor contemplation, this passage in the Politics takes a more integrative view, suggesting the best city makes room for both.
