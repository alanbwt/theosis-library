Introduction

In On Generation and Corruption, Aristotle explains how the four elements (earth, water, air, fire) transform into one another through the interchange of their paired qualities (hot/cold, wet/dry). This cyclical transformation accounts for all coming-to-be and passing-away in the sublunary world.

§II.4, 331a–b · Aristotle, De Generatione et Corruptione 331a7–331b4 (Joachim, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

τὸ μὲν γὰρ πῦρ θερμὸν καὶ ξηρόν, ὁ δὲ ἀὴρ θερμὸν καὶ ὑγρόν

English

Fire is hot and dry, air is hot and wet, water is cold and wet, earth is cold and dry. Each element shares one quality with each of its neighbors, and it is through this shared quality that transformation takes place. Fire can become air, since both are hot; air can become water, since both are wet.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II.4: Aristotle’s theory of elemental transformation through shared qualities replaced the Empedoclean view that the four elements are eternal and unchanging. This theory dominated natural philosophy until the rise of modern chemistry in the 17th century.
