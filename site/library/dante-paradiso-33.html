Paradiso XXXIII: The Beatific Vision

Dante Alighieri, 1265-1321 AD

Introduction

The final canto of Dante’s Paradiso—and of the entire Divine Comedy—is the supreme literary representation of the beatific vision in Western civilization. Having ascended through the nine spheres of heaven, Dante reaches the Empyrean and, with the aid of the Virgin Mary’s intercession, is granted a direct vision of God. What he sees is a single point of light containing the entire universe, bound together by love. Then, within the divine light, he perceives three circles of three colours and one magnitude—the Trinity—and within the second circle, the image of a human face: the Incarnation.

The canto is Dante’s most sustained attempt to describe the indescribable, and he confesses repeatedly that language fails him. The final lines record a transformation: Dante’s own desire and will are turned, like a wheel, by “the Love that moves the sun and the other stars.” This is theosis rendered as narrative: the human will is not annihilated but harmonized with the divine will, so that the two move as one.

Italian

Nel suo profondo vidi che s’interna, legato con amore in un volume, ciò che per l’universo si squaderna:

sustanze e accidenti e lor costume quasi conflati insieme, per tal modo che ciò ch’i’ dico è un semplice lume.

English

Within its depth I saw that it contains, bound by love in one volume, what is scattered throughout the universe—substances and accidents and their relations, as though fused together in such a way that what I speak of is a single light.

§Canto XXXIII, lines 115–126 · Paradiso, Canto XXXIII, lines 115–126

Italian

Ne la profonda e chiara sussistenza de l’alto lume parvermi tre giri di tre colori e d’una contenenza;

e l’un da l’altro come iri da iri parea reflesso, e ’l terzo parea foco che quinci e quindi igualmente si spiri.

English

Within the profound and luminous substance of the exalted light, three circles appeared to me of three colours and one dimension. One seemed reflected by the other, as rainbow by rainbow, and the third seemed fire breathed equally from both. How feeble is speech, and how inadequate to my conception!

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 85–93: The image of the universe as a book whose pages are scattered but whose binding is love (“bound by love in one volume, what is scattered throughout the universe”) is Dante’s most famous metaphor for divine unity. The Aristotelian terminology—“substances and accidents and their relations”—grounds the mystical vision in the philosophical vocabulary of the schools. Dante is saying that the metaphysical structure of reality, which philosophy can only describe piecemeal, is seen in God as a single, simple reality.
  2. §Lines 127–132: The vision of the human face within the second circle of the Trinity—“painted with our likeness” (pinta de la nostra effige)—is the Incarnation seen from God’s side. The human form is not imposed on the divine but discovered already present within it. This is the culmination of the theosis theme in the Comedy: humanity is not alien to divinity but has always been contained within it.
  3. §Lines 142–145: The final line of the Comedy—l’amor che move il sole e l’altre stelle—unites the Aristotelian concept of the Unmoved Mover (who moves all things by being loved) with the Christian doctrine of divine love. The poem ends not with stasis but with motion: Dante’s will, perfectly aligned with God’s, turns like a wheel. Theosis here is not absorption or dissolution but harmonized movement.
