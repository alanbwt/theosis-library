Bayazid Bastami: The Annihilation of Self

Bayazid Bastami, c. 804-874 AD

Introduction

Bayazid Bastami (Abu Yazid al-Bistami, d. c. 874 CE) was one of the earliest and most radical of the Sufi mystics. His ecstatic utterances (shathiyyat)—spontaneous declarations made in states of spiritual intoxication—scandalised the orthodox and fascinated later Sufis. The most famous of these is “Glory be to me! How great is my majesty!” (Subhani! Ma a’thama sha’ni!), a phrase that, taken at face value, claims divinity for the human speaker. Bayazid’s defenders explained that in the state of fana (annihilation of the self), the ego has been entirely dissolved, and it is God speaking through the empty vessel of the mystic.

Bayazid’s utterances represent the Sufi answer to the theosis question at its most extreme. The self does not become God; the self vanishes, and only God remains. This is not union in the Christian sense—two natures joined—but annihilation: one nature erased, the other revealed as having been the only reality all along.

I shed my self as a snake sheds its skin. Then I looked into my essence and saw that I am He.

§Shathiyyat 4 · al-Sahlagī, Kitāb an-Nūr, p. 120
Manuscript
Arabic

كان الله مرآتي ثلاثين سنة. الآن أنا مرآتي. ما كنته لسته لأنّ أنا والله إنكار لوحدانية الله.

English

For thirty years God was my mirror. Now I am my own mirror. What I was I no longer am, for “I” and “God” are a denial of God’s unity. Since I no longer am, God is his own mirror. I am not; God is. That is all. Glory be to him.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Shathiyyat 1: The phrase “from God to God” describes a journey that begins and ends in the same place—because there was never anywhere else to go. The cry “O thou I!” is the moment when the distinction between the divine “Thou” and the human “I” collapses. This is fana (annihilation) in its purest expression.
  2. §Shathiyyat 2: The phrase Subhani (“Glory be to me”) uses the exact grammatical form normally reserved for God alone (Subhanallah, “Glory be to God”). Orthodox critics read this as blasphemy. Sufi defenders argued that Bayazid’s ego was so completely annihilated that only God remained to speak. Al-Junayd, the great sober Sufi, reportedly said: “Bayazid’s tongue spoke the words, but the speaker was God.”
  3. §Shathiyyat 4: The mirror image is characteristic of the more philosophically sophisticated Sufism that developed after Bayazid. The claim that even the distinction between “I” and “God” is a denial of God’s unity (tawhid) pushes Islamic mysticism to its logical extreme: if God is truly One, then there cannot be two realities—God and the self—standing over against each other. The self must either be God or be nothing. Bayazid chooses the latter, and discovers it amounts to the former.
