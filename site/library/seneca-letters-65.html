Introduction

In Epistulae Morales 65, Seneca discusses Plato’s theory of Forms within a Stoic framework. He describes five causes—material, agent, form, model, and purpose—showing how Stoic physics could absorb and transform Platonic metaphysics.

§Ep. 65.7–10 · Seneca, Epistulae Morales 65.7–10 (Reynolds, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Plato his quintam causam adicit exemplar, quam ipse ideam vocat. Habet intra se deus illas omnium rerum formas; numerosque universorum quae agenda sunt animo suo conplexus est.

English

Plato adds a fifth cause: the model, which he calls the Idea. It is that at which the artist looked when creating what he intended. It matters not whether the model is outside, something he directs his eyes to, or within, conceived and placed there by himself. God has within himself these models of all things; his mind contains the measures and patterns of everything that is to be made.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ep. 65.7–10: Seneca’s placement of the Platonic Ideas within the mind of God represents a crucial development in the history of philosophy. This “inner-Ideas” theory was adopted by Middle Platonists and later by Augustine, who made it a cornerstone of Christian metaphysics.
