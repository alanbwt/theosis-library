Introduction

The parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19–31) is unique to Luke and presents a vivid afterlife scenario where economic fortunes are reversed. It is the only parable in which a character is given a proper name.

§16:19–26 · Codex Sinaiticus, Luke 16:19–26
Manuscript
Greek

ἄνθρωπος δέ τις ἦν πλούσιος καὶ ἐνεδιδύσκετο πορφύραν καὶ βύσσον

English

There was a rich man who was clothed in purple and fine linen and who feasted sumptuously every day. And at his gate was laid a poor man named Lazarus, covered with sores. The poor man died and was carried by the angels to Abraham’s bosom. The rich man also died, and in Hades, being in torment, he lifted up his eyes.

Translator's Notes

  1. §16:23: The term “Hades” (ᾅδης) in the Sinaiticus text reflects the Greek underworld concept rather than the later Christian notion of Hell. The parable’s afterlife geography draws on Second Temple Jewish cosmology.
