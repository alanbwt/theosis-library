Colossians 2:9-10: The Fullness of Deity Dwells Bodily

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Colossians 2:9–10 contains one of the most concentrated statements of incarnational theology in the Pauline corpus. In Christ, the whole fullness (pleroma, πλήρωμα) of deity dwells bodily—and believers have been filled in him. The passage makes two extraordinary claims simultaneously: that the totality of God inhabits a physical body, and that this same fullness is communicated to those who are “in him.”

The term pleroma became one of the most contested words in early Christian theology. For the Valentinian Gnostics, the Pleroma was the divine fullness—the totality of emanations from the supreme God—and the material world was a catastrophic fall from it. For the author of Colossians, the fullness is not distributed across a chain of emanations but concentrated entirely in one person, Christ, and through him made available to all believers. This is theosis stated with maximal directness: the divine fullness that dwells in Christ also fills those who belong to him.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:9: The word theotetos (θεότητος, “of deity”) is a hapax legomenon in the New Testament—it appears nowhere else. It is stronger than theiotes (“divine nature”), which Paul uses in Romans 1:20. Theotes denotes not merely divine qualities but the very essence of God. The adverb somatikos (“bodily”) intensifies the claim: this is not a spiritual or metaphorical indwelling but a physical one. The fullness of God’s essence has taken up residence in a body.
  2. §2:10: The verb pepleromenoi (“you have been filled”) is the perfect passive participle of pleroo, the verbal root of pleroma. The same root underlies both the fullness that dwells in Christ and the filling that believers receive. The grammatical connection implies an ontological one: the divine fullness that constitutes Christ’s being is communicated, in some measure, to those who participate in him. This is as close as Paul comes to a formal statement of theosis.
