Haereticarum Fabularum Compendium: On Valentinus (First English Translation)

Theodoret of Cyrrhus, c. 393-458 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Theodoret of Cyrrhus (c. 393–457) composed his Haereticarum Fabularum Compendium (Compendium of Heretical Fables) as the last major Greek heresiology of the patristic era. Book I treats the earliest Gnostic systems, and the section on Valentinus preserves material found nowhere else in the surviving record. Theodoret had access to sources now lost, and his account of Valentinian cosmology—the emanation of the aeons, the fall of Sophia, the production of the Demiurge—supplements and sometimes corrects what we find in Irenaeus and Hippolytus. This is the first English translation of this text. No complete rendering into English has previously been published.

The theological stakes are considerable. Theodoret writes not merely as a cataloguer but as a bishop defending Chalcedonian orthodoxy. His refutations reveal as much about fifth-century christological debates as they do about second-century Gnosticism. Where Valentinus taught that the divine fullness (pleroma) could only be accessed through gnosis, Theodoret insists that the Incarnation has made theosis available to all through the sacraments of the Church.

§I.7.1 · PG 83, col. 345A
Manuscript
Greek

Οὗτος ἐξ Αἰγύπτου παραγενόμενος εἰς τὴν Ῥώμην, ἐπεχείρησεν ἐκ τῶν Ῥλληνικῶν φιλοσόφων καὶ τῆς καλουμένης μυστικῆς θεολογίας συστῆσαι τῆς ἀσεβείας τὸ σύστημα· Βυθόν τινα ἀρχὴν τῶν ἁπάντων ὑπέθετο, καὶ τούτῳ τῷ Βυθῷ συνυπέστησε Σιγήν, ἥν καὶ Χάριν καὶ Ἔννοιαν προσηγόρευσε.

English

Valentinus, having come from Egypt to Rome, attempted to construct a system of impiety from the Greek philosophers and from the so-called mystical theology. He posited a certain Depth (Bythos, Βυθός) as the first principle of all things, and alongside this Depth he placed Silence (Sige, Σιγή), whom he also called Grace and Thought.

§I.7.2 · PG 83, col. 345B
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐκ Βυθοῦ καὶ Σιγῆς Νοῦν καὶ Ἀλήθειαν παρήγαγεν· ἐκ τούτων Λόγον καὶ Ζωήν· ἐκ τούτων πάλιν ῌΑνθρωπον καὶ Ἐκκλησίαν. Ταύτην τὴν ὀγδοάδα πρώτην ἀρχὴν καὶ `ρίζαν τῶν ἁπάντων προσηγόρευσε.

English

From Depth and Silence he derived Mind (Nous, Νοῦς) and Truth (Aletheia, Ἀλήθεια); from these, Word (Logos) and Life (Zoe, Ζωή); from these again, Man (Anthropos, ῌΑνθρωπος) and Church (Ekklesia, Ἐκκλησία). These eight he called the first Ogdoad, the root and foundation of all things.

§I.7.3 · PG 83, col. 345C–D
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΗ τελευταία τῶν τριάκοντα αἰώνων Σοφία πάθος ἔπαθεν· τὸ μέγεθος τοῦ Πατρὸς καταλαβεῖν ἐπεθύμησε χωρὶς τοῦ συζύγου· ἐκ ταύτης τῆς παρανόμου ἐπιθυμίας οὐσία τις ἄμορφος προήχθη, ἥν ἔκτρωμα τῆς Σοφίας προσηγόρευσε.

English

The last of the thirty aeons, Sophia (Wisdom, Σοφία), suffered a passion: she desired to comprehend the greatness of the Father without the consent of her consort. From this unlawful desire arose a formless substance, which he called the abortion of Sophia. And this passion, separated from the Pleroma, became the material from which the visible world was fashioned.

§I.7.4 · PG 83, col. 348A
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΟ Δημιουργός, φησί, τόνδε τὸν κόσμον ἐδημιούργησεν ἀγνοῶν, τοὺς ὑπερκειμένους αἰῶνας οὐκ εἰδώς· μόνον ἑαυτὸν εἶναι Θεὸν ὀιηθείς.

English

The Demiurge, he says, fashioned this world in ignorance, not knowing the aeons above him. He imagined himself to be the only God, and said through the prophets, “I am God and there is no other.” But in truth he was merely the craftsman of matter, ignorant of the spiritual realities above him.

§I.7.5 · PG 83, col. 348B–C
Manuscript
Greek

Πρὸς ταῦτα φημί· εἰ ἄγνωστος ὁ Πατὴρ καὶ τοῖς ἐξ αὐτοῦ προελθοῦσιν αἰῶσι, πῶς οὗτοι διὰ τῶν οἰκείων πλασμάτων γνῶναι αὐτὸν ἐπαγγέλλονται;

English

Against these fables I say: if the Father is unknowable even to the aeons that proceed from him, how can these men claim to know him through their own fabricated genealogies? The Church confesses one God, one Christ, one salvation through the Incarnation—not through elaborate mythologies but through the Word made flesh, who deifies (theopoiei, θεοποιεῖ) those who receive him in faith.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.7.1: Theodoret’s account of Valentinus parallels Irenaeus (Against Heresies I.1) but includes distinctive details suggesting access to independent sources. The pairing of Depth and Silence as the primal syzygy is consistent across all heresiological accounts, but Theodoret’s addition of “Grace” (Χάρις) as an alternate name for Silence is found only here.
  2. §I.7.3: The fall of Sophia is the central drama of Valentinian cosmogony. Her “passion” (πάθος) produces both the material world and the possibility of redemption. Theodoret’s language of “abortion” (ἔκτρωμα) follows the earlier heresiologists and reflects the Valentinian view that matter is a tragic by-product of spiritual overreach.
  3. §I.7.5: The verb theopoiei (θεοποιεῖ) is Theodoret’s own term for theosis, used in his refutation rather than in the Valentinian system itself. This passage demonstrates how fifth-century orthodox theology had fully absorbed the language of deification as a counter to Gnostic soteriology: salvation comes not through secret knowledge but through the transformative power of the Incarnation.
