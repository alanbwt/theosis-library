Introduction

Nemesius of Emesa’s On the Nature of Man (c. 390 CE) is a Christian philosophical anthropology that synthesizes Greek medical knowledge, Platonic psychology, and biblical theology. It became an important source for later Byzantine and Scholastic thought.

§Chapter 1 · Nemesius, On the Nature of Man
Manuscript
Greek

καλῶς ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἐκ ψυχῆς λογικῆς καὶ σώματος συνέστηκεν

English

Man is beautifully constituted from a rational soul and a body, so that if he were soul alone, without a body, he would not be able to perceive material things; and if he were body alone, without a soul, he would not be able to perceive intelligible things. But now, since he is constituted from both, he is able to engage with both kinds of reality. For this reason some have called man a microcosm, saying that he contains all that the universe contains.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: The “microcosm” doctrine—that the human being contains in miniature everything the universe contains—links Greek philosophical anthropology to the biblical claim that humans are made in God’s image. For Nemesius, humanity’s dual nature (material and spiritual) is not a defect but a unique vocation: to bridge the visible and invisible worlds.
