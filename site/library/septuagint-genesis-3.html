Introduction

Genesis 3 recounts the fall: the serpent’s temptation, the eating of the forbidden fruit, and the expulsion from Eden. The serpent’s promise—“you will be like God”—frames the entire theosis question as a matter of means, not ends.

§3:22–24 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ εἶπεν ὁ θεός · ἰδοὺ Αδαμ γέγονεν ὡς εἷς ἐξ ἡμῶν

English

And God said, “Behold, Adam has become like one of us, knowing good and evil. And now, lest he stretch out his hand and take also from the tree of life and eat, and live forever—” So the Lord God sent him out of the garden of delight to work the ground from which he was taken.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:5: The serpent’s promise “you will be like gods” (ἔσεσθε ὡς θεοί) is, paradoxically, true—God confirms it in 3:22. The problem is not the aspiration to godlikeness but the means: seizing it through disobedience rather than receiving it through participation. The Church Fathers argued that theosis is the legitimate fulfillment of what the serpent offered illegitimately.
