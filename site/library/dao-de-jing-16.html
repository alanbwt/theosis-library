Tao Te Ching 16: Returning to the Root

Lao Tzu (attributed), c. 6th c. BCE

Introduction

Chapter 16 of the Tao Te Ching (also written Dao De Jing), attributed to Laozi (traditionally 6th century BCE), is among the most mystical passages in Chinese philosophy. It describes the return to the root (gui gen, 歸根) as the fundamental movement of the Tao—a cosmic stillness underlying all change, to which all things naturally return. The one who perceives this return attains constancy, clarity, and a form of immortality that is not the preservation of the self but its dissolution into the eternal.

The chapter’s relevance to the theosis question lies in its vision of human perfection as return rather than ascent. Where the Greek and Christian traditions often speak of rising toward God, Laozi speaks of sinking back into the source—emptying the self until nothing remains but the Tao itself. The direction is different; the destination is recognisably similar.

§Ch. 16, lines 1–4 · Mawangdui Silk Text A (c. 168 BCE)
Manuscript
Chinese

致虛極也，守靜篤也。萬物並作，吾以觀其復。夫物芸芸，各歸其根。

English

Attain the utmost emptiness. Hold firm to deep stillness. The ten thousand things arise together, and I watch their return. All things flourish, and each returns to its root.

§Ch. 16, lines 5–8 · Mawangdui Silk Text A
Manuscript
Chinese

歸根曰靜，靜曰復命。復命曰常，知常曰明。

English

Returning to the root is called stillness. Stillness is called returning to life. Returning to life is called the constant. Knowing the constant is called clarity.

§Ch. 16, lines 9–13 · Mawangdui Silk Text A
Manuscript
Chinese

不知常，妄作凶。知常容，容乃公，公乃王，王乃天，天乃道，道乃久，殒身不殆。

English

Not knowing the constant, one acts blindly and invites disaster. Knowing the constant, one is all-embracing. Being all-embracing, one is impartial. Being impartial, one is kingly. Being kingly, one is heavenly. Being heavenly, one is the Tao. Being the Tao, one endures. The body perishes, but there is no danger.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 16, lines 1–4: The Mawangdui silk manuscripts (discovered 1973) represent the oldest surviving copies of the Tao Te Ching and differ in significant ways from the received text. The word du (篤, “firm, sincere”) in the Mawangdui version replaces the more common du (篤, “centredness”) of the received text. The imperative to “attain the utmost emptiness” resonates with the Buddhist concept of sunyata and the Christian apophatic tradition’s insistence on emptying the mind of all concepts about God.
  2. §Ch. 16, lines 9–13: The ascending chain—from all-embracing to impartial to kingly to heavenly to the Tao—describes a progressive identification with larger and larger realities, culminating in identity with the Tao itself. The final claim—“the body perishes, but there is no danger”—is not a promise of personal survival after death but an assertion that one who is identified with the Tao is beyond the reach of death because death only touches the individual, not the eternal.
