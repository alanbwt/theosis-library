Introduction

Chapter 53 of the Enchiridion collects maxims for daily practice, including the famous injunction to play one’s assigned role well. Life is a stage, and virtue consists in performing whatever part is given with excellence.

Greek original and English translation.
§Chapter 53 · Epictetus, Enchiridion 53.1–4 (Schenkl)
Manuscript
Greek

ἄγου δέ μ᾽, ὦ Ζεῦ, καὶ σύ γ᾽ ἡ Πεπρωμένη

English

On every occasion keep these sayings at hand: lead me, O Zeus, and you, O Destiny, wherever you have assigned me. I shall follow without hesitation; but even if I become unwilling in a bad cause, I shall follow all the same. Whoever has rightly yielded to necessity is counted wise among us and knows the ways of the gods.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 53: The opening quotation is from Cleanthes’s prayer to Zeus and Destiny. By ending the Enchiridion with this prayer, Epictetus frames the entire Stoic practical teaching within a theological context: the philosophical life is ultimately one of willing alignment with divine providence.
