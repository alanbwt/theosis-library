Introduction

The Atlakviða (“Lay of Atli”) is an archaic heroic poem recounting the Burgundian king Gunnarr’s death at the court of Atli (Attila the Hun). It preserves a version of the Nibelung legend older than the German Nibelungenlied.

§Stanzas 34–36 · Poetic Edda, Atlakviða 34–36
Manuscript
Greek

Lifanda gram lǪgu í garð orma; en hann einn þá á horpu slu, fótum óbundum, fyr orma lagði; glammuðu strengir.

English

Gunnarr was placed living in a pit of serpents. His hands were bound, but he struck the harp with his feet, playing so skillfully that the strings sang. Thus should a bold ring-giver guard his gold against men.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanzas 34–36: Gunnarr playing the harp with his feet in the snake pit is one of the most iconic images in Germanic heroic literature. The scene appears carved on the Ramsund stone (c. 1040 CE) in Sweden. The poem celebrates defiance in defeat—the core Norse heroic ethic.
