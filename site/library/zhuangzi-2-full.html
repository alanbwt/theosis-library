§Chapter 2: The Piping of Heaven · Zhuangzi, Inner Chapters, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Greek

大道不称 大辩不言

English

The Great Way is not named. Great discriminations are not spoken. Great benevolence is not benevolent. Great modesty is not humble. Great courage does not attack. For the Way that is displayed is not the Way. Words that discriminate fail to reach the mark. Benevolence that has a constant object loses its scope. Modesty that is always clean is not sincere. Courage that attacks is not courageous. Once a man dreamed he was a butterfly, fluttering about joyfully. He did not know he was a man. Suddenly he awoke, and there he was, a man again. But he did not know whether he was a man who had dreamed he was a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming he was a man.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 2: The butterfly dream is the most famous passage in all of Chinese philosophy. It does not merely raise the question of whether we are dreaming; it challenges the very category of personal identity. If the transformation between man and butterfly is real, then the fixed self is an illusion—a conclusion that profoundly influenced Chan Buddhism's doctrine of no-self.
