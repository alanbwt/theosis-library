Histories IV.32-36: The Hyperboreans and Their Sacred Offerings

Herodotus, c. 484-425 BCE

Introduction

Herodotus provides the earliest detailed account of the Hyperboreans in his Histories (IV.32–36), written around 430 BCE. While Herodotus himself expresses skepticism about the existence of Hyperborea, he faithfully records the traditions surrounding them, including the remarkable story of sacred offerings sent from the far north to the island of Delos through a chain of intermediary peoples. He also preserves the haunting tale of two Hyperborean maidens, Hyperoche and Laodice, who traveled to Delos to fulfill a vow to Artemis and never returned home.

The passage is significant because it shows that by the fifth century BCE, the Hyperborean tradition was already ancient and deeply embedded in Greek religious practice. The offerings to Delos—wrapped in wheat straw and passed from nation to nation—suggest a real trade route overlaid with mythic meaning. For the theosis tradition, the Hyperboreans represent a people who maintained an unbroken relationship with the divine, sending gifts to Apollo’s birthplace as naturally as breathing. They are humanity as it was meant to be: connected, generous, and oriented toward the sacred.

§IV.32 · Herodotus, Histories IV.32
Manuscript
Greek

Περὶ δὲ τῶν Ὑπερβορέων ἀνθρώπων οὔτε τι Σκύθαι λέγουσι οὐδὲν οὔτε τινες ἄλλοι τῶν ταύτῃ οἰκημένων, εἰ μὴ ἄρα Ἰσσηδόνες. ὡς δὲ ἐγὼ δοκέω, οὐδὲ οὗτοι λέγουσι οὐδέν· ἐλεγον γὰρ ἂν καὶ Σκύθαι. Ἀλλὰ Ἡσίοδός τε εἶρηκε περὶ Ὑπερβορέων καὶ ὅμηρος ἐν Ἐπιγόνοισι.

English

Concerning the Hyperborean people, neither the Scythians nor any other inhabitants of these lands tell us anything, unless it be the Issedones. And, as I believe, even they say nothing; for if they did, then the Scythians too would report it, just as they report concerning the one-eyed men. But Hesiod has spoken of Hyperboreans, and Homer too in the Epigoni, if Homer truly composed that poem.

§IV.33 · Herodotus, Histories IV.33
Manuscript
Greek

Λέγουσι δὲ πλεῖστα περὶ αὐτῶν Δήλιοι· φάμενοι ἱρὰ ἐνδεδεμένα ἐν καλάμῃ πυρῶν ἐξ Ὑπερβορέων φερόμενα ἀπικνέεσθαι ἐς Σκύθας.

English

But those who speak most about them are the Delians. They say that sacred offerings wrapped in wheat straw are brought from the Hyperboreans and come to the Scythians; from the Scythians each neighboring nation in succession receives them, and they carry them to the furthest west, to the Adriatic; from there they are sent southward, and the Dodoneans are the first of the Greeks to receive them. From Dodona they descend to the Malian Gulf and cross over to Euboea, where city sends them on to city until they reach Carystus; after that they pass by Andros, for the Carystians carry them to Tenos, and the Tenians to Delos.

§IV.34 · Herodotus, Histories IV.34
Manuscript
Greek

τὸ δὲ πρῶτον τοὺς Ὑπερβορέους πέμψαι φερούσας τὰ ἱρὰ δύο κόρας, τὰς Ὀνομάζουσι Δήλιοι εἶναι Ὑπερόχην τε καὶ Λαοδίκην.

English

In this way, they say, these offerings come to Delos. But at first, they say, the Hyperboreans sent two maidens to bear the offerings: the Delians say their names were Hyperoche and Laodice. Together with them, for their safety, the Hyperboreans sent five men of their people to escort them—those who are now called Perpherees and are greatly honored in Delos. But when those sent by the Hyperboreans did not return home, the Hyperboreans, thinking it a terrible thing if they should always fail to receive their envoys back, thereafter wrapped their offerings in wheat straw and carried them to their borders, charging their neighbors to send them on to the next nation.

§IV.35 · Herodotus, Histories IV.35
Manuscript
Greek

πρότερον δὲ τούτων ἄλλας δύο παρθένους προπέμψαι τοὺς Ὑπερβορέους, Ἄργην τε καὶ Ἤπιν.

English

And they say that Hyperoche and Laodice, before these maidens, two other Hyperborean virgins had passed through these same nations to Delos: Arge and Opis. And the Delian women collect gifts for them, calling upon their names in the hymn which Olen, a man of Lycia, composed for them; and from the Delians the islanders and the Ionians have learned to do the same, calling upon the names of Opis and Arge and collecting gifts. This Olen came from Lycia and composed also the other ancient hymns that are sung at Delos.

§IV.36 · Herodotus, Histories IV.36
Manuscript
Greek

τοσαῦτα μέν περὶ Ὑπερβορέων εἰρήσθω. τὸν γὰρ περὶ Ἀβάριος λόγον τοῦ λεγομένου εἶναι Ὑπερβορέου οὐ λέγω.

English

This much then is said about the Hyperboreans. For I am not going to tell the story of Abaris, who is said to have been a Hyperborean, and to have carried an arrow around the whole earth while eating nothing. But if there are men beyond the north wind, then there are also men beyond the south wind. And I laugh when I see that many have drawn maps of the world, and not one of them has given a sensible account of it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §IV.32: Herodotus’s skepticism is characteristic: he reports the tradition without endorsing it. His mention of Hesiod and Homer places the Hyperborean tradition among the oldest strata of Greek mythology, predating even the classical period. The reference to the Epigoni (a lost epic about the sons of the Seven against Thebes) suggests the Hyperboreans appeared in epic poetry now lost to us.
  2. §IV.33: The chain of transmission for the sacred offerings—from the Hyperboreans through the Scythians, to the Adriatic, down through Greece, island to island until reaching Delos—traces what may be a genuine ancient trade route from the Baltic or northern Europe to the Aegean. The “offerings wrapped in wheat straw” have been interpreted as amber, which was indeed traded south from the Baltic along routes matching Herodotus’s description.
  3. §IV.34: The story of Hyperoche and Laodice is poignant: the Hyperboreans sent their own maidens on a sacred mission, and when they did not return, devised the impersonal relay system instead. This reflects a theological theme central to theosis: the cost of maintaining connection with the divine. The Hyperboreans’ devotion to Apollo was so deep that they continued sending offerings even after losing their daughters to the journey.
  4. §IV.36: Herodotus’s brief mention of Abaris the Hyperborean—the flying priest of Apollo who carried an arrow around the world while eating nothing—is one of the most tantalizing references in ancient literature. Abaris became a major figure in later Pythagorean and Neoplatonic tradition, representing the possibility of human transcendence over physical limitations. His ability to survive without food and to fly suggests a being who has achieved something very close to theosis: a human who lives by divine power alone.
