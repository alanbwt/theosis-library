Bundahishn 1: Creation Through Thought

Zarathustra (Zoroaster), c. 1500-1000 BCE

Introduction

The Bundahishn (Primal Creation) is the Zoroastrian account of cosmogony—how the world came into being. Composed in Middle Persian (Pahlavi) during the Sassanid and early Islamic periods (6th–9th century CE), it draws on far older Avestan traditions to tell the story of Ahura Mazda’s creation through thought and the primordial conflict between light and darkness. Chapter 1 describes the initial state: Ohrmazd (the Pahlavi form of Ahura Mazda) dwells in endless light above; Ahriman (Angra Mainyu) lurks in endless darkness below. Between them is the Void.

What makes this creation account remarkable is that Ohrmazd creates first through thought—he conceives creation in his mind before it takes material form. This idea of creation by thought or word parallels Genesis 1 (“And God said, let there be light”), John 1 (“In the beginning was the Logos”), and the Egyptian Memphite theology in which Ptah creates through the thought of his heart and the word of his tongue. The Zoroastrian version may be the earliest surviving systematic account of creation by divine intellect.

§1.1–2 · Greater Bundahishn, Chapter 1, Sections 1–2
Manuscript
Middle Persian

Āghāhīh ī dēn ī weh ēdōn pad-ash rōshaniāgīh. Ohrmazd pad hār-wisp āgāhīh ud wehih ī hamē būd. Rōshnīh gāh ud gyāg ī Ohrmazd. Kē-sh asar rōshnīh xānēnd.

English

Thus it is revealed in the Good Religion: Ohrmazd was on high in omniscience and goodness; for infinite time He was ever in the light. That light is the space and place of Ohrmazd. Some call it endless light. That omniscience and goodness are the garment of Ohrmazd. Some call it the Religion. Both are infinite and eternal.

§1.3–4 · Greater Bundahishn, Chapter 1, Sections 3–4
Manuscript
Middle Persian

Ahriman pad zōfrīh ī āgāhīh ud āz-kāmagih ī hamē būd. Ān āz-kāmagīh jāmag ī ōy. Ān-īz a-kanaragomānd ud hamēwāyag. Miyanīg tōhigīh būd. Kē-sh wāy xānēnd.

English

Ahriman was deep in the abyss, in slowness of knowledge and the lust to smite. The lust to smite is his garment. That too is infinite and eternal. Between them was the Void. Some call it the Wind—the place where the two spirits mingle.

§1.7–9 · Greater Bundahishn, Chapter 1, Sections 7–9
Manuscript
Middle Persian

Ohrmazd pad hār-wisp āgāhīh dānisht ku Ganāg Mēnōg hast, ud hameōāwēzēd, ud hamē āwēzēd ī ōy ū arzh bun ahr. Paēstāg dām-ī ān ī abbāyishnīg mēnōgomānd frāz brēhēnīd.

English

Ohrmazd, through omniscience, knew that the Destructive Spirit existed, that he would attack, and that his attack would be mingled with envy. He first created by thought (mēnōg, spiritual form) the creatures that would be needful. For three thousand years they remained in spiritual form—thinking, unmoving, and intangible.

§1.14–16 · Greater Bundahishn, Chapter 1, Sections 14–16
Manuscript
Middle Persian

Pad Ohrmazd ahūnwar frāz srūd, ud Ganāg Mēnōg rā ōy frazaft hāmīhā ī xāgīst pad dīd. Kē stūt ud star xward, ud pad tārīgīh ōfrāft.

English

Then Ohrmazd recited the Ahunwar—the most sacred prayer—and revealed to the Destructive Spirit his own ultimate defeat. Ahriman saw it and was confounded, and fell back into the darkness. He lay stunned for three thousand years. Then Ohrmazd created the world in material form (gētīg): first the sky, then water, then earth, then plants, then the primordial bull, then the first man, Gayomard.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1–2: The phrase “omniscience and goodness are the garment of Ohrmazd” parallels Psalm 104:2 (“who covers himself with light as with a garment”) and 1 Timothy 6:16 (“who dwells in unapproachable light”). The identification of light with divine nature is one of the deepest shared intuitions across traditions. In the Zoroastrian case, light is not a metaphor but an ontological statement: Ohrmazd’s very being is light, and darkness is not merely the absence of light but the substance of the adversary.
  2. §1.7–9: The concept of mēnōg (spiritual, thought-form) versus gētīg (material, physical form) is the Zoroastrian equivalent of the Platonic distinction between the intelligible and sensible worlds. Creation happens first in the mind of God, then is made material. The three-thousand-year period of purely spiritual existence parallels the Kabbalistic concept of the tzimtzum (divine contraction) and the Neoplatonic emanation: the material world is always secondary to and derived from the spiritual. What distinguishes the Zoroastrian account is that matter is not a degradation of spirit but a deliberate divine act—the material world is good, created purposefully by Ohrmazd to be the arena in which evil is finally defeated.
