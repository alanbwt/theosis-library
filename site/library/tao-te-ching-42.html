Tao Te Ching 42: The Tao Produces the One

Lao Tzu (attributed), c. 6th c. BCE

Introduction

Chapter 42 of the Tao Te Ching contains the Taoist emanation sequence, one of the most compressed cosmogonies in world literature. In four short phrases, Lao Tzu traces the entire unfolding of reality from the undifferentiated Tao to the multiplicity of the ten thousand things. The sequence—Tao produces One, One produces Two, Two produces Three, Three produces the ten thousand things—has been interpreted through every lens available to Chinese philosophy: the One as primordial qi (氣), the Two as yin and yang, the Three as their harmonious interaction.

For the theosis tradition, this passage is significant because it describes a process that is both emanation and return. The ten thousand things “carry yin and embrace yang,” and their vital breath (qi) achieves harmony. The movement outward from unity to multiplicity implies a corresponding movement back. The Taoist sage, by emptying the self and returning to simplicity, reverses the emanation and reunites with the source—a structure remarkably parallel to the Neoplatonic procession and return.

The Tao produces the One. The One produces the Two. The Two produces the Three. The Three produces the ten thousand things.

萬物負陰而抱陽，沖氣以為和。

English

The ten thousand things carry yin on their backs and embrace yang. Vital breath (qi, 氣) blends these and creates harmony.

§Chapter 42, lines 5–7 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 42
Manuscript
Chinese

人之所惡，唯孤、寡、不穀，而王公以為稱。故物或損之而益，或益之而損。

English

What people detest is to be orphaned, lonely, and unworthy. Yet kings and lords call themselves by these names. Thus things are sometimes diminished by being increased, and increased by being diminished.

§Chapter 42, lines 8–9 · Tao Te Ching, Chapter 42
Manuscript
Chinese

人之所教，我亦教之。強梁者不得其死，吾將以為教父。

English

What others teach, I also teach: “The violent and aggressive do not die a natural death.” I shall take this as the father of my teaching.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 42, line 1: The identity of the “One,” “Two,” and “Three” has been debated for over two thousand years. The most common reading identifies the One with primordial qi or undifferentiated breath, the Two with yin and yang, and the Three with their blending. The Han dynasty commentator Wang Bi identified the One with the initial unity of being, the Two with the duality of being and non-being, and the Three with their mutual generation. The Gnostic parallel to the Valentinian emanation from the Monad through the Dyad to the Pleroma is striking, though there is no evidence of direct contact.
  2. §Chapter 42, line 3: The term qi (氣) is often translated as “energy” or “breath,” but neither captures its full meaning. Qi is the fundamental substance-energy of the cosmos in Chinese thought, neither purely material nor purely spiritual. It is closer to the Stoic pneuma than to any modern Western concept.
