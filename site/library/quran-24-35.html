Quran 24:35: The Light Verse

Quran (divine revelation), c. 610-632 AD

Introduction

Quran 24:35, known as the Light Verse (Ayat an-Nur), is widely regarded as the most mystical verse in the Quran. It has generated more commentary than perhaps any other single verse in Islamic intellectual history, from the Sunni theologian al-Ghazali’s Mishkat al-Anwar (Niche of Lights) to the Sufi interpretations of Ibn Arabi and Rumi. The verse describes God as the light of the heavens and the earth, then elaborates this through a complex layered image: a niche containing a lamp, the lamp within a glass, the glass like a brilliant star, fuelled by the oil of a blessed olive tree.

The verse’s importance for the theosis question lies in its affirmation that divine light is not merely a metaphor but the fundamental reality underlying all perception and all being. The Sufi tradition read it as describing stages of spiritual illumination: the niche is the body, the glass is the heart, the lamp is the spirit, and the oil is the divine grace that burns without needing to be lit. The verse concludes with a statement that reverberates through all mystical traditions: “Light upon light.”

English

God is the Light of the heavens and the earth. The likeness of His light is as a niche in which is a lamp. The lamp is in a glass. The glass is as it were a brilliant star.

English

Fuelled from a blessed tree, an olive, neither of the East nor of the West, whose oil would almost glow even if no fire touched it. Light upon light. God guides to His light whom He wills.

Translator's Notes

  1. §24:35a: The opening declaration—“God is the Light of the heavens and the earth”—was interpreted in two major ways. The Mu’tazilites and most Sunni theologians read it as “God is the Illuminator”—that is, God is the source of light but is not literally light. The Sufis and some Ash’ari theologians read it more directly: God is light itself, the luminous reality underlying all existence. Al-Ghazali’s Mishkat al-Anwar devotes an entire treatise to this distinction.
  2. §24:35b: The phrase “neither of the East nor of the West” has been read as indicating transcendence: the olive tree belongs to no particular place because it represents a reality that transcends spatial location. The oil that “would almost glow even if no fire touched it” suggests a luminosity inherent in the natural order—a divine light already present before any external illumination. The Sufis identified this with the fitra (primordial nature) of the human soul: it is inherently luminous and needs only to be uncovered, not ignited.
