Sirach 17:1-4: Strength Like His Own

Solomon (attributed), traditional

Introduction

Sirach 17:1–4 offers a meditation on the creation of humanity that expands Genesis 1–2 with remarkable theological boldness. Ben Sira declares not only that God created humankind from earth and returns them to it, but that God “endowed them with strength like his own” and “made them in his own image.” The phrase “strength like his own” (καθʼ ἑαυτὸν ἐνέδυσεν αὐτούς) implies a real communication of divine power to the creature, not mere external resemblance.

The passage continues with the gift of dominion over the animal world, echoing the Priestly creation account. But for the theosis tradition, the critical claim is the endowment of divine-like strength: humanity is not merely God’s image-bearer in some abstract sense but a creature equipped with capacities that mirror the Creator’s own. This text influenced the patristic distinction between “image” (given at creation) and “likeness” (achieved through cooperation with grace)—a distinction that became the backbone of Eastern Christian anthropology.

English

He endowed them with strength like his own, and made them in his own image. He put the fear of them upon all flesh, and gave them dominion over beasts and birds.

Translator's Notes

  1. §17:3: The phrase “endowed them with strength like his own” (καθʼ ἑαυτὸν ἐνέδυσεν αὐτούς ἰσχύν) literally reads “he clothed them with strength like himself.” The verb “clothed” (ἐνέδυσεν) is significant: it suggests an external gift placed upon the creature, not an innate property. Yet the gift is “like his own”—a genuine communication of divine power. This clothing metaphor connects to Paul’s language of “putting on Christ” (Galatians 3:27) and the patristic theme of the “garment of glory” lost at the Fall and restored in Christ.
  2. §17:1: The juxtaposition of creation from earth and return to earth establishes the paradox of the human condition: humanity is simultaneously dust and the image of God, mortal matter endowed with divine strength. Sirach does not resolve the tension; he states both truths side by side. The theosis tradition finds in this tension the starting point for the entire drama of redemption: the creature made from dust and destined for dust is also made for incorruption.
