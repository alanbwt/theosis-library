Exposition of the Orthodox Faith III.15: The Final Synthesis

Ἔκδοσις Ἀκριβὴς τῆς Ὀρθοδόξου Πίστεως
John of Damascus, c. 675–749 AD

Introduction

John of Damascus wrote the Exposition of the Orthodox Faith (Ekdosis akribes tes orthodoxou pisteos) around 743 CE, making it one of the last great systematic theologies of the patristic period. It is the third part of his larger Fount of Knowledge and functions as a compendium of the theological consensus achieved over seven centuries of doctrinal controversy.

Book III, chapter 15 addresses the theosis of Christ’s human nature and, by extension, the theosis of all human nature. John’s treatment is a careful synthesis: he draws on Maximus the Confessor’s Christology, the Cappadocian doctrine of the Spirit, and Cyril of Alexandria’s insistence on real participation. The passage serves as the final patristic summation of the theosis doctrine before the medieval period.

§III.15 · Expositio Fidei Orthodoxae III.15 (Kotter ed., PTS 12; PG 94.1057B–D)
Manuscript
Greek

Εθεώθη η σαρξ και Θεός εγένετο και ομόθεος τω Λόγω τη προς αυτον ενώσει, ουδετέρας των φύσεων τροπήν υπομεινάσης· καθάπερ ο πεπυρωμένος σίδηρος καίει, ουχ ότι λογισμω τινι το καίειν κεκτηται, αλλα τη προς το πυρ ενώσει τουτο κεκτημένος.

English

The flesh was deified and made God and made equal in divinity with the Word by the union with him, without either nature undergoing any change. And the flesh of the Lord was enriched with divine operations on account of its most pure union with the Word, that is, by a hypostatic union, without any loss of its natural properties. For it is not by its own operation that the flesh acts divinely, but by the Word united with it, since the Word manifests its own operation through the flesh. For a red-hot iron burns, not because it has acquired the power of burning by some process of reasoning, but because it has come to possess this power through its union with the fire. The flesh, then, was mortal in itself but life-giving through its hypostatic union with the Word. And we say in like manner that the deification of the will was not a change in its natural character but a union with the Word — its own natural will being fulfilled in his divine and omnipotent will — so that the human nature has all the riches of the divine nature.

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.15: The red-hot iron analogy (πεπυρωμένος σίδηρος) is one of the most famous images in patristic Christology. Iron placed in fire becomes fiery: it glows, it burns, it radiates heat. But it does not cease to be iron. Likewise, Christ’s human nature is deified by union with the divine nature without ceasing to be human. John extends this logic to all human theosis: we become God by participation and union, not by a change in our created nature. The term εθεώθη (“was deified”) is a passive verb — the flesh does not deify itself but is deified by the Word. This preserves the asymmetry between divine initiative and human reception.
