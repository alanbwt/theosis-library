Description of Greece: Hyperborean Visitors to Delphi and Delos

Pausanias, c. 110-180 AD

Introduction

Pausanias, the second-century CE travel writer, encountered the Hyperborean tradition at multiple sacred sites across Greece and recorded his findings in his Description of Greece. Unlike Herodotus, who treated the Hyperboreans as a distant curiosity, Pausanias found their memory actively preserved in the religious practices of Delphi, Delos, and Olympia. His scattered references—in Books I, V, and X—collectively paint a picture of the Hyperboreans as founding figures of Greek religion: they established the Oracle at Delphi, they sent the first offerings to Delos, and their emissary Olen composed the earliest hymns.

For the theosis tradition, Pausanias’s testimony is crucial because it shows the Hyperboreans not as distant mythological figures but as active participants in the founding of Greek sacred institutions. If the Hyperboreans established the Oracle at Delphi and composed the first hymns to Apollo, then the entire apparatus of Greek prophecy and worship derives from a people who lived in unbroken communion with the divine. Greek religion, in this reading, is an inheritance from paradise—a set of practices transmitted from those who knew the gods directly to those who must approach them through ritual and sacrifice.

§I.31.2 · Pausanias, Description of Greece I.31.2
Manuscript
Greek

Ἤλήν, ὃς προφήτης πρῶτος Ἀπόλλωνος ἐγένετο καὶ τοὺς ὕμνους τοὺς ἀρχαιοτάτους ἐποίησεν, Ἀργην καὶ Ἤπιν ἐξ Ὑπερβορέων ἐς Δῆλον ἀφικέσθαι λέγει.

English

Olen, who became the first prophet of Apollo and the first to compose hexameter verses, says that the Hyperborean maidens Arge and Opis came to Delos even before Hyperoche and Laodice. The latter came to bring to Eileithyia the tribute they had vowed for the ease of child-bearing, but Arge and Opis, they say, came with the gods themselves, and distinct honors are paid to them by the Delians.

§V.7.7–9 · Pausanias, Description of Greece V.7.7–9
Manuscript
Greek

τὸν Ἡρακλέα τὸν Ἰδαῖον, πρεσβύτατον πέντε ὄντα ἀδελφῶν, συστήσασθαι τὸν ἀγῶνα δρόμου ἐν Ὀλυμπίᾳ.

English

They say that Heracles of Ida, being the eldest of five brothers, set up the contest of the foot-race at Olympia, and crowned the winner with a branch of wild olive. And they say that this olive grew so abundantly there that they used the still green leaves to make beds to sleep upon. This Heracles, the Idaean, came from the land of the Hyperboreans. It was he who first established the Olympic Games, and gave Olympia its name.

§X.5.7–9 · Pausanias, Description of Greece X.5.7–9
Manuscript
Greek

τὸν δὲ ἀρχαιότατον ναὸν τοῦ Ἀπόλλωνος ἐν Δελφοῖς δάφνης ποιηθῆναι λέγουσιν.

English

The most ancient temple of Apollo at Delphi is said to have been made of laurel, the branches being brought from the laurel in Tempe. This temple would have had the form of a hut. The Delphians say that the second temple was made by bees from wax and feathers, and that it was sent to the Hyperboreans by Apollo. Another account says that the temple was built by a Delphian named Pteras, and that the temple received its name from the builder. After this they say a brazen temple was built by Hephaestus. The fourth temple was built by Trophonius and Agamedes, and this one was of stone.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.31.2: The figure of Olen is remarkable: a Hyperborean who became the first prophet of Apollo and composed the first hexameter hymns. If this tradition is taken seriously, it means that Greek sacred poetry originates in Hyperborea. The Hyperborean maidens Arge and Opis, who “came with the gods themselves,” occupy a liminal space between human and divine—they are mortals who travel in divine company, embodying the Hyperborean closeness to the gods that the theosis tradition holds up as humanity’s original and intended state.
  2. §V.7.7–9: Pausanias distinguishes between the Idaean Heracles—a Hyperborean figure associated with the founding of the Olympic Games—and the more famous Heracles son of Zeus. The Hyperborean Heracles is older and, in Pausanias’s account, the true founder of Olympia. This Hyperborean origin for the Games reinforces Pindar’s account of the olive tree’s Hyperborean provenance: the most important institution of Greek athletic-religious culture traces back to the paradise beyond the north wind.
  3. §X.5.7–9: The tradition that the second temple at Delphi was made of wax and feathers and “sent to the Hyperboreans by Apollo” is extraordinary. It suggests a reciprocal relationship: the Hyperboreans send offerings to Delos, and Apollo sends a temple to the Hyperboreans. The temple of wax and feathers is an impossible, dream-like structure—built from the materials of bees and birds, creatures of air and sweetness. It represents a mode of sacred architecture that belongs to paradise rather than to ordinary human construction.
