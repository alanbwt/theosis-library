Hebrews 1:1-4: The Radiance of God's Glory

Πρὸς Ἑβραίους
Author of Hebrews, c. 60-90 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

The opening of Hebrews is one of the most exalted christological statements in the New Testament. In four verses, the author moves from the fragmentary revelations of the prophets to the definitive revelation in the Son, whom he describes as the radiance of God's glory and the exact imprint of his nature. The Greek term charakter (χαρακτήρ) originally referred to the stamp pressed into wax or metal—an image that exactly reproduces its source. Combined with hypostasis (ὑπόστασις), the underlying reality or substance of God, the claim is breathtaking: the Son is the precise reproduction of the divine essence itself.

For the theosis tradition, this passage establishes the metaphysical ground. If the Son is the exact imprint of God's nature, and if humanity is being conformed to the image of the Son, then the trajectory is clear: through Christ, human beings are drawn toward the very substance of God. The text here follows Codex Sinaiticus, the fourth-century manuscript that provides our earliest complete witness.

Greek

πολυμερῶς καὶ πολυτρόπως πάλαι ὁ θεὸς λαλήσας τοῖς πατράσιν ἐν τοῖς προφήταις · ἐπ’ ἐσχάτου τῶν ἡμερῶν τούτων ἐλάλησεν ἡμῖν ἐν υἱῷ · ὃν ἔθηκεν κληρονόμον πάντων · δι’ οὗ καὶ ἐποίησεν τοὺς αἰῶνας ·

English

In many parts and in many ways God spoke long ago to the fathers through the prophets. In these last days he has spoken to us in a Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, through whom also he made the ages.

§1:2b–4 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ὃς ὢν ἀπαύγασμα τῆς δόξης καὶ χαρακτὴρ τῆς ὑποστάσεως αὐτοῦ · φέρων τε τὰ πάντα τῷ ῥήματι τῆς δυνάμεως αὐτοῦ · καθαρισμὸν τῶν ἁμαρτιῶν ποιησάμενος ἐκάθισεν ἐν δεξιᾷ τῆς μεγαλωσύνης ἐν ὑψηλοῖς · τοσούτῳ κρείττων γενόμενος τῶν ἀγγέλων · ὅσῳ διαφορώτερον παρ’ αὐτοὺς κεκληρονόμηκεν ὄνομα ·

English

He is the radiance (apaugasma, ἀπαύγασμα) of his glory and the exact imprint (charakter, χαρακτήρ) of his nature (hypostasis, ὑπόστασις), upholding all things by the word of his power. Having made purification for sins, he sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high, having become as much superior to the angels as the name he has inherited is more excellent than theirs.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:3: The term charakter (χαρακτήρ) appears only here in the New Testament. It denotes a precise reproduction—the impression left by a seal or die in wax or metal. Combined with hypostasis (ὑπόστασις), which means the underlying substance or essential reality, the phrase asserts that the Son is the exact reproduction of God’s very being. This is not analogy or approximation; it is identity of nature. The Cappadocian Fathers would later use hypostasis as a technical term for the distinct persons of the Trinity, but here it retains its earlier sense of essential nature or substance. For the theosis tradition, the implication is direct: if Christ bears the exact imprint of divine nature, and believers are being conformed to the image of Christ (Romans 8:29), then the path from humanity to divine participation runs through this very charakter.
  2. §1:3: The term apaugasma (ἀπαύγασμα), rendered “radiance,” is ambiguous in Greek: it can mean either the light that radiates outward from a source or the light that is reflected back. If the former, the Son is the active emanation of God’s glory; if the latter, he is its mirror. Origen and the Alexandrian tradition favored the active sense, reading the Son as eternally streaming forth from the Father like light from the sun—never separate, never secondary.
