Dhammapada 1-5: Mind Is the Forerunner

Gautama Buddha (attributed), c. 563-483 BCE

Introduction

The Dhammapada (“Path of the Dhamma”) is the most widely read text in the Theravada Buddhist canon, a collection of 423 verses attributed to the Buddha himself and preserved in the Khuddaka Nikaya of the Pali Tipitaka. These opening verses establish the foundational principle of Buddhist psychology: mind is the forerunner of all states. Everything we experience—suffering, liberation, the entire architecture of samsara—arises from the quality of our thought. This is not metaphysics in the Western sense but a radical empiricism of consciousness. The Buddha is not speculating about the nature of reality; he is reporting what he observed.

For the theosis question, these verses are essential. If mind precedes all things, then the transformation of mind is the transformation of reality itself. The Buddhist path does not posit a divine being to whom one ascends; it posits that the mind, when purified of hatred, delusion, and craving, reveals its own luminous nature. The question is not whether we can become God, but whether we can become what we already are beneath the accumulated distortions of ignorance.

§Verse 1 · Dhammapada, Yamakavagga 1
Manuscript
Pali

Manopubbangamā dhammā, manoseṭṭhā manomayā; manasā ce paduṭṭhena, bhāsati vā karoti vā, tato naṃ dukkhamanveti, cakkaṃ va vahato padaṃ.

English

Mind is the forerunner of all actions. Mind is their chief; they are all mind-wrought. If with an impure mind a person speaks or acts, suffering follows him like the wheel that follows the foot of the ox.

§Verse 2 · Dhammapada, Yamakavagga 2
Manuscript
Pali

Manopubbangamā dhammā, manoseṭṭhā manomayā; manasā ce pasannena, bhāsati vā karoti vā, tato naṃ sukhamanveti, chāyā va anapayini.

English

Mind is the forerunner of all actions. Mind is their chief; they are all mind-wrought. If with a pure mind a person speaks or acts, happiness follows him like his never-departing shadow.

§Verse 3 · Dhammapada, Yamakavagga 3
Manuscript
Pali

Akkocchi maṃ avadhi maṃ, ajīni maṃ ahāsi me; ye ca taṃ upanayhanti, veraṃ tesaṃ na sammati.

English

“He abused me, he struck me, he overpowered me, he robbed me.” Those who harbour such thoughts do not still their hatred.

§Verse 4 · Dhammapada, Yamakavagga 4
Manuscript
Pali

Akkocchi maṃ avadhi maṃ, ajīni maṃ ahāsi me; ye ca taṃ nupanayhanti, veraṃ tesūpasammati.

English

“He abused me, he struck me, he overpowered me, he robbed me.” Those who do not harbour such thoughts still their hatred.

§Verse 5 · Dhammapada, Yamakavagga 5
Manuscript
Pali

Na hi verena verāni, sammantīdha kudācanaṃ; averena ca sammanti, esa dhammo sanantano.

English

Hatred is never appeased by hatred in this world. By non-hatred alone is hatred appeased. This is a law eternal.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Verse 1: The Pali word dhamma here does not mean “teaching” or “cosmic law” as it does in other contexts. It means “mental states” or “phenomena.” The opening line is not a metaphysical claim about the universe but a psychological observation: every experience is shaped by the mind that apprehends it. This is the Buddhist alternative to cosmogony—instead of asking who made the world, ask what makes your experience of it.
  2. §Verse 5: The phrase esa dhammo sanantano (“this is an eternal law”) is striking in a tradition that insists on impermanence (anicca) as a fundamental characteristic of all conditioned things. The law of non-hatred transcending hatred is presented as sanantano—without beginning, eternal. This suggests that even in the earliest Buddhist texts, certain truths are granted an unconditioned, timeless status, pointing toward the later Mahayana distinction between conventional and ultimate truth.
