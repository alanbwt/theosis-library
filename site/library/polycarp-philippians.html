Letter to the Philippians

Polycarp of Smyrna, c. 69-155 AD

Introduction

Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 69–155 AD) is the crucial link between the apostolic age and the Church Fathers. According to Irenaeus, Polycarp was a disciple of the Apostle John himself, making him one of the last people alive who had been taught directly by someone who knew Jesus. His Letter to the Philippians, written around 110–140 AD, is the only surviving work attributed to him. It is not a theological treatise but a pastoral letter, full of practical moral instruction and warnings against false teaching.

The letter matters less for what it says about theosis directly than for what it reveals about the texture of early Christian faith in the generation after the apostles. Polycarp quotes Paul, echoes the Gospels, and invokes the suffering of Christ as the model for Christian endurance. His theology is not speculative but imitative: Christ suffered, therefore we must be willing to suffer; Christ was raised, therefore we shall be raised. The transformation of the believer into the likeness of Christ is assumed rather than argued. It is the air that the letter breathes.

§1.1–2 · Polycarp, Letter to the Philippians 1.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

Πολύκαρπος καὶ οἱ σὺν αὐτῷ πρεσβύτεροι τῇ ἐκκλησίᾳ τοῦ θεοῦ τῇ παροικούσῃ Φιλίππους

English

Polycarp and the elders with him, to the Church of God sojourning at Philippi: mercy and peace from God Almighty and Jesus Christ our Saviour be multiplied to you. I greatly rejoiced with you in our Lord Jesus Christ that you received the images of true love, and escorted, as was fitting, those bound in chains—the ornaments of the truly chosen of God and our Lord.

§2.1–3 · Polycarp, Letter to the Philippians 2.1–3
Manuscript
Greek

Διὸ ἀναζωσάμενοι τὰς ὀσφύας ὑμῶν δουλεύσατε τῷ θεῷ ἐν φόβῳ καὶ ἀληθείᾳ

English

Therefore, girding your loins, serve God in fear and in truth, leaving behind the empty and vain talk and the error of the many, believing in him who raised our Lord Jesus Christ from the dead and gave him glory and a throne at his right hand. To him all things in heaven and on earth were subjected; him every breathing creature serves; he is coming as judge of the living and the dead; and God will require his blood of those who disobey him.

§5.1–2 · Polycarp, Letter to the Philippians 5.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

εἰδότες οὖν ὅτι θεὸς οὐ μυκτηρίζεται ὀφείλομεν ἀξίως της ἐντολης αὐτοῦ περιπατεῖν

English

Knowing, therefore, that God is not mocked, we ought to walk worthy of his commandment and glory. Likewise, deacons must be blameless before his righteousness, as servants of God and Christ and not of men—not slanderers, not double-tongued, not lovers of money, temperate in all things, compassionate, diligent, walking according to the truth of the Lord, who became servant of all.

§8.1–2 · Polycarp, Letter to the Philippians 8.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

μιμηταὶ οὖν γενώμεθα της ὑπομονης αὐτοῦ

English

Let us therefore be imitators of his endurance, and if we suffer for his name’s sake, let us glorify him. For he set this example for us through himself, and this is what we have believed. I exhort all of you, therefore, to obey the word of righteousness and to practise all endurance, such as you saw with your own eyes not only in the blessed Ignatius and Zosimus and Rufus, but also in others from among you, and in Paul himself and the rest of the apostles.

§12.2–3 · Polycarp, Letter to the Philippians 12.2–3
Manuscript
Greek

πέποιθα γὰρ ὅτι εὖ ἐκτεθραμμένοι ἐστὲ ἐν ταῖς ἱεραῖς γραφαῖς

English

I am confident that you are well trained in the sacred Scriptures and that nothing is hidden from you—something not granted to me. Only, as it is said in these Scriptures: “Be angry and do not sin,” and “Do not let the sun go down on your anger.” Blessed is the one who remembers this. And I trust that this is so with you.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1–2: The “chains” referred to are those of Ignatius of Antioch, who had recently passed through Philippi on his way to martyrdom in Rome. Polycarp’s letter was written partly to accompany copies of Ignatius’s letters, which the Philippians had requested. The image of chains as “ornaments” reflects the early Christian theology of suffering as participation in the sufferings of Christ—a form of transformation through imitation.
  2. §8.1–2: The call to be “imitators of his endurance” (mimetai tes hypomones autou) echoes Paul’s language in 1 Corinthians 11:1 (“be imitators of me, as I am of Christ”). For Polycarp, the chain of imitation runs from Christ through the apostles (Paul is mentioned by name) through the recent martyrs (Ignatius, Zosimus, Rufus) to the Philippian community. Theosis in this framework is not mystical ascent but moral imitation: becoming like Christ by enduring what Christ endured.
