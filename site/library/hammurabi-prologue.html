Code of Hammurabi: The Divine Mandate

Codex Hammurabi
Hammurabi, c. 1792–1750 BCE

Introduction

The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE) is the most complete surviving law code from ancient Mesopotamia, inscribed on a black diorite stele now in the Louvre. The prologue, presented here, is not law but theology: it establishes Hammurabi as the divinely appointed agent of justice on earth. The supreme gods Anu and Enlil chose him by name to “cause justice to prevail in the land, to destroy the wicked and the evil, that the strong should not oppress the weak.”

The prologue is a key document in the history of political theology. The king rules not by personal power but by divine mandate. His authority is legitimate precisely because it flows from the gods. This is the earliest fully articulated theory of the ruler as the instrument of cosmic justice.

Akkadian

inuma Anum šīrum šar Anunnaki Enlil bēl šamē u erṣetim šāʾim šīmāt mātim ana Marduk mār reštî ša Ea ellilut kiššat nišī išīmuušum ina ilī Igīgī ušarbūšu Bābilam šumišu ṣīram ibbuū ina kibrāt arbaʾim ušātirūšu ina libbišu šarrūtam dārītam ša kima šamē u erṣetim išdaša šurzū ušēšibū inumišu Ḫammurabi rubaʾam naʾīdam palēḫ ilī yāti ana mīšaram ina mātimšupûʾam ragam ušēnam ḫulluqim dannum enšam ana la ḫabālim kima Šamaš ana ṣalmat qaqqadim waṣʾēmma mātam nuwwurim Anum u Enlil ibbūninnī

Ḫammurabi rēʾūm nibbīt Enlil anāku ša mukammer nūḫšim u tuḫdim ša ušaklil mimma šumšu ana Nibbur naʾīd-ēkur šarrum lêʾûm muttēr Eridim ana ašrišu mudessū parṣī ša E-abzu

English

When the supreme god Anu, king of the Anunnaki, and Enlil, lord of heaven and earth, who determines the destinies of the land, allotted the divine lordship over all peoples to Marduk, the firstborn son of Ea, made him great among the Igigi gods, called Babylon by its exalted name, made it supreme in the four quarters of the world, and established for him within it an everlasting kingship whose foundations are as firm as heaven and earth—

at that time, Anu and Enlil named me, Hammurabi, the devout prince who fears the gods, to cause justice to prevail in the land, to destroy the wicked and the evil, that the strong should not oppress the weak, to rise like the sun over the black-headed people, and to light up the land.

I am Hammurabi, the shepherd called by Enlil. I am he who heaps up abundance and plenty, who makes everything perfect for the city of Nippur. I am the devout provider of Ekur. I am the capable king who restored Eridu to its rightful place, who purified the rites of Eabzu.

I am the storm that rages over the four quarters of the world, who magnifies the fame of Babylon, who gladdens the heart of Marduk his lord, who daily attends upon Esagila. I am the descendant of royalty, whom the god Sin created, who enriched the city of Ur, the pious one who brings abundance to Ekishnugal.

I am the wise king, the obedient one, who heeds the words of Shamash. I am the mighty one who has laid the foundations of Sippar, who clothed with green the shrines of Aya, who made the temple of Ebabbar rise like a mountain in heaven.

The great gods have called me. I am the shepherd who brings peace, whose sceptre is just. My benevolent shade is spread over my city. In my bosom I carry the peoples of Sumer and Akkad. Under my protection they have prospered. I govern them in peace. I shelter them in my wisdom.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Prologue: Divine Mandate of Kingship: The prologue is structured as a single enormous conditional sentence: “When Anu and Enlil established Marduk’s supremacy... at that time they named me, Hammurabi.” The king’s authority is derivative, flowing from the same divine act that established cosmic order. The phrase “to cause justice to prevail in the land” (ana mīšaram ina mātim šupûʾam) uses mīšarum, a term that means not merely legal justice but cosmic rectitude—the same root that gives us the concept of the periodic royal “justice decree” that cancelled debts and freed slaves. The image of the king rising “like the sun over the black-headed people” (the standard Mesopotamian term for humanity) connects royal authority directly to Shamash, the sun god of justice. The prologue thus presents the king as a mediating figure between divine and human realms—not himself divine, but the chosen instrument through which divine justice enters the world.
