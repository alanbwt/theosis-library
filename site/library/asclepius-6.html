Asclepius 6: Man Is a Great Miracle

Hermes Trismegistus (attributed), c. 1st-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The Asclepius is a Latin Hermetic dialogue that survives as a translation (or adaptation) of a lost Greek original sometimes called the Perfect Discourse (Logos Teleios). Long attributed to Apuleius of Madaura, it is now dated to the second or third century CE. The text is a conversation between Hermes Trismegistus and his disciple Asclepius on the nature of God, the cosmos, and humanity’s unique position between the mortal and the divine.

Section 6 contains the famous declaration that “man is a great miracle” (magnum miraculum est homo)—a phrase that would echo through the Renaissance when Pico della Mirandola placed it at the heart of his Oration on the Dignity of Man (1486). The passage argues that humanity occupies a privileged middle position in the chain of being: capable of descending into bestiality or ascending into divinity. This is not merely a moral claim but an ontological one—the human being is the only creature whose nature is not fixed.

Latin original and English translation.
§6a · Asclepius 6
Manuscript
Latin

Magnum miraculum est homo, o Asclepi. Animal honorandum atque adorandum. Hic enim in naturam dei transit quasi ipse sit deus. Hic daemonum genus novit, utpote qui cum isdem ortum se esse cognoscat. Hic humanae naturae partem in se ipse despicit, alterius partis divinitate confisus.

English

A great miracle, Asclepius, is man. An honour-worthy and wonder-worthy living being. For he passes into the nature of a god as though he himself were a god. He knows the race of demons, inasmuch as he recognizes that he shares the same origin with them. He despises that part of his nature which is merely human, trusting in the divinity of his other part.

§6b · Asclepius 6
Manuscript
Latin

O quam felicior est hominis mixtura naturae! Diis cognata divinitate coniunctus est, partem sui qua terrenus est intra se ipse despiciens. Ceterorum omnium animantium necessitudinis sibi nexu devincit, terram colit, elementis celeritate sensus admiscetur, acumine mentis profundo maris descendit.

English

How much happier is the blend of human nature! Joined to the gods by a kindred divinity, he despises inwardly that part of himself by which he is earthly. All other living creatures he binds to himself in a knot of love, knowing himself to be related not only to the gods above but also to the natural world below. He cultivates the earth, he mixes himself with the elements by the swiftness of thought, he descends to the depths of the sea by the keenness of his mind.

§6c · Asclepius 6
Manuscript
Latin

Omnia illi licent. Caelum non putat excelsum, quasi de proximo metitur illud acumine sensus. Nulla aeris caligo intentionem animi eius obcaecat. Non densitas terrae eius operi obstat. Non profunditas aquae prospectum eius obtundit demerso. Omnia idem est et ubique idem est.

English

Everything is permitted to him. Heaven itself does not seem too high, for he measures it as though it were nearby by the keenness of his thought. No mist of the air dims the concentration of his mind. The density of the earth does not impede his work. The depth of the waters does not blunt his downcast gaze. He is at once all things, and he is at once everywhere.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6a: The phrase magnum miraculum est homo became the foundational motto of Renaissance humanism. Pico della Mirandola quoted it directly in his Oration on the Dignity of Man (1486), and Ficino treated it as a central Hermetic doctrine. The Latin miraculum does not mean merely “wonder” but “something that causes astonishment”—the human being is astonishing because it defies ontological categorization. It is neither purely mortal nor purely divine.
  2. §6a: The Latin transit in naturam dei (“passes into the nature of God”) uses the verb transire, which implies crossing a boundary. The human does not merely imitate God or approach God but crosses over into the divine nature. This is the Hermetic theosis claim in its Latin form, and it is unequivocal.
  3. §6c: The claim that man “is at once all things, and is at once everywhere” (omnia idem est et ubique idem est) parallels the ecstatic declaration in Corpus Hermeticum XIII.11: “I am in heaven, in earth, in water, in air.” The fully realized human being, in the Hermetic view, achieves the omnipresence that is properly an attribute of God. This is not metaphor but the logical consequence of the Hermetic ontology: if God is in all things and the human partakes of the divine nature, then the awakened human participates in divine omnipresence.
