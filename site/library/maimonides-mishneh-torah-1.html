Introduction

The Mishneh Torah of Maimonides (1180 CE) is the most comprehensive codification of Jewish law ever produced. Book 1, Sefer ha-Madda (Book of Knowledge), opens with the philosophical foundations of Judaism, beginning with the obligation to know that God exists.

§Laws of the Foundations of Torah, 1:1–4 · Maimonides, Mishneh Torah
Manuscript
Greek

[יסוד היסודות ועמוד החכמות לידע שיש שם מצוי ראשון]

English

The foundation of foundations and the pillar of wisdoms is to know that there is a First Being who brings into being all that exists. All the beings of heaven and earth and what is between them exist only through the truth of his being. If it could be supposed that he does not exist, then nothing else could exist. And if it could be supposed that all beings other than him do not exist, he alone would exist and would not be diminished by their absence.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:1: Maimonides opens a legal code with metaphysics—a revolutionary move that declares philosophical knowledge of God to be the first and foundational commandment. The argument from necessary existence (God exists necessarily; everything else exists contingently) reflects Avicenna’s influence. Aquinas would develop a similar argument in his Five Ways.
