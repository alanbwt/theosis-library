De Re Publica I: The Mixed Constitution

Cicero, 106-43 BCE

Introduction

Cicero’s De Re Publica (On the Republic), written in 54–51 BCE, is Rome’s answer to Plato’s Republic. Where Plato designed an ideal city from first principles, Cicero argues that the ideal already exists—in Rome itself, or rather in the Rome of the ancestors before its recent corruption. Book I, sections 39–43 present the theory of the mixed constitution: the best form of government is not pure monarchy, aristocracy, or democracy but a blend of all three that checks each element against the others.

The argument draws on Polybius, Aristotle, and a century of Roman political experience. Cicero puts it in the mouth of Scipio Africanus the Younger, speaking in a dramatic dialogue set in 129 BCE. The mixed constitution is not a compromise but a synthesis: the consuls provide the monarchic element, the Senate the aristocratic, and the popular assemblies the democratic. Each element prevents the others from degenerating into their corrupt forms. Rome’s greatness, Cicero insists, derives not from the genius of any single legislator but from the accumulated wisdom of many generations—a constitution built by time rather than by theory.

§I.39 (The Three Simple Forms) · Cicero, De Re Publica I.39
Manuscript
Latin

Cum penes unum est omnium summa rerum, regem illum unum vocamus et regnum eius rei publicae statum. Cum autem est penes delectos, tum illa civitas optimatium arbitrio regi dicitur. Illa autem est civitas popularis—sic enim appellant—in qua in populo sunt omnia.

English

When the supreme authority is in the hands of one man, we call him a king, and the form of that state a kingship. When it is in the hands of selected men, that state is said to be ruled by the will of an aristocracy. And that is a popular state—for so they call it—in which all things are in the hands of the people. Any one of these three forms, if it preserves the bond that first united men in political association, is not perfect, to be sure, nor in my opinion the best, but it is tolerable, and one may be better than another.

§I.41–42 (The Instability of Simple Forms) · Cicero, De Re Publica I.41–42
Manuscript
Latin

Sed habet unumquodque eorum genus iter quoddam quasi ad proximum statum declive atque lubricam. Nam ut in Cyro illo tolerabilis aut, si voltis, etiam amabilis rex subest Phalaris imitator, ad cuius similitudinem dominatus unius proclivi cursu defluit.

English

But each of these forms has a path, rough and slippery, leading to a related evil. For behind that tolerable or even lovable king Cyrus—to cite the most eminent example—lurks the utterly cruel Phalaris, toward whose likeness the rule of one man is carried by an easy downward course. And that government of the Massilian aristocracy is akin to the factious conspiracy of the Thirty at Athens. As for the Athenian people—not to seek other examples—when they had converted their absolute power into the fury of a mob, their very licence became their destruction.

§I.43 (The Mixed Constitution) · Cicero, De Re Publica I.43
Manuscript
Latin

Quare quartum quoddam genus rei publicae maxime probandum esse sentio, quod est ex his quae prima dixi moderatum et permixtum tribus. Placet enim esse quiddam in re publica praestans et regale, esse aliud auctoritati principum impartitum ac tributum, esse quasdam res servatas iudicio voluntatique multitudinis.

English

Therefore I consider a fourth form of constitution the most commendable—that which is a well-tempered mixture of the three that I first mentioned. For it is desirable that there should be a supreme and royal element in the state, that some power should be granted to the leading citizens, and that certain matters should be reserved for the judgment and will of the people. This constitution has, in the first place, a certain equality, which free men can hardly do without for any length of time, and secondly, stability.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.39: Cicero’s Latin term res publica does not mean “republic” in the modern sense of “a non-monarchical state.” It means literally “the public thing” or “the common affair”—the political community itself. Cicero defines it elsewhere (I.25) as res populi, “the affair of the people,” specifying that a people is not just any gathering but a group united by agreement on justice and shared advantage. A tyranny is therefore not a bad res publica but no res publica at all.
  2. §I.43: The mixed constitution theory passed from Cicero through Polybius into the mainstream of Western political thought. Machiavelli, Montesquieu, and the American founders all drew on this tradition. The separation of powers in the U.S. Constitution—president, senate, house—mirrors the Ciceronian triad of monarchic, aristocratic, and democratic elements, though the American founders replaced hereditary aristocracy with an elected body.
