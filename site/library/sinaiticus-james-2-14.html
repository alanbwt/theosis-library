Introduction

James 2:14–26 contains the famous argument that “faith without works is dead,” presenting Abraham’s offering of Isaac and Rahab’s hospitality as examples. The passage stands in apparent tension with Paul’s teaching on justification by faith. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§2:14–17 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

τι το οφελος αδελφοι μου εαν πιστιν λεγη τις εχειν

English

What good is it, my brothers, if someone says he has faith but does not have works? Can that faith save him? If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking in daily food, and one of you says to them, “Go in peace, be warmed and filled,” without giving them the things needed for the body, what good is that? So also faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead.

§2:24–26 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ορατε οτι εξ εργων δικαιουται ανθρωπος και ουκ εκ πιστεως μονον

English

You see that a person is justified by works and not by faith alone. And in the same way was not also Rahab the prostitute justified by works when she received the messengers and sent them out by another way? For as the body apart from the spirit is dead, so also faith apart from works is dead.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:24: Luther famously called James an “epistle of straw” because of this verse’s apparent contradiction of Romans 3:28 (“justified by faith apart from works of the law”). The tension is likely resolved by recognizing that Paul and James use “works” differently: Paul means Torah observance, James means acts of mercy.
