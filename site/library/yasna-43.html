Yasna 43: Vision of Ahura Mazda

Zarathustra (Zoroaster), c. 1500-1000 BCE

Introduction

Yasna 43 records Zarathustra’s direct vision of Ahura Mazda—the Wise Lord, the supreme God of Zoroastrian theology. It is one of the most personal and philosophically remarkable passages in the Gathas. Zarathustra does not merely receive commandments or hear a voice; he conceives of God “in his mind” and, through that act of contemplation, realizes that Ahura Mazda is both the First and the Last, the source of all that exists and the final end toward which all things tend.

The language anticipates both the Hebrew prophetic tradition (“I am the first and I am the last,” Isaiah 44:6) and the Greek philosophical tradition of the arche (first principle). Zarathustra’s God is not a tribal deity or a nature spirit but the ground of existence itself—Truth personified, the father of Good Mind, the source of Right.

§43.1–2 · Yasna 43, Stanzas 1–2 (Gathas of Zarathustra)
Manuscript
Avestan

Ushtā ahmāi yahmāi ushtā kahmāichi. Vasnā fshuyantē dat Ahurō, utayūitī hachaitē ashāt hachā.

English

May that come which is the better than good—to him who wishes it! Through his sovereign will, may Ahura Mazda grant us enduring strength and truth, and may Good Mind grant us the enduring power through which one may put down hostility.

§43.5 · Yasna 43, Stanza 5
Manuscript
Avestan

At thvā menghaidī paouryō, Mazdā, yăsmī zī mōi stutō paouruuyīm, hyāt thvā aojyēshē mananghā ashāt-chā.

English

Thus I realized You as the Bounteous One, O Wise Lord, when I first conceived of You in my mind, when I first beheld You with my eyes—that You are the First and the Last for all eternity, the Father of Good Mind, the true Creator of Truth, and the Lord of existence.

§43.7–8 · Yasna 43, Stanzas 7–8
Manuscript
Avestan

Tat thvā perēsā erēsh mōi vaochā, Ahurā. Kash nā tashāt pāthām ashā ratum? Kash hveng starem-chā dāt advanum?

English

You spoke to me first, saying: “Go, teach the peoples!” Before that, I was one man; Your word made me many. And this I ask You—tell me truly, Lord—who was the first father of Truth at the birth of creation? Who established the course of the sun and the stars? Who is it through whom the moon waxes and wanes? These things and more I wish to know, O Wise One.

§43.11 · Yasna 43, Stanza 11
Manuscript
Avestan

At thvā menghaidī spentam, Mazdā, Ahurā, hyāt mā vohū jaghāt manangha. Peresāt mōi: kahī? Kahē ahī?

English

Thus I realized You as the Bounteous One, O Wise Lord, when Good Mind attended me and asked me: “Who are you? To whom do you belong?” And I answered: “Zarathustra am I. A true foe to the follower of the lie, and a powerful support to the follower of truth, shall I be.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §43.5: The phrase “the First and the Last” is strikingly close to the Hebrew rishon ve-acharon in Isaiah 44:6 and the Greek alpha kai omega of Revelation 1:8. Zarathustra’s formulation predates both by centuries. The idea that God is conceived “in the mind” (mananghā) is also significant: for Zarathustra, knowing God is not a matter of ritual or sacrifice but of intellectual and spiritual contemplation. This places the Gathas closer to the Upanishadic and Greek philosophical traditions than to the sacrificial religions of the ancient Near East.
  2. §43.7–8: The questions Zarathustra puts to Ahura Mazda—who created the sun, the moon, the stars, the course of nature?—echo the cosmological questions of the Rig Veda’s Hymn of Creation (10.129) and anticipate the questions God puts to Job from the whirlwind (Job 38–41). But the tone is different: Zarathustra asks not in despair but in wonder, and he asks as a seeker addressing the source of truth. The Gathas preserve what may be the earliest philosophical theology: the attempt to reason about God rather than merely worship Him.
