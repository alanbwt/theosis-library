Introduction

Chapter 5 of the Enchiridion states the core Stoic insight about emotional disturbance: it is not things themselves that disturb us, but our judgments about things. Freedom comes not from changing the world but from correcting our opinions.

Greek original and English translation.
§Chapter 5 · Epictetus, Enchiridion 5 (Schenkl)
Manuscript
Greek

ταράσσει τοὺς ἀνθρώπους οὐ τὰ πράγματα ἀλλὰ τὰ περὶ τῶν πραγμάτων δόγματα

English

It is not things that disturb people, but their judgments about things. Death, for example, is nothing terrible—otherwise it would have appeared so to Socrates—but the judgment about death, that it is terrible, that is what is terrible. Whenever, then, we are hindered or disturbed or grieved, let us never blame others but only ourselves—that is, our own judgments.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 5: This principle became the philosophical foundation of cognitive behavioral therapy. The Greek dogmata (δόγματα, “judgments” or “beliefs”) refers not to idle opinions but to deep evaluative commitments that shape our emotional responses to events.
