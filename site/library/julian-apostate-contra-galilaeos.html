Against the Galileans: The Last Pagan Emperor

Emperor Julian, 331-363 AD

Introduction

Emperor Julian (331–363 CE), called “the Apostate” by Christians, was the last pagan emperor of Rome. Raised Christian, he converted to Neoplatonic paganism and attempted to restore the old religion during his brief reign (361–363). His Contra Galilaeos (Against the Galileans) was a philosophical polemic against Christianity written around 362 CE. Like Celsus’s work, it survives only in fragments preserved by its Christian refuter—in this case, Cyril of Alexandria.

Julian attacks Christianity on multiple fronts: the God of the Old Testament is a petty tribal deity, unworthy of the name; the Galileans have invented absurd doctrines that no Greek philosopher would accept; and the worship of a crucified man as God is an offence against reason. For Julian, the philosophical path to the divine is through Neoplatonic contemplation and the traditional Hellenistic mysteries, not through faith in a Jewish carpenter. His critique is particularly sharp because he knows Christianity from the inside—he was educated in it before rejecting it.

§1 · Julian, Contra Galilaeos (preserved in Cyril, Contra Iulianum I)
Manuscript
Greek

ἄξιον ἔδοξέ μοι τὰς αἰτίας ἐκθεῖναι δι’ ὃς ἐπείσθην ὅτι τὸ τῶν Γαλιλαίων πλάσμα ἀνθρώπων ἐστὶ κακουργίας πλάσμα.

English

It seems to me worthwhile to set forth the reasons why I was convinced that the fabrication of the Galileans is a fiction of men composed by wickedness. Though it has nothing divine in it, by making use of the part of the soul that loves fable and is childish and foolish, it has induced men to believe that this monstrous tale is the truth.

§2 · Julian, Contra Galilaeos (preserved in Cyril, Contra Iulianum II)
Manuscript
Greek

οὔτε Παῦλος οὔτε Ματθαῖος οὔτε Λουκᾶς οὔτε Μάρκος ἐτόλμησαν εἰπεῖν θεὸν τὸν Ἰησοῦν.

English

Neither Paul nor Matthew nor Luke nor Mark dared to say that Jesus is God. Only the good John, having perceived that a great multitude in many of the Greek and Italian cities was already infected with this disease, and having heard, I suppose, that the tombs of Peter and Paul were already being worshipped—secretly, it is true, but still worshipped—John, I say, was the first to dare to make this claim.

§3 · Julian, Contra Galilaeos (preserved in Cyril, Contra Iulianum VI)
Manuscript
Greek

τί δήποτε νεκρὸν προσκυνεῖτε;

English

Why do you worship a dead man? Or rather, why do you worship a man who was crucified by Jews as a criminal? Even if he was some sort of god, as you claim, he was certainly not recognized as such by the Jews—and these are the very people through whom your god is supposed to have spoken. Is this not absurd beyond all absurdity?

§4 · Julian, Contra Galilaeos (preserved in Cyril, Contra Iulianum VIII)
Manuscript
Greek

ἔχουσιν οἱ ἕλληνες τὸν θεῖον Πλάτωνα.

English

The Greeks have their divine Plato, who taught the soul’s ascent to the Good itself. The Galileans have a crucified Jew. Which of these two paths leads the soul upward? The philosophers teach us to purify the intellect through reason and contemplation. The Galileans teach their followers to believe without understanding. They call this “faith.” We call it credulity.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2: Julian’s historical argument—that only John among the Gospel writers calls Jesus God—was remarkably astute for a fourth-century reader. Modern biblical scholarship broadly agrees that the explicit identification of Jesus with God develops progressively across the New Testament, with the Johannine literature making the strongest claims. Julian’s argument anticipates by sixteen centuries the historical-critical approach to Christological development.
  2. §4: Julian’s contrast between Platonic philosophy and Christian faith captures the fundamental tension between the two paths to the divine that competed in late antiquity. The Platonic path is intellectual: the soul ascends through dialectic, mathematical abstraction, and contemplation of the Forms. The Christian path is fiduciary: the soul is saved through trust in a revealed person. Julian saw these as incompatible. The Christian Neoplatonists—Augustine, Pseudo-Dionysius, Maximus the Confessor—spent centuries trying to reconcile them.
