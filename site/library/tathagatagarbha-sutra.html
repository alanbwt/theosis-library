Tathagatagarbha Sutra: Buddha-Nature in All Beings

Gautama Buddha (attributed), c. 563-483 BCE

Introduction

The Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra is the foundational text of the Buddha-nature tradition, the strand of Mahayana Buddhism that comes closest to the theosis question as posed in the Abrahamic and Neoplatonic traditions. Its central claim is extraordinary: every sentient being, without exception, possesses the tathāgatagarbha—the “womb” or “embryo” of the Tathāgata (the Buddha). Beneath the defilements of ignorance, craving, and hatred, every being already contains the seed of complete and perfect Buddhahood. This is not a potential to be created but a reality to be uncovered.

The parallel to theosis is direct and unmistakable. Where Athanasius wrote “God became man so that man might become God,” the Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra teaches that beings already are Buddhas—they simply do not know it. The defilements that obscure Buddha-nature are adventitious (āgantuka-kleśa), not intrinsic. They are like dust on a mirror, clouds before the sun, a dirty cloth wrapped around pure gold. Remove them and the gold was always there.

§The Simile of the Withered Lotus · Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Tadyathāpi nāma kulaputrāḥ madhukara-kośaḥ pratividdhaḥ daśabhir madhukara-sahasraiḥ. Kuśalo puruṣaḥ madhukaṃ dṛṣṭvā madhukarakān apāsya madhukaṃ gṛhṇīyāt. Evam eva kulaputrāḥ sarva-sattvānāṃ tathāgatagarbho ’sti. Yathā tad madhukaṃ madhukara-parivr̩tam, tathā sarva-sattvānāṃ tathāgatagarbhaḥ kleśāvidyā-parivr̩taḥ.

English

It is as if there were a great store of honey hidden in a cave, surrounded by ten thousand bees. A skilled person, seeing the honey, would drive away the bees and take the honey. Just so, O noble sons, all beings possess the Tathāgatagarbha. Like the honey surrounded by bees, it is always present, complete, and unchanged, but it is covered over by the defilements of ignorance and craving.

§The Simile of the Gold in Filth · Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Tadyathāpi nāma kulaputrāḥ kāñcanam aśucau patitaṃ varṣa-śatair api na viśīryate. Divya-cakṣuḥ kaścit puruṣaḥ paśyed vadec ca: atra kāñcanam asti, uddharatāsmat praśodhyāsya. Evam eva kulaputrāḥ sarva-sattvānāṃ tathāgatagarbho rāga-dveṣa-moha-kleśaiś channo bhavati.

English

It is as if a lump of pure gold were to fall into a place of filth and defilement, where it might remain for many years without deteriorating. A person with divine sight might see it there and say to someone: “There is a lump of gold here in this filth. Dig it out and do with it as you please.” Just so, the Tathāgatagarbha of all beings is covered over by the defilements of craving, hatred, and delusion.

§The Direct Declaration · Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Sarva-sattvāḥ ṣaḍ-gati-samsāra-gatā api tathāgatagarbha-samanvitāḥ nityam avipariṇāma-dharmiṇaḥ. Idaṃ kulaputrāḥ tathāgatagarbham ity ucyate.

English

All beings, though they find themselves in all sorts of circumstances among the destinies of the six realms of existence, possess the Tathāgatagarbha eternally and without change. This, O noble sons, is what is called the Tathāgatagarbha.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Simile of the Withered Lotus: The term tathāgatagarbha is deliberately ambiguous. Garbha can mean “womb,” “embryo,” or “essence.” Read one way, beings contain the embryo of Buddhahood—a seed that must grow. Read another way, beings are the womb in which the Tathāgata already exists fully formed. The sutra exploits both meanings. The similes consistently use the second reading: the gold does not grow; the honey does not develop. They are already complete. They only need to be uncovered.
  2. §The Simile of the Gold in Filth: This simile became central to the entire Buddha-nature tradition. The Ratnagotravibhāga (Uttaratantra), the most important systematic treatise on Buddha-nature, built its entire structure around a set of nine such similes drawn from this sutra and related texts. The key philosophical point is that the defilements are āgantuka (adventitious, newly arrived, not intrinsic) while the Buddha-nature is prakṛti (natural, primordial). This directly parallels the Neoplatonic and patristic teaching that evil is a privation, not a substance.
  3. §The Direct Declaration: The phrase nityam avipariṇāma-dharmiṇaḥ (“eternal and unchanging in nature”) is remarkable in a Buddhist context. Orthodox Buddhist teaching insists that all conditioned phenomena are impermanent (anitya). The Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra appears to grant Buddha-nature an unconditioned, permanent status. This created enormous controversy within Buddhism itself. The Yogacara school attempted to harmonize this with śūnyatā doctrine; the Madhyamaka school was more skeptical. The tension is never fully resolved.
