Elements of Theology 1-3: Every Manifold Participates in Unity

Proclus, 412-485 AD

Introduction

The Elements of Theology (Stoicheiosis Theologike) by Proclus (412–485 CE) is the most rigorously systematic work in the Neoplatonic tradition. Written in the style of Euclid’s Elements—a series of propositions, each followed by its proof—it distills the entire Neoplatonic metaphysics into 211 theorems. The first three propositions establish the foundation: every manifold participates in unity, everything that participates in unity is both one and not-one, and everything that becomes one does so by participating in the One.

Proclus’s significance for the theosis tradition is twofold. First, he formalized the triadic structure of emanation: every being remains in its cause, proceeds from its cause, and returns to its cause (mone, proodos, epistrophe). This triad made the soul’s return to God not merely possible but structurally necessary—built into the fabric of reality itself. Second, Proclus’s work was translated into Arabic and Latin and became the hidden backbone of medieval theology. The Liber de Causis, mistakenly attributed to Aristotle and enormously influential in Islamic and Christian philosophy, is largely derived from the Elements of Theology.

§Proposition 1 · Proclus, Elements of Theology, Prop. 1
Manuscript
Greek

Πᾶν πλῆθος μετέχει πῃ τοῦ ἑνός.

English

Every manifold in some way participates in unity. For if it in no way participated in unity, neither would the whole be one whole, nor would each of the many of which the manifold consists be one—for each of them would itself be a manifold, and this to infinity. And each of these infinites would itself be infinitely infinite. For a manifold that participates in unity in no way, neither as a whole nor in respect of each of its parts, would be infinite in every respect and in respect of every one of its parts. For each of the many, whatever one you take, will be either one or not-one; and if not-one, then either many or nothing. But if each is nothing, the whole composed of nothings is nothing. And if each is many, each will be composed of infinitely many infinities. But this is impossible. Therefore every manifold participates in unity.

§Proposition 2 · Proclus, Elements of Theology, Prop. 2
Manuscript
Greek

Πᾶν τὸ τοῦ ἑνὸς μετέχον καὶ ἕν ἐστι καὶ οὐχ ἕν.

English

All that participates in unity is both one and not-one. For if it is not the One itself—since it participates in unity by having unity, not by being unity—it has acquired unity by participation and therefore has been affected in respect of its unity-having. But what has been affected in respect of its participation in unity, insofar as it has been affected, is not-one. And since it is not-one but has come to participate in unity, it is at once both one and not-one. It is not one in the same respect in which it is not-one, but is one by participation and not-one in its own nature.

§Proposition 3 · Proclus, Elements of Theology, Prop. 3
Manuscript
Greek

Πᾶν τὸ γινόμενον ἓν τῇ τοῦ ἑνὸς μεθέξει ἓν γίνεται.

English

All that becomes one does so by participating in the One, and derives its unity from the One. For if what is not essentially one is nevertheless one, it has surely become one by having been affected in a certain way and by having acquired unity. And to acquire unity is to participate in unity. Therefore, if it is one insofar as it participates, and what participates derives what it participates in from its source, then the unity of what is unified derives from the One, which is the first principle of unity.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Proposition 1: The proof structure is a reductio ad absurdum: if a manifold does not participate in unity, then each of its parts is itself a manifold, and each part of each part is a manifold, and so on to infinity—yielding an actual infinity of actual infinities, which is impossible. Therefore unity must be present at every level of reality. This is the logical foundation for the entire Neoplatonic system: the One is not merely the highest being but the condition of possibility for all being.
  2. §Proposition 2: The distinction between “being one” and “participating in unity” is crucial. Only the One itself is unity; everything else has unity by participation. This means that every composite being has a dual nature: one insofar as it participates in the One, and not-one insofar as it is a manifold. The soul’s task in theosis is to reduce its not-one-ness—its multiplicity and dispersal—and increase its participation in the One.
  3. §Proposition 3: The principle that all unity derives from the One establishes the metaphysical dependence of every being on the first principle. Even after emanation, every being maintains a participatory link to its source. This is why return (epistrophe) is possible: the link was never severed, only obscured. Proclus’s later propositions (Prop. 25–39) elaborate this into the full triadic structure of remaining, procession, and return.
