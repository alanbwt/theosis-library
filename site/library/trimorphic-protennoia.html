Trimorphic Protennoia: The First Thought Descends

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Trimorphic Protennoia (“The Three-Formed First Thought”) is one of the most philosophically dense texts in the Nag Hammadi library. It presents the First Thought (Protennoia) of the supreme deity descending into the material world in three forms: as Voice, as Speech, and as Word (Logos). Each descent penetrates deeper into matter, culminating in the Word’s entry into human form to awaken those who carry the divine spark. The parallels to the Johannine Prologue are so striking that scholars have debated which text influenced the other.

The Coptic text survives in Nag Hammadi Codex XIII. The excerpt presented here is from the third subtractate, where Protennoia speaks in the first person as the divine Word descending into flesh. The language is simultaneously cosmological and intimate: the First Thought of God puts on humanity as a garment, enters the prison of the body, and shakes the foundations of the material world from within.

Translator's Notes

  1. §47.13–22: The opening declaration “I am the Word who dwells in the ineffable Silence” mirrors the Johannine Prologue’s “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God.” But where John places the Logos with (pros) God, the Trimorphic Protennoia places it within Silence (Sige), a technical term in Valentinian theology for the consort of the supreme deity. The text presents a feminine divine figure who is the Logos—a radical departure from the masculine Logos of both Stoic philosophy and Johannine theology.
  2. §47.22–35: The phrase “I put on Jesus” inverts the incarnation as described in John 1:14. In the Johannine account, the Word becomes flesh. Here, the divine First Thought “puts on” the human Jesus like a garment. This docetic Christology—in which the divine being inhabits but is not identical with the human body—was the primary reason the orthodox tradition rejected texts like this one. Yet the underlying logic is the same as theosis read from the other direction: if divinity can clothe itself in humanity, humanity can be clothed in divinity.
