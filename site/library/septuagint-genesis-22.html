Introduction

Genesis 22:1–19 recounts the binding of Isaac (the Akedah), where God commands Abraham to sacrifice his son. The test of radical obedience and the last-moment reprieve became paradigmatic for Jewish and Christian theology alike. From the Septuagint.

§22:1–2 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἐγένετο μετὰ τὰ ὅήματα ταῦτα ὁ θεὸς ἐπείρασεν τὸν Αβρααμ

English

And it came to pass after these things that God tested Abraham, and said to him, “Abraham, Abraham!” And he said, “Here I am.” And he said, “Take your beloved son, whom you love, Isaac, and go to the high land, and offer him there as a whole burnt offering on one of the mountains that I will tell you.”

§22:11–14 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἐκάλεσεν αὐτὸν ἄγγελος κυρίου ἐκ τοῦ οὐρανοῦ

English

And an angel of the Lord called to him from heaven, and said, “Abraham, Abraham!” And he said, “Here I am.” And he said, “Do not lay your hand on the child, and do nothing to him; for now I know that you fear God, and you have not spared your beloved son for my sake.” And Abraham looked up and saw a ram caught in a thicket by its horns, and he offered it as a whole burnt offering instead of Isaac his son.

Translator's Notes

  1. §22:2: The Septuagint renders “your beloved son” (τὸν υἱόν σου τὸν ἀγαπητόν) using the same word (agapetos) that appears at Jesus’s baptism: “This is my beloved Son.” The early Christians read the Akedah as a type of the crucifixion: the father offering the beloved son, with the ram as a figure of the substitute sacrifice.
