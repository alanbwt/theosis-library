Great Hymn to Amun-Re: The Sole Creator

Anonymous (Egyptian), c. 1550 BCE

Introduction

The Great Hymn to Amun-Re, preserved on Papyrus Leiden I 350 (c. 1213 BCE, Nineteenth Dynasty), is one of the most theologically sophisticated compositions of ancient Egypt. It presents Amun-Re not merely as the chief deity of the Egyptian pantheon but as the hidden, self-created source of all existence—a being who transcends the world he has made while simultaneously pervading every part of it. The hymn’s language of divine hiddenness, self-origination, and cosmic immanence makes it a crucial precursor to later monotheistic and mystical traditions.

The text’s affirmations—“You are the sole one who made all that is”; “too great to be investigated, too powerful to be known”—resonate with the negative theology of the Upanishads, the Neoplatonists, and the apophatic tradition of Christian mysticism. Amun’s very name means “the hidden one,” and the hymn explores what it means for the ground of all reality to be simultaneously present everywhere and beyond all comprehension.

§Stanza 100 · Papyrus Leiden I 350, recto, col. IV
Manuscript
Egyptian

ntk wʕ ir ntt nbt, wʕ wʕ ir wnnt. ḏ pr.n rmt m irtj.fj, ḏ pr.n nr.w m rā.fj.

English

You are the sole one who made all that is, the one and only who made what exists. From whose eyes humankind came forth, and from whose mouth the gods came into being. He who makes herbage for the cattle and the fruit-tree for humankind, who makes that on which the fish live in the river and the birds in the sky.

§Stanza 200 · Papyrus Leiden I 350, recto, col. VII
Manuscript
Egyptian

nb wʕ spr r ḏrw tāwy rʕ nb, wʕ māā šm.w ḥr.sn. tā nb ḥr mdwt n wbn.f rʕ nb r dwā.f.

English

The sole lord, who reaches the end of the lands each day, being one who beholds those who walk upon them. Every land chatters at his rising, every day, to praise him. Too great to be investigated, too powerful to be known. One falls immediately in a seizure of dread if his name is spoken knowingly or unknowingly.

§Stanza 300 · Papyrus Leiden I 350, recto, col. IX
Manuscript
Egyptian

ẖpr m ḥāt, Imn ẖpr m ḥāt, nn rḫ ẖprw.f. nn nr ẖpr ḥāt.f.

English

He who began coming into being in the beginning, Amun, who came into being at the beginning, whose origin is unknown. No god came into being before him. There was no other god with him to tell of his form. He had no mother who made his name. He had no father who begat him and said, “This is I.” He shaped his own egg. Power of mysterious birth, who created his own beauty. Most divine god, who came into being by himself.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanza 100: The statement that humankind came from the god’s eyes reflects the Egyptian wordplay between remyt (tears) and rmt (people). Humans are the tears of God—a startling image that suggests both intimacy and sorrow. The gods, by contrast, come from his mouth: they are spoken into being. Humanity’s origin is more visceral, more emotional.
  2. §Stanza 300: The self-creation of Amun—“who came into being by himself”—is the Egyptian equivalent of the theological concept of aseity (self-existence), later developed in Jewish, Christian, and Islamic theology. The idea that the ultimate reality has no origin, no cause, no predecessor is shared across traditions. The specific image of the god “shaping his own egg” is distinctively Egyptian but the concept it expresses is universal.
