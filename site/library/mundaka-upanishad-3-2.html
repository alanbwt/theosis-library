Mundaka Upanishad 3.2.9: He Who Knows Brahman Becomes Brahman

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

Mundaka Upanishad 3.2.9 contains what may be the most direct statement of theosis in all of Indian scripture: “He who knows Brahman becomes Brahman.” The Sanskrit is even more compressed—brahma veda brahmaiva bhavati—and admits no qualification. Knowledge of the ultimate reality is not merely an intellectual achievement; it is an ontological transformation. The knower becomes what is known.

This passage represents the culmination of the Upanishadic trajectory that begins with the Chandogya’s “That art thou” and the Brihadaranyaka’s “I am Brahman.” Where those texts identify the individual self (atman) with the cosmic self (Brahman), the Mundaka makes explicit that this identification is not merely a recognition of what was always the case but a genuine becoming. The soul crosses beyond sorrow, beyond death, beyond all distinctions of merit and demerit. The parallel to the Christian Fathers’ doctrine that “God became man so that man might become God” is unmistakable, though the metaphysical frameworks differ profoundly.

§3.2.7 · Mundaka Upanishad 3.2.7
Manuscript
Sanskrit

यथा नद्यः स्यन्दमानाः समुद्रे अस्तं गच्छन्ति नामरूपे विहाय । तथा विद्वान् नामरूपाद् विमुक्तः परात्परं पुरुषमुपैति दिव्यम् ।

English

The rivers flowing into the ocean lose their names and forms. Even so, the wise one, freed from name and form, attains the divine Person who is higher than the highest.

§3.2.8 · Mundaka Upanishad 3.2.8
Manuscript
Sanskrit

स यो ह वै तत्परं ब्रह्म वेद ब्रह्मैव भवति । नास्याब्रह्मवित् कश्चन कुले भवति । तरति शोकं तरति पाप्मानं गुहाहृदयग्रन्थिभ्यो विमुक्तो’मृतो भवति ।

English

He who knows that supreme Brahman becomes Brahman itself. In his family, no one who does not know Brahman is born. He crosses over sorrow. He crosses over sin. Freed from the knots of the heart, he becomes immortal.

§3.2.9 · Mundaka Upanishad 3.2.9
Manuscript
Sanskrit

तदेतदृचाभ्युक्तम् । वेदान्तविज्ञानसुनिश्चितार्थाः संन्यासयोगाच्छुद्धसत्त्वाः । ते ब्रह्मलोकेषु परान्तकाले परामृताः परिमुच्यन्ति सर्वे ।

English

As it is declared in the verse: “Those who have realized the meaning of the Vedanta knowledge, who have purified their minds through the practice of renunciation—they, in the worlds of Brahman, at the end of time, are fully liberated, fully immortal.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §3.2.8: The phrase brahma veda brahmaiva bhavati (“he who knows Brahman becomes Brahman indeed”) uses the verb bhavati (becomes) rather than asti (is). This is significant. The text does not say that the knower always was Brahman and merely realizes it (the Advaita reading), nor that the knower becomes something other than what he was (the dualist reading). It says he becomes—a word that holds both possibilities in tension.
  2. §3.2.7: The image of rivers dissolving into the ocean is one of the most common analogies for liberation in Indian thought. It appears also in the Prashna Upanishad and the Bhagavad Gita. The analogy is imperfect in a revealing way: the river water does not cease to exist when it enters the ocean, but it does lose its individual identity. Whether the liberated soul retains any individuality or is wholly absorbed into Brahman is the central dispute between Advaita (non-dual) and Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dual) Vedanta.
