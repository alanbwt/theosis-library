Ezekiel 1:26-28: The Divine Throne-Chariot

Ἰεζεκιήλ
Ezekiel (attributed), c. 6th c. BCE
Septuagint (LXX)

Introduction

Ezekiel 1:26–28 is the climax of the prophet’s inaugural vision—the passage that gave rise to the entire merkabah (“chariot”) mystical tradition in Judaism. After describing the four living creatures, the wheels within wheels, and the crystalline firmament above their heads, Ezekiel finally looks upward and sees what sits on the throne: “the likeness of the glory of the Lord.” The language is stacked with qualifications—likeness upon likeness, appearance upon appearance—as though the prophet cannot bring himself to say directly what he saw. What he describes is a figure in human form, surrounded by fire and radiance, seated on a throne of sapphire.

The theological significance is immense. The God of Israel, who cannot be seen and who forbids images, appears in a form that is recognizably human. The Greek of the Septuagint uses homoioma (ὁμοίωμα, “likeness”) and doxa (δόξα, “glory”)—terms that became central to the patristic doctrine of the image of God and the theology of deification. If the glory of the Lord takes human form, then humanity bears some essential relation to the divine. The merkabah tradition that grew from this passage held that the mystic who ascends through the heavenly palaces may himself behold this glory—and be transformed by it.

English

And above the firmament that was over their heads was the likeness of a throne, as the appearance of a sapphire stone; and upon the likeness of the throne was a likeness as the appearance of a man upon it above. And I saw as the appearance of amber (elektron, Ἠλεκτρον), as the appearance of fire within it round about, from the appearance of his loins and upward; and from the appearance of his loins and downward I saw as the appearance of fire, and its brightness round about, as the appearance of the bow that is in the cloud on the day of rain—so was the appearance of the brightness round about. This was the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the Lord (homoioma doxes kyriou, ὁμοίωμα δόξης κυρίου). And I saw and fell upon my face, and I heard a voice speaking.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:26: The phrase “likeness as the appearance of a man” (ὁμοίωμα ὡς εἶδος ἀνθρώπου) is the foundation of the merkabah tradition. The rabbis considered this passage so dangerous that it was restricted: the Mishnah (Hagigah 2:1) forbids expounding the “Work of the Chariot” publicly. Yet the vision insists that what sits on the throne of God has human form. This paradox—the invisible God appearing in human likeness—is precisely the tension that the doctrine of the Incarnation would later claim to resolve. Paul’s use of morphe (“form”) in Philippians 2:6–7, where Christ exists in the “form of God” but takes the “form of a servant,” draws on the same conceptual vocabulary.
  2. §1:28: The concluding formula—“this was the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the Lord” (αὕτη ἡ ὅρασις ὁμοιώματος δόξης κυρίου)—piles up layers of mediation: appearance, likeness, glory, Lord. Ezekiel does not say he saw God. He says he saw the appearance of the likeness of the glory. Each term adds distance between the prophet and the divine reality. This careful language became a model for how the Fathers spoke of the knowledge of God: we see the glory, not the essence; the energies, not the nature. Gregory Palamas’s fourteenth-century distinction between God’s essence and energies has its roots here.
