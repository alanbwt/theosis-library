Ambigua 7: The Five Cosmic Divisions

Ἄπορα
Maximus the Confessor, c. 580–662 AD

Introduction

Maximus the Confessor’s Ambigua (Difficulties), written around 628–630 CE, is among the most philosophically dense works in all of Christian theology. Ambiguum 7 addresses a passage from Gregory of Nazianzus and develops it into a vast cosmological vision. Maximus identifies five fundamental divisions in created reality: between uncreated and created, intelligible and sensible, heaven and earth, paradise and the inhabited world, and male and female.

Christ, in the Incarnation, overcomes each of these divisions. Theosis for Maximus is therefore not merely the salvation of the individual soul but the reconciliation of all things. The human being, created as a mediator between all levels of reality, was meant to unite these divisions from the beginning. Christ accomplishes what Adam failed to do. The end of theosis is cosmic: the whole created order transfigured and gathered into God.

§7 · Ambigua 7 (Constas ed., Dumbarton Oaks; PG 91.1084C–1088A)
Manuscript
Greek

Τελευταιον των οντων εισήχθη ο ανθρωπος, οιον τις φυσικός σύνδεσμος δια των οικείων μερων πασι τοις ακροις μεσιτεύων, και εις εν αγων εν εαυτω τα πολλη τη φύσει διεστηκότα και αλλήλων απέχοντα· όλον όλω τω Θεω περιχωρήσαντα, και παν ο εστιν ο Θεός γενόμενον, χωρις της κατ’ ουσίαν ταυτότητος, και όλον εαυτω τον Θεον αντιλαβόν.

English

The human person was introduced last among beings, as a kind of natural bond mediating between the universal extremes through its own parts, and bringing into unity in itself those things that are by nature far distant from each other. Accordingly, the human person was to unite: first, the division of its own nature, as male and female, through a dispassionate relation to divine virtue, for which the distinctions of this division are in no way necessary; then, through the holiness of its life, it was to unite paradise and the inhabited earth, making of them one earth; then, by a life identical with that of the angels, it was to unite heaven and earth, making of them one creation; then, by a knowledge equal to that of the angels, it was to unite the sensible and the intelligible, making of them one world; and finally, through love, it was to unite created nature with the uncreated, showing them to be one and the same through the acquisition of grace — the whole human person wholly pervading the whole God, and becoming everything that God is, save at the level of identity of essence, and receiving to itself the whole of God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §7: The critical qualification in this passage is χωρις της κατ’ ουσίαν ταυτότητος (“save at the level of identity of essence”). Maximus affirms the most radical possible theosis — the human person becomes “everything that God is” and is “wholly pervaded by the whole God” — while maintaining the ontological distinction between Creator and creature. The term περιχώρησις (perichoresis, mutual interpenetration) here applied to the God-human relationship was also used by the Cappadocians to describe the relations of the Trinity. Maximus’s five divisions scheme synthesizes centuries of patristic thought into a single architectonic vision.
