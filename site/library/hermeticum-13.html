Corpus Hermeticum XIII: On Rebirth

Hermes Trismegistus (attributed), c. 1st-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The thirteenth treatise of the Corpus Hermeticum, titled On Rebirth (Peri Palingenesias, Περὶ Παλιγγενεσίας), is a dialogue between Hermes Trismegistus and his son Tat in which Hermes transmits the secret teaching of spiritual regeneration. Tat has been prepared by the earlier treatises but cannot yet grasp the experience of rebirth itself. Hermes tells him that understanding is not enough—one must be “born again” in a body not made of the elements but of divine powers. The old self, composed of the twelve torments (ignorance, grief, incontinence, lust, injustice, greed, deceit, envy, fraud, anger, rashness, malice), must be driven out and replaced by ten divine powers.

The parallels to the Gospel of John’s “you must be born again” (John 3:3–7) are impossible to ignore, though the mechanism is entirely different. In John, rebirth is through water and Spirit; in the Hermeticum, it is through knowledge and the expulsion of material passions. The climax of the treatise is one of the most ecstatic passages in ancient literature: Hermes, reborn, declares himself present in all things—heaven, earth, water, air, animals, plants—everywhere at once. The boundaries of the individual self have dissolved into the divine.

§1–2 · Corpus Hermeticum XIII.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐν τοῖς Γενικοῖς, ὦ πάτερ, αἰνιγματωδῶς καὶ οὐκ ἐπιφανῶς διελέξω περὶ θειότητος

English

Tat said: “In the General Discourses, Father, you spoke in riddles and did not reveal yourself when you spoke of the divine. You said that no one can be saved before the rebirth. When I became your suppliant, after you had spoken with me on the mountain, I asked to learn the discourse on rebirth, since of all discourses this is the only one I do not know. You said you would give it to me when I was ready to become a stranger to the world.”

§3 · Corpus Hermeticum XIII.3
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ γεννηθεὶς ἐκ ταύτης τῆς γεννήσεως, ὦ τέκνον, ἄλλος ἔσται· θεός, θεοῦ παῖς

English

Hermes said: “The one who is born of this rebirth, O son, is a different being: a god, a child of God, the all in all, composed of all the powers.” Tat said: “You are driving me mad, Father. What you say makes no sense. I wish to ask you more plainly: Am I then a stranger to my father’s race?”

§7–8 · Corpus Hermeticum XIII.7–8
Manuscript
Greek

τὴν σκηνὴν παρήλθομεν, ὦ τέκνον. ἐν τῇ ὀγδοάδι συνεστάθημεν.

English

The tent of the body we have passed through, O son. We have been constituted in the Ogdoad. The torments flee away. Let us now receive the powers. Knowledge of God has come to us, and with it knowledge has come: joy. With joy comes self-control. With self-control comes endurance. The tenth power that drives out the twelve: truth.

§14 · Corpus Hermeticum XIII.14
Manuscript
Greek

τὸ λοιπόν, ὦ τέκνον, καθαρεύσαντος ἐν ἡμῖν τοῦ νοῦ ταῖς δυνάμεσιν

English

From now on, O son, since the mind in us has been purified by the powers, the vision of the divine has come. For the rebirth is not something that can be taught, nor can it be seen through this material element. The old composite form has been dissolved. I am no longer what I was. I have been born in Mind.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1–2: The reference to “the mountain” where Hermes first promised this teaching echoes both the Mosaic revelation at Sinai and the Sermon on the Mount. Mountains in Hermetic literature consistently serve as places of ascent where the boundary between human and divine thins. The phrase “stranger to the world” (ξένος τῷ κόσμῳ) resonates with the Gnostic concept of the soul as an alien particle trapped in matter.
  2. §3: The declaration that the reborn one is “a god, a child of God” is the Hermetic theosis claim at its most explicit. Note that this is not a metaphor for moral improvement. The text says the reborn person is literally a “different being” (ἄλλος). The old self has been replaced. Compare Paul’s “I no longer live, but Christ lives in me” (Galatians 2:20) and the Orthodox teaching that in theosis the human person becomes “a new creation.”
  3. §11: This is the ecstatic climax of the treatise and one of the most remarkable passages in all of Hermetic literature. The reborn Hermes experiences himself as omnipresent—no longer confined to a single body or location. The dissolution of spatial boundaries mirrors the dissolution of the individual ego. Compare the Upanishadic formula: “I am the whole universe” (aham brahmasmi); and the Stoic doctrine of cosmic sympathy, in which the wise man recognizes himself as a part of the whole.
