Yoga Sutras 1.1-4: Yoga Is the Stilling of the Mind

Patanjali, c. 2nd c. BCE

Introduction

The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, composed sometime between the 2nd century BCE and the 4th century CE, are the foundational text of classical Yoga philosophy. Unlike the Upanishads, which teach through dialogues and analogies, the Sutras are compressed aphorisms—threads (sūtra literally means “thread”) designed to be memorized and unpacked with a teacher’s guidance. The first four verses contain the entire system in seed form.

Patanjali’s theosis is methodological. He does not argue about the nature of Brahman or the identity of ātman and the absolute. He simply states the problem and the solution. The problem: the mind’s fluctuations (vṛtti) cause the seer to misidentify with what is seen. The solution: still the fluctuations, and the seer abides in its own true nature. The word yoga itself means “union” or “yoking”—the same Indo-European root as the English “yoke” and the Latin iugum. To practise yoga is to yoke the individual self to its own deepest reality.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1: The word atha (“now”) is not merely a formal opening. In the Indian commentarial tradition, atha signals that the student is ready—that the prerequisites have been met. Vyasa’s commentary specifies that yoga presupposes a mind already partially disciplined through ethical practice. You do not begin yoga from zero; you begin it when you are prepared to receive the teaching.
  2. §1.2: This is the most famous definition in all of yoga literature. Citta is the mind-stuff, the field of consciousness. Vṛtti means “turning, revolving, fluctuation”—the constant activity of thoughts, emotions, memories, and projections. Nirodha means “cessation, restraint, stilling.” Yoga is not a posture, not a breathing exercise, not a philosophy. It is the stilling of mental activity. Everything else in the tradition is a means to this end.
  3. §1.3: The word svarūpe means “in its own form” or “in its own true nature.” When the fluctuations of the mind are stilled, what remains is not blankness or unconsciousness but the seer (draṣṭṛ) as it truly is—pure awareness, uncoloured by the objects it perceives. This is Patanjali’s version of theosis: not union with an external God, but the recognition of the self’s own nature as pure consciousness. Whether this consciousness is identical with Brahman (as Advaita Vedanta holds) or a distinct individual soul (as Sāṃkhya and Patanjali’s own system hold) is a major point of disagreement within Hindu philosophy.
  4. §1.4: The word sārūpyam means “conformity, identification, taking the same form.” When the mind is active, the seer takes on the form of its thoughts—it identifies with anger, with desire, with memory, with the body. This misidentification is the root of all suffering in Patanjali’s system. The entire eight-limbed path of yoga (aṣṭāṅga) exists to undo this false identification and restore the seer to its own nature.
