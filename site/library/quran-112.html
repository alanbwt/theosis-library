Quran 112: God Is One

Quran (divine revelation), c. 610-632 AD

Introduction

Surah al-Ikhlas (The Sincerity, or The Purity of Faith), Quran 112, is the shortest and most concentrated statement of Islamic monotheism. Consisting of only four verses, it is recited more frequently than any other surah in Islamic worship, and a hadith tradition records that the Prophet Muhammad declared it equal to one-third of the entire Quran. Its Arabic title, al-Ikhlas, means “purity” or “sincerity”—the purification of the concept of God from every form of association, comparison, or limitation.

The surah’s radical simplicity makes it the Islamic counterpart to the Shema Yisrael of Judaism and the opening of John’s Gospel in Christianity. Each tradition compresses its deepest theological conviction into a single declaration. For Islam, that declaration is tawhid—the absolute oneness of God, which excludes not only polytheism but any suggestion that the divine nature can be divided, shared, or compared.

Translator's Notes

  1. §112:1: The Arabic word Ahad (أحد) is stronger than wahid (واحد). Both mean “one,” but ahad implies an absolute, indivisible, incomparable unity. It is used almost exclusively for God in the Quran. The choice of ahad over wahid signals that God’s oneness is not merely numerical (one among others) but ontological (utterly unique, without category or parallel).
  2. §112:2: The term as-Samad (الصمد) is among the most debated in Quranic exegesis. It does not appear elsewhere in the Quran. Classical commentators offered multiple interpretations: the Eternal, the Self-Sufficient, the one to whom all creatures turn in their need, the Solid (having no hollow interior, i.e., needing nothing). The rendering “Eternal Refuge” captures both the self-sufficiency and the role as the ultimate recourse of all beings.
  3. §112:3: The denial of begetting and being begotten is both an anti-pagan statement (against the Arab belief in divine offspring) and an anti-Christian statement (against the doctrine of the Son as “begotten of the Father”). In the context of the theosis library, this verse represents the Islamic boundary: God can be approached, worshipped, and obeyed, but the gap between Creator and creature is absolute and unbridgeable. Where Christian theosis speaks of “becoming God,” Islam speaks of fana (annihilation of the self in God) without ontological union.
