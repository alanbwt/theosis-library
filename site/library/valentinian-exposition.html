A Valentinian Exposition: The Pleroma and the Fall

Valentinus, c. 100–160 AD

Introduction

A Valentinian Exposition is found in Nag Hammadi Codex XI and is the most technically systematic account of Valentinian cosmology to survive from the Gnostic side. Where the Gospel of Truth offers poetry and parable, this text offers ontological precision. It describes the structure of the Pleroma (the divine fullness), the Father’s self-unfolding through silence and thought, and the catastrophic rupture that produced the material world.

The text is badly damaged—large portions of every page are missing—but enough survives to reconstruct the essential argument. The two passages selected here describe the Father’s relationship to the Pleroma and the fall that necessitates restoration. The cosmology is dense but the soteriological stakes are clear: if the human soul contains a spark of the Pleroma, then knowledge of these structures is not abstract theology but a map for the journey home.

§The Father and the Pleroma (22.1–25.25) · Nag Hammadi Codex XI, tractate 2, plates 22–25
Manuscript
Coptic

ὁ πατὴρ ὑπάρχει ἐν τῷ ἀμερίστῳ · αὐτός ἐστιν ἐν ἷ τὸ πλήρωμα κατοικεῖ · καὶ ὃν τὸ πλήρωμα ὑστερεῖ · ἡ σιγὴ σύνεστιν αὐτῷ

English

The Father exists in the Undivided One. He is the one in whom the Totality dwells and whom the Totality lacks. He projected the Totalities, he who exists within himself, whom the Totality surrounds. It is the Father in whom the root of the Totality is established. He gave them their constitutions. He was not himself the one who brought himself forth. Each one of the Totalities is a thought, and a form, and a root.

Silence is his companion. He it is who projected the Totality from himself—that is, the eternal ones—he projected them as roots and as a spring, as fruits. They are offspring of his thought and of his will. He is the one from whom all things proceed, the one whom they all lack, since he retained within himself what they needed, what he did not give to the Totalities—the knowledge of the Father. He is the one who brought the Totalities into existence, and they knew neither where they came from nor whither they were going.

§The Fall and the Restoration (33.30–39.39) · Nag Hammadi Codex XI, tractate 2, plates 33–39
Manuscript
Coptic

ἡ Σοφία ἡ καλουμένη Ἀχαμώθ · ἐνεθυμήθη ἐπιθυμίαν ἄνευ τῆς συναινέσεως τοῦ συζύγου αὐτῆς · ἡ δὲ ἀποκατάστασις ἔσται · ὅταν τὸ πλήρωμα φανερωθῇ

English

Sophia, who is called Achamoth, had conceived a desire without the consent of her consort. And since she had no partner, her product was imperfect—for it was fashioned without her consort. And it did not resemble its mother, for it was a different form. When she saw the consequence of her desire, it had changed into the form of a serpent and a lion—with eyes that flash with fire.

She cast it away from herself, outside that place, so that none of the immortals might see it. For she had created it in ignorance. And she surrounded it with a luminous cloud and placed a throne in the middle of the cloud, so that no one might see it except the Holy Spirit, who is called the Mother of the Living. And she named it Yaltabaoth.

But the restoration will come when the Totality reveals itself in the one who is the Son. That is the restoration: the return to what is from the beginning. The one who has knowledge is from above. He is called to what is above. The one who has heard is called to turn himself and to ascend to the Father and to receive the Totalities—the restoration—and to discover himself there, in the place of rest.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Father and the Pleroma: The paradox that the Father both contains the Totality and is what the Totality lacks is the central tension of Valentinian theology. The Pleroma (divine fullness) dwells within the Father, yet lacks one thing: knowledge of the Father himself. This withholding is not malice but necessity—if the aeons possessed full knowledge, the dynamic unfolding that constitutes divine life would cease. Silence (Σιγή, Sige) as the Father’s consort appears in Irenaeus’s account of the Valentinian system (Against Heresies I.1.1) and represents the ineffable ground from which all emanation proceeds.
  2. §The Fall and the Restoration: The fall of Sophia (Wisdom) is the pivotal event in Valentinian cosmology. Her desire to know the Father without her syzygy (paired consort) produces an abortion—Yaltabaoth, the ignorant demiurge who creates the material world in imitation of the Pleroma he cannot see. The detail that the product resembled “a serpent and a lion” echoes Egyptian and Jewish apocalyptic imagery. The restoration (apokatastasis, ἀποκατάστασις) is the Valentinian term for universal salvation—the moment when all divine sparks return to the Pleroma and the material world dissolves. Origen would later adopt this concept, and the Church would condemn it at the Fifth Ecumenical Council in 553.
