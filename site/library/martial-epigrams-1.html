Introduction

Martial’s Epigrams Book 1 introduces the genre he perfected: short, witty, often savage poems about Roman life. His preface defends the explicit humor of his verse while his poems dissect social pretension with surgical precision.

Manuscript
Greek

Spero me secutum in libellis meis tale temperamentum ut de illis queri non possit quisquis de se bene senserit

English

I hope that in my little books I have observed such restraint that whoever has a fair mind cannot complain of them. They do not attack persons but spare them, which was not the custom of the ancients. I trust my pages will not be interpreted by the malicious. It is wrong to exercise one’s cleverness upon another’s book.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Preface: Martial’s defense of literary freedom—“my page is wanton but my life is moral”—established a distinction between author and persona that remained central to Western literary theory. His epigrammatic form influenced the entire European tradition of wit from Jonson through Wilde.
