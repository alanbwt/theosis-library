Introduction

The Kalpa Sutra, attributed to Bhadrabahu (c. 4th century BCE), contains the biographies of the Jain Tirthankaras (ford-makers). Its account of Mahavira's life, renunciation, and attainment of omniscience is the central narrative of the Jain tradition.

Translation

§Life of Mahavira: Renunciation · Kalpa Sutra, Section on Mahavira

Then the venerable ascetic Mahavira, having abandoned all worldly possessions, renounced the world. He tore out his hair in five handfuls and cast it away. He walked forth from his house, possessing nothing, accepting nothing, desiring nothing. For twelve years he practiced austerities, keeping silence, meditating, and bearing all hardships. In the thirteenth year, on the bank of the river Rijupalika, under a Sal tree, he obtained the highest knowledge and intuition, called kevala—absolute, complete, and infinite.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Life of Mahavira: The act of pulling out one's hair (keshaloncha) rather than shaving it remains a practice among Jain monastics to this day, symbolizing the voluntary acceptance of pain. Kevala jnana (absolute knowledge) is the Jain concept of omniscience—the simultaneous, complete knowledge of all things past, present, and future. Unlike Buddhist enlightenment, it is explicitly cognitive rather than experiential.
