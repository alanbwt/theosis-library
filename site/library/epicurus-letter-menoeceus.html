Letter to Menoeceus: Death Is Nothing to Us

Epicurus, 341-270 BCE

Introduction

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) wrote his Letter to Menoeceus as a summary of his ethical teaching for a young student. The letter contains the core of Epicurean philosophy in condensed form: the gods are real but indifferent to human affairs; death is the dissolution of atoms and therefore nothing to fear; pleasure (understood as the absence of pain, not sensory indulgence) is the highest good; and the philosophical life can achieve a state of tranquility (ataraxia) equal to that of the gods themselves.

The letter is remarkable for its calm dismantling of every premise that theosis traditions depend on. There is no afterlife. There is no divine judgment. There is no cosmic purpose. The gods live in perfect bliss precisely because they do not care about us. Yet Epicurus does not counsel despair—he counsels freedom. If the gods are not watching, then the fear of divine punishment is groundless, and the human being is liberated to pursue genuine happiness. The Epicurean sage, living without anxiety among friends, “lives like a god among men.”

§1 · Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus (Diogenes Laertius X.122)
Manuscript
Greek

Μήτε νέος τις ὢν μελλέτω φιλοσοφεῖν, μήτε γέρων ὑπάρχων κοπιάτω φιλοσοφῶν.

English

Let no young man delay the study of philosophy, and let no old man grow weary of it. For it is never too early or too late to care for the health of the soul. To say that the time for philosophy has not yet come or has passed is like saying the time for happiness has not yet come or has gone by.

§2 · Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus (Diogenes Laertius X.123–124)
Manuscript
Greek

πρῶτον μὲν τὸν θεὸν ζῷον ἄφθαρτον καὶ μακάριον νομίζων.

English

First, believe that God is a blessed and immortal being, as the common idea of God suggests. Do not attribute to him anything incompatible with his blessedness or his immortality. The gods do exist, for our knowledge of them is clear. But they are not as the many believe them to be, for the many do not preserve the true notion of the gods.

§3 · Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus (Diogenes Laertius X.124–125)
Manuscript
Greek

συνέθιζε δὲ ἐν τῷ νομίζειν μηδὲν πρὸς ἡμᾶς εἶναι τὸν θάνατον.

English

Accustom yourself to the belief that death is nothing to us. For all good and evil lies in sensation, and death is the deprivation of sensation. Therefore a correct understanding that death is nothing to us makes the mortality of life enjoyable—not by adding infinite time, but by taking away the longing for immortality.

§4 · Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus (Diogenes Laertius X.135)
Manuscript
Greek

ταῦτα οὖν καὶ τὰ τούτοις συγγενῆ μελέτα… ζήσεις δὲ ὡς θεὸς ἐν ἀνθρώποις.

English

Practise these and related precepts day and night, both by yourself and with one who is like-minded, and you will never be disturbed either waking or sleeping. You will live as a god among men. For a man who lives among immortal goods is in no way like a mortal being.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2: Epicurus’s theology is subtle. He affirms that the gods exist but denies that they take any interest in human affairs. The gods are composed of the finest atoms, living in the spaces between worlds (metakosmia), enjoying perfect tranquility. They do not create, govern, reward, or punish. This eliminates the entire framework of salvation—there is nothing to be saved from and no one to save you.
  2. §3: The argument that death is “nothing to us” because it is the end of sensation directly attacks the premise of theosis traditions that promise eternal life. If consciousness simply ceases at death, then deification is a solution to a problem that does not exist. The Epicurean does not need to become immortal because mortality is not a deficiency.
  3. §4: The closing promise—“you will live as a god among men” (ζήσεις δὲ ὡς θεὸς ἐν ἀνθρώποις)—is the Epicurean version of theosis. The sage becomes godlike not through union with the divine but through achieving the same state the gods enjoy: freedom from pain, freedom from fear, freedom from desire. It is deification by subtraction rather than addition.
