Deuteronomy 6:4-5: The Shema

Moses (attributed), traditional

Introduction

The Shema—“Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is one”—is the foundational declaration of Jewish monotheism and the most important prayer in Judaism. Recited twice daily by observant Jews, it establishes the unity of God as the first principle from which all else follows. The command that follows—to love God with all your heart, soul, and strength—is not merely an ethical instruction but an ontological one: the proper response to divine unity is total self-giving.

For the theosis tradition, the Shema poses a fundamental question. If God is absolutely one, how can a human being participate in the divine nature without compromising that unity? The Jewish mystical tradition answered through the concept of devekut (cleaving to God)—an intimate union that does not dissolve the distinction between Creator and creature. Jesus cited this passage as the greatest commandment (Mark 12:29–30), and the Christian tradition built its understanding of theosis on the tension between the Shema’s absolute monotheism and the claim that God became human.

English

And you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart (kardia, καρδία), and with all your soul (psyche, ψυχή), and with all your strength (dynamis, δύναμις).

Translator's Notes

  1. §6:4: The Hebrew original reads Shema Yisrael YHWH Eloheinu YHWH Echad. The Greek Septuagint renders the divine name YHWH as Kyrios (Κύριος, “Lord”), following the Jewish practice of substituting Adonai for the unpronounceable name. The word echad (“one”) can mean either numerical oneness or uniqueness. The Septuagint’s heis (εἷς) carries the same ambiguity. Jewish mystical tradition later explored how the divine unity could encompass internal multiplicity through the doctrine of the sefirot.
  2. §6:5: The Hebrew has three terms: levav (heart), nephesh (soul/life), and meod (very, muchness, strength). The Septuagint renders these as kardia, psyche, and dynamis. When Jesus quotes this passage in Mark 12:30, he adds a fourth term, dianoia (mind), perhaps under the influence of Hellenistic anthropology’s finer distinctions between the faculties of the soul. The threefold (or fourfold) enumeration implies that love of God must engage the whole person—not merely the emotions, not merely the intellect, but every capacity of one’s being.
