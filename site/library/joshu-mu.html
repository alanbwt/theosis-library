Joshu's Mu: Does a Dog Have Buddha-Nature?

Wumen Huikai, 1183-1260 AD

Introduction

Case 1 of the Wúménguān (Japanese: Mumonkan, “The Gateless Gate”) is the most famous koan in the Zen tradition. Compiled by the Chinese master Wumen Huikai (1183–1260), the collection begins with this exchange: a monk asks Zhaozhou (Japanese: Jōshū, 778–897), “Does a dog have Buddha-nature?” Zhaozhou answers: “Wú” (Japanese: “Mu”)—“No” or “Nothing.” This answer is deliberately impossible. Orthodox Mahayana doctrine teaches that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature. Zhaozhou knows this. The monk knows this. The answer is not a doctrinal statement but a demolition of the question itself.

For the theosis question, Mu is the most compressed Buddhist response possible. Do beings possess the divine nature? Mu. The answer is not yes and not no. It is the sound of the question breaking apart in your hands. Wumen’s commentary makes this explicit: you must make your whole body a mass of doubt, concentrate on this single word, and pass through the barrier. What lies on the other side cannot be described, because description is what Mu destroys.

§The Case · Wúménguān, Case 1
Manuscript
Chinese

Zhàozhōu yīn sēng wèn: Gǒu zǐ hái yǒu fóxìng yě wú? Zhōu yún: Wú.

English

A monk asked Zhaozhou in all earnestness: “Does a dog have Buddha-nature or not?” Zhaozhou said: “Mu.”

§Wumen's Commentary · Wúménguān, Case 1
Manuscript
Chinese

Cān Chán xū tòu zǔshī guān, miàowù yāo qióng xīnlù jué. Zǔguān bú tòu, xīnlù bú jué, jìn rú yī cǎò mù zhī guǐ. Rúhé shì zǔshī guān? Zhǐ zhè yī gè Wú zì, nǎ shì Zōng mén yī guān. Suǒyǐ huàn zànchéng Chánzōng Wúménguān. Ruò tòudé guò, fēi dàn qīnjìàn Zhàozhōu, biàn yǔ lìdài zǔshī bǎ shǒu gòngxíng, méimaorú, tóngyī yǎn jìng jiàn, tóngyī ěr wén. Qǐ búqunǐzāi!

English

For the practice of Zen, you must pass through the barrier of the patriarchs. To attain wondrous awakening, you must completely cut off the mind-road. If you do not pass through the barrier and do not cut off the mind-road, then you are like a ghost clinging to grasses and trees. What is the barrier of the patriarchs? Simply this single word “Mu”—that is the front gate of Zen. Therefore it is called the “Gateless Barrier of the Zen School.” If you pass through it, you will not only see Zhaozhou face to face, but you will walk hand in hand with the whole line of patriarchs. You will see with the same eyes they see with and hear with the same ears. Is that not a joyful thing?

§Wumen's Verse · Wúménguān, Case 1
Manuscript
Chinese

Gǒu zǐ fóxìng, quántí lìnglù. Cái shè yǒu wú, sàngshēn shīmìng. Ruò zhúcí zī jú, jí sǐ rén yě.

English

The dog, the Buddha-nature—the whole thing is presented. A moment’s hesitation and you miss it. If you chase after words and pursue phrases, you are a dead man.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Case: The Chinese character “無” (wú, Japanese mu) means “without,” “not,” or “nothing.” In the Rinzai Zen tradition, this single syllable is assigned as the first koan to virtually every student. The practitioner is instructed to become Mu—not to think about it, not to analyze it, but to embody it with every breath until the distinction between the questioner and the question collapses. Zhaozhou’s answer contradicts established Mahayana doctrine (the Mahāparinīrvāṇa Sūtra explicitly states that all beings have Buddha-nature). The contradiction is the point: Zhaozhou is not teaching doctrine but destroying the student’s reliance on it.
  2. §Wumen's Commentary: The phrase “cut off the mind-road” (qióng xīnlù jué) describes the moment when conceptual thinking can go no further and collapses. This is not intellectual understanding but an experiential crisis. Wumen’s promise—that passing through the barrier allows you to “see with the same eyes” as the patriarchs—is a remarkable claim of identity with the awakened ones. This is the Zen version of theosis: not becoming something new but recognizing that your seeing was always the patriarchs’ seeing, obscured only by the habit of conceptual overlay.
  3. §Wumen's Verse: The phrase “the whole thing is presented” (quántí lìnglù) is key. The question “Does a dog have Buddha-nature?” already contains the complete answer—not as a proposition to be affirmed or denied but as a reality to be directly perceived. The verse warns that any attempt to process the koan through language (“chasing words, pursuing phrases”) leads to spiritual death. The koan is not a riddle with a clever answer; it is a tool for breaking the mind’s addiction to answers.
