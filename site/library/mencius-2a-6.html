Introduction

Mencius 2A:6 contains the famous argument for the innate goodness of human nature, illustrated by the example of a child about to fall into a well. It is the foundational text for the Confucian doctrine of the four moral sprouts.

§2A.6 · Mencius, Book 2A, Chapter 6
Manuscript
Greek

人皆有不忍人之心

English

Mencius said: "All humans have a mind that cannot bear to see the suffering of others. Suppose someone suddenly sees a child about to fall into a well. Everyone in such a situation would feel alarm and compassion—not to gain favor with the child's parents, not to seek praise from neighbors and friends, and not because they dislike the sound of the child's cries. From this we can see: whoever lacks the feeling of compassion is not human. Whoever lacks the feeling of shame is not human. Whoever lacks the feeling of deference is not human. Whoever lacks the feeling of right and wrong is not human. The feeling of compassion is the sprout of benevolence. The feeling of shame is the sprout of righteousness. The feeling of deference is the sprout of propriety. The feeling of right and wrong is the sprout of wisdom."

Translator's Notes

  1. §2A.6: The "four sprouts" (si duan) doctrine became the cornerstone of Confucian moral psychology. Mencius's argument is that moral sentiments are innate but require cultivation—like sprouts that must be watered to grow into mature plants. Xunzi rejected this, arguing human nature is evil and morality is artificial. This debate between Mencius and Xunzi structured Chinese ethical thought for two millennia.
