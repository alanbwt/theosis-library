Introduction

Book 2 of the Histories is Herodotus’s account of Egypt—its geography, customs, and religion. It is the most extensive description of Egyptian civilization to survive from antiquity and reveals a Greek observer’s fascination with an older culture.

§2.2–3 · Herodotus, Histories
Manuscript
Greek

Οἱ δὲ Αἰγύπτιοι, πρὶν μὲν ἢ Ψαμμήτιχον σφέων βασιλεῦσαι

English

The Egyptians, before the reign of Psammetichus, considered themselves to be the oldest of all peoples. But Psammetichus, wishing to learn the truth, devised an experiment: he gave two newborn children to a goatherd to raise among his flocks, commanding that no one should speak any word in their presence, to discover what word they would first utter.

§2.53 · Herodotus, Histories
Manuscript
Greek

ὅθεν δὲ ἐγένοντο ἕκαστος τῶν θεῶν

English

Where each of the gods came from, or whether they all existed forever, and what they were like in form—these things were not known until just recently. For Homer and Hesiod, I believe, lived not more than four hundred years before my time, and it was they who made a theogony for the Greeks, gave the gods their epithets, assigned their honors and arts, and described their forms.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2.53: Herodotus’s claim that Homer and Hesiod “made” the Greek gods is a remarkable early statement of the constructedness of religious tradition. It anticipates Xenophanes’s critique and the Euhemerist argument that gods are deified humans. The observation that theology has a history was as unsettling in the fifth century BCE as it is today.
