Corpus Hermeticum I: Poimandres

Hermes Trismegistus (attributed), c. 1st-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The Poimandres is the first and most famous treatise of the Corpus Hermeticum, a collection of Greek philosophical and theological texts attributed to Hermes Trismegistus (“Thrice-Greatest Hermes”), a syncretic figure merging the Greek god Hermes with the Egyptian god Thoth. Written in the second or third century CE, the Poimandres describes a visionary encounter in which the divine Mind (Nous, νοῦς) reveals to Hermes the creation of the cosmos, the fall of humanity into matter, and the path of return to the divine. It is the foundational text of the Hermetic theosis tradition: the human being, originally divine, has forgotten its origin and must awaken through gnosis to reclaim its place among the gods.

The text’s influence is difficult to overstate. When Marsilio Ficino translated it into Latin in 1463, he believed it predated Moses and Plato. The Church Fathers—Lactantius, Clement of Alexandria, Augustine—engaged with it seriously. Its vision of the soul’s ascent through the planetary spheres, shedding vices at each level, became one of the most enduring images in Western esotericism.

§1 · Corpus Hermeticum I.1
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐννοίας μοί ποτε γενομένης περὶ τῶν Ὄντων, καὶ μετεωρισθείσης μου τῆς διανοίας σφόδρα, κατασχεθεισῶν μου τῶν σωματικῶν αἰσθήσεων

English

Once, when I had begun to think about the things that are, and my thoughts had soared exceedingly high, while my bodily senses were restrained as in sleep—though not such sleep as comes from heaviness of food or weariness of body—I thought a being of vast and boundless magnitude came toward me, called my name, and said to me: “What do you wish to hear and see, and having seen, to learn and know?”

§2 · Corpus Hermeticum I.2
Manuscript
Greek

τίς εἶ σύ; φησίν. Ἐγὼ μέν εἰμι ὁ Ποιμάνδρης, ὁ τῆς αὐθεντίας νοῦς.

English

I said: “Who are you?” He replied: “I am Poimandres, the Mind of the Sovereignty. I know what you wish, and I am with you everywhere.”

§3–4 · Corpus Hermeticum I.3–4
Manuscript
Greek

βούλομαι μαθεῖν τὰ Ὄντα καὶ νοῆσαι τὴν φύσιν αὐτῶν καὶ γνῶναι τὸν θεόν.

English

I said: “I wish to learn about the things that are, and understand their nature, and know God.” He said: “Hold in your mind all that you wish to learn, and I will teach you.” With these words, he changed his form, and suddenly everything was opened before me in a moment, and I saw a boundless vision: all became light, serene and joyful. And I fell in love with it.

§6 · Corpus Hermeticum I.6
Manuscript
Greek

τὸ φῶς ἐκεῖνο ἐγώ, νοῦς, ὁ σὸς θεός, ὁ πρὸ τῆς ἐκ σκότους φανείσης ὑγρᾶς φύσεως.

English

That light is I, Mind, your God, who was before the watery nature that appeared out of the darkness. And the luminous Word that issued from Mind is the Son of God.

§12–14 · Corpus Hermeticum I.12–14

Greek

ὁ δὲ Νοῦς ὁ πατὴρ πάντων, ὤν ζωὴ καὶ φῶς, ἀπεκύησεν ἄνθρωπον αὐτῷ ἴσον

English

Now Mind, the Father of all, being life and light, brought forth Man, equal to himself, and loved him as his own child. For Man was very beautiful, bearing the image of his Father. For indeed God fell in love with his own form, and gave over to him all his works. And Man, seeing the creation which the Demiurge had fashioned in the fire, wished also to make things, and the Father gave him permission.

§15 · Corpus Hermeticum I.15
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ δὲ πᾶσαν ἐξουσίαν ἔχων τοῦ τῶν θνητῶν κόσμου

English

And Man, who had full power over the world of mortal creatures and of animals without reason, stooped through the harmony of the spheres, having broken through their envelopes, and showed to the downward-tending Nature the beautiful form of God. When Nature saw him who had in himself the inexhaustible beauty and all the energy of the Governors, combined with the form of God, she smiled with love.

§21 · Corpus Hermeticum I.21
Manuscript
Greek

πρῶτον μὲν ἐν τῇ ἀναλύσει τοῦ σώματος ὑλικοῦ παραδίδως αὐτὸ τὸ σῶμα

English

“But first you must tear off this garment which you wear—this cloak of darkness, this web of ignorance, this prop of evil, this bond of corruption, this living death, this conscious corpse, this tomb you carry around with you, this robber in your house, who hates you through what he loves, and envies you through what he hates.”

§24–26 · Corpus Hermeticum I.24–26
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ τότε γυμνωθεῖσα ἡ ψυχὴ ἀπὸ τῶν τῆς ἁρμονίας ἐνεργημάτων... τοῦτο ἐστι τὸ ἀγαθὸν τέλος τοῖς γνῶσιν ἐσχηκόσι, θεωθῆναι.

English

And then the soul, stripped of all that the spheres had wrought upon it, enters the eighth sphere, having only its own proper power, and together with those already there, it hymns the Father. And those present rejoice at its coming, and becoming like its companions, it hears certain powers above the eighth sphere hymning God with a sweet voice. And they ascend in order to the Father, and surrender themselves to the powers, and becoming powers themselves, enter into God. This is the good end of those who have gained knowledge: to become God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1: The name Poimandres (Ποιμάνδρης) has been variously etymologized as “Shepherd of Men” (from Greek poimen andron) or from the Coptic p-eime nte-re (“the knowledge of Re”). The text itself identifies Poimandres as Nous—the divine Mind or Intellect. This is not a personal god but the self-thinking thought of the divine, remarkably close to Aristotle’s noesis noeseos and to what Plotinus would later call the first emanation from the One.
  2. §6: The phrase “the luminous Word that issued from Mind is the Son of God” is striking in its parallel to John 1:1. Early Christian apologists debated whether the Hermetica borrowed from John, John borrowed from Hermes, or both drew from a common source. The current scholarly consensus dates the Hermetica to the second–third centuries CE, making direct dependence on John possible but not certain.
  3. §12–14: The Hermetic creation of Man is radically different from Genesis. Here, Man is not fashioned from clay but born directly from the divine Mind, “equal to himself.” Man’s fall is not disobedience but desire—he sees the created world and wishes to create as well. This is a fall through love and ambition, not sin. The Gnostic resonance is obvious, but the Hermetic framework is more optimistic: the fall can be fully reversed.
  4. §24–26: The Greek word θεωθῆναι (theothenai, “to become God” or “to be deified”) appears here in what may be its earliest philosophical usage. This is not metaphor. The text states plainly that the end of the path of knowledge is to “become God”—not to become god-like, not to approach God, but to enter into God and become a power within the divine. Compare Athanasius’s later formula: “God became man so that man might become God.”
