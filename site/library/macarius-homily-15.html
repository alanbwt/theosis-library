Homily 15: All Light, All Face, All Eye

Pseudo-Macarius, c. 380–390 AD

Introduction

Pseudo-Macarius, Homily 15 contains one of the most visually stunning descriptions of deification in all of patristic literature. The soul that participates in the divine Spirit “becomes all light, all face, all eye.” There is no part of the soul that is not penetrated by light; no hidden recess of darkness remains. The human being becomes transparent to divinity—not by ceasing to be human but by being so thoroughly saturated with divine light that nothing else is visible.

The Macarian Homilies (fourth or fifth century CE) survive in multiple Greek recensions and were enormously influential in both Eastern and Western Christianity. The author—probably a Syrian monk, not the historical Macarius of Egypt—writes from direct spiritual experience rather than philosophical argument. The passage presented here is the climax of a sustained meditation on the transformation of the soul through the indwelling of the Holy Spirit. It represents the experiential, mystical wing of the theosis tradition: not doctrine about deification but a report from inside it.

§15.38a · Pseudo-Macarius, Homily 15.38 (Dorries, PTS 4)
Manuscript
Greek

ἡ ψυχὴ ἡ καταξιωθεῖσα τοῦ πνεύματος τοῦ φωτὸς αὐτοῦ μετασχεῖν καὶ καταλαμφθεῖσα ὑπὸ τοῦ κάλλους τῆς ἀρρήτου δόξης αὐτοῦ · θρόνον καὶ κατοικητήριον ἑαυτῷ κατασκευάσαντος · ὅλη φῶς γίνεται καὶ ὅλη πρόσωπον καὶ ὅλη ὀφθαλμός ·

English

The soul that is deemed worthy to participate in the Spirit of his light, and is illuminated by the beauty of his ineffable glory, since he has prepared it for himself as a throne and dwelling—such a soul becomes all light, all face, all eye.

§15.38b · Pseudo-Macarius, Homily 15.38 (Dorries, PTS 4)
Manuscript
Greek

οὐδὲν γάρ ἐστιν αὐτῆς μέρος ὃ μή ἐστιν πλῆρες τῶν πνευματικῶν ὀφθαλμῶν τοῦ φωτός · τουτέστιν οὐδὲν αὐτῆς μέρος ἐσκοτισμένον ἐστίν · ἀλλ’ ὅλη φῶς καὶ πνεῦμα γεγένηται ·

English

There is no part of the soul that is not full of the spiritual eyes of light. That is to say, there is no part of it that is in darkness, but it has been made entirely light and spirit. It is wholly full of eyes and has no back part, but stands as a face in every direction, with the ineffable beauty of the glory of the light of Christ having mounted and seated itself upon it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §15.38a: The phrase “all light, all face, all eye” (ὅλη φῶς, ὅλη πρόσωπον, ὅλη ὀφθαλμός) draws on the vision of the four living creatures in Ezekiel 1 and Revelation 4, who are described as “full of eyes all around.” But Pseudo-Macarius applies this angelic description to the human soul. The soul does not merely see God; it becomes the organ of vision. The distinction between subject and object collapses: the soul is simultaneously the one who sees and the instrument of seeing.
  2. §15.38b: The statement that the soul “has no back part” (οὐκ ἔχει ὀπίσθιον) deliberately inverts Exodus 33:23, where God tells Moses, “You shall see my back, but my face shall not be seen.” Moses could only see God’s back—the retreating aftermath of divine presence. The deified soul, by contrast, is “face in every direction.” It has achieved what Moses was denied: unmediated, total, omnidirectional encounter with the divine light. This is theosis as the completion of the Exodus narrative.
