Galatians 2:20: Christ Lives in Me

Πρὸς Γαλάτας
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

Galatians 2:20 is among the most compressed statements of mystical theology in the Pauline letters. In a single verse, Paul declares that his old self has been crucified, that he no longer lives, and that Christ now lives in him. The logic is not metaphorical. Paul is describing an exchange of persons: the ego dies, and a divine life takes its place. The verse stands at the pivot point of Paul’s argument against the Judaizers—the law cannot accomplish what the cross has already done.

For the theosis tradition, this verse is foundational. The Greek Fathers read it as direct testimony to mutual indwelling: Christ in the believer, the believer in Christ. Maximus the Confessor built his entire theology of perichoresis (interpenetration) on passages like this one. The text here follows Codex Sinaiticus.

§2:20 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

Χριστῷ συνεσταύρωμαι · ζῶ δὲ οὐκέτι ἐγώ · ζῆ δὲ ἐν ἐμοὶ Χριστός · ὃ δὲ νῦν ζῶ ἐν σαρκί · ἐν πίστει ζῶ τῆ τοῦ υἱοῦ τοῦ θεοῦ · τοῦ ἀγαπήσαντός με καὶ παραδόντος ἑαυτὸν ὑπὲρ ἐμοῦ ·

English

I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me. And the life I now live in the flesh, I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:20: The phrase zo de ouketi ego, ze de en emoi Christos (ζῶ δὲ οὐκέτι ἐγώ, ζῆ δὲ ἐν ἐμοὶ Χριστός) describes a mutual indwelling that the Greek Fathers took as literal ontology rather than pious metaphor. The self does not merely imitate Christ; the self is replaced by Christ while paradoxically remaining a self that lives “in the flesh.” This paradox—identity dissolved yet preserved—is the heart of the theosis tradition. The Western tradition has tended to read this verse in terms of justification by faith. The Eastern tradition reads it as a description of deification: the human person becomes a vessel for divine life without ceasing to be human.
