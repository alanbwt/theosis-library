Yasht 10: Hymn to Mithra

Zarathustra (Zoroaster), c. 1500-1000 BCE

Introduction

Yasht 10 of the Avesta, the Hymn to Mithra (Mihr Yasht), is the longest and most elaborate of the Zoroastrian hymns. Composed in Young Avestan, with archaic elements suggesting an origin well before the Achaemenid period (possibly 1000–600 BCE), it celebrates Mithra as the deity of contracts, justice, and the sun. Mithra watches over all human agreements and punishes the contract-breaker (mithra-druj) with devastating force.

The hymn’s significance extends far beyond Zoroastrianism. Mithra migrated into the Roman Empire as the central figure of the Mithraic mysteries, a solar cult that was Christianity’s principal rival in the second and third centuries. The attributes celebrated here—cosmic oversight, association with light and truth, mediation between heaven and earth—were absorbed and transformed by traditions that came after.

§Yt. 10.1–3 · Yasht 10.1–3 (Geldner edition)
Manuscript
Avestan

mrāoō ahurō mazdā spitāmāi zaraθuštrāi: yəzī him frāθərəsəm vourū-gaoyaoitiō miθrəm, spitāma, itaθa avaōhaō yazatəm daθāmī yim ahurəm mazdām.

English

Ahura Mazda spoke to Spitama Zarathustra: “When I created Mithra of the wide pastures, O Spitama, I made him as worthy of worship and prayer as I myself, Ahura Mazda. The contract-breaker destroys the whole land. He who lies to Mithra brings death upon the whole land. Never break a contract, O Spitama—neither one made with a follower of the Lie nor one made with a follower of Truth.”

§Yt. 10.12–14 · Yasht 10.12–14 (Geldner edition)
Manuscript
Avestan

miθrəm vourū-gaoyaoitiō yazāmaidē, haθrō-vacō hu-xšaθrō hazahra-gaošəm baevare-cašmanəm bərəzaōtəm. yo paoiryō maināvō yazatō haraitiō parā jašaiti huvarə xšaētəm amarəxšām.

English

We worship Mithra of the wide pastures, who is truth-speaking, eloquent, with a thousand ears, well-formed, with ten thousand eyes, lofty, wide-seeing, strong, sleepless, and ever-waking. He first of the heavenly gods reaches over the Hara mountain before the immortal sun with its swift horses. He first, in a gold-adorned form, grasps the beautiful summits and from there surveys the whole land of the Aryans.

§Yt. 10.95–96 · Yasht 10.95–96 (Geldner edition)
Manuscript
Avestan

miθrəm vourū-gaoyaoitiō yazāmaidē, yo raevō vazāiti upaōha raoxašnanəm raθəm.

English

We worship Mithra of the wide pastures, who drives in a high-wheeled chariot made of a heavenly substance. Its one wheel is golden, fashioned in heaven. He drives forth on the right side of this wide, round earth, whose limits are far, shining like the moon among the stars, when he has put on the garment of heaven.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Yt. 10.1–3: The declaration that Mithra is “as worthy of worship and prayer as Ahura Mazda himself” is striking in a tradition that is often presented as monotheistic. Zoroastrian theology resolves this through a hierarchy of divine beings: Ahura Mazda is the supreme creator, while Mithra is a yazata (a being worthy of worship) who operates under and with Ahura Mazda’s authority. The relationship is not equality but delegated sovereignty.
  2. §Yt. 10.12–14: The description of Mithra as having “a thousand ears” and “ten thousand eyes” expresses divine omniscience through hyperbolic imagery. The association with the sun—Mithra rises before the sun and surveys the whole earth—is the link to the later Roman cult of Sol Invictus (the Unconquered Sun) and the Mithraic mysteries, in which the initiate underwent a symbolic death and rebirth associated with solar symbolism.
