Guide for the Perplexed I.68: What God Is Not

Maimonides, 1138-1204 AD

Introduction

Moses ben Maimon (Maimonides, 1138–1204) is the greatest philosopher of the medieval Jewish tradition. His Guide for the Perplexed (Dalalat al-Ha’irin in the original Judeo-Arabic) was written for students torn between the claims of philosophy and revelation. In Part I, Chapter 68, Maimonides articulates what became the definitive Jewish statement of apophatic theology: God’s essence is absolutely unknowable, and every positive attribute we ascribe to God is either a metaphor or a description of God’s actions, not God’s nature. We can only say what God is not.

This via negativa places Maimonides in the same tradition as Pseudo-Dionysius in Christianity and the Mu’tazili theologians in Islam. For the theosis question, the implications are profound. If God’s essence is unknowable, then theosis cannot mean the soul’s acquisition of divine attributes, since we do not know what those attributes truly are. Instead, the highest human achievement is intellectual apprehension of God’s existence and unity—a knowledge that perfects the intellect without revealing the divine essence itself.

English

Know that the description of God by means of negations is the correct description—one in which there is no looseness of expression. Positive attributes, on the other hand, necessarily involve some measure of association with created things and therefore impute imperfection to God.

§Guide I.68 (excerpt 2) · Guide for the Perplexed, Part I, Chapter 68
Manuscript
Arabic/Hebrew

כשאנו אומרים שהקדוש ברוך הוא קיים פירושו שהעדרו ממנו נמנע

English

When we say God exists, we mean that his non-existence is impossible. When we say he is powerful, we mean that powerlessness is not found in him. When we say he is knowing, we mean that ignorance is not found in him. Every attribute is thus a negation of its opposite, not a positive description of the divine essence.

§Guide I.68 (excerpt 3) · Guide for the Perplexed, Part I, Chapter 68
Manuscript
Arabic/Hebrew

אין מציאותו כמציאות שאר הנמצאים ואין ידיעתו כידיעתנו

English

God’s existence is not like the existence of other things. His knowledge is not like our knowledge. His power is not like our power. The difference is not merely one of degree but of kind. The words are the same, but the meanings share nothing in common except the name.

Translator's Notes

  1. §General: Maimonides wrote the Guide in Judeo-Arabic (Arabic written in Hebrew characters), the standard literary language of educated Jews in the Islamic world. It was translated into Hebrew by Samuel ibn Tibbon in 1204 (the year of Maimonides’ death) and became a foundational text for both Jewish and Christian philosophy. Thomas Aquinas knew it through a Latin translation and engaged extensively with its arguments.
  2. §Guide I.68, excerpt 3: The principle that divine attributes share only the name, not the meaning, with their human counterparts is the doctrine of “equivocation” (ishtirak al-ism). It is one of the most rigorous apophatic positions in medieval philosophy. Aquinas modified it into the doctrine of “analogy”: divine attributes are neither identical to nor wholly different from human ones, but stand in a proportional relationship. This seemingly technical distinction has vast implications for the theosis question: if God’s attributes are wholly equivocal, the gap between divine and human nature may be unbridgeable.
