The Melian Dialogue: The Strong Do What They Can

The Melian Dialogue: The Strong Do What They Can
Thucydides, c. 460-400 BCE
Codex Laurentianus 69.2

Introduction

In 416 BCE, Athens sent a fleet to the neutral island of Melos and demanded unconditional surrender. The ensuing dialogue, as reconstructed by Thucydides, strips away all pretense of justice in international relations. The Athenians state openly that might makes right, that the gods themselves follow this principle, and that the Melians' hope in justice is suicidal. When the Melians refuse to submit, Athens kills all the men and enslaves the women and children. The passage remains the foundational text of political realism.

§5.89 · Thuc. 5.89
Greek

ἐπειδὴ... δίκαια μὲν ἐν τῷ ἀνθρωπείῳ λόγῳ ἀπὸ τῆς ἴσης ἀνάγκης κρίνεται, δυνατὰ δὲ οἱ προύχοντες πράσσουσι καὶ οἱ ἀσθενεῖς ξυγχωροῦσιν.

English

Since... in the world of men, justice is only decided between equals in power, while the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must.

§5.105 · Thuc. 5.105
Greek

ἡγούμεθα γὰρ τό τε θεῖον δόξῃ τὸ ἀνθρώπειόν τε σαφῶς διὰ παντὸς ὑπὸ φύσεως ἀναγκαίας, οὗ ἂν κρατῇ, ἄρχειν.

English

We believe that the divine, by common opinion, and the human, by clear evidence, are both compelled by nature to rule wherever they can.
