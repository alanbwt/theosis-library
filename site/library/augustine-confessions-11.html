Introduction

Confessions Book 11 contains Augustine’s famous analysis of time: “What then is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain it to one who asks, I do not know.” It remains one of the most penetrating philosophical examinations of temporality ever written.

Greek original and English translation.
§11.14.17 · Augustine, Confessions 11.14.17
Manuscript
Greek

Quid est ergo tempus? Si nemo ex me quaerat, scio; si quaerenti explicare velim, nescio

English

What then is time? If no one asks me, I know what it is. If I wish to explain it to one who asks, I do not know. Yet I say with confidence that I know this: if nothing passed away, there would be no past time; if nothing were coming, there would be no future time; if nothing were, there would be no present time.

Translator's Notes

  1. §11.14.17: Augustine’s resolution—that time exists as a “distension of the soul” (distentio animi), with past as memory, future as expectation, and present as attention—anticipates Husserl’s phenomenology of time-consciousness by fifteen centuries.
