Introduction

The Iliad opens with the wrath of Achilles, whose quarrel with Agamemnon over a captive woman sets in motion the catastrophe of the Trojan War. This invocation establishes the poem’s theme: the destructive consequences of unchecked rage.

§1:1–7 · Homer, Iliad 1.1–7
Manuscript
Greek

Μῆνιν ἄειδε θεὰ Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος οὐλομένην ἣ μυρί’ Ἀχαιοῖς ἄλγε’ ἔθηκε

English

Sing, O goddess, the wrath of Achilles, son of Peleus, the destructive wrath that brought countless woes upon the Achaeans and hurled many mighty souls of heroes to Hades, and made their bodies a feast for dogs and all the birds—and the will of Zeus was being accomplished.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:1: The first word of the Iliad is μῆνιν (wrath)—not Achilles, not war, but rage itself. This is divine wrath in a mortal vessel. The Greek tradition understood menis as a cosmic force, normally reserved for the gods.
