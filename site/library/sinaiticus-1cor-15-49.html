1 Corinthians 15:42-49: The Image of the Heavenly

Πρὸς Κορινθίους Αʹ
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

First Corinthians 15 is Paul's great treatise on resurrection, and verses 42–49 form its climax: the argument that the resurrection body is not a resuscitated corpse but a fundamentally transformed mode of existence. Paul draws a sharp distinction between the psychical body (soma psychikon, σῶμα ψυχικόν) and the spiritual body (soma pneumatikon, σῶμα πνευματικόν), between the image of Adam and the image of the heavenly Man.

The final verse—“just as we have borne the image of the man of dust, we shall also bear the image of the man of heaven”—became a proof text for the patristic doctrine of theosis. To bear the eikon (εἰκών) of the heavenly is not merely to imitate Christ but to be ontologically reconstituted in his likeness. The Sinaiticus text preserves this passage as it was read in the fourth century, when the theology of deification was taking its definitive shape.

§15:42–45 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

οὕτως καὶ ἡ ἀνάστασις τῶν νεκρῶν · σπείρεται ἐν φθορᾷ · ἐγείρεται ἐν ἀφθαρσίᾳ · σπείρεται ἐν ἀτιμίᾳ · ἐγείρεται ἐν δόξῃ · σπείρεται ἐν ἀσθενείᾳ · ἐγείρεται ἐν δυνάμει · σπείρεται σῶμα ψυχικόν · ἐγείρεται σῶμα πνευματικόν · εἰ ἔστιν σῶμα ψυχικόν · ἔστιν καὶ πνευματικόν · οὕτως καὶ γέγραπται · ἐγένετο ὁ πρῶτος ἄνθρωπος Ἀδὰμ εἰς ψυχὴν ζῶσαν · ὁ ἔσχατος Ἀδὰμ εἰς πνεῦμα ζωοποιοῦν ·

English

So also is the resurrection of the dead. It is sown in corruption; it is raised in incorruption. It is sown in dishonour; it is raised in glory. It is sown in weakness; it is raised in power. It is sown a natural body (soma psychikon, σῶμα ψυχικόν); it is raised a spiritual body (soma pneumatikon, σῶμα πνευματικόν). If there is a natural body, there is also a spiritual body. So also it is written: the first man Adam became a living soul; the last Adam became a life-giving spirit.

Manuscript
Greek

ἀλλ’ οὐ πρῶτον τὸ πνευματικόν · ἀλλὰ τὸ ψυχικόν · ἔπειτα τὸ πνευματικόν · ὁ πρῶτος ἄνθρωπος ἐκ γῆς χοϊκός · ὁ δεύτερος ἄνθρωπος ἐξ οὐρανοῦ · οἷος ὁ χοϊκός · τοιοῦτοι καὶ οἱ χοϊκοί · καὶ οἷος ὁ ἐπουράνιος · τοιοῦτοι καὶ οἱ ἐπουράνιοι · καὶ καθὼς ἐφορέσαμεν τὴν εἰκόνα τοῦ χοϊκοῦ · φορέσομεν καὶ τὴν εἰκόνα τοῦ ἐπουρανίου ·

English

But it is not the spiritual that is first, but the natural, and then the spiritual. The first man was from the earth, made of dust; the second man is from heaven. As was the man of dust, so also are those who are of the dust; and as is the man of heaven, so also are those who are of heaven. And just as we have borne the image (eikon, εἰκών) of the man of dust, we shall also bear the image of the man of heaven.

Translator's Notes

  1. §15:49: The phrase “we shall bear the image of the man of heaven” (φορέσομεν καὶ τὴν εἰκόνα τοῦ ἐπουρανίου) contains a significant textual variant. Codex Sinaiticus reads phoresomen (φορέσομεν), the future indicative: “we shall bear.” Other manuscripts read phoresomen (φορέσωμεν), the aorist subjunctive: “let us bear.” The difference is theologically consequential. The indicative makes the transformation a certainty, something God will accomplish; the subjunctive makes it an exhortation, something believers must strive toward. For the theosis tradition, the Sinaiticus reading is the stronger: bearing the image of the heavenly is not a moral aspiration but an eschatological promise.
  2. §15:44–45: The contrast between soma psychikon (σῶμα ψυχικόν) and soma pneumatikon (σῶμα πνευματικόν) does not map onto the modern distinction between “physical” and “spiritual.” The psychikon body is animated by the soul (psyche); the pneumatikon body is animated by the Spirit (pneuma). Paul is not saying the resurrection body is immaterial. He is saying it is animated by a different power—the Spirit of God rather than mere natural life. This is transformation of the body, not escape from it.
