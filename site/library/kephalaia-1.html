Kephalaia: Light Trapped in Matter

Mani (attributed), 216-274 AD

Introduction

The Kephalaia (“Chapters” or “Discourses”) is a Coptic Manichaean text discovered at Medinet Madi in Egypt in the 1930s. It records the teachings of Mani (216–277 CE), the Persian prophet who founded Manichaeism, one of the great world religions that at its height stretched from Roman North Africa to Tang Dynasty China. The Kephalaia presents Mani’s teachings in a dialogue format, with his disciples asking questions and Mani providing answers.

Chapter 1 lays out the foundational Manichaean cosmology: there are two eternal principles, Light and Darkness. Before the creation of the world, they existed separately. Then Darkness attacked Light, and the Father of Greatness (the supreme God of Light) sent forth emanations to defend the realm of Light. These emanations were partially defeated and their particles of light became trapped in matter. The entire visible world is a mechanism for separating and recovering these light-particles. Every human being contains trapped light. The goal of the religious life is to liberate this light and return it to its source. This is theosis as cosmic rescue operation.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 1, §1-3: Mani’s radical dualism differs from the Gnostic systems of Valentinus or the Sethians in one crucial respect: both principles are eternal and co-equal. There is no fall within the divine realm. Darkness did not arise from a mistake or a deficiency within Light. This makes Manichaeism a true metaphysical dualism, whereas most Gnostic systems are ultimately monist—they derive evil from a disruption within an originally unified divine realm.
  2. §Ch. 1, §7-9: The Manichaean “First Man” (Primus Homo) is not Adam but a divine being sent forth to do battle with Darkness. His defeat and the swallowing of his light-elements is the cosmic catastrophe that gives rise to the material world. This myth has been compared to the Norse myth of Ymir, the Zurvanite creation myth, and the Orphic dismemberment of Dionysus—all stories in which the cosmos is formed from the body of a defeated divine being.
  3. §Ch. 1, §16-18: The sun and moon as “ships of light” carrying liberated light-particles back to the realm of Light is one of the most distinctive Manichaean doctrines. It was widely mocked by Christian and Muslim polemicists, but it reflects a sophisticated cosmological theology in which every natural phenomenon has a soteriological function. Augustine, before his conversion to Christianity, was a Manichaean “Hearer” for nine years and knew this teaching intimately.
