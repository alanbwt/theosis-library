Introduction

The Instruction for Merikare (c. 2050 BCE) is advice attributed to an unnamed pharaoh of the Ninth or Tenth Dynasty to his son. It is the earliest Egyptian text to suggest that moral conduct, not just ritual, determines one’s fate in the afterlife.

§Lines 127–131 · Instruction for Merikare, P. Petersburg 1116A
Manuscript
Greek

stp(.w) qḏ n s āq-jb r jḥ n jwā.t. jr n nṬr, jr=f n=k mj.t.t, m ḫtp.w sāḫḥ(w) ḫāw.t

English

More acceptable is the character of a man who is upright of heart than the ox of one who does wrong. Act for God, that he may do likewise for you, with offerings that make the altar flourish. That is what bears witness to your name; God is aware of whoever acts for him.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 127–131: The statement that “the character of an upright man is more acceptable than the ox of a wrongdoer” anticipates the prophetic critique of sacrifice in the Hebrew Bible (cf. 1 Samuel 15:22, Hosea 6:6). Its appearance centuries before the Israelite prophets suggests the prioritization of ethics over ritual had deep Near Eastern roots.
