Introduction

Book 1 of Socrates Scholasticus’s Church History (c. 440 CE) continues Eusebius’s narrative, covering the Arian controversy, the Council of Nicaea, and Constantine’s reign. Socrates is more critical and evenhanded than Eusebius, often noting both sides of theological disputes.

§1.1–2 · Socrates Scholasticus, Church History
Manuscript
Greek

[Greek: Εὐσέβιος ὁ Παμφίλου ἐπίκλην]

English

Eusebius surnamed Pamphilus has written the history of the church in ten books, ending with the reign of Constantine. I have thought it right to begin my history where he left off. But I shall not attempt to use lofty language; for the greatness of the subject matter demands truth rather than rhetorical display.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1: Socrates’s commitment to truth over rhetoric marks an advance in historical method. Unlike Eusebius, he includes information unfavorable to the orthodox party and gives a relatively fair account of Arian and Novatianist positions. His work is an indispensable source for fourth-century church politics.
