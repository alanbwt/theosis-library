Introduction

Chapter 7, "Fit for Emperors and Kings," concludes the Inner Chapters with parables about ideal rulership and the dangers of imposing order on the natural world. Its closing parable about Hundun (Chaos) is one of the most haunting in the text.

§Chapter 7: The Death of Hundun · Zhuangzi, Inner Chapters, Chapter 7
Manuscript
Greek

南海之帝为儂，北海之帝为忽，中央之帝为浑沌

English

The ruler of the South Sea was called Shu (Brief). The ruler of the North Sea was called Hu (Sudden). The ruler of the Center was called Hundun (Chaos). Shu and Hu from time to time came together for a meeting in the territory of Hundun, and Hundun treated them very generously. Shu and Hu discussed how they could repay his kindness. "All men have seven openings so they can see, hear, eat, and breathe. But Hundun alone doesn't have any. Let us try boring him some!" Every day they bored another hole, and on the seventh day Hundun died.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 7: The death of Hundun is Zhuangzi's most compressed philosophical parable. The well-intentioned gift of sensory openings—the capacity for discrimination and distinction—kills the primal wholeness of undifferentiated reality. The message: every act of ordering, classifying, and distinguishing is simultaneously an act of violence against the original unity of things.
