Katha Upanishad 2.20: The Self in the Heart

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The Katha Upanishad tells the story of Nachiketas, a young boy who is sent to the realm of Death (Yama) by his father and, through his fearlessness and persistence, wins from Yama the secret teaching about the nature of the Self. Verse 2.20 is the jewel of this teaching: the Self is smaller than the smallest and greater than the greatest, hidden in the heart of every creature. It cannot be reached by effort or intellect—only by those whom it chooses.

This verse encapsulates the paradox at the centre of Hindu theosis. The divine is not far away, not in a distant heaven, not accessible only to the learned or the powerful. It is closer than close—hidden in the heart, the innermost cavity of every living being. Yet it cannot be grasped by the will. The Self reveals itself to those who surrender the very faculties that normally grasp at objects. The Katha Upanishad thus stands between two poles of the theosis tradition: the Upanishadic insistence that knowledge liberates, and the later bhakti tradition’s insistence that grace is required.

§2.20 · Katha Upanishad 2.20
Manuscript
Sanskrit

aṇor aṇīyān mahato mahīyān ātmāsya jantor nih ito guhāyām | tam akratuḥ paśyati vītasoko dhātuḥ prasādān mahimānam ātmanaḥ

English

The Self (atman), smaller than the small, greater than the great, is hidden in the heart of every creature. A person who is free from desire, with mind and senses composed, beholds the majesty of the Self and becomes free from sorrow.

Sanskrit

English

Sitting, he travels far. Lying down, he goes everywhere. Who other than me can know this god who rejoices and does not rejoice?

§2.22 · Katha Upanishad 2.22
Manuscript
Sanskrit

aśarīraṃ śarīreṣv anavaastheṣv avasthitam | mahantaṃ vibhum ātmānaṃ matvā dhīro na śocati

English

The wise one who knows the Self as bodiless among bodies, as permanent among the impermanent, as great and all-pervading—he does not grieve.

§2.23 · Katha Upanishad 2.23
Manuscript
Sanskrit

nāyam ātmā pravacanena labhyo na medhayā na bahunā śrutena | yam evaiṣa vṛṇute tena labhyas tasyaiṣa ātmā vivṛṇute tanuṃ svām

English

This Self cannot be attained by instruction, nor by intellect, nor by much learning. It is attained only by the one whom it chooses. To such a one, the Self reveals its own form.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2.20: The phrase aṇor aṇīyān mahato mahīyān (“smaller than the small, greater than the great”) became one of the most quoted descriptions of the Self in later Hindu literature. It captures the paradox that Brahman is simultaneously the subatomic essence of every particle and the totality that exceeds the universe. The word guhāyām (“in the cave” or “in the heart”) locates this infinite reality in the most intimate possible space: the interior of the individual being.
  2. §2.23: This verse introduces a tension that runs through all Hindu soteriology. If the Self cannot be attained by study, effort, or learning—only by its own choosing—then is liberation a matter of grace rather than knowledge? Shankara resolved this by arguing that “choosing” means the Self reveals itself when the seeker is truly prepared. Ramanuja took it more literally: divine grace is necessary. The same tension between human effort and divine gift appears in the Christian debate between Pelagianism and Augustinian grace.
