Quran 42:11: There Is Nothing Like Unto Him

Quran (divine revelation), c. 610-632 AD

Introduction

Surah ash-Shura (Consultation), verse 11, contains what is arguably the most concise theological statement in the Quran: laysa kamithlihi shay’—“There is nothing like unto Him.” In five Arabic words, the verse establishes God’s absolute transcendence and incomparability. No analogy holds. No comparison is valid. Nothing in creation—no image, no concept, no experience—can serve as a bridge to understanding what God is.

For the theosis question, this verse is a wall. If there is nothing like God, then the human being cannot become “like God.” The Orthodox Christian doctrine of theosis through participation in the divine energies, the Neoplatonic ascent to the One, the Hindu tat tvam asi (“thou art that”)—all of these presuppose some point of contact between the divine nature and the human. The Quranic position denies that any such contact exists at the level of essence. Islamic theology would develop this principle into a rigorous doctrine of tanzih (divine transcendence), which stands in permanent tension with the Sufi experience of fana (annihilation in God).

فاطر السماوات والأرض جعل لكم من أنفسكم أزواجا ومن الأنعام أزواجا يذرؤكم فيه ليس كمثله شيء وهو السميع البصير

English

The Originator of the heavens and the earth. He made for you from yourselves mates, and among the cattle, mates. He multiplies you thereby. There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing.

Translator's Notes

  1. §42:11: The Arabic laysa kamithlihi shay’ is grammatically unusual. Literally it reads “there is not like His likeness anything,” which contains a double negation of similarity—not merely “nothing is like Him” but “nothing is even like His likeness.” Some grammarians take ka- as a redundant particle of emphasis, but others read it as intensifying the incomparability: even the concept of similarity fails when applied to God.
  2. §42:11: The verse ends with two divine names—“the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing” (al-Sami’ al-Basir)—which create a theological tension. If nothing is like God, how can God hear and see, which are things creatures also do? The Ash’ari theologians resolved this by arguing that God hears and sees but not in any way analogous to creaturely hearing and seeing (bila kayf, “without asking how”). The Mu’tazilites argued these are metaphors. The tension between tanzih (transcendence) and tashbih (similarity) runs through all of Islamic theology.
