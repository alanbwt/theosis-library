Seneca On Anger

Unknown,

Introduction

Seneca’s De Ira is the most extensive ancient treatise on anger. He defines anger as a temporary madness arising from the judgment that one has been wronged, and argues it must be eliminated entirely rather than merely moderated.

§I.1–2 · Seneca, De Ira I.1.1–2.3 (Reynolds, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Exegisti a me, Novate, ut scriberem quemadmodum posset ira leniri. Omnium enim affectuum foedissimum ac rabidum est.

English

You have asked me, Novatus, to write on how anger may be calmed. I think you were right to fear this passion above all others, for it is the most hideous and frenzied of all. The other passions have in them some element of peace and calm; this one is all excitement and impulse of grief, raging with an inhuman desire for weapons, blood, and punishment.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1–2: Seneca’s absolutist position—that anger is never useful and must be entirely eliminated—contrasts with Aristotle’s view in the Nicomachean Ethics that appropriate anger at the right time and in the right measure is a virtue. The Stoic position demands complete apatheia.
