Meditations IV.3: The Source of Good Within You

Marcus Aurelius, 121-180 AD

Introduction

The Meditations of Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) were never intended for publication. Written in Greek during military campaigns on the Danube frontier, they constitute the private spiritual journal of a Roman emperor struggling to live according to Stoic principles while governing the largest empire on earth. Book IV, section 3 contains the Stoic doctrine of the inner citadel: within every person there is an inviolable source of good—the hegemonikon, the ruling faculty of reason—which participates in the universal logos that orders the cosmos.

For the theosis tradition, the Stoic contribution is distinctive. The Stoics did not believe in a transcendent God separate from the world; for them, God is the world, or more precisely, God is the rational principle (logos) that pervades and governs all matter. The divine is not above or beyond nature but within it, and most intimately within the human mind. Marcus Aurelius’s instruction to “dig within” is not an invitation to introspection in the modern sense but a call to discover the divine reason that constitutes one’s truest self.

§IV.3 (opening) · Meditations IV.3
Manuscript
Greek

ἀναχωρήσεις αὑτοῖς ζητοῦσιν ἄνθρωποι · ἀγροικίας · αἰγιαλούς · ὄρη · εἴωθας δὲ καὶ σὺ μάλιστα τοιαῦτα ποθεῖν · ὅλον δὲ τοῦτο ἰδιωτικώτατόν ἐστιν ·

English

People seek retreats for themselves—in the country, by the sea, in the mountains. You too are in the habit of longing for such things. But this is altogether the mark of a most common sort of person, when it is possible at any hour you please to retreat into yourself. For nowhere does a person retreat with more quiet or more freedom than into his own soul (psyche, ψυχή).

§IV.3 (the inner spring) · Meditations IV.3
Manuscript
Greek

μάλιστα ὅταν τοιαῦτα ἐν ἑαυτῷ ἔχῃ τις εἰς ἃ ἐγκύψας εὐθὺς ἐν πάσῃ εὐμαρείᾳ γίνεται · συνεχῶς οὖν δίδου τοῦτο σεαυτῷ τὸ ἀναχωρεῖν καὶ ἀνανέου σεαυτόν · ἔστω δὲ πηγὴ τοῦ ἀγαθοῦ ἐν σοὶ ἀεὶ ἀναβλύζουσα ἐὰν ἀεὶ σκάπτῃς ·

English

Most of all when he has within himself such things that, by gazing upon them, he is immediately in perfect ease. And by ease I mean nothing other than good order. Continually, then, grant yourself this retreat, and renew yourself. Within you there is a source of good (pege tou agathou, πηγὴ τοῦ ἀγαθοῦ) that will always spring up, if you dig.

§IV.3 (conclusion) · Meditations IV.3
Manuscript
Greek

μέμνησο δὲ τῶν δογμάτων τούτων · ὓν μὲν ὅτι τὰ πράγματα οὐχ ἅπτεται τῆς ψυχῆς · ὅ κόσμος ἀλλοίωσις · ὁ βίος ὑπόληψις ·

English

And remember these principles: first, that things outside the mind cannot touch the soul—they stand quiet outside, and disturbances come only from the opinion within. Second, that everything you see will change in a moment and will be no more; and constantly consider how many such changes you have already witnessed. The universe is change; life is opinion.

Translator's Notes

  1. §IV.3, the inner spring: The image of the “source of good” (pege tou agathou) that springs up when one digs is remarkably close to the language of John 4:14, where Jesus speaks of a “spring of water welling up to eternal life.” The parallel is almost certainly coincidental—Marcus Aurelius shows no awareness of Christianity beyond a brief, dismissive reference in XI.3—but it illustrates how the Stoic and Christian traditions converge on the intuition that the divine is to be found within.
  2. §IV.3, conclusion: The aphorism “the universe is change; life is opinion” (ho kosmos alloiosis, ho bios hypolepsis) compresses the entire Stoic worldview into eight Greek words. The external world is in constant flux and therefore cannot be the source of the good. The inner world of judgment and opinion is the only domain over which one has control. The Stoic sage is not the one who controls events but the one who controls his response to events—and that response is governed by the divine logos within.
