Christmas Sermon 21.3: Recognize Your Dignity

Pope Leo the Great, c. 400–461 AD

Introduction

Pope Leo I (c. 400–461), known as Leo the Great, is one of the most significant figures in the history of the Western church. His Tome to Flavian defined Chalcedonian Christology, and his Christmas sermons remain among the finest examples of Latin homiletic literature. Leo preached with a Roman directness that cuts through theological abstraction to make the Incarnation a matter of immediate personal consequence.

Sermon 21, delivered on the Nativity, contains Leo’s most famous exhortation to the Christian faithful. The claim that believers are “partakers of the divine nature” draws directly on 2 Peter 1:4, but Leo frames it not as speculative theology but as a moral imperative. If divinity has entered humanity, then Christians must live accordingly. This fusion of theosis language with ethical demand is characteristic of the Latin patristic tradition.

§Sermon 21.3 · Sermo XXI.3 (PL 54.192)
Manuscript
Latin

Agnosce, o Christiane, dignitatem tuam, et divinae consors factus naturae, noli in veterem vilitatem degeneri conversatione redire. Memento cuius capitis et cuius corporis sis membrum.

English

Recognize your dignity, O Christian, and having been made a partaker of the divine nature, do not return to your former baseness by degenerate conduct. Remember whose head and whose body you are a member of.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Sermon 21.3: Leo’s phrase divinae consors factus naturae (“having been made a sharer in the divine nature”) is a direct quotation of the Latin Vulgate rendering of 2 Peter 1:4 (divinae consortes naturae). The term consors implies genuine partnership or co-sharing, not merely external association. Leo’s ethical turn—“do not return to your former baseness”—is characteristic of how the Latin West integrated theosis language into moral exhortation rather than mystical theology. The passage was widely cited in medieval Western theology and appears in the Catechism of the Catholic Church.
