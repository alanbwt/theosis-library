Vimalakirti Sutra: The Thunderous Silence

Anonymous (Vimalakirti), c. 1st c. CE

Introduction

The Vimalakirti Nirdesa Sutra is the most iconoclastic text in the Mahayana canon. Its protagonist is not a monk or a Buddha but a wealthy householder, Vimalakirti, who feigns illness to create an occasion for teaching. The climax of the sutra comes when thirty-two bodhisattvas are each asked to explain the entrance into non-duality. Each gives a progressively subtler answer, dissolving one pair of opposites after another. Then Manjushri, the bodhisattva of wisdom, gives what seems to be the final word: non-duality is beyond all words and explanations. He turns to Vimalakirti and asks for his answer.

Vimalakirti responds with silence. Manjushri praises this as “the true entrance into non-duality.” The scene became one of the most celebrated moments in Buddhist literature—“the thunderous silence of Vimalakirti.” For the theosis tradition, this passage is a powerful statement of the apophatic principle: the ultimate reality cannot be spoken, and the attempt to speak it—even to say “it cannot be spoken”—is already a departure from truth.

§Chapter 9 (excerpt, opening) · Vimalakirti Nirdesa Sutra, Chapter 9
Manuscript
Sanskrit

अथ विमलकीर्तिर्बोधिसत्त्वानामेतदवोचत् । कथं बोधिसत्त्वः अद्वयधर्ममुखं प्रविशति ।

English

Then Vimalakirti said to the assembled bodhisattvas: “Sirs, how does a bodhisattva enter the dharma-gate of non-duality? Let each speak as he understands it.”

§Chapter 9 (selected responses) · Vimalakirti Nirdesa Sutra, Chapter 9
Manuscript
Sanskrit

धर्मविकतो बोधिसत्त्व आह । संसारनिर्वाणमिति द्वयम् ।

English

The bodhisattva Dharmavikata said: “Samsara and nirvana are two. But one who sees the nature of samsara sees neither arising nor ceasing, neither bondage nor liberation. Such a one enters the gate of non-duality.” The bodhisattva Shrigandha said: “‘I’ and ‘mine’ are two. Where there is no presumption of a self, there will be no possessiveness. One who is free from presumption enters the gate of non-duality.”

§Chapter 9 (Manjushri’s response) · Vimalakirti Nirdesa Sutra, Chapter 9
Manuscript
Sanskrit

मञ्जुश्रीराह । यथा मम बुद्ध्या सर्वधर्मेषु निरुक्तिरनक्षरमनाभिलापमनुद्ग्रहम् ।

English

Then Manjushri said: “As I understand it, when in regard to all dharmas there are no words, no speech, no indication, and no cognition, and when one has departed from all questions and answers—this is the entrance into the gate of non-duality.”

§Chapter 9 (Vimalakirti’s silence) · Vimalakirti Nirdesa Sutra, Chapter 9
Manuscript
Sanskrit

अथ मञ्जुश्रीर्विमलकीर्तिमेतदवोचत् । विमलकीर्तिस्तूष्णीमभवत् । साधु साधु नाक्षरं न वाग्वाक् । एषा सत्यमद्वयधर्ममुखप्रवेशः ।

English

Then Manjushri said to Vimalakirti: “We have each given our explanations. Now, sir, it is your turn. How does the bodhisattva enter the gate of non-duality?” At this, Vimalakirti remained silent, saying nothing. Manjushri praised him, saying: “Excellent! Excellent! To have neither syllables nor speech—this is truly the entrance into the gate of non-duality.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 9, Vimalakirti’s silence: The “thunderous silence” of Vimalakirti became a paradigmatic moment in Chan (Zen) Buddhism. It established the principle that the highest teaching transcends verbal expression. Manjushri’s own answer—that non-duality is beyond words—is already excellent, but it still uses words to say so. Vimalakirti’s silence goes one step further: it does not merely describe the transcendence of language but enacts it.
  2. §Chapter 9, context: The Vimalakirti Sutra was composed in Sanskrit, probably in the first or second century CE, but the Sanskrit original is largely lost. The text survives in Tibetan translation, in two Chinese translations (by Kumarajiva in 406 CE and Xuanzang in 650 CE), and in fragments from Central Asian manuscripts. The Sanskrit passages given here are reconstructions based on these sources and the surviving fragments. The Kumarajiva translation became the most influential version in East Asian Buddhism.
