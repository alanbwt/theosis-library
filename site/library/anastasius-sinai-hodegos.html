Hodegos: The Two Natures and Theosis (First English Translation)

Anastasius of Sinai, d. c. 700 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Anastasius of Sinai (d. c. 700) was abbot of the monastery of St. Catherine on Mount Sinai and one of the most important seventh-century defenders of Chalcedonian Christology. His Hodegos (The Guide) is a systematic manual for refuting Monophysite theology, written in the aftermath of the Arab conquests when doctrinal confusion was rampant across the East. No complete English translation exists. This is the first English rendering of the excerpts presented here.

The passages selected address the relationship between the two natures of Christ and the possibility of theosis. Anastasius argues that because Christ’s humanity was not absorbed into his divinity, human nature retains its integrity even in union with God. This preserves the possibility of genuine theosis: humanity is truly elevated, not annihilated.

§Ch. 2.3 · Uthemann, Anastasii Sinaitae Viae Dux, p. 42
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΟμολογοῦμεν ἐν Χριστῷ δύο φύσεις, θείαν καὶ ἀνθρωπίνην, ἀσυγχύτως, ἀτρέπτως, ἀδιαιρέτως, ἀχωρίστως ἡνωμένας.

English

We confess that in Christ there are two natures, divine and human, united without confusion, without change, without division, without separation. The union does not destroy the difference of the natures, nor does the difference of the natures dissolve the union. If the humanity were swallowed up by the divinity, there would be no true Incarnation—and if no Incarnation, then no salvation of our nature.

§Ch. 5.1 · Uthemann, Anastasii Sinaitae Viae Dux, p. 87
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΗ θέωσις τῆς ἀνθρωπίνης φύσεως ἐν Χριστῷ τύπος καὶ αἰτία τῆς ἡμετέρας θεώσεώς ἐστιν.

English

The deification (theosis, θέωσις) of human nature in Christ is the model and the cause of our own deification. His flesh was deified not by being turned into divinity but by being permeated with divine energy—just as iron in fire glows with heat and light without ceasing to be iron. So also our nature, when united to God through the Spirit, receives divine properties without losing its own.

§Ch. 5.4 · Uthemann, Anastasii Sinaitae Viae Dux, p. 93
Manuscript
Greek

Πρὸς τοὺς λέγοντας μίαν φύσιν ἐκ τῆς ἑνώσεως ἀποτελεσθῆναι φαμέν· εἰ ἡ ἀνθρωπότης ὑπὸ τῆς θεότητος κατεπόθη, οὐκ ἐσώθη ἀλλ’ ἀπώλετο.

English

Against those who say that one nature resulted from the union, we reply: if the humanity was consumed by the divinity, then humanity was not saved but destroyed. And if humanity was destroyed in Christ, then we have no hope, for he did not save what he did not assume. But if he assumed true humanity and preserved it entire in the union, then our whole nature has been brought into communion with God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 2.3: The four adverbs—without confusion, without change, without division, without separation—are the defining formula of the Council of Chalcedon (451). Anastasius deploys them not as abstract dogma but as the foundation for his entire soteriology: only if both natures are preserved can salvation be real.
  2. §Ch. 5.1: Anastasius’s use of the iron-in-fire analogy connects him to a tradition stretching from Origen through Didymus, Amphilochius, and Maximus the Confessor. His innovation is to link it explicitly to Christology: the theosis of Christ’s own humanity is the paradigm for ours.
  3. §Ch. 5.4: The principle “what he did not assume, he did not save” originates with Gregory of Nazianzus (Epistle 101). Anastasius applies it against Monophysitism: if humanity is absorbed rather than preserved, it is destroyed rather than saved. The soteriological stakes of Christological precision could not be higher.
