Introduction

Suetonius’s Life of Augustus records the signs and portents surrounding Rome’s first emperor, including divine omens at his birth, his adoption of the title Augustus, and the establishment of the imperial cult that would define Roman religion for centuries.

§Chapters 94, 97, 100 · Suetonius, Divus Augustus 94, 97, 100 (Ihm, Teubner)
Manuscript
Greek

Auctor est Iulius Marathus, ante paucos quam nasceretur menses prodigium Romae factum publice, quo denuntiabatur regem populo Romano naturam parturire.

English

Before his birth, a portent was observed which foretold that nature was pregnant with a king for the Roman people. When Augustus lay dying at Nola, he asked his friends whether he had played the comedy of life well, and added the tag: Since I have played my part well, give me your applause. He was enrolled among the gods by formal decree, and his worship was established by the Senate.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapters 94–100: Augustus’s deathbed question—whether he had played the “comedy of life” well—uses the same theatrical metaphor found in Stoic philosophy. The formal deification (consecratio) of Augustus established the model for imperial apotheosis that Christians would later challenge as idolatrous.
