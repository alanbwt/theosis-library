Daniel 7:13-14: One Like a Son of Man

Δανιήλ
Daniel (attributed), c. 6th-2nd c. BCE
Septuagint (LXX)

Introduction

Daniel 7:13–14 introduces a figure who will dominate the theological imagination for centuries: “one like a son of man” (hos huios anthropou, ὡς υἱὸς ἀνθρώπου) coming with the clouds of heaven to the Ancient of Days. This figure receives dominion, glory, and a kingdom that shall not be destroyed. In the original context of Daniel, the “one like a son of man” probably represents the faithful people of Israel, or perhaps an angelic figure. But the passage took on an entirely new dimension when Jesus adopted “Son of Man” as his preferred self-designation in the Gospels.

The scene is a heavenly investiture: power is transferred from the Ancient of Days to the one who approaches on the clouds. The Septuagint’s rendering preserves the ambiguity of the figure’s identity—he is “like” a son of man, not necessarily a mere mortal. When Jesus stands before the Sanhedrin and declares that they will see “the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven” (Mark 14:62), he is fusing Daniel 7 with Psalm 110, claiming for himself both the cosmic authority of Daniel’s figure and the priestly enthronement of the psalm. The high priest tears his robes. The claim is understood.

Greek

ἐθεώρουν ἐν ὁράματι τῆς νυκτὸς καὶ ἰδοὺ ἐπὶ τῶν νεφελῶν τοῦ οὐρανοῦ ὡς υἱὸς ἀνθρώπου ἐρχόμενος ἦν καὶ ἕως τοῦ παλαιοῦ τῶν ἡμερῶν ἔφθασεν καὶ προσηνέχθη αὐτῷ · καὶ αὐτῷ ἐδόθη ἡ ἀρχὴ καὶ ἡ τιμὴ καὶ ἡ βασιλεία καὶ πάντες οἱ λαοί φυλαί καὶ γλῶσσαι αὐτῷ λατρεύσουσιν · ἡ ἐξουσία αὐτοῦ ἐξουσία αἰώνιος ἥτις οὐ παρελεύσεται καὶ ἡ βασιλεία αὐτοῦ οὐ διαφθαρήσεται ·

English

I was watching in the night visions, and behold, one like a son of man (hos huios anthropou, ὡς υἱὸς ἀνθρώπου) was coming with the clouds of heaven. And he came as far as the Ancient of Days, and he was brought before him. And to him was given the dominion and the honour and the kingdom, and all peoples, tribes, and tongues shall serve him. His dominion is an everlasting dominion that shall not pass away, and his kingdom shall not be destroyed.

Translator's Notes

  1. §7:13: The phrase “one like a son of man” (ὡς υἱὸς ἀνθρώπου) is a Semitism meaning simply “one who looked human.” In the context of Daniel 7, the figure is contrasted with the four beasts that emerge from the sea—the empires of the earth are monstrous, but the one who receives God’s kingdom appears in human form. The Septuagint’s hos (“like, as”) preserves the simile: this is not necessarily a human being, but one who resembles a human. When Jesus uses the title in the Gospels (over eighty times), the simile drops away. He does not say he is “like” a son of man; he calls himself the Son of Man, transforming a descriptive phrase into a messianic title.
  2. §7:13–14: The verb latreusosin (λατρεύσουσιν, “they shall serve”) is significant because the Septuagint elsewhere reserves latreuo almost exclusively for the worship or service of God. If all peoples shall latreuo the Son of Man, then the Son of Man receives what belongs to God alone. This is precisely the logic that the early Church applied to Christ. The passage also provides the foundation for the “coming on the clouds” imagery that dominates early Christian eschatology (Mark 13:26, 14:62; Revelation 1:7; 1 Thessalonians 4:17).
