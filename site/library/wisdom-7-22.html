Wisdom 7:22-8:1: Wisdom as Divine Emanation

Solomon (attributed), traditional

Introduction

The Wisdom of Solomon is a Jewish text composed in Greek, almost certainly in Alexandria, probably in the first century BCE. It is attributed to King Solomon but is actually a product of the Hellenistic Jewish diaspora, written by an author deeply immersed in both Jewish Scripture and Greek philosophy. The book is canonical in Catholic and Orthodox traditions and appears in the Septuagint, though it was excluded from the later rabbinic canon.

Chapter 7, verse 22 through chapter 8, verse 1 contains the most sustained description of divine Wisdom (Sophia) in Jewish literature. Wisdom is described as a “breath of the power of God, a pure emanation of the glory of the Almighty,” a “reflection of eternal light” and an “image of his goodness.” This language of emanation—Wisdom flowing from God as light flows from the sun, without diminishing the source—became foundational for both Christian theology (where it was applied to the Son and the Holy Spirit) and Gnostic cosmology (where it described the procession of aeons from the divine Pleroma). The passage stands at the headwaters of the entire theosis tradition.

Translator's Notes

  1. §7:22-23: The twenty-one attributes of Wisdom’s spirit (seven times three, both sacred numbers) echo Stoic descriptions of the pneuma that pervades all things. The author of Wisdom is deliberately translating Jewish Sophia theology into the language of Greek philosophy. The term monogenes (“unique”) here applied to Wisdom’s spirit is the same word later applied to Christ in John 1:14.
  2. §7:25-26: The Greek aporroia (“emanation”) is the key term. It implies that Wisdom flows from God as fragrance flows from a substance—distinct from the source, yet sharing its essence. This concept of divine emanation became the foundation of Neoplatonic theology, Gnostic cosmology, and the Christian doctrine of the procession of the Son from the Father. Hebrews 1:3 (“the radiance of God’s glory”) directly echoes this passage.
  3. §7:27: The statement that Wisdom “passes into holy souls and makes them friends of God, and prophets” is a direct theosis claim within the Jewish wisdom tradition. It asserts that the divine Wisdom enters individual human beings, transforming them into something more than they were. The phrase “friends of God” (philous theou) echoes the designation of Abraham in Isaiah 41:8 and anticipates the language of John 15:15.
