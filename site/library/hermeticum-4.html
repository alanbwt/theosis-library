Corpus Hermeticum IV: The Crater of Nous

Hermes Trismegistus (attributed), c. 1st-3rd c. AD

Introduction

The fourth treatise of the Corpus Hermeticum, sometimes titled The Crater or The Mixing Bowl, presents one of the most vivid images in Hermetic literature: God fills a great bowl (krater, κρατήρ) with Nous—divine Mind—and sends it down to earth, proclaiming that those who wish to may baptize themselves in it. Those who do are “perfected” and receive gnosis. Those who do not remain trapped in ignorance, possessing only logos (reason) without nous (intellect), not knowing why they were made or by whom.

The baptismal imagery is unmistakable and has fueled centuries of debate about the relationship between Hermetic initiation and Christian sacrament. The krater is not water but Mind itself—the medium of transformation is knowledge, not ritual. Yet the language of immersion, of being “filled,” of ascending to the one who sent the bowl, maps closely onto early Christian baptismal theology. The text insists on a sharp division within humanity: those who have received Nous and those who have not. This is not predestination but choice—the bowl is available to all, but only some choose to drink.

§1–2 · Corpus Hermeticum IV.1–2
Manuscript
Greek

ἐπειδὴ τὸν κόσμον ὅλον ἐποίησεν ὁ δημιουργὸς οὐ χερσίν, ἀλλὰ λόγῳ

English

Since the Maker made the whole cosmos not with hands but by the Word, you should conceive of him as present and as always existing, as having made all things, as one alone, and as having fashioned by his own will the things that are. For that is his body: neither tangible, nor visible, nor measurable, nor dimensional, nor like any other body. It is not fire, nor water, nor air, nor breath, but all things were made from it.

§3–4 · Corpus Hermeticum IV.3–4
Manuscript
Greek

πληρώσας κρατῆρα μέγαν τούτου καταπέμψας, δοὺς κήρυκα καὶ ἐκέλευσε κηρύξαι ταῖς τῶν ἀνθρώπων καρδίαις τάδε· βάπτισον σεαυτὴν τῷ κρατῆρι τούτῳ

English

Having willed to constitute the human race, God filled a great mixing bowl (krater, κρατήρ) with Mind (Nous) and sent it down, appointing a herald and commanding him to proclaim to the hearts of men: “Baptize yourself in this bowl, you who are able, you who believe that you shall ascend to him who sent the bowl down, you who know for what purpose you were made.”

§5 · Corpus Hermeticum IV.5
Manuscript
Greek

οἱ μὲν οὖν συνέντες τοῦ κηρύγματος καὶ βαπτισάμενοι τοῦ νοός, οὗτοι μετέσχον τῆς γνώσεως

English

Those who heeded the proclamation and were baptized in Mind, these partook of gnosis and became perfect men, having received Mind. But those who missed the proclamation, these have reason (logos) but not Mind (nous), not knowing why they were made or by whom.

§7 · Corpus Hermeticum IV.7
Manuscript
Greek

διὰ τί, ὦ πάτερ, οὐκ ἐμέρισεν πᾶσι τὸν νοῦν; ἐβουλήθη, ὦ τέκνον, τοῦτον ἐν μέσῳ ταῖς ψυχαῖς ἆθλον ἱδρῦσθαι.

English

“But why, Father, did God not distribute Mind to all?” “Because he wished, my child, that Mind should be set in the midst as a prize for souls to win.”

§9–11 · Corpus Hermeticum IV.9–11
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ γὰρ μετασχὼν τοῦ νοὸς... ἤδη θεῖός ἐστι.

English

For the one who has received Mind—whither does he hasten? To the light. Where does he wish to go? To the Father. What does he desire? To be freed from evil. And this cannot happen to one who has not received Mind. Therefore the one who has knowledge is good and pious and already divine.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3–4: The krater (κρατήρ) is a large mixing bowl used in Greek symposia to blend wine with water. The image is charged: Plato’s Timaeus (41d) describes the Demiurge mixing the World Soul in a “mixing bowl.” The Hermetic text repurposes this cosmogonic image for soteriological purposes—the bowl now contains not the substance of the World Soul but Nous itself, and the mixing is not cosmological but individual. The verb baptison (βάπτισον) literally means “immerse yourself,” the same root used for Christian baptism.
  2. §5: The distinction between logos (reason, speech) and nous (direct intellectual apprehension of the divine) is fundamental to Hermetic anthropology. All humans have logos; only those who have baptized themselves in the krater possess nous. This maps onto the later Neoplatonic distinction between discursive reasoning (dianoia) and intellectual intuition (noesis), and onto the Gnostic division between psychics and pneumatics.
  3. §9–11: The phrase “already divine” (ἤδη θεῖος) is remarkable for its immediacy. There is no process of gradual sanctification, no purgatorial ascent. The reception of Nous is itself divinization. Compare this with the Eastern Orthodox concept of theosis, which in most formulations is a gradual process of synergy between divine grace and human effort.
