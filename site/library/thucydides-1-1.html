Introduction

Book 1 of Thucydides’s History of the Peloponnesian War (c. 400 BCE) opens with the most methodologically rigorous statement of historical inquiry in antiquity. Thucydides rejects myth and hearsay in favor of eyewitness evidence and critical analysis.

§1.1 · Thucydides, History
Manuscript
Greek

Θουκυδίδης Ἀθηναῖος ξυνέγραψε τὸν πόλεμον τῶν Πελοποννησίων καὶ Ἀθηναίων

English

Thucydides, an Athenian, wrote the history of the war between the Peloponnesians and the Athenians, beginning at the moment that it broke out, and believing that it would be a great war and more worthy of relation than any that had preceded it.

§1.22 · Thucydides, History
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ὅσα μὲν λόγῳ εἶπον ἕκαστοι

English

And with regard to the speeches, it was difficult to recall the exact words that were spoken, both for me concerning those I heard myself and for my informants about speeches elsewhere. The speeches are therefore given in the language in which it seemed to me each speaker would most likely have expressed the sentiments suited to the occasion, while adhering as closely as possible to the general sense of what was actually said.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.22: Thucydides’s method statement—that speeches represent what he thought speakers would likely have said—is the most honest admission of historical reconstruction in ancient literature. He aspires to create a “possession for all time” (κτῆμα ἐς αἰεί) rather than a crowd-pleasing performance piece.
