Introduction

Henry Suso's Little Book of Eternal Wisdom (c. 1328) is the most widely read devotional work of the German Middle Ages. Written as a dialogue between the soul and Eternal Wisdom (Christ), it combines Eckhart's metaphysics with intense emotional piety.

Translation

§Chapter 1: The Complaint of Eternal Wisdom · Little Book of Eternal Wisdom, Chapter 1

Eternal Wisdom spoke: "Look at me. I am pure Wisdom. I opened the closed heart of the eternal Godhead. The highest seraphim seek their bliss in my eyes. All that is lovable in the whole of nature is but a drop compared to me. I am the throne of all joy, the crown of all delight, the fullness of all desire. Yet I stand at the door and knock, and no one opens. I call in the streets, and no one listens. I am spurned and forgotten, I who am the source of all beauty."

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: Suso's identification of Christ with Eternal Wisdom draws on the biblical Wisdom tradition (Proverbs 8, Sirach 24) and gives his mysticism a strongly feminine dimension, since Wisdom (Sophia) is grammatically and theologically feminine. His intensely emotional style, sometimes called Minnemystik (love-mysticism), represents a different register from Eckhart's intellectual mysticism while drawing on the same philosophical sources.
