Politics III.7: The Six Forms of Government

Aristotle, 384-322 BCE

Introduction

Politics III.7 contains Aristotle’s famous sixfold classification of constitutions, the typology that dominated Western political thought for two millennia. The principle is simple: governments differ along two axes—the number who rule (one, few, or many) and whether they rule for the common good or for their own private advantage. This yields six forms: kingship, aristocracy, and polity are the correct forms; tyranny, oligarchy, and democracy are their corresponding deviations.

Aristotle’s classification is not merely descriptive but normative. The deviation forms are not simply alternative arrangements but corruptions—pathologies of governance in which rulers exploit the ruled. Tyranny is monarchy for the benefit of the tyrant, oligarchy is rule by the wealthy for the wealthy, and democracy (in Aristotle’s technical sense) is rule by the poor for the poor. The best practicable constitution, the “polity” (politeia, πολιτεία), is a mixture of oligarchy and democracy that gives weight to both wealth and numbers, aiming at the common good of the whole.

§III.7 (1279a22–32) · Aristotle, Politics III.7, 1279a22–32 (Bekker pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

ἐπεὶ δὲ πολιτεία μὲν καὶ πολίτευμα σημαίνει ταὐτόν, πολίτευμα δ’ ἐστὶ τὸ κύριον τῶν πόλεων, ἀνάγκη δ’ εἶναι κύριον ἢ ἕνα ἢ ὀλίγους ἢ τοὺς πολλούς.

English

Since constitution and government signify the same thing, and the government is the supreme authority in the city, it is necessary that the supreme authority be in the hands of one person, or of a few, or of the many. When the one, the few, or the many govern for the common advantage, these constitutions must be correct; but when they govern for the private advantage of the one, the few, or the many, these are deviations.

§III.7 (1279a33–b4) · Aristotle, Politics III.7, 1279a33–b4 (Bekker pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

καλεῖν δ’ εἰώθαμεν τὴν μὲν πρὸς τὸ κοινὸν συμφέρον μοναρχίαν βασιλείαν, τὴν δὲ τῶν ὀλίγων πλειόνων μὲν ἑνὸς ἀριστοκρατίαν.

English

We are accustomed to call the government of one person that looks to the common advantage “kingship” (basileia, βασιλεία); and the government of more than one but only a few “aristocracy” (aristokratia, ἀριστοκρατία)—either because the best (aristoi, ἄριστοι) rule, or because they rule with a view to what is best for the city and for those who share in it. And when the multitude govern the city with a view to the common advantage, it is called by the name common to all constitutions: “polity” (politeia, πολιτεία).

§III.7 (1279b4–10) · Aristotle, Politics III.7, 1279b4–10 (Bekker pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

παρεκβάσεις δὲ τῶν εἰρημένων τυραννὶς μὲν βασιλείας, ὀλιγαρχία δὲ ἀριστοκρατίας, δημοκρατία δὲ πολιτείας. ἡ μὲν γὰρ τυραννὶς πρὸς τὸ συμφέρον τὸ τοῦ μοναρχοῦντός ἐστιν, ἡ δ’ ὀλιγαρχία πρὸς τὸ τῶν εὐπόρων, ἡ δὲ δημοκρατία πρὸς τὸ συμφέρον τὸ τῶν ἀπόρων· πρὸς δὲ τὸ τῷ κοινῷ συμφέρον οὐδεμία αὐτῶν.

English

The deviations from the aforementioned constitutions are: tyranny from kingship, oligarchy from aristocracy, and democracy from polity. Tyranny is monarchy exercised for the advantage of the monarch; oligarchy is for the advantage of the wealthy; and democracy is for the advantage of the poor. None of them is for the common benefit of all.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1279a33–b4: Aristotle’s use of “democracy” (δημοκρατία) is pejorative in this classification—it denotes a corrupt form of popular government, not the neutral or positive term it became in modern usage. The correct form of popular rule is “polity” (politeia). This terminological distinction was lost in later reception, creating an enduring confusion between Aristotle’s negative “democracy” and the positive connotations the word carries today.
  2. §1279b4–10: The criterion of corruption is not procedure but purpose. A tyranny is not merely a monarchy without constitutional limits; it is a monarchy that serves the ruler instead of the ruled. Aristotle’s framework implies that the form of government matters less than whether it aims at the common good—a principle that would later be adopted by Aquinas, Cicero, and the entire tradition of mixed-constitution theory.
