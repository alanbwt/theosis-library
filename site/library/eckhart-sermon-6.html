Sermon 6: God Must Become Me

Meister Eckhart, c. 1260-1328 AD

Introduction

Sermon 6 (Iusti vivent in aeternum, “The just shall live forever”) is one of Eckhart’s most concentrated statements on the unity of the soul with God. Where Sermon 52 strips away everything—will, knowledge, selfhood—until nothing remains, Sermon 6 builds upward toward a positive claim: in the just person, God and the soul become one “is.” The distinction between Creator and creature does not vanish in metaphysical abstraction but in the concrete act of justice. The just person does not merely obey God; the just person is justice, because God is justice, and in that identity the two become indistinguishable.

The language of this sermon is among the most extreme in the Christian mystical tradition. “God must become me and I must become God, so entirely one that this ‘he’ and this ‘I’ become one ‘is.’” Eckhart is not speaking metaphorically. He means that in the ground of the soul, the distinction between divine and human is overcome—not destroyed, but transcended in a unity that is prior to all distinction.

§Opening · Sermon 6 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Iusti vivent in aeternum et apud Dominum est merces eorum.

English

“The just shall live forever, and their reward is with the Lord.” Mark well the word: “the just.” The just person is one who is so formed and poured into the form of justice that he has justice as his very being. Only the person who has abandoned all creaturely things can be truly just.

§On Justice · Sermon 6 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Vrâget mich ieman, waz ein gerehter mensche sî:

English

If anyone were to ask me what a just person is, I would answer: a just person is one who is so conformed to justice that whatever happens—be it joy or sorrow, bitterness or sweetness—none of that matters to him, so long as justice is present in him. Nothing could be more joyful to the just person than justice itself.

§God and the Soul as One · Sermon 6 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Got und ich wir sint ein. Durch bekantnisse nîme ich got in mich; durch minne gân ich in got.

English

God and I, we are one. By knowledge I take God into myself; by love I enter into God. Some say that blessedness consists in knowledge, some say it consists in love. But I say it consists in neither, but rather in a something in the soul from which knowledge and love flow forth. That something does not know or love. Whoever knows this, knows what blessedness consists in.

§The One 'Is' · Sermon 6 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Got muoz werden ich und ich muoz werden got, sô gar ein, daz diz “er” und diz “ich” ein “ist” werde.

English

God must become me and I must become God—so entirely one that this “he” and this “I” become one “is” and, in this “is-ness,” eternally perform one work. For this “he” and this “I,” that is, God and the soul, are very fruitful. Not two; one.

§The Son in the Soul · Sermon 6 (Pfeiffer edition)
Manuscript
Latin/German

Der vater gebirt sînen sun in der sêle in der selben wîse als er in gebirt in der êwicheit und niht anders.

English

The Father gives birth to the Son in the soul in the very same way as he gives birth to him in eternity, and no differently. He must do it whether he wills it or not. The Father begets the Son without ceasing, and I say more: he begets me as his Son and the same Son. I say still more: he begets me not merely as his Son; he begets me as himself, and himself as me, and me as his being and nature.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The One 'Is': The German ist (is) is used here as a noun—“one is”—collapsing being and identity into a single term. Eckhart’s claim is not that the human person ceases to exist but that in the ground of the soul, the act of being (esse) is shared between God and the soul. This parallels the Thomistic doctrine that God is ipsum esse subsistens (subsistent being itself), but Eckhart draws a far more radical conclusion: if God is being, and the soul participates in being, then at the level of being itself there is no distinction.
  2. §The Son in the Soul: The claim that the Father “begets me as himself” was among the propositions investigated during Eckhart’s heresy trial. Eckhart defended himself by arguing that he spoke of the soul in a state of grace, not of the soul by nature. The distinction matters: Eckhart is not claiming that every human being is God by nature, but that in the state of perfect detachment, the soul participates in the same act of generation by which the Father eternally begets the Son. This is theosis expressed in Trinitarian terms.
