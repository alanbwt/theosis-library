Introduction

Timon of Phlius (c. 320–230 BCE), student of Pyrrho, composed the Silloi (Lampoons)—a satirical poem mocking dogmatic philosophers. The surviving fragments ridicule thinkers from Thales to the Stoics for their pretensions to certain knowledge.

§Fragments 1–9 · Timon, Silloi (Diels, PPF; Sextus, Adv. Math. I.53)
Manuscript
Greek

εἴπατέ νυν μοι, ὅσοι πολυπράγμονες ἐστὲ βροτοί

English

Tell me now, you wretched mortals who busy yourselves with empty conjectures, you swollen windbags—how did the nature of the divine and of all things come to be known to you? Come, tell me, garrulous tribe of squabblers, how did you learn this? For when you look at each thing, the impressions do not agree.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragments 1–9: The Silloi survives only in fragments quoted by later authors. Timon’s attack on dogmatic philosophy served a serious purpose: by exposing the mutual contradictions among philosophical schools, he reinforced Pyrrho’s case for the suspension of judgment.
