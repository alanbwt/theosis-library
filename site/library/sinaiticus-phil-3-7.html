Introduction

Philippians 3:7–14 contains Paul’s most intense autobiographical passage, renouncing all prior advantages to “know Christ and the power of his resurrection.” The language of participation and transformation is central to the theosis tradition. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§3:7–11 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

αλλα ατινα ην μοι κερδη ταυτα ηγημαι δια τον χριστον ζημιαν

English

But whatever gain I had, I counted as loss for the sake of Christ. Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord. For his sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ and be found in him, not having a righteousness of my own, but that which comes through faith in Christ. That I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death.

§3:12–14 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ουχ οτι ηδη ελαβον η ηδη τετελειωμαι

English

Not that I have already obtained this or am already perfect, but I press on to make it my own, because Christ Jesus has made me his own. Brothers, I do not consider that I have made it my own. But one thing I do: forgetting what lies behind and straining forward to what lies ahead, I press on toward the goal for the prize of the upward call of God in Christ Jesus.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:10: The phrase “becoming like him in his death” (συμμορφιζόμενος τω θανατω αυτου) uses the verb symmorphizo, to be conformed to the same form. Participation in Christ’s death is not imitation but ontological transformation—a reshaping of one’s very being.
