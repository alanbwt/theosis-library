John 6:53-58: Eat My Flesh, Drink My Blood

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

John 6:53–58 is the most physically shocking passage in the New Testament. Jesus tells his audience that unless they eat his flesh and drink his blood, they have no life in them. The Greek is unsparing: the verb trogon (τρώγων) means not merely “to eat” but “to gnaw, to munch”—the word used for animals feeding. Many of his disciples left him over these words. The passage is deliberately offensive because the claim it makes is deliberately extreme: the divine enters the human not through the mind but through the mouth, not through contemplation but through consumption.

The Church Fathers read this passage as the foundation of Eucharistic theology—the bread and wine of the Lord’s Supper are the flesh and blood of Christ. But the theosis tradition heard something more: that the one who consumes Christ’s body is transformed by what is consumed. As Irenaeus put it, the Eucharist is the medicine of immortality. The text is from Codex Sinaiticus.

English

So Jesus said to them, “Truly, truly, I say to you: unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you. Whoever feeds on (trogon, τρώγων) my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise him up on the last day.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §6:53–54: The shift from phagein (φαγεῖν, “to eat”) in verse 53 to trogon (τρώγων, “to gnaw, to chew”) in verse 54 is deliberate and shocking. The first verb is neutral; the second is visceral. Jesus is not softening his language as the crowd grows uncomfortable—he is intensifying it. The insistence on physical consumption was the primary text the Church used against any purely symbolic reading of the Eucharist.
  2. §6:55–56: The mutual indwelling formula—“abides in me, and I in him”—is the Johannine expression of what later theology would call perichoresis (mutual interpenetration). It is the same logic as Gospel of Thomas Saying 108: the consumer and the consumed become one. Cyril of Alexandria used this passage to argue that the Eucharist effects a physical union between Christ and the believer, making the human body a vessel of divine life. This is theosis through the stomach.
