Introduction

1 Corinthians 1:18–31 presents Paul’s theology of the cross as divine foolishness that overturns human wisdom. It is the foundation of his critique of Greek philosophical culture and Corinthian factionalism.

§1:18–25 · Codex Sinaiticus, 1 Corinthians 1:18–25
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ λόγος γὰρ ὁ τοῦ σταυροῦ τοῖς μὲν ἀπολλυμένοις μωρία ἐστίν

English

For the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God. For Jews demand signs and Greeks seek wisdom, but we preach Christ crucified, a stumbling block to Jews and folly to Gentiles, but to those who are called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:23: The term “stumbling block” (σκάνδαλον) to Jews reflects the Deuteronomic curse on anyone hanged on a tree (Deut. 21:23). A crucified messiah was not merely unexpected but theologically scandalous within Jewish categories.
