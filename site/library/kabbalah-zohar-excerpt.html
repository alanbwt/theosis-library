Zohar I.15a: God, Torah, and Israel Are One

Moses de León (attributed), c. 1240-1305 AD

Introduction

The Zohar (“Radiance”) is the foundational text of Kabbalah, the Jewish mystical tradition. Attributed to the second-century Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai but almost certainly composed in thirteenth-century Spain by Moses de León, it is a vast, multi-layered commentary on the Torah written in an artificial literary Aramaic. The passage at Zohar I.15a contains one of the most daring theological claims in Jewish literature: “God and Torah and Israel are all one.” This statement collapses the distinction between the divine, the revealed word, and the community that receives it.

For the theosis tradition, the Zohar’s significance lies in its reimagining of the relationship between God and creation. Through the doctrine of the sefirot—the ten emanations or attributes through which the infinite God (Ein Sof) manifests in the world—the Zohar provides a framework in which human beings can participate in the divine life without violating the Shema’s insistence on God’s unity. The human soul originates in the divine and returns to it; study of Torah is not merely learning about God but entering into God’s own self-expression.

English

Rabbi Shimon said: Woe to the person who says that the Torah merely tells stories and ordinary words. If this were so, we could compose a Torah from ordinary words, and even more excellent ones. Rather, all the words of the Torah are supernal words and supernal mysteries.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Zohar I.15a, excerpt 2: The formula “God, Torah, and Israel are all one” became one of the most cited and debated statements in Jewish mysticism. It should not be read as a simple identity claim (God is Torah, Torah is Israel). The Zohar immediately qualifies it: “these are levels upon levels, secret within secret.” The unity is real but complex, mediated through the sefirot and accessible only through the deepest layers of interpretation.
  2. §General: The Zohar’s Aramaic is a literary construction, not the spoken Aramaic of any historical period. Moses de León (c. 1250–1305) appears to have composed it in imitation of the Talmudic-era Aramaic of the Babylonian schools, lending it the authority of antiquity. The text mixes genuine Aramaic forms with Hebraisms and neologisms, creating a sacred language uniquely suited to its mystical content. Gershom Scholem’s philological analysis in the 1930s definitively established the medieval origin of the text, though many Kabbalists continue to attribute it to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai.
