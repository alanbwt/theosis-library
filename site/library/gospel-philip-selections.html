Gospel of Philip: You Saw Christ, You Became Christ

Εὐαγγέλιον Φιλίππου
Valentinus, c. 100–160 AD

Introduction

The Gospel of Philip is a Valentinian anthology preserved in Nag Hammadi Codex II, immediately following the Gospel of Thomas. It is not a gospel in any conventional sense—there is no continuous narrative, no birth story, no passion account. It is a collection of theological reflections, parables, and ritual instructions organized by association rather than chronology. Its central concerns are the sacraments, especially chrism (anointing) and the bridal chamber, and their role in restoring the soul to its original unity with the divine.

The two passages selected here contain Philip’s most direct statements on theosis. The mirror passage describes a transformation through vision: to see the Spirit is to become spirit, to see Christ is to become Christ. The chrism passage elevates anointing above baptism and declares that those who receive it become “Christs”—anointed ones—not merely followers of Christ but participants in his nature.

§The Mirror Passage (61.20–35) · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 3, plate 69
Manuscript
Coptic

οὐ δύνατόν ἐστιν οὐδένα ιἰδεῖν τι τῶν πραγμάτων τῶν ὑπαρχόντων · ἐὰν μὴ γένηται ὅμοιος αὐτοῖς · τὸ πνεῦμα εἶδες · πνεῦμα ἐγένου · τὸν Χριστὸν εἶδες · Χριστὸς ἐγένου

English

It is not possible for anyone to see anything of the things that actually exist unless he becomes like them. This is not the way with man in the world: he sees the sun without being a sun; and he sees the heaven and the earth and all other things, but he is not these things. This is quite in keeping with the truth. But you saw something of that place, and you became those things. You saw the Spirit, you became spirit. You saw Christ, you became Christ. You saw the Father, you shall become Father. So in this place you see everything and do not see yourself, but in that place you do see yourself—and what you see you shall become.

§The Chrism Passage (74.12–25) · Nag Hammadi Codex II, tractate 3, plate 82
Manuscript
Coptic

τὸ χρῖσμα ὑπερέχει τοῦ βαπτίσματος · ἐκ γὰρ τοῦ χρίσματος · Χριστιανοὶ ἐκλήθημεν

English

The chrism is superior to baptism, for it is from the word “chrism” that we have been called “Christians,” certainly not because of the word “baptism.” And it is because of the chrism that “the Christ” has his name. For the Father anointed the Son, and the Son anointed the apostles, and the apostles anointed us. He who has been anointed possesses everything. He possesses the resurrection, the light, the cross, the Holy Spirit. The Father gave him this in the bridal chamber; he merely accepted the gift.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Mirror Passage: The epistemological principle here—that seeing transforms the seer into what is seen—reverses the Platonic model in which knowledge is contemplation of a separate Form. In Philip, knowledge is assimilation. This has direct parallels to 2 Corinthians 3:18 (“we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image”) and to the later Orthodox doctrine of theosis through the vision of God. The difference is that Philip applies the principle universally: you saw the Father, you shall become Father. The orthodox tradition would insist that only the Son is consubstantial with the Father; Philip makes no such restriction.
  2. §The Chrism Passage: Philip’s elevation of chrism over baptism reflects a distinctive Valentinian sacramental theology. The etymology is accurate: Christos (Χριστός) means “anointed one,” from chriein (χρίειν), “to anoint.” Philip’s logic is that if Christians derive their name from Christ, and Christ derives his name from chrism, then the anointing is the foundational sacrament. The chain of anointing—Father to Son to apostles to the faithful—creates a direct ontological link between the believer and the divine source. The “bridal chamber” mentioned here is Philip’s highest sacrament, representing the reunion of the separated soul with its heavenly counterpart.
