Introduction

The Twelve Tables (451–450 BCE) are the earliest written legislation of the Roman Republic, codifying customary law on topics from debt to property to burial. They represent the foundation of Roman law and, through it, of the entire Western legal tradition.

§Tables I and III (selections) · Twelve Tables, Tables I and III
Manuscript
Greek

Si in ius vocat, ito. Ni it, antestamino; igitur em capito

English

If a plaintiff summons a defendant to court, the defendant shall go. If he does not go, the plaintiff shall call witnesses and then take him by force. If a debtor has been adjudged to owe money and does not pay, the creditor may seize him and bring him before the magistrate. The debtor shall have thirty days of grace.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Table I: The Twelve Tables were not a law code in the modern sense but a public display of legal rules previously known only to patrician priests. Their publication was a democratic achievement: making the law visible and accessible to all citizens, not just the ruling class.
