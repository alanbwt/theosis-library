Fragments: All Things Are One

Fragments: All Things Are One
Heraclitus, c. 535-475 BCE
Diels-Kranz, Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker

Introduction

Heraclitus of Ephesus, known as 'the Obscure' even in antiquity, left behind a single work from which only fragments survive, preserved as quotations in later authors. His central insight — that all things are unified through the Logos, a rational principle governing perpetual change — profoundly influenced Stoic philosophy, early Christian theology (the Gospel of John opens with the Logos), and Western metaphysics.

§B1 · DK 22 B1
Greek

τοῦ δὲ λόγου τοῦδ᾿ ἐόντος ἀεὶ ἀξύνετοι γίνονται ἄνθρωποι καὶ πρόσθεν ἢ ἀκοῦσαι καὶ ἀκούσαντες τὸ πρῶτον.

English

Though this Logos exists forever, people fail to comprehend it both before hearing it and after they have heard it for the first time.

§B30 · DK 22 B30
Greek

κόσμον τόνδε, τὸν αὐτὸν ἁπάντων, οὔτε τις θεῶν οὔτε ἀνθρώπων ἐποίησεν, ἀλλ᾿ ἦν ἀεὶ καὶ ἔστιν καὶ ἔσται πῦρ ἀείζωον, ἁπτόμενον μέτρα καὶ ἀποσβεννύμενον μέτρα.

English

This cosmos, the same for all, no god nor man has made, but it always was and is and shall be: an ever-living fire, kindling in measures and going out in measures.

§B50 · DK 22 B50
Greek

οὐκ ἐμοῦ, ἀλλὰ τοῦ λόγου ἀκούσαντας ὁμολογεῖν σοφόν ἐστιν ἓν πάντα εἶναι.

English

Listening not to me but to the Logos, it is wise to agree that all things are one.

§B53 · DK 22 B53
Greek

πόλεμος πάντων μὲν πατήρ ἐστι, πάντων δὲ βασιλεύς.

English

War is the father of all and king of all; some he shows as gods, others as men; some he makes slaves, others free.

§B91 · DK 22 B91
Greek

ποταμοῖσι τοῖσιν αὐτοῖσιν ἐμβαίνουσιν ἕτερα καὶ ἕτερα ὕδατα ἐπιρρεῖ.

English

Upon those who step into the same rivers, different and different waters flow.
