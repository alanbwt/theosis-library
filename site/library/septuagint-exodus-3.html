Exodus 3:13-15: I AM WHO I AM

Ἔξοδος
Moses (attributed), traditional
Septuagint (LXX)

Introduction

Exodus 3:13–15 contains the most consequential act of divine self-disclosure in the Hebrew Bible: the revelation of God’s name. When Moses asks whom he should say has sent him, God answers with the enigmatic ego eimi ho on (ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν)—“I am the one who is.” The Septuagint translators rendered the Hebrew ehyeh asher ehyeh with a participial construction that carries unmistakable ontological weight: ho on, “the Being,” “the Existing One.” This is not merely a name but a claim about the nature of reality itself. Greek-speaking Jews and early Christians heard in this phrase the language of philosophical theology—God as pure being, self-subsistent existence.

The passage became a cornerstone of Christian theology when it was read alongside John 8:58, where Jesus declares ego eimi (ἐγώ εἰμι)—“Before Abraham was, I am.” The verbal echo is unmistakable. Whether Jesus is claiming the divine name or merely pre-existence, his audience understood the implication clearly enough to pick up stones. The Fathers of the Church built entire Christologies on the connection between these two passages.

English

And Moses said to God, “Behold, I shall go to the sons of Israel and say to them, ‘The God of your fathers has sent me to you.’ And they will ask me, ‘What is his name?’ What shall I say to them?” And God said to Moses, “I am the one who is” (ego eimi ho on, ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν). And he said, “Thus shall you say to the sons of Israel: ‘The one who is’ (ho on, ὁ ὤν) has sent me to you.” And God said again to Moses, “Thus shall you say to the sons of Israel: ‘The Lord, the God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, has sent me to you.’ This is my name forever, and this is my memorial to all generations.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:14: The Septuagint renders the Hebrew ehyeh asher ehyeh (אהיה אשר אהיה) as ego eimi ho on (ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν). The Hebrew is verbal and dynamic—“I will be what I will be”—while the Greek shifts the meaning toward ontology: “I am the Being,” or “I am the Existing One.” The participle ho on (ὁ ὤν) became a standard divine title in Greek-speaking Judaism and early Christianity. It appears on the halo of Christ in Byzantine iconography to this day. This translation choice profoundly shaped how Greek-speaking theologians understood the nature of God—as self-subsistent Being itself (ipsum esse subsistens, as Thomas Aquinas would later formulate it in Latin).
  2. §3:14–15: The connection between this passage and John 8:58 (“Before Abraham was, ego eimi”) was not lost on the early Church. When Jesus uses the phrase ego eimi (ἐγώ εἰμι) without a predicate, the Gospel of John appears to be evoking this very text. The ego eimi sayings of John (6:35, 8:12, 8:58, 10:9, 11:25, 14:6, 15:1) form a sustained argument that Jesus bears the divine name revealed at the burning bush. Whether this represents historical memory or theological interpretation by the evangelist remains one of the central questions of New Testament scholarship.
