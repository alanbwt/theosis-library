2 Corinthians 8:9: Rich Became Poor

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Second Corinthians 8:9 is Paul’s most compressed statement of the exchange formula that lies at the heart of theosis theology. In a single sentence, embedded within a discussion of financial generosity, Paul articulates the logic of the Incarnation: “Though he was rich, for your sake he became poor, so that by his poverty you might become rich.” The “riches” and “poverty” are not monetary but ontological—Christ’s divine fullness emptied into human limitation so that human beings might be filled with divine fullness.

This verse became a proof text for the patristic exchange formula. Irenaeus wrote that the Word “became what we are in order to make us what he is.” Athanasius declared that “he was made man that we might be made God.” Both are commenting on the logic Paul states here. The grace (charis, χάρις) of Christ is not a transaction but a transformation: divine self-emptying produces human enrichment, and the enrichment is participation in divinity itself.

English

For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich.

Translator's Notes

  1. §8:9: The participle “being rich” (πλούσιος ὤν) is concessive and refers to Christ’s pre-incarnate divine glory. The verb “became poor” (ἐπτώχευσεν) is aorist, pointing to a specific event—the Incarnation. The structural parallel with Philippians 2:6–8 is unmistakable: in both texts, Christ voluntarily descends from divine status into human limitation. The purpose clause (“so that you might become rich”) makes the descent purposive: Christ’s self-emptying is not loss but the mechanism of human divinization.
