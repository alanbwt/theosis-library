Introduction

Xunzi’s essay on human nature argues that human nature is inherently bad and that goodness is the result of conscious effort, education, and ritual. This directly opposes Mencius’s view that human nature is inherently good.

§Chapter 23: Human Nature is Evil · Xunzi, Chapter 23
Manuscript
Greek

人之性惡，其善者偽也

English

Human nature is evil; goodness is the result of conscious activity. The nature of man is such that he is born with a fondness for profit. If this tendency is followed, strife and rapacity result. He is born with feelings of envy and hate. If these are followed, violence results. Therefore, a man must be transformed by the instruction of a teacher and guided by ritual and righteousness.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 23: Xunzi’s position parallels Hobbes’s view of the state of nature and Augustine’s doctrine of original sin, though his solution is Confucian: ritual and education, not social contract or divine grace. The Mencius-Xunzi debate on human nature remains one of the most productive disagreements in world philosophy.
