Al-Hallaj: Ana al-Haqq (I Am the Truth)

Mansur al-Hallaj, c. 858-922 AD

Introduction

In 922 AD, the Sufi mystic Husayn ibn Mansur al-Hallaj was executed in Baghdad for uttering the words Ana al-Haqq—“I am the Truth,” or, since al-Haqq is one of the ninety-nine names of God, “I am God.” He was flogged, his hands and feet were cut off, he was crucified, and finally decapitated. His crime was not blasphemy in the simple sense but something more dangerous: he had claimed, in public, the identity of the human soul with the divine. The orthodox authorities understood exactly what he meant, and they killed him for it.

Hallaj’s declaration is the Islamic parallel to two other traditions’ most radical claims. In Christianity, Jesus says “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30)—and the authorities sought to stone him. In the Upanishads, the great declaration (mahavakya) “Aham Brahmasmi” (“I am Brahman”) asserts the same identity, though in Hinduism this was celebrated rather than punished. What Hallaj experienced in mystical ecstasy (shath), the Upanishadic seers experienced in meditation, and Jesus claimed as ontological fact. The question is always the same: can a human being truthfully say “I am God”?

§From the Tawasin · Kitab al-Tawasin, Tasin al-Azal wa-l-Iltibas
Manuscript
Arabic

Raʼaytu Rabbi bi-ʻayni qalbi. Qultu: man Anta? Qala: Anta.

English

I saw my Lord with the eye of my heart. I said: Who are You? He said: You. For You, there is no “where,” and no “where” is where You are not. The imagination has no image of You, so that it might know where You are. You are the one who encompasses every “where” until there is no “where”—so where are You?

§The Ecstatic Utterance · Akhbar al-Hallaj, compiled by his followers
Manuscript
Arabic

Ana man ahwa wa man ahwa Ana, nahnu ruhani halalna badana. Fa-idha absartani absartahu, wa idha absartahu absartana.

English

I have become the One I love, and the One I love has become me. We are two spirits infused in one body. So to see me is to see Him, and to see Him is to see us.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Declaration: Al-Haqq (الحق) means “the Truth” or “the Real” and is one of the ninety-nine names of God in Islamic theology. When Hallaj said Ana al-Haqq, he was not claiming to be a prophet or a saint but claiming identity with the divine essence itself. His defenders (including later Sufis like Rumi) argued that it was not Hallaj speaking but God speaking through him—that the ego had been annihilated (fana) and only God remained. His prosecutors argued that no human can claim identity with God under any circumstances. The debate has never been settled.
  2. §The Ecstatic Utterance: This poem is the clearest expression of mystical union (ittihad) in Islamic literature. The phrase “two spirits infused in one body” parallels the Christological formula of two natures in one person, though Hallaj’s formulation goes further: the distinction between the two spirits is itself dissolved in the act of seeing. Orthodox Islamic theology rejected ittihad (union with God) as heresy, permitting only qurb (nearness to God). The Sufis walked the razor’s edge between these concepts.
