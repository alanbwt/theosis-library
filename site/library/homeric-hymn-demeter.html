Introduction

The Homeric Hymn to Demeter (c. 7th century BCE) is the foundational text of the Eleusinian Mysteries, narrating Persephone’s abduction by Hades and Demeter’s grief. The hymn concludes with the institution of secret rites at Eleusis that promised initiates a blessed afterlife.

§Lines 1–21 · Homeric Hymn 2, opening
Manuscript
Greek

Δημήτερα ἐύκομον ἁγνὴν θεὰν ἄρχομ’ ἀείδειν, αὐτὴν ἠδὲ θύγατρα τανύσφυρον ἣν Ἀιδωνεὺς ἥρπαξεν· δῶκεν δὲ βαρύκτυπος εὐρυόπα Ζεύς

English

I begin to sing of rich-haired Demeter, the revered goddess, and of her slim-ankled daughter whom Aidoneus seized—Zeus the deep-thunderer granted it—apart from Demeter of the golden blade and the splendid fruits, as she played with the deep-bosomed daughters of Ocean, gathering flowers.

§Lines 470–482 · Homeric Hymn 2, closing
Manuscript
Greek

ὄλβιος ὃς τάδ’ ὄπωπεν ἐπιχθονίων ἀνθρώπων· ὃς δ’ ἀτελὴς ἱερῶν ὅς τ’ ἄμμορος, οὔ ποθ’ ὁμοίων αἶσαν ἔχει φθίμενός περ ὑπὸ ζόφῳ εὐρώεντι

English

Blessed is he among mortal men who has seen these things; but he who is uninitiated and has no part in the rites never has a share of like blessings, even when he is dead, down in the murky darkness.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 470–482: The “macarism” or beatitude for initiates (ὄλβιος, “blessed”) is the most explicit promise in surviving Eleusinian literature. Pindar and Sophocles echo the same formula. The promise is not moral instruction but experiential transformation through seeing (epopteia).
