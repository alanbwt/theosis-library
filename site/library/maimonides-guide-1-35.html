Guide I.35: God Has No Body, No Form

Maimonides, 1138-1204 AD

Introduction

Moses Maimonides (1138–1204), the greatest Jewish philosopher of the medieval period, wrote the Guide for the Perplexed (Dalalat al-Ha’irin) in Judeo-Arabic around 1190. In Book I, Chapter 35, he establishes the absolute incorporeality of God. God has no body, no form, no likeness, no spatial location, no physical attributes whatsoever. Any biblical passage that ascribes physical characteristics to God—God’s “hand,” God’s “face,” God “descending”—must be understood as metaphor accommodated to human understanding.

Maimonides’s position represents the Jewish philosophical rejection of any blurring between the divine and human natures. If God has no body and no form, then the Christian claim that God became incarnate in human flesh is not merely wrong but logically incoherent. The entire framework of theosis—that humanity can participate in the divine nature—presupposes some point of contact between God and the human being. Maimonides denies that any such point exists. God is pure intellect, absolutely simple, and the gap between Creator and creature is unbridgeable by nature.

§I.35.1 · Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed I.35
Manuscript
Arabic/Hebrew

اعلم أن سيد العارفين موسى ربنا سأل سؤالين

English

Know that the master of those who know, Moses our teacher, made two requests of God. The first was that He should let him know His essence and true nature. The second was that He should let him know His attributes. God promised to make known to him all His attributes, and said that they are His actions. He also informed him that His true essence could not be perceived.

§I.35.2 · Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed I.35
Manuscript
Arabic/Hebrew

قد تبرهن أن الله تعالى ليس بجسم ولا صورة له

English

It has been demonstrated that God is incorporeal. He has no body, no form, no figure, no composition. He is not subject to any of the conditions of body. He does not occupy space. He is not affected by physical accidents. He has no likeness at all.

§I.35.3 · Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed I.35
Manuscript
Arabic/Hebrew

وإذ قد ثبت أنه ليس بجسم… فكل من يتصوره جسما فقد خرج عن الإيمان

English

Since it has been proven that He is not a body, it follows that all those who imagine God in bodily form, or ascribe to Him any bodily attribute, have departed from faith in God. They are worse than idolaters, for the idolater at least does not confuse the idol with God’s essence, but one who imagines God as a body has confused the Creator with creation.

§I.35.4 · Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed I.35
Manuscript
Arabic/Hebrew

نعلم أنه موجود وأنه واحد وأنه ليس بجسم… ولكن لا نعلم ما هو

English

We can know that God exists, that He is one, and that He is not a body. But we cannot know what He is. We can only say what He is not. This is the utmost that the human intellect can achieve regarding knowledge of God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.35.1: Maimonides’s reference to Moses’s request (Exodus 33:18–23) is foundational. Even Moses—the greatest prophet—was denied knowledge of God’s essence. He was shown God’s “back” but not God’s “face.” For Maimonides, this establishes an absolute epistemic limit: if Moses could not know God’s essence, no human being can. This directly contradicts both Eunomius (who claimed perfect knowledge of God’s essence) and the Orthodox theosis tradition (which claims experiential participation in the divine nature).
  2. §I.35.3: Maimonides’s via negativa—the approach to God through negation—has parallels in both Neoplatonism and apophatic Christian theology. But Maimonides draws a harder conclusion than the Christian apophatics. For Pseudo-Dionysius or Gregory of Nyssa, the unknowability of God’s essence is the beginning of mystical union. For Maimonides, it is simply the end of the road. The human intellect can approach God through philosophical contemplation, but it cannot cross over into the divine nature.
