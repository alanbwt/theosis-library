Introduction

        
The opening chapter of the Enchiridion (Handbook) contains the foundational principle of Stoic ethics: the distinction between what is 'up to us' (eph' hēmin) and what is not. Epictetus — himself a former slave — teaches that freedom is not a political condition but a philosophical one. You are free when you desire only what is within your power (your own judgments, desires, and aversions) and are indifferent to everything outside it (your body, property, reputation, and life itself).
This single distinction, properly understood and practiced, is the whole of Stoic philosophy. Everything else — the exercises, the meditations, the disciplines — is an elaboration of this one insight.

            §1.1 · Ench. 1.1
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Τῶν ὄντων τὰ μέν ἐστιν ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν, τὰ δὲ οὐκ ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν. ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν μὲν ὑπόληψις, ὁρμή, ὄρεξις, ἔκκλισις, καὶ ἑνὶ λόγῳ ὅσα ἡμέτερα ἔργα· οὐκ ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν δὲ τὸ σῶμα, ἡ κτῆσις, δόξαι, ἀρχαί, καὶ ἑνὶ λόγῳ ὅσα οὐχ ἡμέτερα ἔργα.

          


          

            
English

            
Of things that exist, some are in our power and some are not. In our power are opinion, impulse, desire, and aversion — in a word, whatever is our own doing. Not in our power are the body, property, reputation, and office — in a word, whatever is not our own doing.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.2 · Ench. 1.2
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
τὰ μὲν ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν ἐστι φύσει ἐλεύθερα, ἀκώλυτα, ἀπαραπόδιστα· τὰ δὲ οὐκ ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν ἀσθενῆ, δοῦλα, κωλυτά, ἀλλότρια.

          


          

            
English

            
The things in our power are by nature free, unhindered, unimpeded. The things not in our power are weak, slavish, hindered, belonging to others.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.3 · Ench. 1.3
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
μέμνησο οὖν, ὅτι, ἐὰν τὰ φύσει δοῦλα ἐλεύθερα οἰηθῇς καὶ τὰ ἀλλότρια ἴδια, ἐμποδισθήσῃ, πενθήσεις, ταραχθήσῃ, μέμψῃ καὶ θεοὺς καὶ ἀνθρώπους.

          


          

            
English

            
Remember, then, that if you regard what is by nature slavish as free, and what belongs to others as your own, you will be hindered, you will grieve, you will be troubled, and you will blame both gods and men. But if you regard only what is your own as your own, and what belongs to others as belonging to others, no one will ever compel you, no one will hinder you, you will blame no one, you will accuse no one, you will do nothing unwillingly, no one will harm you, and you will have no enemies — for no harm can touch you.
