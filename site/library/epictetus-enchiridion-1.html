Introduction

        
The Enchiridion (Handbook) is the practical core of Stoic philosophy. Compiled by Arrian from the lectures of Epictetus — a former slave who became one of the greatest moral philosophers of antiquity — it begins with the foundational distinction: some things are 'up to us' (our judgments, desires, aversions), and some things are not (everything else). All Stoic ethics flows from this single insight. The first chapter is the seed of the entire Stoic system.

            §1.1 · Ench. 1.1
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Τῶν ὄντων τὰ μέν ἐστιν ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν, τὰ δὲ οὐκ ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν. ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν μὲν ὑπόληψις, ὁρμή, ὄρεξις, ἔκκλισις, καὶ ἑνὶ λόγῳ ὅσα ἡμέτερα ἔργα· οὐκ ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν δὲ τὸ σῶμα, ἡ κτῆσις, δόξαι, ἀρχαί, καὶ ἑνὶ λόγῳ ὅσα οὐχ ἡμέτερα ἔργα.

          


          

            
English

            
Of all existing things, some are up to us, and some are not up to us. Up to us are opinion, impulse, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is our own action. Not up to us are body, property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not our own action.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.2 · Ench. 1.2
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Καὶ τὰ μὲν ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν ἐστι φύσει ἐλεύθερα, ἀκώλυτα, ἀπαραπόδιστα· τὰ δὲ οὐκ ἐφ᾿ ἡμῖν ἀσθενῆ, δοῦλα, κωλυτά, ἀλλότρια.

          


          

            
English

            
The things up to us are by nature free, unhindered, unimpeded; the things not up to us are weak, servile, can be hindered, belong to others.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.3 · Ench. 1.3
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Μέμνησο οὖν, ὅτι, ἐὰν τὰ φύσει δοῦλα ἐλεύθερα οἰηθῇς καὶ τὰ ἀλλότρια ἴδια, ἐμποδισθήσῃ, πενθήσεις, ταραχθήσῃ, μέμψῃ καὶ θεοὺς καὶ ἀνθρώπους.

          


          

            
English

            
Remember, then, that if you regard what is by nature servile as free, and what belongs to others as your own, you will be hindered, you will lament, you will be disturbed, you will blame both gods and men. But if you regard only what is your own as your own, and what belongs to others as in fact belonging to others, no one will ever compel you, no one will hinder you, you will blame no one, you will accuse no one, you will not do a single thing unwillingly, you will have no enemy, no one will harm you — for you cannot be harmed at all.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.4 · Ench. 1.4
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Τηλικούτων οὖν ἐφιέμενος μέμνησο, ὅτι οὐ δεῖ μετρίως κεκινημένον ἅπτεσθαι αὐτῶν, ἀλλὰ τὰ μὲν ἀφιέναι παντελῶς, τὰ δ᾿ ὑπερτίθεσθαι πρὸς τὸ παρόν.

          


          

            
English

            
If, then, you aim at such great goals, remember that you must not lay hold of them with mere ordinary effort, but you must let some things go entirely, and put off others for the present. But if you wish for these great things, and at the same time wish for office and wealth, perhaps you will not get even these latter things, because you also aim at the former; and certainly you will fail in those things by which alone freedom and happiness are obtained.

          

        

        
        


          

            §1.5 · Ench. 1.5
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
Εὐθὺς οὖν πάσῃ φαντασίᾳ τραχείᾳ μελέτα ἐπιλέγειν ὅτι 'φαντασία εἶ καὶ οὐ πάντως τὸ φαινόμενον.'

          


          

            
English

            
Therefore, practice from the very beginning to say to every harsh impression: 'You are an impression, and not at all what you appear to be.' Then examine it and test it by these rules which you have, and first and foremost by this: whether it concerns the things which are up to us, or those which are not up to us. And if it concerns one of the things not up to us, be ready to say: 'It is nothing to me.'
