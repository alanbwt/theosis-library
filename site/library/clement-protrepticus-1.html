Protrepticus 1.8.4: The Word Became Man So You May Learn How Man Becomes God

Προτρεπτικός
Clement of Alexandria, c. 150–215 AD

Introduction

The Protrepticus (Exhortation to the Greeks) is Clement of Alexandria’s earliest major work, written around 195 CE. It is an appeal to the educated pagans of Alexandria to abandon their mysteries and turn to the true Logos. Clement was uniquely positioned to make this argument: trained in Greek philosophy, steeped in the Platonic and Stoic traditions, yet convinced that Christ was the fulfilment of everything the philosophers had been reaching toward.

In chapter 1, section 8.4, Clement makes what may be the single most concise statement of the theosis principle in all of patristic literature. The Word became man so that humanity might learn from a man how to become God. This is not metaphor for Clement. He means it as a description of the soul’s real trajectory when it turns from idols to the living God.

§1.8.4 · Protrepticus 1.8.4 (Stahlin ed., GCS 12)
Manuscript
Greek

Λόγος ο του Θεου άνθρωπος γενόμενος, ίνα δη και συ παρα ανθρώπου μάθης πή ποτε άνθρωπος γένηται Θεός.

English

The Word of God became man so that you too may learn from a man how a man becomes God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.8.4: This passage is sometimes cited in a slightly different form depending on the edition. The key verb is μάθης (“learn”): for Clement, theosis is a pedagogical process. The Logos teaches by example. God does not simply decree human divinization; he demonstrates it by becoming human himself. This pedagogical framing distinguishes Clement from later writers like Athanasius, for whom the exchange is more ontological than educational.
