Introduction

The Hippolytus of Euripides (428 BCE) dramatizes the destruction caused when Aphrodite punishes Hippolytus for his exclusive devotion to Artemis. The play explores the danger of rejecting any dimension of human nature—even in the name of purity.

§Lines 1–22 · Euripides, Hippolytus
Manuscript
Greek

πολλὴ μὲν ἐν βροτοῖς κοὐκ ἀνώνυμος θεὰ Κύπρις

English

Great and of high renown among mortals am I, the goddess Cypris, and in heaven too. Of all those who dwell between the Pontic Sea and the boundaries of Atlas, who see the light of the sun, I honor those who reverence my power, and I trip up those who are proud against me.

§Lines 73–87 · Euripides, Hippolytus
Manuscript
Greek

σοὶ τόνδε πλεκτὸν στέφανον ἐξ ἀκηράτου λειμῶνος

English

For you, O mistress, I bring this woven garland, gathered from an uncut meadow, where no shepherd dares to pasture his flock, and no iron has ever come; only the bee passes through it in spring. Purity tends it with river water. For those to whom nothing is taught but in whose nature moderation has been allotted in all things—for these it is right to pluck; for the wicked, it is not permitted.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 1–22: Aphrodite’s opening speech announces a theology of divine jealousy: the gods demand acknowledgment, and those who deny any god will be destroyed. The play thus presents a tragic polytheism in which no single virtue—not even chastity—can be pursued to the exclusion of all others without catastrophe.
