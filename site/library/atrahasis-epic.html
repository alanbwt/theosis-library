Atrahasis Epic: Humanity Created from Divine Blood

Anonymous (Babylonian), c. 1100 BCE

Introduction

The Atrahasis Epic is a Mesopotamian creation and flood narrative composed in Akkadian, with the earliest surviving copies dating to the Old Babylonian period (c. 1700 BCE). The text describes the creation of humanity from clay mixed with the blood of a slain god, We-ilu—a divine being sacrificed so that his flesh and blood could animate the clay, giving humans both material substance and a divine element. This is the Mesopotamian divine spark.

The theological implications are profound. In Atrahasis, humans are not purely material beings; they carry within them the essence of a god. The “spirit” (etemmu) that haunts human existence is the ghostly residue of the slain deity. This concept—that humanity contains a divine component trapped in matter—anticipates Gnostic, Orphic, and Platonic ideas by over a millennium and provides essential background for understanding the theosis traditions of the ancient Mediterranean world.

§Tablet I, lines 190–217 · BM 78941, British Museum (Lambert-Millard edition)
Manuscript
Akkadian

ilū ina puhrim ittalkamū. dÉa pî-šu īpusamma iqabbi ana ilī rabûti. ina arhi ina ūmi ešri ina ūmi hamsi šeris tallilta lu aškun ramkī. ištēn ilam linērū ilūma ina rimkisūnu lizzakkū. ina šīrisū u damisūnu dNintu lī-bal-lil ţīţţa. ilu u awelu lu šu-te-el-lu-ú ina ţīţţim.

English

The gods assembled in the council. Enki opened his mouth and addressed the great gods: “On the first, seventh, and fifteenth of the month, I will establish a purification, a bath. Let one god be slaughtered, and let the gods be purified by immersion. From his flesh and his blood let Nintu mix the clay, that god and man may be thoroughly mixed in the clay.”

§Tablet I, lines 218–230 · BM 78941, British Museum
Manuscript
Akkadian

We-ilam ša išū ţēmam ina puhrisūnu inērū. ina šīrisū u damisūnu dNintu ī-bal-lil ţīţţa. ana ūm šāti uppa išmuū. ina šīr ilim etemmu ibbašši. balţū iššīšū uttassû. ana la mašēm etemmu ibbašši.

English

They slaughtered We-ilu, the god who had intelligence (temu), in their assembly. From his flesh and his blood Nintu mixed the clay. For the rest of time they heard the drumbeat—from the flesh of the god there was a spirit (etemmu). It made the living know its sign. So that it not be forgotten, there was a spirit.

§Tablet I, lines 231–243 · BM 78941, British Museum
Manuscript
Akkadian

ištu ţīţţa ī-bal-li-lu išī dAnunnakī ilī rabûti. dIgīgī ilū rabûti ru’tāsūnu ittadû ana ţīţţim.

English

After she had mixed the clay, she summoned the Anunna-gods, the great gods. The Igigi, the great gods, spat upon the clay. Mami opened her mouth and addressed the great gods: “You commanded me a task—I have completed it. You slaughtered a god together with his intelligence. I have removed your heavy work; I have imposed your toil on man.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Tablet I, lines 190–217: The phrase “that god and man may be thoroughly mixed in the clay” is the foundational statement of the Mesopotamian divine anthropology. Unlike Genesis, where God breathes life into already-formed clay, in Atrahasis the divine element is physically blended into the raw material. The result is not merely an animated body but a being that is ontologically composite: part divine, part material.
  2. §Tablet I, lines 218–230: The etemmu (spirit or ghost) that persists in human beings is the residue of the slain god. This concept explains the Mesopotamian preoccupation with the dead and the afterlife: the etemmu survives bodily death because it is of divine origin. The “drumbeat” (uppu) that is heard “for the rest of time” may refer to the human heartbeat—the audible sign of the divine presence within mortal flesh.
