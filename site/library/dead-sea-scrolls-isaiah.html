Introduction

        
The discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls in 1947 was the greatest manuscript find of the twentieth century. The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsa-a), found in Cave 1 at Qumran near the Dead Sea, is the crown jewel of the collection: the only complete book of the Hebrew Bible found among the scrolls, and over a thousand years older than any previously known Hebrew manuscript of Isaiah.
The scroll's significance is twofold: it confirms that the Hebrew text of Isaiah was transmitted with extraordinary fidelity over more than a millennium, and it provides variant readings that illuminate the history of the biblical text. The scroll is now housed in the Shrine of the Book at the Israel Museum in Jerusalem.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · dead-sea-scrolls-isaiah
          
The text of the Great Isaiah Scroll is substantially identical to the Masoretic text that forms the basis of modern Hebrew Bibles, despite being copied over a thousand years earlier. The scroll contains all 66 chapters of Isaiah, written on 17 sheets of parchment sewn together, measuring 7.34 meters (24 feet) in length. Among its most significant passages: the Servant Songs (chapters 42, 49, 50, 52-53), the prophecy of Immanuel (7:14), and the vision of the new heavens and new earth (65:17). Minor variants from the Masoretic text provide scholars with invaluable evidence for the history of the biblical text.
