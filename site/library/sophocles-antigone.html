Antigone: The Ode to Man

Antigone: The Ode to Man
Sophocles, c. 496-406 BCE
Mediceus Laurentianus 32.9

Introduction

Sophocles' Antigone stages the collision between human law and divine law. When Creon forbids the burial of her brother Polyneices, Antigone defies him in the name of the 'unwritten laws' that transcend any ruler's decree. The play's choral ode — polla ta deina, 'many are the wonders' — is the most celebrated meditation on human nature in all of Greek literature: humanity as simultaneously magnificent and monstrous, capable of mastering nature yet unable to escape death.

§332-375 · Ant. 332-375
Greek

πολλὰ τὰ δεινὰ κοὐδὲν ἀνθρώπου δεινότερον πέλει. τοῦτο καὶ πολιοῦ πέραν πόντου χειμερίῳ νότῳ χωρεῖ, περιβρυχίοισιν περῶν ὑπ᾿ οἴδμασιν.

English

Many are the wonders, and nothing is more wondrous than man. This creature crosses the grey sea in the winter storm, passing through the surging waves.

§450-460 · Ant. 450-460
Greek

οὐ γάρ τί μοι Ζεὺς ἦν ὁ κηρύξας τάδε, οὐδ᾿ ἡ ξύνοικος τῶν κάτω θεῶν Δίκη... οὐδὲ σθένειν τοσοῦτον ᾠόμην τὰ σὰ κηρύγμαθ᾿ ὥστ᾿ ἄγραπτα κἀσφαλῆ θεῶν νόμιμα δύνασθαί θνητὸν ὄνθ᾿ ὑπερδραμεῖν.

English

It was not Zeus who proclaimed this to me, nor Justice who dwells with the gods below... nor did I think your decrees were of such force that a mortal could override the unwritten and unfailing laws of the gods.
