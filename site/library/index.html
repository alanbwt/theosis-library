Apostolic (1st-2nd century)

  • Gospel of John, Chapter 1:1–18 (The Logos Prologue) published

    John (attributed) · c. 90–110 AD · Greek
    The Prologue of John's Gospel from the earliest complete New Testament manuscript. The foundational text for Logos theology and the starting point for the theosis debate. Contains the key variant: Codex Sinaiticus reads 'only-begotten God' (monogenes theos) where later manuscripts read 'only-begotten Son.'
    logostheosisincarnationdivine-naturemonogenespleroma

  • Gospel of John 10:22–39: “I Said, You Are Gods” published

    John (attributed) · c. 90–110 AD · Greek
    The most direct confrontation in the Gospels over Christ's divinity. Jesus declares 'I and the Father are one,' is accused of blasphemy, and responds by quoting Psalm 82: 'I said, you are gods.' The single most contested passage in the theosis debate.
    theosisdivine-naturepsalm-82incarnation

  • 2 Peter 1:3–4: “Partakers of the Divine Nature” published

    Peter (attributed) · c. 100–120 AD · Greek
    The most explicit theosis statement in the New Testament: 'that you may become partakers of the divine nature.' The scriptural foundation for the entire Eastern Orthodox doctrine of divinization.
    theosisdivine-naturedivinizationepignosis
Ante-Nicene (before 325 AD)

  • Against Heresies, Book III, Chapter 19: "He Became What We Are" published

    Irenaeus of Lyon · c. 130–202 AD · Latin
    The earliest systematic articulation of the theosis doctrine. Irenaeus argues that the Word became what we are so that we might become what he is.
    theosisincarnationdivine-naturedivinizationanti-gnostic

  • Refutation of All Heresies, Book 6, Chapters 24–37 queued

    Hippolytus of Rome · c. 170–235 AD · Greek
    Hippolytus describes Valentinian Gnostic theology in detail: the divine Pleroma, the emanation of aeons, the fall of Sophia, the creation of the material world by the Demiurge, and the divine spark trapped within humanity.
    gnosticismvalentinianismpleromasophiadivine-sparkdemiurgetheosis

  • Against the Valentinians queued

    Tertullian · c. 155–220 AD · Latin
    Tertullian’s sharp, satirical attack on Valentinian Gnosticism. Written entirely in Latin by one of the most vivid prose stylists in Christian history.
    gnosticismvalentinianismpleromaaeonslatin-fathers
Nicene (325-451 AD)

  • On the Incarnation, Chapter 54: “He Became Man That We Might Become God” published

    Athanasius of Alexandria · c. 296–373 AD · Greek
    The most famous sentence in the history of the theosis doctrine. Athanasius argues that the entire purpose of the Incarnation was not merely to save humanity from sin but to transform human nature: to make the mortal immortal, the human divine.
    theosisincarnationdivine-naturedivinizationnicene-theology

  • The Book of Various Heresies, Chapters 29–43: The Gnostic Schools published first translation

    Filastrius of Brescia · d. c. 397 AD · Latin
    The first English translation of any part of Filastrius’s catalog of heresies. These fifteen chapters cover every major Gnostic school known to the late fourth-century Church: the Simonians, Basilideans, Valentinians, Marcosians, Carpocratians, and the Barbelo-Gnostics.
    gnosticismvalentinianismbasilideanssimonianscarpocratiansdivine-sparkaeonspleromademiurgelatin-fathers

  • Against Arius, Book 1 queued

    Marius Victorinus · c. 290–364 AD · Latin
    A Neoplatonist philosopher who converted to Christianity in old age, Marius Victorinus brought the full apparatus of Greek philosophical thought to bear on the question of Christ’s divinity.
    neoplatonismtrinitydivine-naturetheosislatin-fathersphilosophy
Ancient Near East ()

  • Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet XI: The Flood published

    Sin-leqi-unninni · c. 1300–1000 BCE · Akkadian
    The oldest flood narrative in world literature, predating Genesis by over a millennium. Utnapishtim tells Gilgamesh how the gods destroyed humanity with a great flood, and how he alone was granted immortality. Contains the earliest meditation on human mortality and the question of whether divinity can be shared with mortals.

c. 2600 BCE

Instructions of Shuruppak

Sumerian · Earliest known wisdom literature
One of the oldest surviving literary texts. A father's advice to his son, preserved on clay tablets from ancient Sumer. Predates the Hebrew Bible by over a millennium.
Forthcoming
c. 2100 BCE

Epic of Gilgamesh (Standard Version)

Akkadian · Mesopotamia
The oldest great work of literature. A king's search for immortality and the meaning of human mortality. Contains the earliest flood narrative, predating Genesis by centuries.
Forthcoming
c. 1550 BCE

Egyptian Book of the Dead (Papyrus of Ani)

Egyptian · Thebes
Spells and instructions for navigating the afterlife. The most complete surviving copy of the funerary texts that shaped Egyptian religion for two millennia.
Forthcoming
c. 250 BCE

Septuagint (LXX)

Greek · Alexandria
The Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, produced in Ptolemaic Alexandria. The Bible of the early Church and the textual basis for most New Testament quotations of the Old.
Forthcoming
c. 50–120 AD

Earliest Gospel Manuscripts

Greek · From Codex Sinaiticus (4th c. copy)
The words of Jesus as preserved in the earliest surviving manuscripts. John 1 (the Logos), John 10 ("ye are gods"), 2 Peter 1:4 ("partakers of the divine nature").
Forthcoming
c. 130–200 AD

Irenaeus, Against Heresies

Greek/Latin · Lyon
The earliest systematic account of Gnostic theology and its refutation. "God became man so that man might become God."
Forthcoming
c. 385 AD

Filastrius, The Gnostic Schools

Latin · Brescia · First English translation
Fifteen chapters covering every major Gnostic school: Simonians, Basilideans, Valentinians, Marcosians, Carpocratians, Barbelo-Gnostics. With manuscript scans.
