Introduction

Genesis 2 presents the second creation account, centering on the formation of the first human from dust and the planting of Eden. The Septuagint text here uses plasma (πλάσμα) for God’s shaping of humanity, language that Irenaeus would later develop into his theology of divine craftsmanship.

§2:7 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἔπλασεν ὁ θεὸς τὸν ἄνθρωπον χοῦν ἀπὸ τῆς γῆς καὶ ἐνεφύσησεν εἰς τὸ πρόσωπον αὐτοῦ πνοὴν ζωῆς

English

And God formed the man, dust from the earth, and breathed into his face a breath of life, and the man became a living soul.

§2:15–17 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἔλαβεν κύριος ὁ θεὸς τὸν ἄνθρωπον ὃν ἔπλασεν

English

And the Lord God took the man and placed him in the garden of delight to work it and to guard it. And the Lord God commanded the man, saying, “From every tree in the garden you may eat for food, but from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, you shall not eat from it; for on the day you eat from it, you shall surely die.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:7: The Septuagint uses eplassen (ἔπλασεν), “molded” or “shaped,” the verb used for a potter working clay. The “breath of life” (πνοὴν ζωῆς) directly from God establishes an intimate link between human life and divine spirit that the theosis tradition would develop extensively.
