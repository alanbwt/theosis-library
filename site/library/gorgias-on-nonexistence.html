Gorgias On Nonexistence

Unknown,

Introduction

Gorgias of Leontini (c. 483–375 BCE) composed On Non-Existence as a radical challenge to Eleatic metaphysics. Its three theses—nothing exists, if it did it could not be known, if known it could not be communicated—represent the most extreme skepticism in ancient philosophy.

Greek original and English translation.
§Three Theses · DK 82 B3 (Sextus Empiricus, Adv. Math. VII.65–87)
Manuscript
Greek

οὐδὲν ἔστιν· εἰ καὶ ἔστιν, ἄγνωστον ἀνθρώπῳ· εἰ καὶ γνωστόν, ἀλλὰ ἀνέξοιστον καὶ ἀνερμήνευτον τῷ πέλας

English

Nothing exists. Even if something exists, it cannot be apprehended by a human being. Even if it can be apprehended, it is without doubt incapable of being expressed or explained to the next person.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Three Theses: Whether Gorgias intended this as a serious philosophical argument or as a rhetorical tour de force parodying Parmenides remains debated. The work survives through Sextus Empiricus and an anonymous paraphrase in De Melisso Xenophane Gorgia.
