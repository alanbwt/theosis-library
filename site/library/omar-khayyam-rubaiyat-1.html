Introduction

The Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam, as rendered by Edward FitzGerald, is the most famous collection of Persian quatrains in the English-speaking world. The poems celebrate the fleeting present, wine, and the impermanence of all things against the backdrop of cosmic mystery.

§Quatrains 1, 12, 71 · Omar Khayyam, Rubaiyat (FitzGerald translation)
Manuscript
Greek

بامدادان که مخمور جهان افتاب شد

English

Awake! for Morning in the Bowl of Night / Has flung the Stone that puts the Stars to Flight: / And Lo! the Hunter of the East has caught / The Sultan’s Turret in a Noose of Light. A Book of Verses underneath the Bough, / A Jug of Wine, a Loaf of Bread—and Thou / Beside me singing in the Wilderness— / Oh, Wilderness were Paradise enow!

Translator's Notes

  1. §Quatrain 12: FitzGerald’s translation is more an English poem inspired by Khayyam than a faithful rendering. The original Persian is more philosophically ambiguous—wine may be literal or Sufi metaphor for divine intoxication. The tension between hedonism and mysticism is never fully resolved in either version.
