Introduction

The Seafarer (c. 10th century) is an Old English elegy from the Exeter Book in which a speaker describes the hardships of life at sea and transforms them into a meditation on earthly exile and the longing for an eternal homeland.

§Lines 58–67 · The Seafarer, Exeter Book
Manuscript
Greek

Forþon mē hatran sind Dryhtnes drēamas þonne þis dēade līf, lǣne on londe. Ic gelȳfe nō þæt him eorðwelan ēce stondeð.

English

Indeed the joys of the Lord are warmer to me than this dead, transitory life on land. I do not believe that earthly wealth stands forever. Always, in every case, one of three things will bring uncertainty before one’s final day: illness, or age, or sword-hate will wrest life from the doomed.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 58–67: The tension between earthly sorrow and divine joy has generated centuries of debate: is the speaker a literal sailor or an allegorical pilgrim? The poem resists resolution. Its power lies in the unresolved fusion of Germanic stoicism and Christian eschatology.
