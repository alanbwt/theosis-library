Discourses I.1: You Carry God Within You

Epictetus, c. 50-135 AD

Introduction

Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) was born a slave and became one of the most influential Stoic philosophers. His Discourses, recorded by his student Arrian, open with a meditation on the divine origin of the rational faculty. For Epictetus, the human capacity for reason is not merely a natural endowment—it is a fragment of God. The divine logos that governs the cosmos is the same logos that operates in the human mind. To exercise reason rightly is therefore to participate in divinity.

This is the Stoic version of theosis, and it differs fundamentally from both the Christian and the Neoplatonic versions. There is no need for incarnation, no mystical ascent, no sacramental transformation. The divine is already present within every rational being. What is needed is not grace but discipline—the systematic training of the will to desire only what is in its power and to accept everything else with equanimity. The Stoic sage, who has perfected this discipline, “carries God within him” not as a metaphor but as a description of reality.

§I.1.1–3 · Epictetus, Discourses I.1.1–3 (Arrian’s transcription)
Manuscript
Greek

Τῶν δυνάμεων καθόλου οὐδεμίαν εὑρήσετε αὐτὴν αὑτῆς θεωρητικήν.

English

Of our faculties in general, you will find that none can take cognizance of itself; none therefore has the power to approve or disapprove of itself. How far does the power of grammar extend? As far as the judging of language. Of music? As far as the judging of melody. Does either of them take cognizance of itself? By no means.

§I.1.4–6 · Epictetus, Discourses I.1.4–6
Manuscript
Greek

ὅτε δ’ ἔδει θεωρητικὴν εἶναι δύναμιν καὶ αὑτῆς καὶ τῶν ἄλλων, θεὸς τὴν προαίρεσιν ἔδωκεν.

English

But when it was necessary for there to be a faculty capable of contemplating itself and all other things, God gave us reason—which alone of all faculties is able to comprehend itself: what it is, what its powers are, what its value, and also to comprehend every other faculty. For what else is it that tells us that gold is beautiful? The gold does not speak for itself.

§I.1.10–12 · Epictetus, Discourses I.1.10–12
Manuscript
Greek

τί οὖν ἐποίησεν; αὐτοῦ μέρος σε ἐποίησεν.

English

What did God do? He made you a part of himself. He gave you a certain portion of himself—the faculty of choice and refusal, of desire and aversion, and in a word the faculty of using impressions. If you care for this and place your good in it, you will never be obstructed, never hindered, you will not groan, will not blame, will not flatter any man.

§I.14.6 · Epictetus, Discourses I.14.6
Manuscript
Greek

ἀπόσπασμα εἶ τοῦ θεοῦ. ἔχεις τι μέρος ἐκείνου ἐν σαυτῷ.

English

You are a fragment of God. You carry God within you, poor wretch, and you do not know it. Do you think I speak of some external god of gold or silver? It is within yourself that you carry him, and you do not perceive that you defile him with impure thoughts and filthy actions.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1.10–12: The phrase “He made you a part of himself” (αὐτοῦ μέρος σε ἐποίησεν) is literal in Stoic physics: the human rational soul is a fragment of the divine fire that pervades the cosmos. Unlike the Platonic or Christian soul, which is a separate substance, the Stoic soul is materially continuous with God. This means that theosis, in the Stoic framework, is not a transformation of the self but a recognition of what the self already is.
  2. §I.14.6: The term apospasma (ἀπόσπασμα, “fragment,” literally “something torn off”) is striking in its physicality. The human being is not made in the image of God (as in Genesis) but is a piece torn from God. The early Church Fathers were drawn to Stoic language but had to reinterpret it: for the Christians, the soul’s kinship with God is analogical, not material. Marcus Aurelius uses the same term (Meditations V.27), showing that this was standard Stoic vocabulary.
