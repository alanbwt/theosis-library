4Q521: The Messianic Apocalypse

Anonymous (Enochic), c. 3rd c. BCE

Introduction

4Q521, known as the Messianic Apocalypse, is one of the most remarkable texts recovered from the Qumran caves. Written no later than the first century BC, it describes a coming age in which the Lord will “heal the wounded, resurrect the dead, and proclaim good news to the poor.” When John the Baptist sent messengers to ask Jesus whether he was “the one who is to come,” Jesus answered by quoting almost exactly this list of signs (Luke 7:22, Matthew 11:5). Either Jesus knew this text, or both drew on a shared messianic expectation that predates Christianity.

The fragment matters for the theosis tradition because it places resurrection and healing not merely in the category of miracle but in the category of divine identity markers. These are the things God does. When the Messiah does them, he is acting with divine authority. The boundary between the human agent and the divine principal becomes, in this text, deliberately unclear—and that ambiguity is precisely the theological space in which theosis operates.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragment 2, II, lines 1–4: The phrase “the heavens and the earth will listen to his Messiah” is extraordinary. In the Hebrew Bible, it is God to whom heaven and earth are called as witnesses (Deuteronomy 32:1, Isaiah 1:2). Here the same cosmic obedience is directed toward the Messiah, suggesting a figure who exercises divine authority. Whether “his Messiah” refers to a royal, priestly, or prophetic figure—or to God acting directly—remains debated.
  2. §Fragment 2, II, lines 8–13: The list—heal the wounded, resurrect the dead, proclaim good news to the poor—draws on Isaiah 61:1 and Isaiah 35:5–6, but combines them in a way that is unique to this text. When Jesus recites a nearly identical list in Luke 7:22, he is not inventing new messianic criteria but invoking an existing tradition. The question is whether the subject of these verbs is God or the Messiah. The ambiguity may be deliberate: in the messianic age, divine and human action converge.
