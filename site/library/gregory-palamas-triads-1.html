Triads I.3.5: The Divine Light Surpasses Knowledge

Gregory Palamas, 1296–1359 AD

Introduction

Gregory Palamas (1296–1359) is the foremost theologian of the hesychast tradition and the architect of the Orthodox distinction between God’s essence and God’s energies. Writing in response to the Calabrian philosopher Barlaam, who argued that God could not be known directly, Palamas insisted that the uncreated light experienced by monks in prayer was not a created intermediary but God himself—God as he acts, as distinct from God as he is in his inaccessible essence. The Triads in Defense of the Holy Hesychasts is his major work on this question.

Triads I.3.5 contains one of Palamas’s clearest statements on the nature of deification. The divine light, he argues, is not reducible to intellectual knowledge. It surpasses all conceptual categories because it is a direct participation in God’s own activity. This passage became foundational for later Orthodox theology’s insistence that theosis is real participation in God, not merely moral improvement or metaphorical language.

§I.3.5 · Triads I.3.5 (ed. Meyendorff)
Manuscript
Greek

Τὸ θεῖον φῶς οὐ γνῶσίς ἐστιν, οὐδὲ εἰς τὸ γένος τῆς γνώσεως ἀνήκει, ἀλλ᾽ ὑπὲρ γνῶσιν.

English

The divine light is not knowledge, nor does it belong to the category of knowledge, but it surpasses knowledge. It is not that which is known, but that by which we are transformed. For the light deifies, and deification is beyond knowledge.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.3.5: Palamas’s distinction between God’s essence (ousia, οὐσία) and God’s energies (energeiai, ἐνέργειαι) is the linchpin of his theology. The divine light that the hesychasts experience in prayer is an uncreated energy—it is truly God, but it is not God’s essence, which remains forever inaccessible. This distinction allows Palamas to affirm real deification without collapsing the Creator-creature boundary. Western theology generally did not adopt this framework, preferring instead the Thomistic concept of the lumen gloriae, a created grace that elevates the intellect to see God.
