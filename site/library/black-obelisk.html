The Black Obelisk: Israel's King Bows to Assyria

        
Babylonian Astronomers, c. 7th century BCE

        
British Museum (BM 118885)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Black Obelisk, discovered at Nimrud in 1846, is a four-sided limestone pillar covered in carved reliefs depicting the military campaigns and tribute-receiving ceremonies of the Assyrian king Shalmaneser III (858-824 BCE). Its importance for biblical studies cannot be overstated: one panel shows 'Jehu, son of Omri' (Jehu, king of Israel) or his ambassador prostrating himself before the Assyrian emperor — the only known contemporary image of any person named in the Hebrew Bible.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · black-obelisk
          
The tribute of Jehu, son of Omri: I received from him silver, gold, a golden bowl, a golden vase with pointed bottom, golden tumblers, golden buckets, tin, a staff for a king, and spears. On the carved relief panel, a bearded figure in Israelite dress prostrates himself before Shalmaneser III. Behind the kneeling figure, Israelite attendants carry tribute. This is the only surviving contemporary depiction of any king of Israel or Judah.
