Consolation of Philosophy III.10: Every Happy Person Is God

Boethius, c. 477-524 AD

Introduction

Boethius wrote The Consolation of Philosophy around 524 AD while awaiting execution in prison, having been condemned on charges of treason by the Ostrogothic king Theodoric. The work takes the form of a dialogue between Boethius and Lady Philosophy, who visits him in his cell. Book III, Prose 10 contains the argument that is most relevant to the theosis tradition: every truly happy person is God—not by nature, but by participation. Since God is happiness itself, and since happiness is the supreme good, whoever attains genuine happiness participates in divinity.

This argument became foundational for medieval theology. Thomas Aquinas cited it repeatedly. The distinction between being God “by nature” and being God “by participation” became the standard orthodox framework for discussing theosis without falling into pantheism. Boethius provided the Latin West with its philosophical vocabulary for the claim that human beings can become divine—a claim that the Greek East had been making for centuries in the language of the Church Fathers.

§III.10.1 · Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 10
Manuscript
Latin

Cum omnes beati dii sint, sed natura quidem unus, participatione vero nihil prohibet esse quam plurimos.

English

Since all who are happy are God, it follows that every happy person is God. By nature, indeed, there is one God; but by participation, nothing prevents there being as many gods as you please.

§III.10.2 · Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 10
Manuscript
Latin

Boni vero formam talem esse monstravisti ut in eo cuncta quae petuntur contineri videantur.

English

You have shown that the form of the good is such that it contains everything which is sought. But you have proved that happiness is the same as the good. Therefore happiness itself is the same as the divine.

§III.10.3 · Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 10
Manuscript
Latin

Omnes igitur boni adeptione boni boni fiunt. Qui vero boni sunt dii fiunt.

English

It follows therefore that all who are good are made so by the attainment of goodness. But those who attain goodness become God. Every good person, therefore, is God. But by nature there is only one God; by participation, however, nothing prevents there being as many as possible.

§III.10.4 · Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 10
Manuscript
Latin

Haec igitur humanae condicio beatitudinis: cum eam adeptus fueris, divinitatem adeptus es.

English

Therefore this is the condition of human happiness: that when you attain it, you attain divinity. Just as justice makes people just and wisdom makes them wise, so divinity by the same reasoning must make them divine. Every happy person is therefore divine. But while there is nothing to prevent as many as possible from being divine, God by nature is one; by participation, many.

§III.10.5 · Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 10
Manuscript
Latin

Deum vero bonum ipsum esse ac beatitudinem statuisti. Quare ipsam boni formam dei quoque esse substantiam necesse est.

English

You have also determined that God and true happiness are one and the same thing. From this we may safely conclude that the substance of God resides in the good itself and nowhere else.

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.10.1: The distinction between being God “by nature” (natura) and “by participation” (participatione) is Boethius’s most influential contribution to the theosis debate. It draws on Platonic participation theory (things are good by participating in the Form of the Good) and applies it to the question of divinity. This framework allowed the Latin West to affirm human deification while maintaining the absolute distinction between Creator and creature—a distinction that the Greek East sometimes seemed to blur.
  2. §III.10.3: The logical structure is worth noting: (1) happiness = the good; (2) the good = God; (3) therefore happiness = God; (4) therefore whoever attains happiness attains God; (5) therefore the happy person is God by participation. This is a philosophical argument, not a mystical claim. Boethius does not describe an experience of union with God; he demonstrates through logic that the structure of reality requires it. This made the argument acceptable to the Scholastics in a way that more experiential accounts of theosis sometimes were not.
