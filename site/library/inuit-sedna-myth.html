Introduction

The myth of Sedna is the central sacred narrative of Inuit religion. Sedna, a young woman cast into the sea by her father, becomes the goddess of the deep ocean and the mother of marine animals. When humans violate taboos, Sedna withholds the animals, and a shaman must journey to the sea floor to appease her.

§The Descent of Sedna · Inuit oral tradition (Central Arctic)

[Inuit oral tradition, multiple dialects]

English

Sedna clung to the edge of the boat, but her father, terrified of the storm-spirit, struck her hands with his knife. Her frozen fingers broke off and fell into the sea, becoming seals, walruses, and whales. Sedna sank to the bottom of the ocean, where she sits now, tangled hair covering her face, ruler of all sea creatures. When the people break the laws, their sins tangle in her hair, and she holds back the animals. Then the shaman must travel in spirit to the bottom of the sea, comb her hair, and confess the people’s wrongs. Only then does she release the animals, and the hunt can resume.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Descent: The Sedna myth establishes a moral ecology: the relationship between humans and animals is mediated by a divine figure who enforces reciprocity. Human transgression disrupts the flow of life from the spiritual realm; shamanic intercession restores it. The structure parallels the priestly mediation in biblical religion, where the priest enters the holy of holies to atone for the people’s sins and restore the flow of divine blessing.
