Introduction

At Phaedo 105b–e, Socrates presents his final argument for the immortality of the soul. Since the soul is that which brings life to the body, it cannot admit death and must therefore be deathless and imperishable.

§105b–e · Plato, Phaedo 105b9–e8 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

τί δ᾽ ἂν ἓν τι ἐγγένηται σώματι ζῶν ἔσται; ψυχή

English

What is it that, when present in a body, makes it living? The soul. And does the soul admit the opposite of what it always brings? Never. The soul does not admit death. Therefore the soul is deathless. And if the deathless is also imperishable, then the soul is imperishable.

Translator's Notes

  1. §105b–e: This argument from contraries—that the soul, as the principle of life, cannot admit death—was widely discussed by ancient commentators. Aristotle found it unconvincing, but the Neoplatonists accepted it as demonstrating the soul’s kinship with the eternal Forms.
