Mandukya Upanishad: AUM and the Four States

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The Mandukya Upanishad is the shortest of the principal Upanishads—only twelve verses—yet the philosopher Gaudapada and his student Shankara considered it sufficient, by itself, to convey the entire teaching of Vedanta. Its subject is the syllable AUM (oṃ) and its relationship to the four states of consciousness: waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and the “fourth” (turīya), which is not a state at all but the silent ground from which the other three arise.

The Mandukya’s theosis claim is structural rather than narrative. It does not tell a story of humans becoming divine. Instead, it shows that the Self (ātman) that experiences waking, dreaming, and deep sleep is identical with Brahman. The waking self perceives the external world. The dreaming self perceives internal impressions. In deep sleep, the self rests in undifferentiated consciousness. But turīya—the fourth—is none of these. It is “not inwardly cognitive, not outwardly cognitive, not cognitive in both ways, not a mass of cognition, not cognitive, not non-cognitive.” It is simply the Self as it truly is, prior to all experience. To realize this is liberation.

§Verse 1 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

oṃ ity etad akṣaram idaṃ sarvam tasyopavykhyanam bhūtam bhavad bhaviṣyad iti sarvam oṃkāra eva yac cānyat trikālātītam tad apy oṃkāra eva

English

AUM. This syllable is this whole world. Its explanation is as follows: the past, the present, the future—everything is the sound AUM. And whatever else there is beyond the three times—that too is the sound AUM.

§Verse 2 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

sarvam hy etad brahma ayam ātmā brahma so’yam ātmā catuṣpāt

English

All this is Brahman. This Self (atman) is Brahman. This Self has four quarters.

§Verse 3 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

jāgaritasthāno bahiprājñaḥ saptanga ekonaviṃśatimukhaḥ sthūlabhug vaiśvānaraḥ prathamaḥ pādaḥ

English

The first quarter is Vaishvanara, the waking state, whose field is the external world, who has seven limbs and nineteen mouths, and who experiences gross objects.

§Verse 4 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 4
Manuscript
Sanskrit

svapnasthāno’ntaḥprājñaḥ saptanga ekonaviṃśatimukhaḥ praviviktabhuk taijaso dvitīyaḥ pādaḥ

English

The second quarter is Taijasa, the dreaming state, whose field is the internal world, who has seven limbs and nineteen mouths, and who experiences subtle objects.

§Verse 5 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 5
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yatra supto na kaṃcana kāmaṃ kāmayate na kaṃcana svapnaṃ paśyati tat suṣuptam suṣuptasthāna ekībhūtaḥ prajñānaghana evānandamayo hy ānandabhuk ceto mukhaḥ prājñas tṛtīyaḥ pādaḥ

English

Where the sleeper desires no desires and sees no dreams—that is deep sleep. The third quarter is Prajna, the deep sleep state, who has become one, who is a mass of consciousness, who is full of bliss, who truly enjoys bliss, whose mouth is thought.

§Verse 6 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 6
Manuscript
Sanskrit

eṣa sarveśvara eṣa sarvajña eṣo’ntaryāmy eṣa yoniḥ sarvasya prabhavāpyayau hi bhūtānām

English

This is the lord of all. This is the knower of all. This is the inner controller. This is the source of all, for it is the origin and the end of all beings.

§Verse 7 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 7
Manuscript
Sanskrit

nāntaḥprajñaṃ na bahiprājñaṃ nobhayataḥprājñaṃ na prajñānaghanaṃ na prajñaṃ nāprajñam adṛṣṭam avyavahāryam agrāhyam alakṣaṇam acintyam avyapadeśyam ekātmapratyayasarāṃ prapañcopaśamaṃ śāntam śivam advaitam caturthaṃ manyante sa ātmā sa vijñeyaḥ

English

Not inwardly cognitive, not outwardly cognitive, not cognitive in both ways, not a mass of cognition, not cognitive, not non-cognitive. Unseen, beyond all dealings, ungraspable, without distinguishing marks, unthinkable, indescribable, its essence the firm conviction of the oneness of itself, the cessation of the manifold world, peaceful, auspicious, without a second—that is the fourth (turiya). That is the Self. That is to be known.

§Verse 8 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 8
Manuscript
Sanskrit

so’yam ātmādhyakṣaram oṃkāro’dhimātraṃ pādā mātrā mātrāś ca pādā akāra ukāro makāra iti

English

That same Self, considered from the standpoint of the syllable, is AUM. Considered from the standpoint of the letters, the quarters of the Self are the letters of AUM, and the letters are the quarters: A, U, M.

§Verse 9 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 9
Manuscript
Sanskrit

jāgaritasthāno vaiśvānaro’kāraḥ prathamā mātrāpter ādimatvad vāpnoti ha vai sarvān kāmān ādiś ca bhavati ya evaṃ veda

English

The waking state, Vaishvanara, is the letter A, the first element, from apti (obtaining) or from adimatva (being first). He who knows this obtains all desires and becomes first.

§Verse 10 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 10
Manuscript
Sanskrit

svapnasthānas taijasa ukāro dvitīyā mātrōtkarṣād ubhayatvād vōtkarṣati ha vai jñānasantatim samānaś ca bhavati nāsyābrahmavit kule bhavati ya evaṃ veda

English

The dreaming state, Taijasa, is the letter U, the second element, from utkarsha (exaltation) or from ubhayatva (being in between). He who knows this exalts the stream of knowledge and becomes equal. In his family, no one ignorant of Brahman will be born.

§Verse 11 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 11
Manuscript
Sanskrit

suṣuptasthānaḥ prājño makāras tṛtīyā mātrā miter apīter vā minoti ha vā idaṃ sarvam apītiś ca bhavati ya evaṃ veda

English

The deep sleep state, Prajna, is the letter M, the third element, from miti (measuring) or from apiti (merging). He who knows this measures all this and becomes the place into which all merges.

§Verse 12 · Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 12
Manuscript
Sanskrit

amātraś caturthaḥ avyavahāryaḥ prapañcopaśamaṃ śivam advaitam evam oṃkāra ātmaiva saṃviśaty ātmānam ātmanā ya evaṃ veda ya evaṃ veda

English

The fourth (turiya) is without a letter. It is beyond all dealings, the cessation of the manifold world, auspicious, without a second. Thus AUM is the Self indeed. He who knows this enters the Self through the Self.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Verse 2: The declaration ayam ātmā brahma (“This Self is Brahman”) is the third of the four mahāvākyas. It does not say the Self resembles Brahman, or reflects Brahman, or participates in Brahman. It says the Self is Brahman. The identity is total.
  2. §Verse 7: The description of turīya proceeds entirely by negation: not this, not that, not cognitive, not non-cognitive. This apophatic method—defining the absolute by what it is not—parallels the via negativa of Christian mystical theology (Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart) and the neti neti (“not this, not this”) method found elsewhere in the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad. When all categories of experience have been stripped away, what remains is not nothing but the Self as it truly is.
  3. §Verse 12: The final phrase—saṃviśaty ātmānam ātmanā, “he enters the Self through the Self”—is the Mandukya’s version of theosis. There is no external saviour, no grace from above, no ritual required. The Self realizes its own nature, and that realization is identical with liberation. The repetition of ya evaṃ veda (“he who knows this”) at the very end emphasizes that knowledge—not faith, not works—is the means.
