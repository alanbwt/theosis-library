Sumerian King List: When Kingship Descended from Heaven

Anonymous (Sumerian), c. 2600 BCE

Introduction

The Sumerian King List opens with one of the most consequential theological claims in ancient literature: “When kingship descended from heaven, kingship was in Eridu.” With this single line, the boundary between divine and human authority is erased. Kingship is not a human invention; it is a divine institution that literally fell from the sky. The kings who wield it are not merely powerful men—they are conduits of a heavenly force.

The text survives in multiple copies, the most complete being the Weld-Blundell Prism (c. 1800 BCE) now in the Ashmolean Museum. The list enumerates kings from before the Flood through historical dynasties, blending myth and chronicle without distinction. The antediluvian kings reign for tens of thousands of years; after the Flood, the reigns shorten to human scale. The theological implication is that the Flood did not merely destroy life—it diminished the divine element in kingship itself, pushing humanity further from its heavenly origin.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Column i, lines 1–14: The phrase “namship descended from heaven” (nam-lugal an-ta ed-de-a-ba) uses the verb ed, “to descend,” the same verb used for divine beings coming down to earth. Kingship is treated as a me—one of the fundamental divine powers or cosmic principles that the gods hold and occasionally grant to humanity. The city of Eridu, the oldest city in Sumerian tradition, is associated with the god Enki (Ea), the god of wisdom and the same deity who features in the Adapa myth. The convergence is not accidental: the first city, the first king, and the first sage all emerge from the same place where heaven touched earth.
  2. §Column ii, lines 1–5: The Flood marks the great rupture. Before it, kings reign for fantastical periods—28,800 years, 43,200 years—numbers that are multiples of 3,600 (the Sumerian shar, a fundamental unit of large numbers). After the Flood, reign lengths gradually approach historical plausibility. The theological implication: the Flood did not just destroy life; it reduced the divine charge in human kingship. The antediluvian kings were semi-divine; the postdiluvian kings are merely human with a divine mandate. This is a narrative of progressive de-divinisation—the mirror image of the theosis tradition’s claim that humanity can move in the opposite direction.
