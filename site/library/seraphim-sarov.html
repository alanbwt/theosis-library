Conversation with Motovilov: The Holy Spirit Testimony

Seraphim of Sarov, 1754-1833 AD

Introduction

Seraphim of Sarov (1754–1833) is one of the most beloved saints in Russian Orthodoxy. After decades of solitary ascetic life in the forests near the Sarov Monastery, he emerged as a spiritual counsellor of extraordinary radiance. His conversation with Nicholas Motovilov, recorded in 1831 and published in 1903, is the most famous modern testimony to the experience of theosis.

The conversation took place on a snowy November day in the forest. Motovilov asked Seraphim what the true purpose of the Christian life was. Seraphim answered that it was the acquisition of the Holy Spirit of God, and then—at Motovilov’s continued questioning—demonstrated the answer by allowing the grace within him to become visible. Motovilov described seeing Seraphim’s face become brighter than the sun, feeling an extraordinary warmth and fragrance, and experiencing a joy and peace beyond anything he had known. The account is remarkable because it is not ancient hagiography but a near-contemporary report, and because Seraphim insisted that this experience was not extraordinary but the normal, intended state of every Christian.

Молитва, пост, бдение и всякие другие дела христианские, сколько ни хороши они сами по себе

English

“Prayer, fasting, vigils, and all the other Christian practices, however good they may be in themselves, do not constitute the aim of our Christian life, although they serve as the indispensable means of reaching this aim. The true aim of our Christian life consists in the acquisition of the Holy Spirit of God.”

§On the Holy Spirit · Motovilov MS
Manuscript
Russian

Но, батюшка, — сказал я, — как же я могу знать, что Дух Святый со мною?

English

“But, Father Seraphim,” I said, “how can I know whether the Holy Spirit is with me or not?” Father Seraphim answered: “It is very simple, your Godliness. The Lord said: ‘Everything is simple for those who acquire understanding.’ The trouble is that we do not seek the grace of God, and in our pride of mind we do not allow it to enter our souls.”

§The Transfiguration · Motovilov MS
Manuscript
Russian

Тогда о. Серафим взял меня весьма крепко за плечи и сказал мне

English

Then Father Seraphim took me firmly by the shoulders and said: “We are both now in the Spirit of God, my friend. Why do you not look at me?” I replied: “I cannot look, Father, because your face has become brighter than the sun, and my eyes ache with pain.”

§The Light · Motovilov MS
Manuscript
Russian

Не устрашайтесь, ваше Боголюбие. Вы сами теперь так же светлы, как и я.

English

Father Seraphim said: “Do not be afraid, your Godliness. You too are now shining like me. You are now yourself in the fullness of the Spirit of God; otherwise you would not be able to see me as you do.” And bowing my head, I looked and was seized with holy fear. Imagine the face of a man who speaks to you from the centre of the sun, from the blinding light of its midday rays.

§The Fragrance and Warmth · Motovilov MS
Manuscript
Russian

Что же вы чувствуете? — спросил о. Серафим.

English

“What do you feel?” asked Father Seraphim. “An extraordinary well-being,” I said. “But what kind of well-being?” “I feel such a calm, such peace in my soul, that I can find no words to express it.” “What else do you feel?” “An extraordinary joy in my whole heart.” “What else?” “An extraordinary warmth.” “But we are in the forest, sitting in the snow. How can you feel warmth?” “The kind of warmth you feel in a bathhouse, when they pour water on the heated stones.” “And the fragrance?” “There is nothing on earth like this fragrance.”

§Conclusion · Motovilov MS
Manuscript
Russian

Запомните это, ваше Боголюбие, и крепко запомните.

English

Father Seraphim said: “Remember this, your Godliness, and remember it well: this state in which we both now find ourselves is what the Lord meant when he said, ‘The kingdom of God is within you.’ This is the grace of the Holy Spirit. This is what all the Fathers and the saints were speaking of when they described the aim and purpose of the Christian life. And the Lord has been pleased to grant this to you, a sinner, so that you may bear witness to others.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Opening: Seraphim’s distinction between the “means” (prayer, fasting, vigils) and the “aim” (acquisition of the Holy Spirit) echoes Evagrius Ponticus and John Cassian. The entire tradition insists that ascetic practices are instrumental, not ends in themselves. The danger of mistaking the means for the end—of becoming a rigorous faster who never encounters God—is one of the oldest warnings in monastic literature.
  2. §The Light: Seraphim’s statement that Motovilov is “also shining” is theologically significant: the uncreated light is not visible only in the saint but in the person who witnesses it. Motovilov must himself be in the Spirit to perceive the Spirit in Seraphim. This mutuality—the light is shared, not observed from outside—is characteristic of the Orthodox understanding of theosis as communal and participatory.
  3. §The Fragrance and Warmth: The sensory details—warmth in the snow, fragrance in the winter forest—correspond exactly to the patristic descriptions of the experience of the Holy Spirit. The warmth echoes the “fire” imagery of the Pentecost narrative (Acts 2:3); the fragrance echoes 2 Corinthians 2:15 (“we are the aroma of Christ to God”) and the anointing imagery of the chrismation rite. Seraphim’s insistence on the bodily, sensory nature of the experience is anti-Gnostic: the body participates in theosis, not only the mind.
