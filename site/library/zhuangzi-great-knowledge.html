Zhuangzi 6: The True Person Breathes from the Heels

Chuang Tzu, c. 369-286 BCE

Introduction

Chapter 6 of the Zhuangzi, titled “The Great Ancestral Teacher” (Da Zong Shi, 大宗師), describes the Taoist sage or “True Person” (zhenren, 真人) who has achieved complete harmony with the Tao. Attributed to Zhuangzi (c. 369–286 BCE), the text is the most important Taoist account of what it means for a human being to transcend ordinary consciousness and live from the source of all things.

The True Person does not struggle against nature but moves with it. The description of one who “breathe from the heels”—drawing life from the deepest possible ground—is an image of total integration: body, breath, and spirit unified in the Tao. This chapter represents the Taoist answer to the theosis question: perfection is not departure from the natural world but complete immersion in its deepest pattern.

§6.1 · Zhuangzi, ch. 6 (Guo Xiang recension)
Manuscript
Chinese

古之真人，不逆寡，不雄成，不謨士。若然者，過而弗悔，當而不自得也。若然者，登高不慄，入水不濡，入火不熱。是知之能登假於道者也若此。

English

The True Person of old did not oppose the few, did not boast of accomplishments, did not scheme about affairs. Being like this, he could miss the mark without regret and hit it without self-satisfaction. Being like this, he could climb the heights without trembling, enter water without getting wet, enter fire without feeling hot. His knowledge was able to ascend to the Tao—such was his level.

§6.2 · Zhuangzi, ch. 6
Manuscript
Chinese

古之真人，其寢不夢，其覺無憂，其食不甘，其息深深。真人之息以踵，眾人之息以喉。屈服者，其嗝言若哇。其者欲深者，其天機淺。

English

The True Person of old slept without dreaming and woke without anxiety. His food was plain and his breathing was deep. The True Person breathes from the heels; the common person breathes from the throat. The subservient gurgle out their words as if retching. Those whose desires run deep have a shallow wellspring of heaven.

§6.4 · Zhuangzi, ch. 6
Manuscript
Chinese

古之真人，不知說生，不知惡死。其出不欣，其入不距。億然而往，億然而來而已矣。不忘其所始，不求其所終。受而喜之，忘而復之。是之謂不以心损道，不以人助天。是之謂真人。

English

The True Person of old did not know to love life or to hate death. He emerged without delight and returned without resistance. He came casually and went casually, and that was all. He did not forget where he began, and he did not seek where he would end. He received his body and rejoiced in it; he forgot it and returned to the source. This is called not using the mind to oppose the Tao, not using the human to assist heaven. This is called the True Person.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6.2: The image of “breathing from the heels” became foundational in Taoist cultivation practices, including qigong and internal alchemy (neidan). It describes not a literal physical practice but a state in which the breath—and thus life-force (qi)—is drawn from the deepest possible source. The contrast with the common person who “breathes from the throat” describes a life lived from the surface rather than the depths.
  2. §6.4: The phrase “not using the mind to oppose the Tao, not using the human to assist heaven” encapsulates the Taoist concept of wu wei (non-action, effortless action). The True Person does not strive toward perfection; perfection arises naturally when striving ceases. This stands in productive tension with the ascetical effort emphasised in Christian theosis, while sharing the ultimate insight that union with the divine cannot be achieved by force of will alone.
