Introduction

Athenagoras’s Legatio pro Christianis (c. 177 CE) is a philosophically sophisticated petition addressed to Emperor Marcus Aurelius. Athenagoras argues against the charges of atheism leveled at Christians, using Platonic philosophy to defend monotheism.

§Chapter 10 · Athenagoras, Legatio 10
Manuscript
Greek

Εἰ μὲν μόνον ἔλεγον θεὸν εἶναι μὴ ἔχοντες ἀποδείξεις, εἶχεν ἄν τις ἐναντιοῦσθαι

English

If we merely said that God is one, without having proofs at our disposal, anyone might object. But since poets, philosophers, and writers in general agree that there is one God, and since we are able to demonstrate it more clearly than they, should we not rightly be freed from the charge of atheism?

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 10: Athenagoras’s rhetorical strategy is to turn the charge of atheism back on the accusers: if the best pagan philosophers already acknowledged one God, then Christians are simply more consistent monotheists. His appeal to Marcus Aurelius, a Stoic philosopher-emperor, was strategically calibrated.
