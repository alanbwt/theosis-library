Introduction

Tatian’s Address to the Greeks (c. 165 CE) is among the most combative early Christian apologetic works. A student of Justin Martyr, Tatian rejects Greek culture wholesale, arguing that everything valuable in Greek philosophy was stolen from older “barbarian” traditions.

§Chapter 1 · Tatian, Oratio ad Graecos 1
Manuscript
Greek

Μὴ πάνυ φιλεχθρῶς διάκεισθε πρὸς τοὺς βαρβάρους, ὦ ἕλληνες, μηδὲ τοῖς δόγμασιν αὐτῶν βασκάνως ἐπίκεισθε

English

Be not disposed, O Greeks, to be hostile to the barbarians, nor look with ill will on their opinions. For which of your institutions has not been derived from the barbarians? Do not on all occasions accuse us of speaking ill, but examine your own heritage.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: Tatian’s “barbarian philosophy” argument—that Greek learning derives from older Near Eastern sources—was widely adopted by later Christian apologists. Modern scholarship has partially vindicated the claim: Greek philosophy did absorb significant Egyptian, Babylonian, and Phoenician influences.
