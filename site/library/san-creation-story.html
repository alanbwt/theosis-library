Introduction

The San (Bushmen) of southern Africa preserve one of the oldest continuous oral traditions on earth. Their creation narratives center on /Kaggen (the Mantis), a trickster-creator figure who shaped the world through dreams, cunning, and transformation.

Translation

§The Mantis and the First People · San Oral Tradition, /Xam narratives recorded by Bleek and Lloyd

/Kaggen the Mantis dreamed the world into being. He made the eland, the most beautiful of animals, from a shoe and honey. He fed it in secret by a pool, singing to it as it grew. When the other beings discovered and killed the eland, /Kaggen wept. But from the eland's blood, scattered across the ground, came all the other game animals of the world. From destruction came abundance. /Kaggen said: "I will not always be here in this form. I will be in the wind. I will be in the falling rain."

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Mantis and the First People: The /Xam San narratives were recorded in the 1870s by Wilhelm Bleek and Lucy Lloyd from some of the last speakers of the /Xam language. /Kaggen's role as both creator and trickster—simultaneously sacred and comic—resists the Western distinction between theology and folklore. The eland held profound spiritual significance for the San, featuring prominently in their rock art dating back thousands of years.
