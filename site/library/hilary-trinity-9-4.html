De Trinitate IX.4: Born as God

Hilary of Poitiers, c. 310–367 AD

Introduction

Hilary of Poitiers (c. 310–367), known as the “Athanasius of the West,” was the foremost Latin defender of Nicene orthodoxy against Arianism. His De Trinitate is the first major Latin treatise on the Trinity and represents Hilary’s effort to bring the sophisticated Greek theological debates of the East to a Western audience that had largely been insulated from them during his exile.

Book IX, Chapter 4 contains one of the most striking exchange formulas in Latin patristic literature. Hilary states the logic of the Incarnation with lapidary brevity: God was born as man so that man might be born as God. The symmetry is deliberate and theologically precise. For Hilary, the Incarnation is not merely a rescue mission but a transformation of human nature from within, accomplished by God’s assumption of what is ours so that we might receive what is his.

§IX.4 · De Trinitate IX.4 (CCSL 62A)
Manuscript
Latin

Deus homo natus est, ut homo Deus nasceretur. Hoc est enim magni et arcani consilii sacramentum, ut qui Deus est fieret quod homo est, ut homo fieret quod Deus est.

English

God was born as man so that man might be born as God. For this is the mystery of the great and hidden counsel: that he who is God might become what man is, so that man might become what God is.

Translator's Notes

  1. §IX.4: Hilary’s formula Deus homo natus est, ut homo Deus nasceretur is among the most compressed statements of the exchange principle in all of patristic literature. The verb nasceretur (“might be born”) is carefully chosen: Hilary conceives of deification not as a gradual moral ascent but as a new birth, paralleling the birth of God in human flesh. The phrase magni et arcani consilii sacramentum (“the mystery of the great and hidden counsel”) alludes to Isaiah 9:6 (LXX: “counsellor of the great counsel”) and frames the Incarnation as the unfolding of an eternal divine plan, not an emergency response to human sin.
