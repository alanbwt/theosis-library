The Nicene Creed (Niceno-Constantinopolitan, 381 AD)

Σύμβολον τῆς Νικαίας
Council of Nicaea / Constantinople, 325 / 381 AD
Acts of the Council of Constantinople (381 AD) · Denzinger, H. (ed.), Enchiridion Symbolorum, 43rd ed., San Francisco: Ignatius Press, 2012

Introduction

The Nicene Creed is the formal resolution of the first great theological crisis in Christianity. It defines Christ as homoousios (ὁμοούσιος, of one substance) with the Father, settling—for the orthodox—the question of whether divinity is shared or exclusive. For the theosis tradition, the Creed is both foundation and constraint: it affirms Christ’s full divinity (necessary for theosis to work) while drawing a sharp line between Creator and creation.

The text presented here is the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed of 381 AD, the expanded form adopted at the First Council of Constantinople and affirmed at Chalcedon in 451. It builds on the shorter creed issued at Nicaea in 325, adding the full article on the Holy Spirit and the clauses on the Church, baptism, and resurrection. This is the creed that remains normative for Eastern Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and most Protestant churches. The Greek text follows the 381 version without the later Western filioque addition.

English

And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the only-begotten Son of God, begotten of the Father before all ages; light from light, true God from true God; begotten, not made, of one substance (homoousios, ὁμοούσιος) with the Father, through whom all things were made. Who for us humans and for our salvation came down from the heavens and was incarnate of the Holy Spirit and the Virgin Mary, and became man. He was crucified for us under Pontius Pilate, and suffered, and was buried. And on the third day he rose again according to the Scriptures. And ascended into the heavens and sits at the right hand of the Father. And he shall come again with glory to judge the living and the dead, of whose kingdom there shall be no end.

§III. The Spirit, Church, and Resurrection · Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed (381 AD), articles on the Spirit, Church, and eschatology
Manuscript
Greek

Καὶ εἰς τὸ Πνεῦμα τὸ Ἅγιον, τὸ Κύριον, τὸ ζωοποιόν, τὸ ἐκ τοῦ Πατρὸς ἐκπορευόμενον, τὸ σὺν Πατρὶ καὶ Υἱῷ συμπροσκυνούμενον καὶ συνδοξαζόμενον, τὸ λαλῆσαν διὰ τῶν προφητῶν· εἰς μίαν, ἁγίαν, καθολικὴν καὶ ἀποστολικὴν Ἐκκλησίαν· ὁμολογοῦμεν ἓν βάπτισμα εἰς ἄφεσιν ἁμαρτιῶν· προσδοκῶμεν ἀνάστασιν νεκρῶν καὶ ζωὴν τοῦ μέλλοντος αἰῶνος. Ἀμήν.

English

And in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life, who proceeds from the Father, who together with the Father and the Son is worshipped and glorified, who spoke through the prophets. In one, holy, catholic, and apostolic Church. We confess one baptism for the remission of sins. We look forward to the resurrection of the dead and the life of the age to come. Amen.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II. The Son: The term homoousios (ὁμοούσιος, “of one substance” or “consubstantial”) is the creed’s most consequential word and the one most directly relevant to theosis. The Arian position held that the Son was homoiousios (ὁμοιούσιος, “of similar substance”)—like the Father but not identical in being. The difference is a single iota, but the theological stakes are absolute. Athanasius argued that if Christ is not fully God—not homoousios with the Father—then the Incarnation cannot deify humanity: “He became man that we might become god” (De Incarnatione 54.3) works only if the one who became man was truly God. A created intermediary, however exalted, cannot bridge the gap between Creator and creation. The entire logic of theosis depends on the homoousios: God must become fully human so that humans can participate fully in God. This is why the Cappadocian Fathers—Basil, Gregory of Nazianzus, Gregory of Nyssa—defended the Nicene formula so fiercely. It was not an abstract metaphysical debate; it was a soteriological one. Get the Christology wrong and salvation by deification collapses.
  2. §II. The Son: The phrase “light from light, true God from true God” (φῶς ἐκ φωτός, Θεὸν ἀληθινὸν ἐκ Θεοῦ ἀληθινοῦ) uses the language of emanation rather than manufacture. Light proceeding from light does not diminish the source; the beam is the same substance as the sun. This metaphor was deliberately chosen to counter the Arian claim that the Son was “made” (ποιηθέντα)—hence the creed’s emphatic “begotten, not made” (γεννηθέντα, οὐ ποιηθέντα). The emanation language matters for theosis because it establishes a model of divine communication that does not involve loss or division. If divinity can be shared from Father to Son without being diminished, then in principle divinity can be shared from God to humanity without being diminished either. Gregory of Nazianzus develops this point explicitly: the Spirit proceeds, the Son is begotten, and humanity is deified—all by the same logic of divine self-communication that the light metaphor encodes. The Neoplatonic background is unmistakable: Plotinus used the same image of light emanating from its source to describe the procession of being from the One. The Nicene Fathers baptized the metaphor.
