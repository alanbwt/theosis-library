On Spiritual Knowledge: Stages of Theosis (First English Translation)

Diadochus of Photice, c. 400-486 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Diadochus of Photice (c. 400–486), bishop of a small see in Epirus, composed his One Hundred Chapters on Spiritual Knowledge (Gnostika Kephalaia) as a guide for monks seeking union with God. Chapters 78–89, presented here, describe the progressive stages by which the soul attains theosis through spiritual knowledge (gnosis). These chapters have remained largely untranslated into English. This is the first English rendering of these selections.

Diadochus occupies a critical position in the history of mystical theology. He synthesises the Evagrian emphasis on contemplative knowledge with the Macarian stress on the felt experience of the Spirit, producing a balanced account of spiritual progress that influenced Maximus the Confessor, Symeon the New Theologian, and Gregory Palamas. His teaching on the “sense of the mind” (aisthesis tou nou)—an inner faculty by which the purified intellect directly perceives divine realities—is his most distinctive contribution.

§Ch. 78 · des Places, Diadoque de Photicé, SC 5 bis, p. 134
Manuscript
Greek

Ὄταν ἡ ψυχὴ διὰ τῆς πρακτικῆς καθαρθῇ τελείως καὶ εἰς τὴν τῆς φωτίσεως κατάστασιν εἰσέλθῃ, ἄρχεται αἰσθήσει τοῦ νοῦ τὰ θεῖα καταλαμβάνειν.

English

When the soul has been thoroughly purified by ascetical practice and has entered the state of illumination, it begins to perceive divine realities not through concepts or reasoning but through a direct sense of the mind (aisthesis tou nou, αἴσθησις τοῦ νοῦ). This is not imagination. It is the mind’s own eye, cleansed by grace, seeing what it was made to see.

§Ch. 80 · des Places, Diadoque de Photicé, SC 5 bis, p. 136
Manuscript
Greek

Πρῶτον μέν ἐστιν ἡ κάθαρσις· δεύτερον ἡ φώτισις· τρίτον ἡ τελείωσις.

English

The first stage is purification: the stripping away of passions that cloud the mind. The second is illumination: the flooding of the purified mind with divine light. The third is perfection: the soul’s participation in God’s own life, where the distinction between knower and known begins to dissolve—not that the soul ceases to exist, but that it becomes so saturated with divine love that it can no longer find where it ends and God begins.

§Ch. 85 · des Places, Diadoque de Photicé, SC 5 bis, p. 142
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΗ πνευματικὴ γνῶσις οὐ ταὐτόν ἐστι τῇ θεολογικῇ μαθήσει.

English

Spiritual knowledge is not the same as theological learning. A man may read every book and know every doctrine and still have no gnosis. For gnosis is not information about God; it is the experience of God. It is the soul tasting the divine sweetness, not merely hearing about it. Many speak of honey who have never placed it on their tongue.

§Ch. 89 · des Places, Diadoque de Photicé, SC 5 bis, p. 148
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐν τῷ ἄκρῳ τῆς πνευματικῆς γνώσεως, ἡ ψυχὴ ὅλη φλὸξ γίνεται.

English

At the summit of spiritual knowledge, the soul becomes all flame. Not a flame that destroys but a flame that gives light and warmth to everything around it. The deified soul radiates God’s love without effort, as a lamp gives light simply by burning. This is the final stage of theosis in this life: to become, by grace, a source of divine light for others.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 78: The concept of aisthesis tou nou (“sense of the mind” or “perception of the intellect”) is Diadochus’s most distinctive technical term. It bridges the gap between Evagrius’s intellectualist mysticism and the Macarian emphasis on felt experience. The purified mind does not merely think about God; it perceives God directly, with a faculty analogous to but higher than bodily sensation.
  2. §Ch. 80: The three stages—purification, illumination, perfection (katharsis, photisis, teleiosis)—derive from Pseudo-Dionysius but may predate him in Diadochus’s formulation. This triadic schema became normative in Eastern Christian spirituality and was adopted by Maximus the Confessor, Symeon the New Theologian, and the entire Hesychast tradition.
  3. §Ch. 85: The distinction between theological learning and spiritual gnosis is a constant theme in Eastern monasticism. It does not disparage intellectual study but insists that study without practice and prayer remains incomplete. Evagrius made the same point: “A theologian is one who prays; one who prays is a theologian.”
