Introduction

Revelation 20:1–15 describes the binding of Satan for a thousand years, the first resurrection, and the final judgment before the great white throne. The “millennium” passage has generated more interpretive controversy than perhaps any other text in Scripture. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§20:1–3 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

και ειδον αγγελον καταβαινοντα εκ του ουρανου

English

Then I saw an angel coming down from heaven, holding in his hand the key to the bottomless pit and a great chain. And he seized the dragon, that ancient serpent, who is the devil and Satan, and bound him for a thousand years, and threw him into the pit, and shut it and sealed it over him, so that he might not deceive the nations any longer, until the thousand years were ended.

§20:11–15 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

και ειδον θρονον μεγαν λευκον και τον καθημενον επ αυτον

English

Then I saw a great white throne and him who was seated on it. From his presence earth and sky fled away, and no place was found for them. And I saw the dead, great and small, standing before the throne, and books were opened. Then another book was opened, which is the book of life. And the dead were judged by what was written in the books, according to what they had done.

Translator's Notes

  1. §20:2: The “thousand years” (χίλια ετη) generated three major interpretive traditions: premillennialism (Christ returns before the millennium), postmillennialism (Christ returns after), and amillennialism (the thousand years is symbolic of the present church age). Augustine’s amillennial reading in City of God became dominant in the West.
