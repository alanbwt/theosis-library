The Rock Edicts of Ashoka: A King Renounces Violence

        
The Rock Edicts of Ashoka: A King Renounces Violence

        
Emperor Ashoka, 304-232 BCE

        
Rock inscriptions across India, Pakistan, Afghanistan

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
Ashoka's Rock Edicts, carved into cliff faces and pillars across the Indian subcontinent around 260 BCE, are the earliest surviving Indian inscriptions and the first written records of Buddhism. They are remarkable for their content: a powerful emperor publicly confessing remorse for violence and pledging to govern by dharma (moral law) rather than force.
The edicts were written in Prakrit using the Brahmi script, which was itself only deciphered in 1837 by James Prinsep. Before that decipherment, Ashoka was a forgotten figure — now recognized as one of the most important rulers in world history. His edicts proclaim religious tolerance, non-violence, respect for all living beings, and the establishment of hospitals for both humans and animals.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · ashoka-rock-edicts
          
Beloved-of-the-Gods, King Piyadasi, conquered the Kalingas eight years after his coronation. One hundred and fifty thousand were deported, one hundred thousand were killed, and many more died from other causes. After the Kalingas had been conquered, Beloved-of-the-Gods came to feel a strong inclination toward the Dharma, a love for the Dharma, and instruction in the Dharma. Now Beloved-of-the-Gods feels deep remorse for having conquered the Kalingas. The conquest of a country that has not been conquered involves killing, death, and deportation of people, and this is a matter of profound sorrow and regret to Beloved-of-the-Gods. Today, if a hundredth or a thousandth part of the people who were killed, died, or were deported during the conquest of Kalinga were to suffer the same fate, it would weigh heavily on the mind of Beloved-of-the-Gods.
