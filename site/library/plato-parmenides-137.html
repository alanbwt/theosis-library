Introduction

At Parmenides 137c–142a, the first hypothesis explores what follows if the One is absolutely one. The conclusion is that the One cannot be named, spoken of, or known—a result the Neoplatonists took as Plato’s deepest teaching about the ultimate principle.

§137c–d · Plato, Parmenides 137c4–d3 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ ἕν ἐστιν, ἄλλο τι οὐκ ἂν εἴη πολλὰ τὸ ἕν

English

If the One is one, it would not be many. Therefore it cannot have parts, nor can it be a whole. If it has no parts, it has no beginning, middle, or end. It is therefore unlimited. And if unlimited, it has no shape. It is neither in itself nor in another.

Translator's Notes

  1. §137c–d: The first hypothesis of the Parmenides became the cornerstone of Neoplatonic negative theology. Plotinus identified the One of the first hypothesis with the transcendent first principle, beyond being and beyond thought. This reading shaped all subsequent apophatic theology.
