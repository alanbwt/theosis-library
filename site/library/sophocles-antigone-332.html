Introduction

The “Ode to Man” (Antigone 332–375) is the most famous choral ode in Greek tragedy, celebrating humanity’s astonishing capacities while warning that these same powers can lead to ruin. It defines the human as deinon—wonderful and terrible at once.

§Lines 332–375 · Sophocles, Antigone 332–375
Manuscript
Greek

πολλὰ τὰ δεινὰ κοὐδὲν ἀνθρώπου δεινότερον πέλει

English

Many are the wonders, and none is more wonderful than man. He crosses the sea in winter’s storms; he wears away the Earth, the eldest of the gods. He has taught himself speech and wind-swift thought, and all the moods that mold a state. Resourceful in all things, he meets nothing that is to come without resource. Only against death shall he call for aid in vain. But possessing skill beyond imagining, he moves now to evil, now to good.

Translator's Notes

  1. §332: The word deinon (δεινόν) means simultaneously “wonderful,” “terrible,” and “strange”—no single English word captures it. Heidegger devoted extensive analysis to this ode, arguing it expresses the essential uncanniness of human existence as the being that does not fit into the natural order.
