Introduction

The Samkhya Karika of Ishvarakrishna (c. 350 CE) is the foundational text of the Samkhya system, one of the six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy. It presents a dualistic cosmology of consciousness (purusha) and matter (prakriti).

§Karikas 1–3 · Ishvarakrishna, Samkhya Karika 1–3
Manuscript
Greek

दुःखत्रयाभिघातात् जिज्ञासा

English

From the torment of threefold suffering arises the inquiry into the means of removing it. The manifest, the unmanifest, and the knower—these are the three fundamental principles. Prakriti is uncreated. The seven principles—intellect, ego, and the five subtle elements—are both created and creative. The sixteen are only created. Purusha is neither created nor creative.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Karika 1: Samkhya’s starting point—that philosophical inquiry arises from suffering—parallels the Buddha’s First Noble Truth. Its strict dualism between consciousness (purusha) and matter (prakriti) influenced both Yoga philosophy and Buddhist Abhidharma, though Buddhism rejected the permanent self that purusha implies.
