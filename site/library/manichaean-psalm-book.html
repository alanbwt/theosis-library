Manichaean Psalm Book: I Am in Everything

Mani (attributed), 216-274 AD

Introduction

The Manichaean Psalm Book is a collection of hymns and psalms used in Manichaean worship, preserved in Coptic in the same Medinet Madi discovery that yielded the Kephalaia. The psalms are attributed to various early Manichaean teachers and are organized into cycles: Psalms of Thomas, Psalms of Heraclides, Psalms of the Bema, and others. They represent the devotional and liturgical heart of Manichaean religious life.

The psalms are among the most beautiful religious poetry of late antiquity. They express the longing of the trapped light-soul for liberation, the grief of the divine particles imprisoned in matter, and the ecstatic joy of reunion with the Light. The recurring theme is identity: “I am in everything, I am in the light”—the realization that the divine is not separate from the self but is the deepest truth of what the self actually is. This is Manichaean theosis: not becoming divine, but recognizing that one has always been divine, a fragment of the original Light temporarily lost in Darkness.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Psalm of Thomas I: The declaration “I am in everything” is spoken not by an individual but by the Living Self—the collective divine light scattered throughout creation. This is a pantheistic statement within a dualist framework: the divine is in all things not because all things are divine, but because particles of the original Light are trapped in everything material. The Manichaean project is to extract these particles and return them to their source.
  2. §Psalm of Heraclides I: The motif of the soul as a king’s daughter who has forgotten her royalty is strikingly parallel to the “Hymn of the Pearl” in the Acts of Thomas, one of the most famous Gnostic poems. Both texts present the same narrative arc: divine origin, descent into matter, forgetfulness, and awakening through a messenger from the Father. Whether the Manichaean version depends on the earlier Gnostic text or both draw on a common source is debated.
  3. §Psalm of the Wanderers: The phrase “we are your own members, scattered in the world” reflects the Manichaean doctrine that the cosmos is literally the body of the First Man, broken apart and distributed through matter. Salvation is therefore the reassembly of a divine body—a corporate theosis in which individual liberation contributes to the healing of a cosmic wound.
