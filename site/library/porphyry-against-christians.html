Against the Christians: Philosophy Vulgarized

Porphyry, c. 234-305 AD

Introduction

Porphyry of Tyre (c. 234–305 CE) was the most important student of Plotinus and the editor of the Enneads. His treatise Against the Christians (Kata Christianon), written in fifteen books around 270 CE, was the most formidable philosophical attack on Christianity produced in antiquity. The work was so dangerous that it was ordered burned by imperial decree in 448 CE; only fragments survive, preserved in the refutations of Jerome, Augustine, Eusebius, and others.

Porphyry’s critique is distinctive because he was himself a committed mystic who believed in the soul’s ascent to the divine. He did not reject theosis—he rejected the Christian version of it. For Porphyry, the philosophical path to union with the One (as taught by Plotinus) is the genuine article, and Christianity is a vulgar counterfeit that replaces intellectual discipline with faith, replaces the immaterial One with a crucified body, and opens the mysteries to the uneducated masses. Christianity does not divinize—it degrades the very concept of divinization.

§1 · Porphyry, Against the Christians, Fragment 1 (preserved in Eusebius, Praeparatio Evangelica)
Manuscript
Greek

πῶς δὲ παραδεξώμεθα ὅτι τὸ θεῖον ἔμβρυον ἐγένετο;

English

How can we accept that the divine became an embryo, that after being born he was wrapped in swaddling clothes, covered with blood from childbirth, and all the rest that is offensive even to describe? This is not worthy of God but of impious and senseless men.

§2 · Porphyry, Against the Christians, Fragment (preserved in Jerome)
Manuscript
Greek

[Latin fragment from Jerome, Comm. in Danielem, Prol.]

English

The Christians say that the Scriptures have a hidden meaning, accessible only through allegory. But if the literal text is unworthy of God, as they themselves admit, then why was it written literally in the first place? The philosophers speak plainly when they can and obscurely only when the subject demands it. The Jewish scriptures speak obscurely because their authors were confused, not because the truth is deep.

§3 · Porphyry, Against the Christians, Fragment (preserved in Augustine, De Civitate Dei XIX.23)
Manuscript
Greek

[Latin paraphrase in Augustine: universalem viam animae liberandae nondum in suam notitiam historiae receptam]

English

The way to the liberation of the soul has not yet been established by any universal path—not by any philosophy, not by the moral teachings of the Indians, not by the practice of the Chaldaeans, and not by any other way. No such universal path has yet come to my knowledge from the study of history.

§4 · Porphyry, Against the Christians, Fragment (preserved in Eusebius)
Manuscript
Greek

τὸν Χριστὸν εὐσεβῆ ἄνδρα γεγονέναι… τοὺς δὲ προσκυνοῦντας αὐτὸν ὡς θεὸν πλανᾶσθαι.

English

Christ himself was a pious man, and his soul, like the souls of other pious men, was received into heaven after death. The Christians err in worshipping him as God. One should honour Christ as a man who was dear to the gods, not as a god himself. Those who worship him as divine dishonour both philosophy and the true gods.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1: Porphyry’s disgust at the physicality of the Incarnation reveals the deep Platonic assumption that matter is degrading. For a Neoplatonist, the soul’s goal is to escape the body, not to glorify it. A god who voluntarily enters a body—who becomes an embryo, is born in blood, suffers crucifixion—is moving in exactly the wrong direction. The Christian answer (developed especially by Maximus the Confessor) was that the Incarnation does not degrade the divine but elevates the material: God enters matter precisely to transfigure it.
  2. §3: This fragment, preserved by Augustine, is one of the most remarkable in the history of philosophy. Porphyry concedes that no “universal path” for the liberation of the soul has been found—not in Greek philosophy, not in Indian wisdom, not in Chaldaean magic. Augustine seized on this passage as evidence that Porphyry himself knew his own philosophical path was insufficient, and that only Christ provides the universal way. The passage reveals Porphyry at his most honest: a mystic who recognized the limits of his own tradition.
