Isaiah 6:1-8: The Throne Vision

Ἡσαΐας
Isaiah (attributed), c. 8th c. BCE
Septuagint (LXX)

Introduction

Isaiah 6 records the most fearsome theophany in the prophetic literature. The prophet sees the Lord seated on a throne, high and lifted up, with the train of his robe filling the temple. Seraphim—fiery beings with six wings—cry out to one another the Trisagion: “Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory.” The triple repetition is unique in Hebrew rhetoric, an intensification beyond the superlative. The foundations shake. The temple fills with smoke. Isaiah is undone.

What follows is the pattern that the Church Fathers identified as the archetype of theosis: the encounter with divine holiness that first destroys and then transforms. Isaiah’s lips are unclean, and he knows it. A seraph takes a burning coal from the altar and presses it to his mouth. The impurity is burned away. Only then does Isaiah hear the divine council deliberating—“Whom shall I send?”—and only then can he answer, “Here am I; send me.” Purification precedes commission. The human cannot stand in the divine presence, let alone serve it, without first being remade by fire.

English

In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord sitting on a throne, high and lifted up, and the house was full of his glory. And seraphim stood around him; each one had six wings: with two they covered their face, and with two they covered their feet, and with two they flew. And one cried out to another and said, “Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory.” And the lintel was lifted up by the voice with which they cried out, and the house was filled with smoke.

English

And I said, “Woe is me, for I am pierced to the heart! For I am a man of unclean lips, and I dwell in the midst of a people of unclean lips, and my eyes have seen the King, the Lord of hosts!” And one of the seraphim was sent to me, and in his hand he held a burning coal, which he had taken with tongs from the altar. And he touched my mouth and said, “Behold, this has touched your lips and will take away your iniquities, and will cleanse your sins.” And I heard the voice of the Lord saying, “Whom shall I send, and who will go to this people?” And I said, “Here am I; send me.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §6:3: The Trisagion (“Thrice Holy”)—ἅγιος ἅγιος ἅγιος κύριος σαβαωθ—became one of the most important liturgical texts in Christian worship. The triple repetition was read by the Fathers as a veiled reference to the Trinity, a reading that Jewish exegetes would reject. The Hebrew sabaoth (“of hosts” or “of armies”) is transliterated rather than translated in the Septuagint, preserving its strangeness for Greek readers. The word hagios (ἅγιος) in Greek means “set apart,” “other”—holiness as radical difference from the created order.
  2. §6:5–7: Isaiah’s response to the vision is not worship but terror: “Woe is me, for I am undone.” The Septuagint uses katanenymai (κατανένυγμαι), “I am pierced” or “I am stunned.” The purification by the burning coal from the altar became a foundational image for the patristic theology of theosis: the human being cannot approach the divine without being transformed, and the transformation involves something analogous to burning. Gregory of Nazianzus and Maximus the Confessor both returned to this image repeatedly. The coal does not destroy Isaiah; it purifies him. The fire that annihilates sin is the same fire that enables communion with God.
  3. §6:8: The divine question “Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?” shifts between singular and plural. The “us” was read by rabbinic interpreters as the divine council (cf. Genesis 1:26, “Let us make”), while Christian exegetes saw in it another Trinitarian hint. John 12:41 explicitly identifies the Lord whom Isaiah saw as Christ: “Isaiah said this because he saw his glory and spoke about him.” This identification made Isaiah 6 one of the key Old Testament proof-texts for the pre-existence and divinity of the Son.
