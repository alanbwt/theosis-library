Coffin Text 330: I Am Atum, I Made Myself

Anonymous (Egyptian), c. 1550 BCE

Introduction

Coffin Text Spell 330 is one of the most extraordinary documents from ancient Egypt. Dating to the Middle Kingdom (c. 2055–1650 BCE), it records the deceased’s identification with Atum, the self-created god who brought the universe into being through his own will. The speaker declares: “I am Atum... I am the one who made myself.” This is not mere aspiration; in the Egyptian understanding, the correct recitation of these words effects the transformation it describes. To speak as Atum is to become Atum.

The Coffin Texts represent one of the earliest known traditions of theosis in human civilization. The Pyramid Texts (c. 2400 BCE) reserved this identification with the gods for the pharaoh alone; the Coffin Texts democratized it, extending the possibility of divine identification to any person of sufficient means to have the spells inscribed on their coffin. The deceased does not merely enter the company of the gods but becomes a god—participating in the act of creation itself, joining the solar barque, and ruling in the afterlife as a divine being.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Spell 330, opening: The declaration “I am Atum” uses the first person directly: the deceased does not pray to Atum but speaks as Atum. In Egyptian theology, the boundary between speech and reality is porous; the god Ptah created the world through speech, and the correct utterance of a spell effects what it describes. The deceased is not pretending to be Atum; by speaking these words in the proper ritual context, the deceased becomes Atum. This is performative theosis.
  2. §General: The Coffin Texts were painted or inscribed on the interior surfaces of wooden coffins during the Middle Kingdom. They evolved from the earlier Pyramid Texts (inscribed on the walls of royal pyramids) and later developed into the Book of the Dead (written on papyrus scrolls). The transition from Pyramid Texts to Coffin Texts represents a significant democratization of the afterlife: what was once the exclusive privilege of the pharaoh—identification with the gods and eternal life—became available to members of the provincial elite. This is one of the earliest known instances of a theosis tradition expanding beyond a priestly or royal class.
