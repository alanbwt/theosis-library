Great Hymn to the Aten: The Earliest Monotheism

Hymn to Aten
Akhenaten (attributed), c. 1353–1336 BCE

Introduction

The Great Hymn to the Aten is inscribed in the tomb of Ay at Amarna and is attributed to Pharaoh Akhenaten (r. c. 1353–1336 BCE), who abolished the traditional Egyptian pantheon in favor of exclusive worship of the Aten, the solar disk. It is the earliest known expression of something approaching monotheism in world literature, predating Deutero-Isaiah by eight centuries.

The hymn celebrates the Aten as sole creator and sustainer of all life. Its language is striking for what it excludes: no other gods are mentioned, no mythology is invoked, no afterlife is described. The entire theological focus is on the visible world as the creation and ongoing gift of a single divine power. Scholars have long noted parallels with Psalm 104, though the question of direct influence remains debated.

English

Splendid you rise in the horizon of heaven, O living Aten, creator of life! When you have dawned in the eastern horizon, you fill every land with your beauty. You are beautiful, great, radiant, high above every land. Your rays embrace the lands to the limit of all that you have made.

You are Re: you have reached their limits. You subdue them for your beloved son. Though you are far away, your rays are on earth. Though you are in the faces of men, your movements are unseen.

When you set in the western horizon, the land is in darkness, in the manner of death. They sleep in their chambers, their heads wrapped up, and no eye sees another. All their goods that are under their heads could be stolen, and they would not know it. Every lion comes out of its den. All the serpents bite. Darkness is a shroud, and the earth is in stillness, for he who made them rests in his horizon.

But when the earth brightens, and you rise in the horizon and shine as the Aten by day, you drive away the darkness and offer your rays. The Two Lands are in festivity. People awake and stand on their feet, for you have raised them up. Their bodies are washed. They take their garments. Their arms are raised in praise at your appearance. The entire land goes about its work.

English

How manifold are your works! They are hidden from the face of man, O sole god, like whom there is no other! You made the earth as you wished, you alone: all peoples, herds, and flocks. All that is on earth, that walks on legs, that rises up and flies on wings.

The foreign countries—Syria and Kush—and the land of Egypt: you set every man in his place, you make their necessities. Each one has his food, and his lifetime is reckoned. Their tongues are separate in speech, and their natures likewise. Their skins are different, for you have distinguished the foreign peoples.

You make the Nile in the underworld, and you bring it forth as you desire, to give life to the people, inasmuch as you made them for yourself, lord of all of them, who wearies himself on their behalf. O lord of every land, who rises for them, O Aten of the day, great in majesty!

All distant foreign countries, you make their life also. You have placed a Nile in heaven, that it may descend for them and make floods upon the mountains, like the great green sea, to water their fields among their towns.

How effective they are, your plans, O lord of eternity! The Nile in heaven, it is your gift to the foreign peoples and to the beasts of every desert that go upon their feet. But the true Nile comes from the underworld for Egypt.

Your rays suckle every field. When you rise, they live and grow for you. You make the seasons to nourish all that you have made: winter to cool them, and the heat of summer so they may taste you.

You are in my heart, and there is no other who knows you except your son Akhenaten. You have made him wise in your plans and in your power. The world came into being by your hand, as you made them. When you have risen, they live. When you set, they die. You yourself are lifetime. One lives by you.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Praise of the Sole God: The contrast between day and night is the central theological argument of the hymn: without the Aten, the world falls into death-like darkness. Lions and serpents emerge. People cannot see each other. The entire created order depends moment by moment on the presence of the sole god. This is not abstract monotheism but experiential theology: the sun’s rising is the daily proof of divine sustenance.
  2. §Creation by the Sole God: The passage “O sole god, like whom there is no other” (nṯr wʿ nn ky ḥr-ḫw=f) is the strongest monotheistic declaration in pre-Israelite literature. Equally remarkable is the universalism: the Aten created all peoples, distinguished their languages and skins, and provides for each nation differently—a Nile in the underworld for Egypt, rain from a “Nile in heaven” for foreign lands. The claim that “you are in my heart, and there is no other who knows you except your son Akhenaten” is both a mystical statement and a political one: the pharaoh alone mediates between the sole god and humanity. The closing formula—“When you rise, they live. When you set, they die. You yourself are lifetime”—expresses a radical dependence theology that anticipates later monotheistic formulations by centuries.
