Introduction

At Ion 533c–536d, Socrates compares poetic inspiration to a magnetic chain: the Muse inspires the poet, who inspires the performer, who moves the audience. The poet creates not by skill but by divine possession.

§533d–534b · Plato, Ion 533d3–534b6 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

κοῦφον γὰρ χρῆμα ποιητής ἐστιν καὶ πτηνὸν καὶ ἱερὸν χρῆμα

English

The poet is a light and winged and holy thing, and is unable to compose until he is inspired and out of his mind, and reason is no longer in him. For not by art do they speak these things, but by divine power. The god takes away their minds and uses them as his servants, so that we who hear them may know that it is not they who speak, but the god himself.

Translator's Notes

  1. §533d–534b: Whether Plato’s account of divine inspiration is sincere or ironic remains debated. The Neoplatonists read it as genuine: Proclus argued that the highest form of poetry is indeed god-possessed, and this reading influenced the entire tradition of “divine furor” in Renaissance thought.
