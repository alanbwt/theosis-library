Acts 17:28-29: In Him We Live and Move

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Acts 17:28–29 records the climax of Paul’s address to the Athenian philosophers on the Areopagus, where he quotes pagan poets to establish a theological point of immense consequence: “In him we live and move and have our being... we are his offspring.” The first quotation is attributed to Epimenides of Crete, the second to Aratus of Soli’s Phaenomena. Paul does not cite these poets as mere rhetorical ornament; he uses them to articulate a claim about the ontological dependence of all human beings on God.

The theosis tradition has drawn deeply on this passage. If all humanity lives and moves and exists within God, then the relationship between Creator and creature is not one of external manufacture but of intimate sustaining presence. And if human beings are God’s “offspring” (genos, γένος), then there is a kinship between the divine nature and the human that makes participation in divinity not a violation of the created order but its fulfillment. The Cappadocian Fathers, especially Gregory of Nazianzus, cited this verse to argue that the human capacity for God is built into the structure of creation itself.

English

For in him we live and move and have our being, as also some of your own poets have said, “For we are indeed his offspring (genos, γένος).”

§17:29 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

γένος οὖν ὑπάρχοντες τοῦ θεοῦ · οὐκ ὀφείλομεν νομίζειν χρυσῷ ἢ ἀργύρῳ ἢ λίθῳ · χαράγματι τέχνης καὶ ἐνθυμήσεως ἀνθρώπου · τὸ θεῖον εἶναι ὅμοιον ·

English

Being then God’s offspring, we ought not to think that the divine nature (to theion, τὸ θεῖον) is like gold or silver or stone, an image formed by the art and imagination of man.

Translator's Notes

  1. §17:28: The phrase “in him we live and move and have our being” (ἐν αὐτῷ ζῶμεν καὶ κινούμεθα καὶ ἐσμέν) is traditionally attributed to Epimenides, a sixth-century BC Cretan poet-prophet. The triadic formula—living, moving, being—ascends from biological existence to kinetic activity to ontological foundation. All three are located “in him” (ἐν αὐτῷ), establishing a panentheistic framework: God is not identical with creation but creation exists within God.
  2. §17:29: The term to theion (τὸ θεῖον), “the divine nature” or “the divinity,” is a term from Greek philosophical theology rather than biblical vocabulary. Paul meets the Athenians on their own intellectual ground. The argument proceeds from kinship to theology: if we are God’s offspring, then the divine nature must be at least as personal and living as we are—it cannot be mere metal or stone. The implication for theosis is direct: the creature that is God’s offspring has an innate orientation toward the divine.
