Introduction

At Protagoras 320c–322d, Protagoras tells the myth of Prometheus and Epimetheus to explain how humans acquired the arts and political virtue. Prometheus steals fire and technical skill from the gods, but Zeus must later grant justice and shame to all.

§320c–321d · Plato, Protagoras 320c8–321d5 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

ἦν γάρ ποτε χρόνος ὅτε θεοὶ μὲν ἦσαν, θνητὰ δὲ γένη οὐκ ἦν

English

There was a time when gods existed but mortal creatures did not. When the appointed time came for these also to be born, the gods fashioned them within the earth. Epimetheus distributed various abilities to the animals but left man naked and unshod. Prometheus, seeing man with no means of preservation, stole fire and technical wisdom from Hephaestus and Athena.

Translator's Notes

  1. §320c–321d: The Prometheus myth here serves Protagoras’s argument that political virtue can be taught. The distinction between technical skill (given by Prometheus) and political virtue (given later by Zeus) reflects an important Platonic theme about the limits of mere cleverness without justice.
