Chandogya Upanishad 6.8-16: Tat Tvam Asi (You Are That)

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The sixth chapter of the Chandogya Upanishad contains one of the most famous teaching dialogues in world literature. The sage Uddalaka Aruni instructs his son Shvetaketu, who has returned from twelve years of Vedic study proud of his learning but ignorant of the one thing that matters: the nature of the Self. Through a series of brilliant analogies—salt dissolved in water, rivers merging into the ocean, a fig seed containing an invisible essence—the father demonstrates that the ultimate reality (Brahman) is an invisible essence that pervades everything, and that this essence is identical with the Self (ātman) of his son.

The refrain tat tvam asi (“You are That”) is repeated nine times across sections 6.8 through 6.16, driving the lesson home with the force of a mantra. This phrase became one of the four mahāvākyas (great sayings) of Vedanta philosophy and is arguably the single most important statement in Hindu metaphysics. It is the Eastern theosis declaration: not “God is within you” or “you may become divine,” but “you already are the ultimate reality.” There is nothing to attain—only ignorance to dissolve.

§6.8.1–2 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.1–2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

uddālako hāruṇiḥ śvetaketuṃ putram uvāca svapiteti ha somya vijajānīhiti yadā puruṣaḥ svapiteti ācakṣate satā somya tadā sampanno bhavati svam apīto bhavati tasmād enaṃ svapitety ācakṣate svam hy apīto bhavati

English

Uddalaka Aruni said to his son Shvetaketu: “Learn from me, dear one, the nature of sleep. When a person sleeps, as it is called, then he has merged with Being (sat). He has gone to his own. That is why they say he sleeps (svapiti), for he has gone to his own (svam apītaḥ).”

§6.8.3–4 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.3–4
Manuscript
Sanskrit

sa yathā śakuniḥ sūtreṇa prabaddhaḥ diśaṃ diśam apatītvānyatrāyatanam alab dhvā bandhanam evōpaśrayata evam eva khalu somya tan mano diśaṃ diśam apatītvānyatrāyatanam alabdhvā prāṇam evōpaśrayate prāṇabandhanaṃ hi somya mana iti

English

“As a bird tied by a string, after flying in every direction without finding rest elsewhere, settles down at the place where it is bound—so too, dear one, the mind, after flying in every direction without finding rest elsewhere, settles down in breath (prāṇa), for the mind is bound to breath.”

§6.8.6 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.6
Manuscript
Sanskrit

sa ya eṣo’ṇimāitad ātmyam idaṃ sarvam tat satyam sa ātmā tat tvam asi śvetaketo iti

English

“Now, that which is the finest essence—this whole world has that as its Self. That is the True. That is the Self. You are That (tat tvam asi), Shvetaketu.”

§6.12.1–3 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.12.1–3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

nyagrodhaph alam ata āhāreti idam bhagava iti bhinddhīti bhinnam bhagava iti kim atr a paśyasi iti aṇvya itvā bha gava iti tāsām aṅga ekāṃ bhinddhīti bhinnā bhagava iti kim atra paśyasi iti na kiṃcana bhagava iti | tam hovāca yaṃ vai somyait am aṇimānam na nibhālayase etasmād vā are’ṇim na etasmād evāyaṃ mahany agrodhas tiṣṭhati śraddhasva somya iti sa ya eṣo’ṇimāitad ātmyam idaṃ sarvam tat satyam sa ātmā tat tvam asi śvetaketo iti

English

“Bring me a fruit of that banyan tree.” “Here it is, sir.” “Break it.” “It is broken, sir.” “What do you see there?” “These tiny seeds, sir.” “Break one of them.” “It is broken, sir.” “What do you see there?” “Nothing at all, sir.” He said: “That finest essence which you do not perceive—from that very essence this great banyan tree stands. Believe it, dear one. That which is the finest essence—this whole world has that as its Self. That is the True. That is the Self. You are That, Shvetaketu.”

§6.13.1–3 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.13.1–3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

lavaṇam etad udake’v adhāyātha mā prātar upasīdetīti sa ha tathā cakāra tam hovāca yad doṣā lavaṇam udake’vadhithā aṅga tad āhāreti tad dhāvamṛśya na viveda yathā vīliīnam evāsyāgraād ācāmeti katham iti lavaṇam iti madhyat ācāmeti katham iti lavaṇam iti antād ācāmeti katham iti lavaṇam iti | sa ya eṣo’ṇimāitad ātmyam idaṃ sarvam tat satyam sa ātmā tat tvam asi śvetaketo iti

English

“Place this salt in water, and come to me in the morning.” He did so. The father said: “Bring me the salt you placed in the water last night.” He looked for it but could not find it, for it had dissolved. “Sip from this end. How is it?” “Salty.” “Sip from the middle. How is it?” “Salty.” “Sip from the far end. How is it?” “Salty.” “Throw it away and come to me.” He could not find the salt, but it was always there. “That finest essence which you do not perceive—that is the Self of this whole world. That is the True. That is the Self. You are That, Shvetaketu.”

§6.14.1–3 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.14.1–3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

yathā somya puruṣaṃ gandhārebhyo’bhina ddham ānayaṃ taṃ tato’tij ane vi sṛjet sa yathā tatra prāṅ vōdaṅ vā pratyaṅ vā udancam iti abhinaddho’bhip raṇīto’bhijanaṃ visṛṣṭaḥ | tasya yathābhina hanaṃ pramu cya pravṛjeta taṃ sa evam eva somya vijanavatpuruṣo vid vān | sa ya eṣo’ṇimāitad ātmyam idaṃ sarvam tat satyam sa ātmā tat tvam asi śvetaketo iti

English

“Just as a man from Gandhara, blindfolded and led away, might be left in a deserted place, and just as he would call out in every direction, ‘I have been brought here blindfolded, I have been left here blindfolded!’—and just as someone might remove his blindfold and say, ‘Gandhara is in that direction; go that way,’ and he, asking his way from village to village, would finally arrive home—so too a person who has a teacher knows: ‘I shall remain in this world only until I am liberated. Then I shall reach home.’ That which is the finest essence—this whole world has that as its Self. That is the True. That is the Self. You are That, Shvetaketu.”

§6.16.3 · Chandogya Upanishad 6.16.3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

sa ya eṣo’ṇimāitad ātmyam idaṃ sarvam tat satyam sa ātmā tat tvam asi śvetaketo iti bhūya eva mā bhagavān vijñāpayatv iti tathā somyeti hovāca

English

“That which is the finest essence—this whole world has that as its Self. That is the True. That is the Self. You are That, Shvetaketu.” “Please, sir, teach me more.” “So be it, dear one,” he said.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6.8.6: The phrase tat tvam asi (“You are That”) is the axis on which Vedanta philosophy turns. Shankara (8th century CE) read it as a statement of absolute identity: the individual self is Brahman, and apparent difference is illusion. Ramanuja (11th century) read it as a statement of intimate relationship: the self is a part of Brahman, as a body is part of a person, but not identical. Madhva (13th century) read it as a statement of dependence: “you are of That.” The same three words generated three incompatible schools of philosophy.
  2. §6.12.1–3: The fig-seed analogy is a masterpiece of empirical theology. The father does not argue from scripture or authority. He has his son perform an experiment: break a seed, look inside, see nothing. The invisible essence that you cannot see is the same essence that grows into a tree spanning fifty metres. Brahman is like that: imperceptible, yet the ground of all perceptible reality.
  3. §6.13.1–3: The salt-water analogy makes the same point through a different sense. You cannot see salt in water, but you can taste it everywhere. So too with Brahman: you cannot perceive it as an object, but it pervades every experience. This teaching method—using concrete experiments to convey metaphysical truths—is unique to the Upanishads and stands in contrast to the purely mythological or revelatory modes of other ancient traditions.
