Lankavatara Sutra: Mind-Only

Gautama Buddha (attributed), c. 563-483 BCE

Introduction

The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra is one of the most philosophically dense texts in the Buddhist canon, the foundational scripture of the Yogacara (“Mind-Only”) tradition and a text of central importance to early Chan (Zen) Buddhism. Bodhidharma, the legendary founder of Chan, is said to have transmitted it as the essential teaching. The sutra’s core doctrine is citta-mātra: all that appears—the external world, other beings, even the distinction between samsara and nirvana—is a projection of mind. There is no reality outside consciousness.

This is not Western idealism, which typically posits a divine mind creating the world. The Laṅkāvatāra teaches that the discriminating mind (vikalpa) fabricates the appearance of an external world through its own habitual patterns (vāsanā). When these patterns are recognized and exhausted, what remains is not a different world but this same world seen without distortion. For the theosis question, the implication is staggering: there is no distance between the being and the divine because there is no distance between mind and reality. The separation was always imaginary.

§Mind-Only · Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Atha khalu Bhagavān āha: Mahāmate traidhātukaṃ citta-mātram. Bāhyo ’rtho vikalpa-viṣayo bālānāṃ na vidyate. Yad bāhyam iva bhāsate citta-mātram eva tad vāsanā-viparyasta-dṛṣṭam. Yathā svapna-dṛṣṭaḥ puruṣo ’vidyamānaṃ jagat paśyati, evam bālā bāhyam arthaṃ paśyanti sva-citta-mātram.

English

Then the Blessed One said: “Mahāmati, the three realms of existence are mind-only. The external world, which is the object of discrimination by the unenlightened, does not exist. What appears to be external is nothing but mind itself, seen through the distortions of habitual patterns. Like a man dreaming, who sees a world that is not there, so the unenlightened see an external reality that is merely their own mind.”

§The Turning of the Store-Consciousness · Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra, Chapter 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Mahāmate ālaya-vijñānaṃ sāgaravat, sapta-vijñānāni tarangavat. Viṣaya-vāyunā ālaya-sāgare ksobhite sapta-vijñāna-tarangaḥ satātam utpadyante. Yadā tu vikalpo nivṛtto vāsanāś ca kṣīṇāḥ, tadā āśraya-parāvr̩ttiḥ saṃpadyate, saṃkliṣṭaṃ vijñānaṃ viśuddhaṃ jñānaṃ bhavati.

English

“Mahāmati, the store-consciousness (ālaya-vijñāna) is like the ocean, and the seven active consciousnesses are like its waves. When the wind of objects stirs the ocean of the store-consciousness, the waves of the seven consciousnesses arise ceaselessly. But when the discriminating mind is stilled and the habitual patterns are exhausted, the store-consciousness undergoes a revolution at its base (āśraya-parāvr̩tti), and what was defiled consciousness becomes undefiled wisdom.”

§Beyond Words · Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra, Chapter 3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Mahāmate tathāgatair adhigataṃ tattvaṃ na vāk nāvāk. Vāg na tattvaṃ, na ca tattvaṃ vāg-vyatiriktaṃ. Yathā kaścit puruṣaḥ candram aṅgulyagreṇa nirśdisati, bālās tv aṅgulyagram eva paśyanti na candraṃ. Evaṃ ye akṣareṣu sajjante na tattvaṃ jānanti yan mama vākyaṃ sandhatte.

English

“Mahāmati, the truth realized by the Tathāgatas is neither words nor non-words. Words are not truth, yet truth is not apart from words. It is like a man pointing at the moon with his finger: the foolish look at the finger, not the moon. So those who cling to the letter do not understand the truth that my words are intended to convey.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Mind-Only: The term citta-mātra (“mind-only”) does not mean that a cosmic mind creates the physical world, as in Western idealism. It means that what we call “the external world” is an appearance within consciousness, structured by habitual patterns (vāsanā) accumulated over beginningless time. The metaphor of the dream is precise: the dream world is “real” as experience but does not exist independently of the dreaming mind. The Laṅkāvatāra is not denying that experience occurs; it is denying that what appears in experience has the independent, mind-external existence we naively attribute to it.
  2. §The Turning of the Store-Consciousness: The concept of āśraya-parāvr̩tti (“revolution at the base” or “transformation of the support”) is the Yogacara tradition’s account of enlightenment. The store-consciousness (ālaya-vijñāna), which contains the seeds of all experience, does not disappear at awakening. It is transformed. Defiled consciousness becomes wisdom; the eight consciousnesses become the four wisdoms. This is a conversion, not a destruction—the water in the waves is the same water in the still ocean. The parallel to the Christian doctrine of transformation rather than annihilation of human nature in theosis is striking.
  3. §Beyond Words: The finger-pointing-at-the-moon metaphor became one of the most celebrated images in Chan/Zen Buddhism. It appears in the Shurangama Sutra as well and was a favourite of the sixth patriarch Huineng. The metaphor captures the fundamental Mahayana Buddhist insight that language and conceptual thought are tools, not destinations. The Laṅkāvatāra is particularly insistent on this point because its own teaching—that all is mind-only—can easily become another conceptual fixation if taken as a philosophical thesis rather than a description of direct experience.
