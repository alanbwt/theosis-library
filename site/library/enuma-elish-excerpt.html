Enuma Elish: Creation from Divine Blood

Enūma Eliš
Anonymous (Babylonian), c. 1100 BCE

Introduction

The Enuma Elish (“When on High”) is the Babylonian creation epic, composed in Akkadian probably during the late second millennium BCE. It was recited annually at the New Year festival in Babylon. The poem describes the creation of the cosmos from primordial chaos—the mingling of the salt waters (Tiamat) and the fresh waters (Apsu)—and the rise of Marduk as king of the gods.

Two passages are presented here. Tablet I opens with the birth of the gods from primordial waters. Tablet VI describes the creation of humanity from the blood of the slain god Qingu, Tiamat’s champion. This is the Mesopotamian account of the divine spark in human nature: we are made from divine substance, fashioned so that the gods might be free from labor. The theological implications are striking—humanity carries divinity in its blood, yet exists to serve.

English

When on high the heavens had not yet been named, and below the earth had not yet been called by name—when primordial Apsu, their begetter, and Mummu-Tiamat, she who bore them all, were mingling their waters as one, when no marsh had formed and no island could be found, when no god at all had appeared, had not been named, no destinies decreed—then were the gods created within them.

Lahmu and Lahamu emerged, were called by name. Before they had grown, before they had matured, Anshar and Kishar were created, surpassing them. They lived for many days, adding years to years. Anu was their firstborn son, the rival of his fathers. Anshar made his firstborn Anu his equal. And Anu begat Nudimmud in his likeness.

Nudimmud it was who was the master of his fathers: broad of understanding, wise, mighty in strength, far mightier than his grandfather Anshar. He had no rival among the gods, his brethren.

§Tablet VI, lines 1–38: Creation of Humanity from Divine Blood · Enuma Elish, Tablet VI, lines 1–38

enuma Marduk zīkir ilānū išmû-ma libbašu ibniššu-ma eptuāti iḫḫat pāšu ippušamma ana Ea iqabbi amata ša ina libbišu ibsimmu inambi dami arkaṣṣa eṣemta lušabni lušzizma lullû amīlu šumšu lubni lullû-amīla tudulšu lullû dullu ilānū šunu-ma lū pšuḫū Ea īpulšu-ma amata iqabbîšu mīlku ana tub ilānū ušannyēšu ištēn aḫḫū-šun limmani luḫliqu-ma amīlūtū libni ilānū rabûtu lippaḫru-ma āggu limmani-ma šunu likkunū Marduk ilānū rabûtu upahhirma ṭābiš ussāhiršunūti uš īzu ussi ilānū izakkarūm ana qabēšu šarru ana ilānū Anunnakī amata izakkara šumma anniūkunšun kiššatu kittu-ma ina maḫri lemī lušmuū mannu-ma tēḫû tamharta ibšû Tiāmta ustebbirū qabla iṣbatū ša tēḫû tamharta limmani-ma aranšu lū ina muhhi-šu attuma lušabšākunu ilānū rabûtu īpulūšu šar ilānū šamû u erṣeti milku dumāqī Qingu-ma ša tēḫû tamharta ibšû Tiāmta ustebbiruma qabla iṣbatū ikamūšu-ma maḫar Ea ukallumšu āranu emuṣūšu-ma damēšu ibtuqū ina damēšu ibnu amīlūtu tudulta ilānū imiddušu-ma ilānū ušēṣi

English

When Marduk heard the speech of the gods, his heart moved him to create artful things. He opened his mouth and spoke to Ea, conveying the plan he had conceived in his heart:

“I will bind together blood and cause bones to be. I will establish a savage, ‘Man’ shall be his name. Truly, savage-man I will create. He shall be charged with the service of the gods, that they might be at rest.”

Ea answered him, speaking a word to him, telling him a plan for the relief of the gods:

“Let one of the brothers be given up. Let him perish that mankind may be fashioned. Let the great gods assemble and let the guilty one be given up, that they may be confirmed.”

Marduk assembled the great gods. Graciously he gave instruction, issued a command. The gods paid heed to his utterance. The king addressed a word to the Anunnaki:

“If your earlier declaration was true, now declare the truth on oath by me! Who was it who contrived the uprising and made Tiamat rebel, and joined battle? Let him who contrived the uprising be given up, that I may lay his punishment upon him. You shall dwell in peace.”

The great gods answered him, the king of the gods of heaven and earth:

“It was Qingu who contrived the uprising and made Tiamat rebel, and joined battle.”

They bound him, holding him before Ea. They imposed the punishment on him and severed his blood-vessels. From his blood they created mankind. Ea imposed the service of the gods upon them and set the gods free.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Tablet I, lines 1–20: The opening lines are among the most famous in ancient literature. The repeated negations (“not yet named,” “no marsh had formed,” “no god had appeared”) describe creation as a process of differentiation from undifferentiated primordial waters. In Mesopotamian thought, to name something is to bring it into being. The genealogy of gods emerging from water establishes the fundamental cosmological principle: order arises from chaos through successive generations.
  2. §Tablet VI, lines 1–38: This is the Mesopotamian account of the “divine spark” in human nature. Humanity is created from the blood of a god—specifically Qingu, who led the rebellion against the younger gods. The theological implications are layered: humans carry divine substance in their veins, yet they are created for servitude (dullu, “forced labor”). The divine element in humanity comes from a guilty god, a rebel. This is a radically different anthropology from Genesis, where humanity is made in the “image of God” and given dominion. Here, divinity in the blood coexists with a destiny of service. The passage raises the question that runs through all ancient Near Eastern theology: if we are made from divine material, what is our proper relationship to the gods?
