Ashtavakra Gita 1: You Are Already Free

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The Ashtavakra Gita is the most radical text in the Advaita (non-dual) tradition. Attributed to the sage Ashtavakra and structured as a dialogue with King Janaka, it dispenses entirely with the gradual path of spiritual practice. Its message is blunt: you are already pure consciousness, already free, already identical with the absolute. There is nothing to attain, nothing to renounce, nothing to do. The very effort to become liberated is the obstacle, because it presupposes a separate self that needs liberation.

Chapter 1 establishes this position with an uncompromising directness that has no parallel in world literature. Where the Upanishads build toward the identity of self and Brahman through analogy and argument, and the Bhagavad Gita prescribes multiple paths of action, devotion, and knowledge, Ashtavakra simply declares: “You are pure awareness. The world is appearance. You are already free.” The text is dated anywhere from the eighth century BCE to the fourteenth century CE, but its philosophical radicalism places it at the extreme limit of the theosis tradition: if the self is already divine, there is no process of divinization to undertake.

§1.1 · Ashtavakra Gita 1.1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

कथं ज्ञानमवाप्नोति कथं मुक्तिर्भविष्यति । वैराग्यश्च कथं प्राप्यः एतद् ब्रूहि प्रभो ।

English

Janaka said: How is knowledge to be acquired? How is liberation to be attained? And how is dispassion to be reached? Tell me this, O Lord.

§1.2 · Ashtavakra Gita 1.2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

मुक्तिमिच्छसि चेत्तात् विषयान् विषवत् त्यज । क्षमार्जवदयातोषसत्यं पीयूषवदमृतम् ।

English

Ashtavakra said: If you desire liberation, my child, shun the objects of the senses like poison. Seek forgiveness, sincerity, kindness, contentment, and truth as you would seek nectar.

§1.3–4 · Ashtavakra Gita 1.3–4
Manuscript
Sanskrit

न पृथ्वी न जलं नाग्निर्न मारुतो नभः । एषां साक्षिणं ज्ञात्वा चिद्रूपं विमुच्यसे ।

English

You are neither earth, nor water, nor fire, nor air, nor space. For liberation, know yourself as the witness of all these, as consciousness itself. If only you will separate yourself from the body and rest in consciousness, this very moment you will be happy, at peace, free from bondage.

§1.5–6 · Ashtavakra Gita 1.5–6
Manuscript
Sanskrit

न त्वं विप्रादिको वर्णो नाश्रमी न द्विजः । असङ्गो’सि निराकारो’सि सर्वद्रष्टा सुखी भव ।

English

You do not belong to any caste or stage of life. You are not perceived by the eyes. You are unattached and formless, the witness of all things. Know this and be happy. Right and wrong, pleasure and pain, exist in the mind only, not in you. You are neither the doer nor the enjoyer. You are free—truly, you have always been free.

§1.7–8 · Ashtavakra Gita 1.7–8
Manuscript
Sanskrit

एको द्रष्टासि सर्वस्य त्वं प्रमुक्त एव सदा । अयमेव हि ते बन्धो द्रष्टारं पश्यस्यात्मना ।

English

You are the one witness of all, and are always totally free. Your only bondage is that you see the witness as something other than this. You have been bitten by the great black serpent of ego—“I am the doer.” Drink the nectar of the faith “I am not the doer” and be happy.

§1.9–10 · Ashtavakra Gita 1.9–10
Manuscript
Sanskrit

एकं चैतन्यमात्मानं इति निश्चयवह्निना । अज्ञानवनं दग्ध्वा सुखी भव प्रभो ।

English

Burn down the forest of ignorance with the fire of the understanding: “I am the one pure awareness.” Be free from sorrow and be happy. That in which all this appears—imagined like the snake in the rope—that joy, supreme joy, awareness: that is what you are. Be happy.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.3–4: The enumeration of the five elements (earth, water, fire, air, space) that the self is not follows the same neti neti (“not this, not this”) method found in the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad. But where the Brihadaranyaka uses this negation to approach the unknowable Brahman, Ashtavakra uses it to reveal what you already are. The negation is not a path to knowledge; it is the removal of a misunderstanding.
  2. §1.7–8: The metaphor of the serpent of ego (ahamkara) echoes the famous rope-snake analogy used throughout Advaita Vedanta. Just as a rope in dim light is mistaken for a snake, the pure self is mistaken for a limited, individual agent. The “cure” is not action but recognition: the snake was never there. This stands in sharp contrast to the Christian theosis tradition, which generally requires a genuine transformation rather than a mere shift in perspective.
