Introduction

The Eudemian Ethics opens at Delos, where an inscription on the temple of Leto names the most beautiful, the most just, and the most pleasant as the highest goods. Aristotle asks whether these are distinct or whether the best life unites them all.

Greek

τὸ ἐν Δήλῳ γράμμα· κάλλιστον τὸ δικαιότατον, λῷστον δ᾽ ὑγιαίνειν

English

The inscription at Delos declares: most beautiful is what is most just, best is to be healthy, and most pleasant by nature is to gain what one desires. But we do not agree with this, for happiness, which is the most beautiful and best of all things, is also the most pleasant.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1214a–1214b: The Delian inscription separates beauty, health, and pleasure as three distinct goods. Aristotle’s response—that happiness (eudaimonia) unifies all three—establishes the integrative approach to the good life that distinguishes his ethics from those that prize a single value.
