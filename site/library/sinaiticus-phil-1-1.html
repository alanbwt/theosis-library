Introduction

Philippians 1:1–26 is Paul’s opening to his most affectionate letter, written from prison. He expresses confidence that “to live is Christ and to die is gain,” revealing a mystical identification with Christ. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§1:3–6 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ευχαριστω τω θεω μου επι παση τη μνεια υμων

English

I thank my God in all my remembrance of you, always in every prayer of mine for you all making my prayer with joy, because of your partnership in the gospel from the first day until now. And I am sure of this, that he who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ.

§1:21–24 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

εμοι γαρ το ζην χριστος και το αποθανειν κερδος

English

For to me to live is Christ, and to die is gain. If I am to live in the flesh, that means fruitful labor for me. Yet which I shall choose I cannot tell. I am hard pressed between the two. My desire is to depart and be with Christ, for that is far better. But to remain in the flesh is more necessary on your account.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:21: The phrase “to live is Christ” (το ζην χριστος) expresses an identification so complete that Paul’s own life and Christ’s become indistinguishable. This is not metaphor but ontological claim, foundational for the Pauline mysticism explored by Albert Schweitzer and others.
