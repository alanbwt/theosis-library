Introduction

The Bacchae of Euripides (c. 405 BCE) dramatizes the arrival of Dionysus in Thebes and King Pentheus’s fatal refusal to acknowledge the god. The play explores what happens when a city denies the divine its proper place.

§Lines 1–12 · Euripides, Bacchae
Manuscript
Greek

ἥκω Διὸς παῖς τήνδε Θηβαίων χθόνα Διόνυσος

English

I have come, Dionysus, the son of Zeus, to this land of the Thebans—I, whom Semele, the daughter of Cadmus, once bore, delivered by a lightning-bearing flame. Having changed my form from a god’s to a mortal’s, I am here at the streams of Dirce and the water of Ismenus.

§Lines 860–876 · Euripides, Bacchae
Manuscript
Greek

ἆρα χορεύσω πόδα θείσα λευκόν

English

Shall I set my white foot in the night-long dance, throwing back my head to the dewy air, like a fawn playing in the green delights of a meadow, when it has escaped the fearful hunt, beyond the watchers, past the well-woven nets?

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 1–12: Dionysus’s prologue announces the central paradox: a god who disguises himself as a mortal to test whether mortals will recognize divinity. The theme of divine incognito resonates with the Christian Incarnation, as the Church Fathers noted. Justin Martyr and Clement of Alexandria both discussed Dionysiac parallels to Christ.
