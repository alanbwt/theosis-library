Introduction

The Oedipus Rex of Sophocles (c. 429 BCE) is Aristotle’s model of the perfect tragedy. Oedipus’s relentless pursuit of truth about his own identity leads to the discovery that he has killed his father and married his mother—the very crimes he sought to prevent.

§Lines 1–13 · Sophocles, Oedipus Tyrannus
Manuscript
Greek

ὦ τέκνα, Κάδμου τοῦ πάλαι νέα τροφή

English

O children, latest-born of Cadmus’s line, why are you set before me thus, with branches wreathed in supplication? The whole city is full of incense, full of prayers for healing and of groans. I thought it unfitting, children, to hear this from messengers. I, Oedipus, whom all call famous, have come myself.

§Lines 1186–1196 · Sophocles, Oedipus Tyrannus
Manuscript
Greek

ἰὼ γενεαὶ βροτῶν ὡς ὑμᾶς ἴσα καὶ τὸ μηδὲν ζώσας ἐναριθμῶ

English

O generations of mortals, how I count you as equal to nothing while you live! For who, what man wins more of happiness than just the seeming and, after the semblance, a falling away? With your fate as my example, your fate, O wretched Oedipus, I call no mortal blessed.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 1186–1196: The chorus’s declaration that no mortal should be called blessed while alive echoes Solon’s famous warning to Croesus (Herodotus 1.32). Greek tragedy’s insistence on human limitation (“call no man happy until he is dead”) stands in tension with the theosis tradition’s claim that humans can participate in divine blessedness.
