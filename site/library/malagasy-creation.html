Introduction

The Malagasy creation tradition from Madagascar tells of Zanahary, the supreme creator, and Ratovantany, the earth-god who shapes humans from clay. Their collaboration and rivalry in creating humanity reflects the dual nature of human existence: heavenly spirit and earthly body.

§Creation of Humanity · Malagasy oral tradition
Manuscript
Greek

[Malagasy oral tradition]

English

Zanahary, who dwells above, and Ratovantany, who dwells below, agreed to create human beings. Ratovantany shaped the bodies from earth and clay, fashioning limbs and features with care. But the figures were lifeless. Then Zanahary breathed life into them from above, and they rose and walked. But a dispute arose: Zanahary claimed them because he gave the breath; Ratovantany claimed them because he gave the body. They agreed: at death, Zanahary takes back the breath, and Ratovantany takes back the body. This is why the living look up to heaven and the dead return to the earth.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Creation: The Malagasy creation account bears striking structural resemblance to Genesis 2:7 (God forms man from dust and breathes life into him) and to the Promethean myth in Greek tradition. The motif of divine breath animating earthly clay appears independently across cultures, suggesting a deep human intuition that life results from the meeting of transcendent spirit and material substance.
