Fragments: Desire and the Divine

Fragments: Desire and the Divine
Sappho, c. 630-570 BCE
P.Oxy. 1231; Longinus, On the Sublime

Introduction

Sappho of Lesbos was called 'the Tenth Muse' by Plato. Of her nine books of poetry, only fragments survive — preserved on papyrus scraps and in quotations by later authors who could not bear to let her words disappear entirely. Her poetry explores desire, beauty, and loss with an intensity and directness that remains unmatched. Fragment 31, describing the physical symptoms of desire, was so famous that Catullus translated it into Latin and Longinus cited it as the supreme example of sublimity.

§Fr.1 · Voigt 1
Greek

ποικιλόθρον᾿ ἀθανάτ᾿ Ἀφρόδιτα, παῖ Δίος δολόπλοκε, λίσσομαί σε, μή μ᾿ ἄσαισι μηδ᾿ ὀνίαισι δάμνα, πότνια, θῦμον.

English

Richly-throned immortal Aphrodite, child of Zeus, weaver of wiles, I beg you — do not with pains and sorrows break my spirit, Lady.

§Fr.31 · Voigt 31
Greek

φαίνεταί μοι κῆνος ἴσος θέοισιν ἔμμεν᾿ ὤνηρ, ὄττις ἐνάντιός τοι ἰσδάνει.

English

He seems to me equal to the gods, that man who sits across from you and listens close to your sweet speaking.

§Fr.16 · Voigt 16
Greek

οἰ μὲν ἰππήων στρότον, οἰ δὲ πέσδων, οἰ δὲ νάων φαῖσ᾿ ἐπὶ γᾶν μέλαιναν ἔμμεναι κάλλιστον, ἔγω δὲ κῆν᾿ ὄττω τις ἔραται.

English

Some say a host of cavalry, others of infantry, and others of ships, is the most beautiful thing on the dark earth. But I say it is whatever one loves.
