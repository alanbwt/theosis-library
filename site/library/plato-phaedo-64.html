Introduction

In this passage from the Phaedo, Socrates defines philosophy as the practice of dying and being dead. The philosopher seeks to separate the soul from the body’s distractions in order to apprehend truth through pure thought alone.

§64a–b · Plato, Phaedo 64a2–b6 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

οἱ ὀρθῶς φιλοσοφοῦντες ἀποθνῄσκειν μελετῶσι

English

Those who practice philosophy in the right way are in training for dying, and they find death the least of all things to be feared. The philosopher desires to free the soul from communion with the body as much as possible.

Translator's Notes

  1. §64a–b: The equation of philosophy with preparation for death (μελέτη θανάτου) became a defining idea in the later Platonic tradition. Plotinus adopted this theme, arguing that the soul’s ascent requires progressive detachment from bodily existence.
