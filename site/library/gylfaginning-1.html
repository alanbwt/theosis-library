Gylfaginning 1-3: Creation from Ymir

Snorri Sturluson, 1179-1241 AD

Introduction

The Gylfaginning (“The Tricking of Gylfi”) is the first section of Snorri Sturluson’s Prose Edda, composed around 1220 CE in Iceland. Snorri, a Christian scholar and chieftain, set out to preserve the old Norse myths as a handbook for poets. The framing device has King Gylfi of Sweden traveling in disguise to Ásgarð to question the gods about the origin of the world. The opening chapters recount the creation of the cosmos from the body of the primordial giant Ymir: his flesh became earth, his blood the seas, his bones the mountains, his skull the sky, his brains the clouds.

The creation of the world from the dismembered body of a primordial being is one of the most widespread mythological patterns in the world. The Vedic Purusha Sukta (Rigveda 10.90) describes the gods sacrificing the cosmic man Purusha and creating the world from his body parts. The Babylonian Enuma Elish describes Marduk splitting the body of Tiamat to form heaven and earth. The Norse version shares the fundamental structure: cosmos from corpse, order from the body of a primordial being. Whether these parallels reflect shared Indo-European inheritance, independent convergence, or ancient cultural contact remains one of the great questions of comparative mythology.

§Chapter 1 (excerpt) · Gylfaginning, Ch. 1
Manuscript
Old Norse

Gylfi konungr var maðr vitr ok fjölkunnigr. Hann undraðisk þat mjök er Ása-fólk var svá kunnigt at allir hlutir gengu at vilja þeira.

English

King Gylfi was a wise man and skilled in magic. He wondered greatly that the Aesir-folk were so learned that all things went according to their will. He pondered whether this might be of their own nature, or whether the gods they worshipped might be responsible. He set out on a journey to Ásgarð and traveled in secret, having taken on the likeness of an old man as a disguise.

§Chapter 2 (excerpt) · Gylfaginning, Ch. 2
Manuscript
Old Norse

Gylfi kom til hallarinnar. Hann sá mann í dyrunum er leikaoi með handsaöxum ok hafði sjau senn á lopti.

English

Gylfi came to the hall and saw a man at the door juggling short swords, keeping seven in the air at once. This man asked his name, and Gylfi said he was called Gangleri (“Wanderer”). He said he had come a long way and asked for a night’s lodging. He asked also who owned the hall. The man said it belonged to their king: “I shall take you to see him.” And inside Gylfi saw many rooms and many people, some drinking, some fighting, some playing games.

§Chapter 3 (creation, excerpt) · Gylfaginning, Ch. 3–5
Manuscript
Old Norse

Gangleri mælti: „Hvat var upphaf? Hvernig hófsk? Hvat var áðr?“ Hár svarar: „Svá sem segir í Völuspá...“

English

Gangleri said: “What was the beginning? How did it start? What was before?” High One replied: “As it says in the Völuspá: in the beginning there was nothing. Neither sand nor sea, nor cold waves. No earth, nor sky above. Only Ginnungagap, the Yawning Void, and grass was nowhere.”

Many ages before the earth was made, Niflheim was formed. In its midst lies the well called Hvergelmir, from which flow rivers. The southern region is called Muspelheim—it is bright and hot, blazing and burning, impassable to those who are foreign there.

§Chapter 4–5 (Ymir and creation from his body) · Gylfaginning, Ch. 4–5
Manuscript
Old Norse

Þar er mættusk hitinn ok hrimio ok tók at drjúpa ok renna sem af siðu þeim er ír eldi leyoir... Synir Bors drápu Ymi. En er hann fell, þá hljop svá mikit blóð ór sárum hans at með því druknaðu allir hrímhursar.

English

Where the warm airs of Muspelheim met the frost of Niflheim in the midst of Ginnungagap, the ice thawed and dripped, and from the drops there formed the likeness of a man. He was named Ymir. He was evil. While he slept, he sweated. Under his left arm grew a man and a woman, and one of his legs begot a son with the other. From these came the race of frost-giants.

The sons of Bor—Odin, Vili, and Vé—killed Ymir. When he fell, so much blood flowed from his wounds that they drowned the whole race of frost-giants in it. They carried Ymir into the midst of Ginnungagap and made from him the earth. From his blood they made the sea and the lakes. From his flesh the earth was made, and from his bones the mountains. From his teeth and broken bones they made stones and gravel.

§Chapter 5 (continued) · Gylfaginning, Ch. 5
Manuscript
Old Norse

Úr hausi hans gerðu þeir himin ok settu hann upp yfir jörðina... Úr holdi Ymis var jörð um sköpuð, en ór sveita sær, björg ór beinum, baðmr ór hári, en ór hausi himinn.

English

From Ymir’s skull they made the sky and set it up over the earth, with four corners, and under each corner they set a dwarf. Their names are: Austri, Vestri, Norðri, Suðri. From his brains they made the clouds, as it says: “From Ymir’s flesh the earth was fashioned, and from his blood the sea; mountains from his bones, trees from his hair, and from his skull the sky. From his eyelashes the kindly gods made Midgard for the sons of men, and from his brain the hard-tempered clouds were all created.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: Snorri’s framing device is significant: a mortal king seeks knowledge from the gods by traveling to their realm in disguise. This mirrors the pattern of initiatory journeys in many traditions—the seeker must cross a threshold into the sacred world. Gylfi’s name means “one who deceives,” yet it is he who is being tricked: the entire Gylfaginning is, according to Snorri’s Christian framework, a demonstration that the old gods were clever men, not real deities. Whether Snorri actually believed this euhemerist frame or used it as protective cover for preserving pagan material is debated.
  2. §Chapter 4–5: The creation of the world from Ymir’s body belongs to the widespread mythological pattern of the cosmic man or Urmensch. The Vedic Purusha Sukta (Rigveda 10.90) is the closest parallel: the gods sacrifice the cosmic man Purusha, and from his body create the earth, sky, sun, moon, animals, and the four social classes. The Babylonian Enuma Elish describes Marduk splitting Tiamat’s body to form heaven and earth. The shared structure—cosmos from dismembered body—suggests either very deep Indo-European inheritance or a fundamental human tendency to imagine the world as an organism.
  3. §Chapter 5: The verse Snorri quotes—“From Ymir’s flesh the earth was fashioned”—is from the poem Grímnismál (stanza 40–41) in the Poetic Edda. Snorri frequently quotes older poetry as his source, making the Prose Edda a secondary witness to earlier tradition. The four dwarves at the corners of the sky—Austri (East), Vestri (West), Norðri (North), Suðri (South)—reflect a cosmic geography that oriented the Norse world along the cardinal directions, with Midgard (the human realm) at the center.
