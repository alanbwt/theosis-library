Introduction

Job 1 introduces the righteous man Job and the heavenly wager between God and “the Adversary” (Satan) that sets the entire drama in motion. Job’s response to catastrophic loss—“the Lord gave, and the Lord has taken away”—is the first great test. From the Septuagint.

§1:8–12 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ εἶπεν ὁ κύριος τῷ διαβόλῳ

English

And the Lord said to the devil, “Have you considered my servant Job, that there is none like him on the earth, a blameless and upright man, who fears God and turns away from evil?” Then the devil answered the Lord and said, “Does Job fear God for no reason? Have you not put a hedge around him? But stretch out your hand and touch all that he has, and he will curse you to your face.”

§1:20–22 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

οὕτως ἀναστὰς Ιωβ ἔρρηξεν τὰ ἱμάτια αὐτοῦ

English

Then Job arose and tore his robe and shaved his head and fell on the ground and worshiped. And he said, “Naked I came from my mother’s womb, and naked shall I return. The Lord gave, and the Lord has taken away; blessed be the name of the Lord.” In all this Job did not sin or charge God with wrong.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:9: The Adversary’s question—“Does Job fear God for no reason?” (δωρεάν)—is the deepest question in the book. The Hebrew hinnam means “for nothing” or “without cause.” The entire drama tests whether human devotion to God can be genuinely disinterested—whether goodness can exist apart from reward.
