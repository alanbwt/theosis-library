Introduction

Aristotle’s Constitution of Athens, rediscovered in 1879, traces the history of Athenian democracy from its origins through Solon, Cleisthenes, and Pericles. It is the most detailed ancient account of how Athenian democratic institutions actually functioned.

§Chapters 1–3 · Aristotle, Constitution of Athens 1–3
Manuscript
Greek

ἡ δὲ τάξις τῆς ἀρχαίας πολιτείας

English

Before the time of Solon, the constitution was oligarchic in every respect, and in particular the poor were enslaved to the rich, they and their children and their wives. They were called dependents and sixth-parters, for it was at this rate of rent that they worked the fields of the rich. All the land was in the hands of a few.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 2: The “sixth-parters” (hektemoroi) gave one-sixth of their produce to aristocratic landowners. Solon’s cancellation of debts (seisachtheia) and prohibition of debt-slavery were the foundational acts of Athenian reform, demonstrating that democracy emerged from economic crisis, not abstract idealism.
