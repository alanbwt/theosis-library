Introduction

The dragon episode (lines 2200–2711) is Beowulf’s final battle. The aged king faces a dragon awakened by a stolen cup, and unlike the youthful Grendel fight, this combat ends in mutual destruction—the hero dies of his wounds after slaying the beast.

§Lines 2669–2687 · Beowulf, Lines 2669–2687
Manuscript
Greek

Ðā gen for þriðdan siðe, frēcne fȳr-draca fǣhða gemyndig, rǣsde on ðone rōfan, hāt ond heaðo-grim, heals ealne ymbefeng biteran bānum.

English

Then for the third time the fire-dragon, mindful of feuds, rushed upon the brave one. Hot and battle-grim, it seized his whole neck with sharp fangs; he was bloodied with life-blood, and the blood welled forth in waves.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 2669–2687: The dragon fight inverts the Grendel episode: youth becomes age, bare-handed victory becomes armed defeat. The mutual destruction of hero and dragon enacts the Norse fatalistic vision where even the greatest warrior cannot escape death—only the manner of dying is in one’s control.
