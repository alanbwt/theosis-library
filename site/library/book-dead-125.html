Book of the Dead, Chapter 125: The Weighing of the Heart

rw nw prt m hrw
Anonymous (Egyptian), c. 1550 BCE

Introduction

Chapter 125 of the Egyptian Book of the Dead is the most famous passage in all of Egyptian funerary literature. It describes the judgment of the deceased before Osiris in the Hall of Two Truths, where the heart is weighed against the feather of Ma’at (truth, justice, cosmic order). The deceased must recite the “Negative Confession”—a series of declarations of innocence addressed to Osiris and the forty-two divine assessors.

The text reveals an Egyptian moral universe in which the afterlife depends not on faith or divine grace but on conduct. The deceased must account for every category of wrongdoing. If the heart balances against the feather, the deceased is declared “true of voice” (ma’a-kheru) and admitted to the realm of Osiris. If it does not, the heart is devoured by the monster Ammit, and the soul ceases to exist.

English

Words spoken by the Osiris N when he arrives at this Hall of Two Truths, that he may be purified of all the evil that he has done, and that he may behold the faces of the gods:

“Hail to you, great god, lord of the Two Truths! I have come before you, my lord. I have been brought that I might see your beauty. I know you. I know the names of the forty-two gods who are with you in this Hall of Two Truths, who live on those who store up evil, who swallow their blood on that day of the reckoning of characters before Wennefer.

Behold, I have come to you. I have brought you truth. I have driven out falsehood for you. I have not done evil against any person. I have not mistreated cattle. I have not committed wrong in the Place of Truth.”

“I have not done falsehood against any person. I have not impoverished my associates. I have not done wrong in the Place of Truth. I have not learned that which is not. I have not done any harmful thing. I have not made the beginning of each day with demands in excess of what was due to me. I have not brought my name forward for honors. I have not deprived the orphan of his property. I have not done what the gods detest. I have not caused a servant to be mistreated by his master. I have not caused anyone to suffer. I have not caused anyone to go hungry. I have not caused anyone to weep. I have not killed. I have not ordered anyone to kill. I have not caused suffering to any person. I have not diminished the offerings in the temples. I have not debased the bread of the gods. I have not taken the milk from the mouths of children. I have not driven cattle from their pasture. I have not snared the birds of the gods. I have not dammed water in its season. I have not built a dam against running water. I have not quenched a fire in its time. I have not neglected the days of meat-offerings. I am pure. I am pure. I am pure. I am pure.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Approach to Osiris: The phrase “Osiris N” is standard in Egyptian funerary texts—the deceased is identified with Osiris, the god who died and was resurrected. This is itself a form of theosis: in death, the individual becomes divine, is merged with the god of the underworld. The “Hall of Two Truths” (Wsḫ.t n.t Māʿt.j) refers to the dual aspects of Ma’at—truth and justice—which govern both cosmic and moral order.
  2. §Key Declarations of Innocence: The Negative Confession is remarkable for what it includes. Alongside expected prohibitions against murder and theft are declarations about not damming water, not quenching fire in its season, and not taking milk from children. The moral universe of ancient Egypt encompassed what we would call ecological and social ethics alongside personal morality. The fourfold declaration of purity at the end corresponds to the four cardinal directions and signals total purification. The ethical standard is comprehensive: not merely avoiding great crimes but maintaining the cosmic order (Ma’at) in every dimension of daily life.
