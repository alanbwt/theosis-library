Introduction

The Maori creation narrative describes the separation of Ranginui (Sky Father) and Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) by their children, who had been trapped in darkness between their parents' embrace. It is the foundational cosmogonic text of Aotearoa (New Zealand).

Translation

§The Separation of Earth and Sky · Maori Oral Tradition

In the beginning, Ranginui the Sky Father and Papatuanuku the Earth Mother lay locked in a close embrace, and their children dwelt in darkness between them. The children debated what to do. Tumatauenga, god of war, said: "Let us slay our parents." But Tane Mahuta, god of the forests, said: "No. Let us separate them—push the sky upward and let the earth remain below." Tane placed his shoulders against the earth and his feet against the sky and pushed with all his strength. Slowly, painfully, the parents were separated. Light flooded in. Ranginui's tears became the rain; Papatuanuku's sighs became the morning mist. And so the world of light was born from the grief of separation.

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Separation of Earth and Sky: The separation of Earth and Sky is one of the most widespread motifs in world mythology, found also in Egyptian (Geb and Nut), Greek (Gaia and Ouranos), and Sumerian traditions. The Maori version is distinctive in making the separation an act of filial sorrow rather than violence: creation is simultaneously an act of liberation and an act of loss, and the natural world (rain, mist) perpetually mourns it.
