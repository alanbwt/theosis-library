Interrogatio Iohannis: The Cathar Creation Myth

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Interrogatio Iohannis (The Secret Supper, or The Questions of John) is the central mythological text of the Cathar movement. It survives in two Latin manuscripts, one from Carcassonne and one from Vienna, both dating to the twelfth or thirteenth century. According to Cathar tradition, it was brought from Constantinople by the Bogomil bishop Nazarius. The text presents itself as a secret revelation given by Christ to the apostle John during the Last Supper.

The narrative recounts a Gnostic creation myth: Satan (here called Satanael) was once a high angel who rebelled against the invisible Father, descended to the lower waters, and created the material world in imitation of the heavenly one. He fashioned human bodies from clay and water, then tricked two angels into entering them—thus trapping divine spirits in material flesh. This myth served as the foundational narrative for Cathar communities across southern France and northern Italy, and it was this text, more than any other, that the Inquisition sought to suppress.

§1-2 · Vienna MS, Cod. lat. 1137, f. 1r
Manuscript
Latin

Ego Iohannes frater vester, qui particeps sum tribulationis et regni, cum recumberem in cena super pectus Domini nostri Iesu Christi.

English

I, John, your brother, who share in the tribulation and the kingdom, when I reclined at the supper on the breast of our Lord Jesus Christ, I said to him: “Lord, who is the one who will betray you?” And the Lord said to me: “The one who dips his hand with me in the dish. Then Satan will enter into him, and he will seek to betray me.”

§3-5 · Vienna MS, f. 1v
Manuscript
Latin

Et dixi: Domine, antequam Satanas caderet, in qua gloria stabat ante Patrem tuum?

English

And I said: “Lord, before Satan fell, in what glory did he stand before your Father?” And he answered me: “He was among the virtues of heaven. He had power over all things, and he was the steward of the heavenly places. He sat at the right hand of the Father, and his glory was so great that he commanded the angels of every order.”

§6-9 · Vienna MS, f. 2r
Manuscript
Latin

Sed cogitavit in corde suo, volens ponere thronum suum super nubes et esse similis Altissimo.

English

“But he conceived a plan in his heart, wishing to place his throne above the clouds and to be like the Most High. And descending from heaven, he came down to the lower air and to the waters which were upon the face of the earth. And he said to the angel of the air and to the angel of the waters: ‘All these things are mine. If you will hear me, I will set my throne upon the clouds and will be like the Most High.’”

§10-13 · Vienna MS, f. 2v
Manuscript
Latin

Et descendens ad infimas aquas, consideravit ordinem omnium rerum visibilium.

English

“And descending further to the lowest waters, he considered the order of all things visible. And he took the form of a youth, and he went to the angel of the air, saying: ‘I am the son of God. Do what I command.’ And he began to create the visible world, following the pattern of the invisible world: he made the firmament, the sun, the moon, the stars, and every creature.”

§14-17 · Vienna MS, f. 3r
Manuscript

Tunc accepit lutum de terra et fecit corpus humanum. Et iussit angelum de secundo caelo intrare in corpus luti.

English

“Then he took clay from the earth and made a human body. And he commanded an angel of the second heaven to enter into the body of clay. And he took a part of it and made another body in the form of a woman, and commanded an angel of the first heaven to enter into it. And the angels wept greatly, seeing that they were clothed in mortal form.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §3-5: The portrait of Satan as originally seated at the right hand of the Father draws on Isaiah 14:12-15 (the fall of the “morning star”) and Ezekiel 28:12-17 (the fall of the guardian cherub). The Cathar innovation is to make this fallen angel the creator of the entire material world, not merely a tempter within it.
  2. §10-13: Satan creates the visible world “following the pattern of the invisible world.” This is a direct inversion of the Platonic notion that the material world is an image of the Forms—here, the copy is made not by a benevolent craftsman (as in the Timaeus) but by a malevolent impostor. The material world is thus a counterfeit, a trap that mimics heaven in order to deceive.
  3. §14-17: The angels who “wept greatly” upon being imprisoned in mortal flesh represent the Cathar understanding of the human condition: the soul is a fallen angel, divine by nature, suffering in a body it did not choose. This is why the Cathar consolamentum was understood as liberation—not the forgiveness of sins, but the release of the angel from its prison.
