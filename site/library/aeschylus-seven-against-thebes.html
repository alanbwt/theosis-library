Introduction

The Seven Against Thebes of Aeschylus (467 BCE) dramatizes the final act of the curse on the house of Oedipus: his sons Eteocles and Polynices kill each other in battle for control of Thebes. The play explores how inherited guilt works itself out across generations.

§Lines 1–20 · Aeschylus, Seven Against Thebes
Manuscript
Greek

Κάδμου πολῖται, χρὴ λέγειν τὰ καίρια

English

Citizens of Cadmus, he who manages the affairs of the city must speak what the time demands, holding the tiller at the stern and not letting his eyelids close in sleep. For if we succeed, the cause is god’s; but if—may it not happen—disaster comes, the name of Eteocles alone would be on every tongue, hymned and groaned in a great clamor of lamentation.

§Lines 653–676 · Aeschylus, Seven Against Thebes
Manuscript
Greek

ἰὼ θεομανὲς γένος Οἰδίπου μέγα

English

O race of Oedipus, maddened by the gods! O great curse of a father come to fulfillment! But enough: it is not fitting to weep. Lest lamentation breed still more disaster, we must watch the outcome.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 653–676: The chorus’s cry “O race of Oedipus, maddened by the gods” (θεομανὲς γένος) captures the tragic view that divine action can manifest as madness. The concept of inherited curse (ατη, ate) challenged later Christian theology, which had to reconcile it with the doctrine of individual moral responsibility.
