Introduction

Nicholas of Cusa’s De Visione Dei (1453) uses the metaphor of an omnivoyant icon—a painting whose eyes follow every viewer—to explore how God sees all things simultaneously. It is a masterpiece of mystical theology and philosophical imagination.

§Chapter 4 · Nicholas of Cusa, De Visione Dei
Manuscript
Greek

[Latin: Visio tua, Domine, est facies tua]

English

Your gaze, Lord, is your face. Therefore he who looks upon you with a loving face will find nothing but your face looking upon him with love. And the more he strives to look upon you with greater love, the more lovingly will he discover your face to be turned toward him. Your seeing, Lord, is your loving. And just as your gaze attends me so closely that it never turns away from me, so too your love never turns away.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 4: Cusanus’s equation of God’s seeing with God’s loving transforms the omniscience tradition from a surveillance metaphor into one of infinite care. The icon experiment—where each viewer perceives the painted eyes as directed uniquely at them—illustrates how the infinite God relates personally to each finite creature without division or partiality.
