400 Chapters on Love III.25: A Deified Soul

Maximus the Confessor, c. 580–662 AD

Introduction

Maximus the Confessor (c. 580–662) is often called the greatest Byzantine theologian. He earned his title “Confessor” by suffering imprisonment, the amputation of his right hand, and the cutting out of his tongue for his refusal to accept the Monothelite heresy (the doctrine that Christ had only one will). His theological writings are vast, dense, and profoundly influential on all subsequent Eastern Christian thought.

The Four Hundred Chapters on Love (Capita de Caritate) is one of his most accessible works, organized in four “centuries” of one hundred aphorisms each. Century 3, Chapter 25 contains one of the most concise and powerful definitions of theosis in the entire patristic corpus: a deified soul is one that, through grace, possesses all the attributes of God while remaining distinct from God in essence. This is the Maximian synthesis: the human being becomes “by grace everything that God is by nature,” without confusion of natures.

§3.24 · PG 90, col. 1024
Manuscript
Greek

Ὁ Θεὸς πεποίηκεν ἡμᾶς ἵνα γενώμεθα θείας κοινωνοὶ φύσεως

English

God made us so that we might become partakers of the divine nature, so that we might enter into eternity, and so that we might appear like him, being deified by the grace of him who begot us—through whom all that is has received its being and its coming to be.

§3.25 · PG 90, col. 1024
Manuscript
Greek

Θεοποιηθεῖσα ψυχή ἐστιν ἥτις τῇ μεθέξει τῆς θείας χάριτος πάντα ἔχει ὃσα ἔχει κατὰ φύσιν ὁ Θεός

English

A deified soul is one that, through participation in divine grace, has all the attributes that God has by nature. It has become by grace everything that God is by nature, without confusion of natures. The deified person is called “god” by grace, since God has wholly filled him.

§3.26 · PG 90, col. 1025
Manuscript
Greek

Σκοπὸς τοῦ Θεοῦ ποιήσαντος τοὺς ἀνθρώπους

English

God’s purpose in creating human beings was that they should become participants in his own nature, that they should be resplendent with the illumination of his grace, and that they should be perfectly united with him through the power of love.

§3.27 · PG 90, col. 1025
Manuscript
Greek

Ἀγάπη πλήρωμά ἐστι πασῶν τῶν ἐντολῶν

English

Love is the fulfillment of all the commandments. For whoever has attained love is established in God, and God in him. If God is love, then whoever has love has God. “And God is love, and whoever abides in love abides in God, and God abides in him.”

§3.46 · PG 90, col. 1032
Manuscript

English

The one who loves God cannot help but love every person as himself, even though he is grieved by the passions of those who are not yet purified. But when they return and amend, his joy is indescribable and boundless.

§3.99 · PG 90, col. 1048
Manuscript
Greek

Ὁ ἀληθῶς τὸν Θεὸν ἀγαπῶν πάντως ἀπερισπάστως προσεύχεται

English

The human being who truly loves God prays entirely without distraction, and the one who prays without distraction loves God truly. But the one who has his mind fixed on some earthly thing does not pray without distraction; and therefore does not love God. The mind that tarries upon some earthly thing has its passions intertwined with that thing.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3.25: This chapter is the locus classicus for the Maximian definition of theosis. The phrase “become by grace everything that God is by nature” (γέγονε χάριτι πάντα ὅσα ἐστὶν ὁ Θεὸς φύσει) is quoted more than any other single sentence in Orthodox discussions of deification. The qualifier “without confusion of natures” prevents this from collapsing into pantheism: the human essence remains human, but it is wholly permeated by divine qualities.
  2. §3.26: The idea that theosis is God’s original “purpose” in creating humanity—not a response to the fall, but the original design—is central to Maximus’s theology. Even without the fall, human beings were created to grow into divine likeness. The fall is a detour, not the reason for the journey. This optimistic anthropology stands in tension with the Augustinian emphasis on sin and depravity that dominates much Western theology.
  3. §3.27: Maximus identifies love as the means of theosis, citing 1 John 4:16. This distinguishes his approach from purely intellectual or ascetic models. For Maximus, the deified person is not primarily the one who knows the most or the one who fasts the most, but the one who loves the most. Love is not merely a consequence of theosis but its very mechanism.
