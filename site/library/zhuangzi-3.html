§Chapter 3: Cook Ding · Zhuangzi, Inner Chapters, Chapter 3

Greek

庖丁为文惠君解牛

English

Cook Ding was cutting up an ox for Lord Wen-hui. At every touch of his hand, every heave of his shoulder, every move of his feet, every thrust of his knee—zip! zoop!—he slithered the knife along with a zing, and all was in perfect rhythm. Lord Wen-hui said: "How wonderfully you have mastered your art!" Cook Ding laid down his knife and replied: "What I care about is the Way, which goes beyond mere art. When I first began cutting up oxen, all I could see was the ox itself. After three years I no longer saw the whole ox. Now I go at it by spirit and don't look with my eyes."

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 3: Cook Ding's knife has not been sharpened in nineteen years because it passes through the natural spaces in the joints. This is Zhuangzi's paradigm for wu wei—acting in perfect accord with the grain of reality. The parable influenced Chinese aesthetics, martial arts, and craft traditions, all of which prize the effortless mastery that comes from aligning with nature rather than opposing it.
