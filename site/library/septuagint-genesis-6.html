Introduction

Genesis 6 recounts the corruption of humanity and God’s decision to send the flood, sparing only Noah. The mysterious “sons of God” who take human wives became a central text in the Enochic tradition. From the Septuagint.

§6:1–4 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἐγένετο ἡνίκα ἤρξαντο οἱ ἄνθρωποι πολλοὶ γίνεσθαι ἐπὶ τῆς γῆς

English

And it came to pass when humankind began to become numerous on the earth, and daughters were born to them, that the angels of God, having seen the daughters of men that they were beautiful, took for themselves wives from all whom they chose. And the Lord God said, “My spirit shall certainly not remain among these people forever, because they are flesh.” The giants were on the earth in those days.

§6:8–9 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

Νωε δὲ εὕρεν χάριν ἐναντίον κυρίου τοῦ θεοῦ

English

But Noah found grace before the Lord God. These are the generations of Noah. Noah was a righteous man, being perfect in his generation. Noah was well-pleasing to God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6:2: The Septuagint reads “angels of God” (οἱ ἄγγελοι τοῦ θεοῦ) where the Hebrew has “sons of God” (bene elohim). This interpretive translation reflects the tradition developed extensively in 1 Enoch, where fallen angels (Watchers) descend to corrupt humanity. The Enochic reading profoundly shaped early Christian demonology.
