Introduction

Eclogue 4, the “Messianic Eclogue,” prophesies the birth of a child who will inaugurate a new Golden Age. Christians from Constantine onward read it as a pagan prophecy of Christ, making Virgil a bridge between classical and Christian worlds.

Greek original and English translation.
§Lines 4–25 · Virgil, Eclogae IV.4–25 (Mynors, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Ultima Cumaei venit iam carminis aetas; magnus ab integro saeclorum nascitur ordo. Iam redit et Virgo, redeunt Saturnia regna; iam nova progenies caelo demittitur alto.

English

Now the last age of the Cumaean prophecy has come; the great order of the ages is born anew. Now the Virgin returns, the reign of Saturn returns; now a new generation descends from heaven on high. Only do you, pure Lucina, favor the child now being born, under whom the iron race shall first cease and a golden race spring up throughout the world.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 4–25: The identity of the child has been debated since antiquity. Candidates include the expected offspring of Octavian or Pollio, or a purely symbolic figure. The Christian reading, championed by Constantine in his Oration to the Saints, transformed Virgil into a prophet despite himself.
