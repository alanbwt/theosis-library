Introduction

Pindar’s First Olympian Ode, composed for Hieron of Syracuse’s horse-race victory, opens with the declaration “Water is best” and weaves myth, praise, and philosophical reflection into the most celebrated victory ode in Greek literature.

§Lines 1–18 · Pindar, Olympian 1.1–18
Manuscript
Greek

ἄριστον μὲν ὕδωρ ὁ δὲ χρυσὸς αἰθόμενον πῦρ

English

Water is best, and gold, like a blazing fire in the night, shines preeminent among lordly wealth. But if you wish to sing of contests, my heart, look no further than the sun for a star that shines warmer by day through the empty sky, and let us not proclaim any contest greater than Olympia.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1: The opening “Water is best” (ἄριστον μὲν ὕδωρ) may echo Thales’ cosmological principle. Pindar creates a hierarchy of excellence—water, gold, Olympia, the sun—that moves from natural to human to cosmic, establishing the victory ode as a form of philosophic praise.
