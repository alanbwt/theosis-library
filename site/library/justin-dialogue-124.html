Dialogue with Trypho 124: Called Gods

Justin Martyr, c. 100–165 AD

Introduction

Justin Martyr (c. 100–165) is the earliest Christian apologist whose major works survive. His Dialogue with Trypho, a literary conversation with a learned Jew, represents the first sustained Christian engagement with Jewish scriptural interpretation. Justin argues that the Hebrew scriptures, properly understood, point to Christ and to the transformation of those who follow him.

Chapter 124 addresses Psalm 82:6, “I said, you are gods.” Justin reads this psalm as a prophecy about the destiny of believers: those who are deemed worthy to become “sons of the Most High” shall be called gods. This is significant as one of the earliest Christian exegetical treatments of the psalm that would become the single most cited biblical text in the theosis tradition.

§124 · Dialogus cum Tryphone 124 (ed. Goodspeed)
Manuscript
Greek

Κληθήσονται θεοί, υἱοὶ ὑψίστου ὄντες, καὶ ἕκαστος καθ᾽ ἑαυτὸν στήσεται καὶ κριθήσεται.

English

They shall be called gods, being sons of the Most High, and each one shall stand individually and be judged and be made worthy to become gods, having the power to become children of the Most High.

Translator's Notes

  1. §124: Justin’s exegesis of Psalm 82:6 (81:6 LXX) established a pattern that virtually every subsequent patristic author would follow. The psalm’s declaration “I said, you are gods” (εγὼ εἶπα θεοί ἐστε) was read by Jesus himself in John 10:34 as applicable to those who receive the word of God. Justin extends this: the title “gods” is not merely honorific but describes an eschatological reality. His emphasis on individual judgment alongside deification shows that he conceives of theosis not as automatic or universal but as contingent on moral worthiness.
