Geography: Abaris the Flying Priest of Apollo

Strabo, c. 64 BCE-24 AD

Introduction

Strabo of Amaseia, writing his Geography around 7 BCE–23 CE, brings a characteristically critical eye to the Hyperborean tradition. As a geographer committed to empirical knowledge, Strabo questions whether the Hyperboreans exist as a real people or are merely a poetic invention. Yet even in his skepticism, he preserves traditions that would otherwise be lost, including important details about Abaris the Hyperborean—the legendary flying priest of Apollo who carried a golden arrow around the earth.

Strabo’s treatment matters for the theosis tradition precisely because of his doubt. By the first century BCE, the Hyperborean legend had to survive the scrutiny of rationalist geography. That it did—that Strabo felt compelled to discuss it at length even while questioning it—testifies to the tradition’s deep roots in Greek religious consciousness. The Hyperboreans could not simply be dismissed because they were woven into the fabric of Apollo worship at Delos and Delphi. Strabo’s account of Abaris is particularly significant: a Hyperborean who traveled the entire world, performing purifications and prophecies, carrying an arrow that enabled him to fly or to go without food. Abaris represents the Hyperborean ideal made mobile—the divine human who walks among ordinary mortals.

§Geography I.3.22 · Strabo, Geography I.3.22
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ τὰ περὶ τῶν Ὑπερβορέων δὲ μυθώδη. οἱ γὰρ περὶ τῶν Ὑπερβορέων γράψαντες, ἐν οἷς καὶ Σιμωνίδης καὶ Πίνδαρος καὶ Αἰσχύλος καὶ Ἑκαταῖος, οὐχ ὁ Μιλήσιος ἀλλ’ ὁ Ἀβδηρίτης, ἄπιστα καὶ τερατώδη λέγουσιν.

English

But the account of the Hyperboreans also belongs to the category of myths. For those who have written about the Hyperboreans, including Simonides, Pindar, Aeschylus, and Hecataeus—not the Milesian but the Abderite—and others, say things that are incredible and fabulous. Still, we should not examine these stories too closely, since it is the way of poets to invent such things. And perhaps the tradition arose from the fact that a certain people do dwell far to the north, near the ocean, at the ends of the earth, and that their country lies under the Bears, beyond the northerly blasts of Boreas.

§Geography VII.3.1 · Strabo, Geography VII.3.1
Manuscript
Greek

οἱ μὲν οὖν ἕλληνες τούς τε Σκύθας τοῖς Ὑπερβορέοις παραπλησίως ἐν τοῖς δικαιοτάτοις ἔταττον. τοῦ δὲ Ἀβάριδος πέρι τοῦ Ὑπερβορέου λέγεται, τὸν οἰστὸν περιενεχθῆναι τὴν γῆν ὅλην φέροντα τὸν οἰστόν, μηδὲν σιτούμενον.

English

Now the Greeks classed the Scythians, like the Hyperboreans, among the most just of men. Homer calls them “the most just of men” and “milk-drinkers.” Ephorus says that the reason for their good repute for justice was their simple mode of life. For they lived on milk and cheese from their mares, and on the fruit of the earth; they had no interest in money or commerce; and they shared all things in common. And concerning the Hyperboreans, Abaris is said to have been one of them—he who went about the whole world carrying his arrow, eating nothing, as Herodotus says.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.3.22: Strabo’s rationalist concession—“perhaps the tradition arose from the fact that a certain people do dwell far to the north”—represents the characteristic move of ancient geographical criticism: to preserve the kernel of truth while discarding the mythological husk. His careful distinction between Hecataeus of Miletus (the earlier geographer) and Hecataeus of Abdera (the author of On the Hyperboreans) shows scholarly precision. The mention that Aeschylus wrote about the Hyperboreans is tantalizing, since no surviving Aeschylean text contains such a passage, suggesting lost works.
  2. §VII.3.1 (justice): Strabo’s linkage of the Scythians and the Hyperboreans as “most just” peoples reflects a widespread Greek tendency to idealize distant northern peoples. Homer’s “most just of men” (Iliad XIII.6) originally referred to the Thracians or Mysians, but by Strabo’s time the phrase had been extended to encompass all noble northern barbarians. The association of simplicity with justice—no money, no commerce, all things in common—anticipates the later philosophical trope of the “noble savage” but has deeper roots in the Greek conviction that moral corruption follows material complexity.
  3. §VII.3.1 (Abaris): Abaris the Hyperborean is one of the most enigmatic figures in ancient tradition. He appears in Herodotus, Pindar, Plato, and numerous later sources. The arrow he carries is Apollo’s—a symbol of the god’s power. His ability to travel the world without eating suggests he is sustained by divine power alone, a living demonstration of what theosis looks like in practice: a human being so thoroughly filled with the divine that ordinary physical necessities are transcended. Later Pythagorean tradition claimed that Abaris met Pythagoras and recognized him as Apollo incarnate, creating a direct lineage from Hyperborean piety to Greek philosophy.
