Introduction

Book 1 of Origen’s De Principiis (c. 230 CE) is the first systematic Christian theology. It addresses God, Christ, the Holy Spirit, rational creatures, and the end of all things. Much of the original Greek is lost; the work survives primarily in Rufinus’s Latin translation.

§1.1.1 · Origen, De Principiis
Manuscript
Greek

[Latin: Scio quoniam quidam quaerent dicere Deum corpus esse]

English

I know that some will seek to say that God is a body, for they find it written that God is a consuming fire, and that the Spirit of the Lord fills the world. But we say that God is not a body. He is the simple intellectual nature, admitting within himself no addition of any kind, so that he cannot be thought to have in himself a greater and a lesser, but is unity throughout, and the mind and fount from which all intellectual nature or mind takes its beginning.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1.1: Origen’s insistence that God is incorporeal was directed against both Stoic materialism and anthropomorphic popular piety. His definition of God as “simple intellectual nature” (natura intellectualis simplex) draws on Middle Platonic theology and set the trajectory for all subsequent Christian metaphysics. Some of his more speculative conclusions (pre-existence of souls, apokatastasis) were later condemned.
