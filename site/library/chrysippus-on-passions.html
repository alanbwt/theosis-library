Chrysippus On Passions

Unknown,

Introduction

Chrysippus’s theory of the passions (pathe) defined them as false judgments of value—mistaken beliefs that something indifferent is truly good or evil. This cognitive theory of emotion made the elimination of passions a matter of correcting one’s beliefs.

§On the Passions (fragments) · SVF III.456, 461 (Galen, De Placitis Hippocratis et Platonis IV.2)
Manuscript
Greek

τὸ πάθος εἶναι λόγον πονηρόν καὶ ἀκόλαστον καὶ κρίσιν

English

The passion is itself a judgment. Grief is a fresh judgment that an evil is present; fear is a judgment that an evil is to come; desire is a judgment that a future thing is good; pleasure is a judgment that a present thing is good. All passions arise from false opinion and weak assent.

Translator's Notes

  1. §On the Passions: Chrysippus’s cognitive theory of emotion was revolutionary: passions are not irrational forces opposing reason but are themselves forms of reason gone wrong. This challenged the Platonic and Aristotelian view of the soul as divided into rational and irrational parts.
