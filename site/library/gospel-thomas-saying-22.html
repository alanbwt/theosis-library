Gospel of Thomas, Saying 22: When You Make the Two One

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

Saying 22 of the Gospel of Thomas contains one of the most radical statements in all early Christian literature. When his disciples ask when they will enter the kingdom, Jesus does not answer with moral instruction or apocalyptic timetable. Instead he points to nursing infants and declares that entry requires making “the two one”—the inside like the outside, the above like the below, the male and the female into a single one. This is not metaphor in the usual sense. It is a description of ontological reversal: the dissolution of every binary that structures fallen existence.

The saying has no direct parallel in the canonical Gospels, though echoes appear in Galatians 3:28 (“there is neither male nor female”) and 2 Corinthians 5:17 (“a new creation”). The Coptic text survives in Nag Hammadi Codex II, with fragments of the Greek preserved in the Oxyrhynchus Papyri. The vision here is not Gnostic escapism but total transformation—the body itself remade, eyes replaced with an eye, a hand with a hand, the human form reconstituted as an “image” (eikon) in place of the old image.

Translator's Notes

  1. §22b: The phrase “make the two one” recurs throughout Thomas and in other Nag Hammadi texts. It describes the reversal of the primordial division—the splitting of the original androgynous human into male and female as narrated in Genesis. The Gospel of Philip develops this theme extensively. The idea has deep roots in Platonic thought (Aristophanes’ speech in the Symposium) and finds resonance in Paul’s claim that in Christ “there is neither male nor female” (Galatians 3:28).
  2. §22c: The replacement of bodily members—eye for eye, hand for hand—is not mutilation but transfiguration. The language deliberately inverts the lex talionis of Exodus 21:24. Where the old law demanded destruction for destruction, the new creation demands replacement of the corruptible body with a spiritual one. Paul uses similar language in 1 Corinthians 15:42–44: “It is sown a natural body, it is raised a spiritual body.” The “image in place of an image” (eikon) echoes Genesis 1:26—humanity made in the image of God, now remade into the true image.
