Introduction

The Oresteia of Aeschylus (458 BCE) is the only surviving Greek tragic trilogy. It traces the cycle of blood vengeance from Agamemnon’s murder through Orestes’s matricide to the founding of the Athenian law court. Violence is finally resolved through justice.

§Agamemnon, Lines 160–183 · Aeschylus, Agamemnon
Manuscript
Greek

Ζεύς, ὅστις ποτ' ἐστίν, εἰ τόδ' αὐτῷ φίλον κεκλημένῳ

English

Zeus, whoever he is, if this name is pleasing to him by this name I address him. I can compare nothing to Zeus, weighing all things, except Zeus himself, if from my mind this vain burden must be cast aside in truth. The one who was great before, swelling with boldness for every fight, shall not even be spoken of as having existed. And he who came after has met his match and is gone. But whoever gladly sings the victory of Zeus shall gain wisdom altogether: Zeus, who set mortals on the path to understanding, who established the law that wisdom comes through suffering.

§Eumenides, Lines 681–710 · Aeschylus, Eumenides
Manuscript
Greek

κλύοιτ' ἂν ἤδη θεσμόν, Ἀττικὸς λεώς

English

Hear now my ordinance, people of Athens. As you judge this first trial for bloodshed, so for the future this council of judges shall remain for the people of Aegeus forever. This hill of Ares shall be the seat of reverence and its kinsman fear, which shall restrain the citizens from injustice by day and night alike.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Agamemnon 176–178: The phrase pathei mathos (πάθει μάθος), “wisdom through suffering,” is the theological center of the Oresteia and perhaps of all Greek tragedy. It asserts that understanding comes not through detached contemplation but through the experience of pain. Hebrews 5:8 echoes this principle: “Although he was a Son, he learned obedience through what he suffered.”
