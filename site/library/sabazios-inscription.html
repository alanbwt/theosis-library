Introduction

Sabazios inscriptions (c. 2nd–3rd century CE) attest the worship of this Phrygian-Thracian deity associated with Zeus and Dionysus. The cult spread widely through the Roman Empire and was sometimes conflated with Judaism due to the similarity between Sabazios and Sabaoth.

§CIL VI.142 · Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum VI.142
Manuscript
Greek

Iovi Sabazio invicto sancto. L. Cornelius Scipio lib(ertus) aram visu monitus d(edicavit) v(otum) s(olvit) l(ibens) m(erito).

English

To Jupiter Sabazius, unconquered and holy. Lucius Cornelius Scipio, freedman, dedicated this altar on account of a vision, willingly and deservedly.

Translator's Notes

  1. §CIL VI.142: The syncretism of Sabazios with both Jupiter and Dionysus illustrates the fluid boundaries of ancient polytheism. Valerius Maximus records that Jews were expelled from Rome in 139 BCE for attempting to “infect Roman morals with the worship of Jupiter Sabazius,” suggesting early Roman confusion between Jewish and Sabazian worship.
