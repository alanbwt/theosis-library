Yasna 30: The Two Spirits

Zarathustra (Zoroaster), c. 1500-1000 BCE

Introduction

Yasna 30 is the foundational text of ethical dualism in human history. Composed by Zarathustra (Zoroaster) himself—likely between 1500 and 1000 BCE, making the Gathas among the oldest surviving religious poetry—this hymn describes the primordial choice between two spirits: Spenta Mainyu (the Bounteous Spirit, associated with truth, asha) and Angra Mainyu (the Destructive Spirit, associated with the lie, druj). These two spirits are twins who chose their natures freely, and every conscious being must make the same choice.

The influence of this passage on subsequent religious thought is immense. Before Zarathustra, the ancient Near Eastern religions had no systematic doctrine of cosmic good versus evil. After him, the idea appears everywhere: the Dead Sea Scrolls speak of the “Spirit of Truth” and the “Spirit of Falsehood”; Jewish apocalypticism develops Satan as God’s adversary; Christianity inherits the cosmic battle between light and darkness; Gnosticism builds entire mythologies around it. Yasna 30 is where it all begins.

§30.1 · Yasna 30, Stanza 1 (Gathas of Zarathustra)
Manuscript
Avestan

At ta vakhshya ishentu yathanish mazdathanish haurvatatem ashim-cha hyat sravishtā ahurō vahmeng ahubya.

English

Now I shall speak to those who wish to hear of the two primal spirits, who declared themselves as a pair, in thought, word, and deed—the better and the bad. And of these two, the wise rightly choose, not the foolish.

§30.2 · Yasna 30, Stanza 2
Manuscript
Avestan

Sraotā gaeushāish vahishtā avanatā sūchā manangha, āvarēnāu vichithāhyai narēm narem khvakhyai tanuyē, para mazē yaonhē ahmāi nē sazdō pathibyō.

English

Hear with your ears the best things. Reflect with a clear mind—each one of you—upon the two choices of decision, being aware to declare yourselves to Him before the great retribution.

§30.3 · Yasna 30, Stanza 3
Manuscript
Avestan

At tā mainyū pauruuyē yā yēmā khvafnā asrvatēm, manahiā vachaĭhiā shyaothanā hī vahyah akēm-chā.

English

Now the two primal spirits, who revealed themselves in vision as twins, are the Better and the Bad, in thought, word, and deed. And between these two, the wise choose rightly, not the foolish.

§30.4–5 · Yasna 30, Stanzas 4–5
Manuscript
Avestan

At tā mainyū hamēmīthēm dazdē paouruuyīm gaem-chā ajyāiti-chā, apaēm-chā anghvah anghat apiyēm achīshtahīm drugēvantaeibhyah, at ashavanē vahishtēm manah.

English

And when these two spirits first came together, they created life and death, and that at the end the worst existence shall be for the followers of the lie, but the best mind for the follower of truth. Of these two spirits, the one who followed the lie chose to do the worst things, but the Most Bounteous Spirit, clothed in the hardest stones of heaven, chose truth—as do those who shall satisfy Ahura Mazda by righteous deeds.

§30.9–11 · Yasna 30, Stanzas 9–11
Manuscript
Avestan

At chī mahī vah-ishām yōi īm frashyēm kerēnaun ahum. Mazdāonhō anghatū hachā armaitish hachā ashāt.

English

So may we be those who make this existence brilliant! You Wise Lords, come together with Truth, for there, where understanding converges, is the abode of the Good Mind. Through truth, the glory of Ahura Mazda shall increase, and through good thinking he will give wholeness and immortality. The wise person shall proclaim it.

Translator's Notes

  1. §30.3: The Avestan term mainyū means “spirit” or “mentality.” The two spirits are not equal gods in the later Manichaean sense; they are emanations or aspects of a single cosmic order created by Ahura Mazda. Zarathustra’s dualism is ethical, not ontological—good and evil are choices, not substances. This distinction was sometimes lost in later Zoroastrian theology, where Angra Mainyu (Ahriman) became an independent cosmic force. The original Gathic teaching is closer to the Jewish yetzer ha-tov and yetzer ha-ra (good and evil inclinations) than to Manichaean absolute dualism.
  2. §30.4–5: The phrase “created life and death” (gaem-cha ajyaiti-cha) directly parallels Isaiah 45:7: “I form light and create darkness, I make peace and create evil.” The Hebrew Bible was composed during and after the Babylonian Exile (586–539 BCE), when Jews lived under Persian Zoroastrian rule. The influence of Zoroastrian concepts—resurrection, final judgment, cosmic dualism, angels and demons—on Judaism during this period is well documented by scholars. Yasna 30 is the original source of ideas that would reshape the entire biblical tradition.
  3. §30.9–11: The Avestan frasho-kereti (making brilliant, renovation) is the Zoroastrian concept of the final perfection of the world. It is the earliest known eschatology: the idea that history has a direction and will culminate in a cosmic renewal. This concept directly influenced the Jewish-Christian apocalyptic tradition—the “new heaven and new earth” of Revelation 21:1 is a distant echo of the Zoroastrian frashokereti.
