Metaphysics XII: The Unmoved Mover

Metaphysics XII: The Unmoved Mover
Aristotle, 384-322 BCE
Codex Parisinus Graecus 1853

Introduction

In Book XII (Lambda) of the Metaphysics, Aristotle arrives at the summit of his philosophy: the Unmoved Mover, a being of pure actuality with no potentiality, whose only activity is thinking itself. This God does not create the world but moves it by being the object of cosmic desire — all things strive toward the perfection that the Unmoved Mover already is. The concept of 'thinking thinking itself' (noēsis noēseōs noēsis) became central to Islamic, Jewish, and Christian theology for two millennia.

Greek original and English translation.
§1072a-b · Met. XII.7 1072a-b
Greek

ἔστι τι ὃ οὐ κινούμενον κινεῖ, ἀΐδιον καὶ οὐσία καὶ ἐνέργεια οὖσα. κινεῖ δὲ ὡς ἐρώμενον.

English

There is something which moves without being moved, being eternal, and both substance and actuality. It moves as an object of desire.

§1072b · Met. XII.7 1072b
Greek

ἡ γὰρ νοῦ ἐνέργεια ζωή, ἐκεῖνος δὲ ἡ ἐνέργεια· ἐνέργεια δὲ ἡ καθ᾿ αὑτὴν ἐκείνου ζωὴ ἀρίστη καὶ ἀΐδιος.

English

The activity of Mind is life, and God is that activity. God's self-dependent activity is life most good and eternal.

§1074b · Met. XII.9 1074b
Greek

αὑτὸν ἄρα νοεῖ, εἴπερ ἐστὶ τὸ κράτιστον, καὶ ἔστιν ἡ νόησις νοήσεως νόησις.

English

It thinks itself, if indeed it is the most excellent thing, and its thinking is a thinking of thinking.
