Psalm 82: "I Said, You Are Gods"

Ψαλμός 81 (LXX)
Psalmist (attributed to Asaph), c. 10th-6th c. BCE
Septuagint (LXX), Psalm 81 · Rahlfs, A. (ed.), Septuaginta, Stuttgart: Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, 2006

Introduction

This is the psalm Jesus quotes when accused of blasphemy (John 10:34). God stands in the divine assembly, judges the gods for failing to defend the poor, and declares: “I said, you are gods, all of you sons of the Most High.” The psalm is only eight verses, but it is the most important Old Testament text for the theosis debate. The Hebrew has Elohim (אלהים); the Septuagint translates it as theoi (θεοί)—the same word used for God himself. The key question that has divided interpreters for two millennia: who are the “gods” being addressed? Angels? Human rulers acting as God’s delegates? The people of Israel at Sinai? Or does the psalm preserve an older, more radical claim about human participation in divinity?

The numbering differs between traditions. In the Septuagint and Vulgate this is Psalm 81; in the Hebrew Masoretic Text and modern English Bibles it is Psalm 82. The translation follows the Septuagint Greek. The Hebrew is cited in the notes where it diverges or where the original terminology matters.

Translator's Notes

  1. §82:1, 82:6: The Hebrew word translated “gods” throughout this psalm is Elohim (אלהים), the same word used for God himself in Genesis 1:1. The Septuagint renders it theoi (θεοί)—again, the standard Greek word for gods. This creates a genuine ambiguity that no translation can resolve. In verse 1, God (ho theos) stands in the assembly of gods (theon); in verse 6, God says “you are gods” (theoi este). The word is the same in every instance. English translations that render the subordinate figures as “judges” or “magistrates” are interpreting, not translating. The text says gods.
  2. §82:1, 82:6–7: The identity of these “gods” has been debated since antiquity. The major readings: (1) They are divine beings, members of a heavenly council—this is the oldest interpretation and fits the ancient Near Eastern background, where gods assembled under a chief deity. (2) They are human rulers or judges who exercise God’s authority on earth—the Targum takes this view, and it became dominant in rabbinic Judaism. (3) They are the people of Israel at Sinai, who received the Torah and were temporarily elevated to divine status—this reading appears in the Talmud (Avodah Zarah 5a) and is the one Jesus seems to invoke in John 10:34–36. (4) They are angelic beings who were given charge over the nations (cf. Deuteronomy 32:8 LXX, which reads “sons of God” where the Masoretic has “sons of Israel”). Each reading yields a different theology of human participation in divinity.
  3. §82:6 (cf. John 10:34–36): When Jesus is accused of blasphemy for saying “I and the Father are one,” he responds by quoting this verse: “Is it not written in your Law, ‘I said, you are gods’? If he called them gods to whom the word of God came—and Scripture cannot be broken—do you say of him whom the Father consecrated and sent into the world, ‘You are blaspheming,’ because I said, ‘I am the Son of God’?” The argument is a qal vahomer (lesser to greater): if Scripture itself calls mere recipients of God’s word “gods,” how much more may the one whom God sent claim the title? The Church Fathers read this as Jesus affirming human deification: Athanasius, in Against the Arians, argues that the psalm proves humans can be called gods by grace, while Christ alone is God by nature. This distinction—theosis by participation, not by nature—became the orthodox framework.
