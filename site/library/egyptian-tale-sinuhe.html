Introduction

The Tale of Sinuhe is the masterpiece of Egyptian literature, narrating the flight, exile, and eventual return of an official who fled Egypt after the assassination of Pharaoh Amenemhat I (c. 1960 BCE). It explores themes of identity, loyalty, and the longing for home.

Manuscript
Greek

[Hieratic Egyptian text, Middle Kingdom, c. 1875 BCE]

English

The hereditary prince, the royal seal-bearer, Sinuhe, says: I was a follower who followed his lord, a servant of the royal harem. I fled when I heard the news of the king’s death. My heart was not in my body; my arms spread apart; trembling fell upon all my limbs. Years later the king summoned me home, saying: ‘What have you done that you should be punished? Return, and you shall see the homeland.’

Translator's Notes

  1. §Opening: Sinuhe’s flight is never fully explained—he may have been implicated in the assassination or simply panicked. This ambiguity is deliberate, making the tale a meditation on guilt, exile, and the grace of royal (and by extension, divine) forgiveness. It was the most copied literary text in ancient Egypt.
