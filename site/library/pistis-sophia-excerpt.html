Pistis Sophia: Sophia's Fall and Redemption

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Pistis Sophia is a massive Gnostic text, likely composed in the third century, preserved in a single Coptic manuscript (the Askew Codex, now in the British Library). It presents itself as a post-resurrection dialogue: Jesus has spent eleven years teaching his disciples after rising from the dead, and now reveals to them the highest mysteries of the cosmos. The title means “Faith-Wisdom,” and the central drama concerns the fall and redemption of Sophia (Wisdom), a divine being who descended from the Pleroma (the fullness of divine light) and became trapped in the lower realms by the archons.

The opening chapters establish the setting and introduce the cosmological framework. Jesus ascends in a pillar of light, the disciples are terrified, and he returns transformed, radiant, speaking in a voice they barely recognize. He then begins to recount Sophia’s story: how she saw a false light below her, pursued it, and was dragged down by the rulers of the material world. Her cries for help—her “repentances”—form some of the most haunting devotional poetry in the Gnostic corpus. The text is essential to the theosis question because it maps the journey of the soul: fall, suffering, repentance, and restoration to divine fullness.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ch. 1: The “first mystery” is a recurring concept in the Pistis Sophia. It refers to the highest divine principle, sometimes identified with Christ himself, sometimes with the Father. The phrase “the mystery within the veil” echoes the Holy of Holies in the Jewish Temple, suggesting that Gnostic cosmology repurposes Temple theology.
  2. §Ch. 3: The description of light-beams of “different kinds and types” reflects the Gnostic concept that divine light is not uniform but emanates in distinct qualities or aspects, each corresponding to a different level of spiritual reality. This graduated light-theology is central to how the Pistis Sophia maps the structure of the cosmos.
  3. §Ch. 6: The “thirteenth aeon” is the dwelling place of Pistis Sophia herself. In the Gnostic system, the twelve lower aeons correspond roughly to the zodiacal powers, while the thirteenth stands outside and above this system. Sophia’s position in the thirteenth aeon makes her a liminal figure: not fully within the Pleroma, but above the material world.
