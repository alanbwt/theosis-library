Eunomius: The Divine Essence Is Incommunicable

Eunomius, c. 335-394 AD

Introduction

Eunomius of Cyzicus (c. 335–394) was the most philosophically rigorous of the Arian theologians. Where Arius had called the Son a “perfect creature,” Eunomius went further: the Son is “unlike” (anomoios, ἀνόμοιος) the Father in essence. His Apology, written around 360, argues that the essence of God is “unbegottenness” (agennesia, ἀγεννησία) itself. Since the Son is begotten, he cannot share in the divine essence. The gap between God and everything else is absolute and unbridgeable.

This is the most radical anti-theosis position within the Christian tradition. If even Christ cannot share the Father’s essence, then human participation in the divine nature is inconceivable. The Cappadocian Fathers—Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Gregory of Nyssa—devoted enormous intellectual energy to refuting Eunomius, and in doing so developed the distinction between God’s unknowable essence and his communicable energies that would become central to Orthodox theosis theology.

§7 · Eunomius, Apology 7
Manuscript
Greek

τὴν οὐσίαν τοῦ θεοῦ αὐτὴν τὴν ἀγεννησίαν εἶναι.

English

The essence of God is unbegottenness itself. This is not a name applied by human invention, nor is it one among many attributes. It is what God is. To be God and to be unbegotten are the same thing.

§8 · Eunomius, Apology 8
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ δὲ ἀγέννητος ὁ θεός, γεννητὸς δὲ ὁ υἱός, ἀνόμοιος κατ’ οὐσίαν.

English

If unbegottenness is the essence of God, then the begotten cannot share in that essence. The Son, being begotten, is unlike the Father in essence. He is the first and greatest of all begotten things, but he does not possess the nature of the Unbegotten.

§12 · Eunomius, Apology 12
Manuscript
Greek

γνωστὴν εἶναι τὴν οὐσίαν τοῦ θεοῦ… οἱ δὲ τὴν οὐσίαν ἀκατάληπτον λέγοντες ἄγνωστον θεὸν σέβουσιν.

English

We can know the essence of God with perfect clarity. It is not hidden behind mystery or veiled in darkness. God is unbegotten—this we know, and this is all that God is. Those who claim that God’s essence is incomprehensible make knowledge of God impossible and worship an unknown deity.

§15 · Eunomius, Apology 15
Manuscript
Greek

βουλήσει τοῦ πατρὸς γεννηθέντα… ἀνόμοιον τῷ βουληθέντι.

English

The Son was generated by the will of the Father. He is the product of the Father’s activity, not an emanation of the Father’s substance. What is generated by will is unlike the one who wills it, just as a craftsman’s product is unlike the craftsman himself.

Translator's Notes

  1. §7: Eunomius’s identification of God’s essence with agennesia (ἀγεννησία, “unbegottenness”) is a philosophical move of considerable audacity. It reduces the infinite God to a single knowable predicate. The Cappadocians attacked this on multiple fronts: Basil argued that “unbegotten” is a negative term describing how God exists, not what God is. Gregory of Nyssa argued that the divine essence is inexhaustible and cannot be captured by any single concept.
  2. §12: Eunomius’s claim that the human mind can fully comprehend God’s essence is, paradoxically, both rationalist and anti-theosis. If God can be entirely known by the intellect, there is no need for deification—philosophy suffices. But if the divine nature is fully knowable, it is also fully bounded, which makes participation in it logically impossible. Gregory of Nyssa’s doctrine of epektasis—eternal progress into the infinite God—was developed directly against this Eunomian rationalism.
