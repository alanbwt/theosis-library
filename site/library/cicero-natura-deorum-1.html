De Natura Deorum I: Three Views of the Gods

Cicero, 106-43 BCE

Introduction

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) wrote De Natura Deorum (On the Nature of the Gods) in 45 BCE, staging a philosophical dialogue among representatives of the three major Roman schools. Velleius speaks for the Epicureans: the gods exist but are indifferent to human affairs. Balbus speaks for the Stoics: the gods are identical with nature, and divine providence permeates the cosmos. Cotta speaks for the Academic Sceptics: we cannot know the nature of the gods with certainty, and all theological claims should be held provisionally.

The dialogue is invaluable for the theosis question because it preserves the full spectrum of pagan philosophical theology in a single text. The Epicurean position makes theosis irrelevant (the gods do not care). The Stoic position makes it natural (the divine logos is already within us). The Academic position makes it unknowable (we cannot be sure enough about the gods to say whether becoming like them is possible). Cicero’s own sympathies lie with the Academics, but his real achievement is showing that the question “what are the gods?” was as contested in the ancient world as it is today.

§I.18 · Cicero, De Natura Deorum I.18 (Velleius speaking)
Manuscript
Latin

Solus enim vidit primum esse deos, quod in omnium animis eorum notionem impressisset ipsa natura. Quae est enim gens aut quod genus hominum, quod non habeat sine doctrina anticipationem quandam deorum? quam appellat πρόληψιν Epicurus.

English

Epicurus alone perceived that the gods exist because nature herself has imprinted a conception of them on the minds of all people. For what nation or what race of men does not have, without being taught, some preconception of the gods? Epicurus calls this prolepsis—that is, a kind of preconceived notion of a thing, without which nothing can be understood, investigated, or discussed.

§I.39 · Cicero, De Natura Deorum I.39 (Velleius speaking)
Manuscript
Latin

Natura deorum aeterna sit et beatissima. Nec enim quicquam aliud restat quod natura desideret.

English

The divine nature must be eternal and blessed, free from every burden and every task. It neither labours itself nor imposes labour on others. It is not moved by anger or by favour, for everything of that kind belongs to weakness.

§II.4 · Cicero, De Natura Deorum II.4 (Balbus speaking)
Manuscript
Latin

Cum enim non oculis solum sed etiam ratione coelestia contemplamur.

English

When we gaze up at the sky and contemplate the heavenly bodies, what can be so obvious and so manifest as that there must exist some power possessing transcendent intelligence by whom these things are governed? If anyone doubts this, I really cannot understand why he should not also doubt whether the sun exists.

§III.6 · Cicero, De Natura Deorum III.6 (Cotta speaking)
Manuscript
Latin

Anum me putatis, quae ista credam? Etsi quid natura deorum sit nondum plane intellego, tamen quid non sit plane dicam.

English

Do you think I am some old woman who would believe such things? Even if I cannot explain what the nature of the gods is, I would still be prepared to say what it is not. I say that the gods do not have human form. But you will ask me: what form do they have? I do not know, and I am not ashamed to admit it in a matter where knowledge is so difficult.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.18: The Epicurean concept of prolepsis (πρόληψις, “preconception”) holds that all human beings have an innate, pre-theoretical notion of the gods as blessed and eternal. This notion comes not from revelation or tradition but from the impact of atomic images (eidola) emanating from the gods’ bodies. The universality of religious belief is thus explained materialistically—everyone perceives the gods because the gods emit particles, not because the soul has a divine origin.
  2. §III.6: Cotta’s Academic Scepticism represents a third position in the theosis debate: not that deification is impossible (Epicurean), not that it is natural (Stoic), but that we lack sufficient knowledge to settle the question. If we cannot determine the nature of the gods, we cannot determine whether humans can become like them. This epistemic humility was Cicero’s own position, and it anticipates the agnostic strain in modern philosophy of religion.
