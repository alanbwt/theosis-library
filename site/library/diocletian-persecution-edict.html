Diocletian Persecution Edict

Unknown,

Introduction

The first edict of Diocletian against the Christians (303 CE) ordered the destruction of churches, the burning of scriptures, and the removal of Christians from positions of honor. It inaugurated the Great Persecution, the last and most severe Roman campaign against Christianity.

§Edict provisions · Lactantius, De Mortibus Persecutorum 13; Eusebius, HE VIII.2
Manuscript
Greek

Decretum est ut ecclesiae solo aequarentur, ut scripturae igni traderentur, ut honorati degradarentur, ut domestici si in professione Christiana persisterent libertate privarentur.

English

It was decreed that the churches should be razed to the ground, that the scriptures should be destroyed by fire, that those holding positions of honor should be degraded, and that household servants, if they persisted in the Christian profession, should be deprived of their liberty.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Edict provisions: Diocletian’s persecution (303–311 CE) was the last systematic attempt to suppress Christianity. Its failure proved Tertullian’s maxim: the persecution produced more martyrs, and within a decade Constantine had legalized the faith.
