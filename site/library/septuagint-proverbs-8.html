Proverbs 8:22-31: Wisdom Before Creation

Παροιμίαι
Solomon (attributed), traditional
Septuagint (LXX)

Introduction

Proverbs 8:22–31 is the great speech of personified Wisdom (Sophia, Σοφία), who declares herself present at the creation of the world. She was there “before the hills,” “before the depths,” “when he established the heavens.” She was beside God as a “master worker” (harmosousa, ἁρμόζουσα) or “nursling,” depending on how one reads the Hebrew. The passage stands at the centre of one of the most consequential theological debates in history: the Arian controversy of the fourth century.

The Septuagint’s rendering of verse 22 became the Arians’ most powerful proof-text. Where the Hebrew is ambiguous—qanah can mean “created,” “acquired,” or “begot”—the Septuagint chose “created”: ektise me (ἔκτισέ με), “he created me.” If Christ is the Wisdom of God (as Paul says in 1 Corinthians 1:24), and Wisdom was “created,” then Christ is a creature—exalted, pre-existent, but not eternal God. The Nicene party responded that “created” here must be understood as “begot” or “established,” and that the passage describes the eternal generation of the Son. The Council of Nicaea (325) settled the matter doctrinally, but the exegetical argument over these verses continued for decades.

English

The Lord created me (ektise me, ἔκτισέ με) as the beginning of his ways, for his works. Before the age he established me, in the beginning, before he made the earth, and before he made the depths, before the springs of waters came forth, before the mountains were settled, and before all hills, he begets me (genna me, γεννᾷ με).

English

When he prepared the heaven, I was present with him, and when he marked out his own throne upon the winds, and when he made strong the clouds above, and when he secured the fountains of the earth, and when he made strong the foundations of the earth, I was beside him, fitting all things together (harmosousa, ἁρμόζουσα). I was that in which he delighted; daily I rejoiced before his face at every time, when he rejoiced at having completed the world, and rejoiced among the sons of men.

Translator's Notes

  1. §8:22: The Septuagint’s ektisen (ἔκτισεν, “he created”) translates the Hebrew qanani, which more naturally means “he acquired me” or “he possessed me.” The translation choice was fateful. Arius of Alexandria (c. 256–336) built his entire theology on this verse: if Wisdom—identified with the Son—was “created,” then there was a time when the Son did not exist. Athanasius replied in his Orations against the Arians that ektisen here refers not to creation from nothing but to the Incarnation, or alternatively that it must be read in light of verse 25, where the verb shifts to genna (“begets”). The Nicene Creed’s insistence that the Son is “begotten, not made” is a direct response to the Arian reading of this verse.
  2. §8:25: The shift from ektisen (“created,” v. 22) to genna (“begets,” v. 25) within the same speech was the Nicene party’s strongest counter-argument. If Wisdom says both “he created me” and “he begets me,” then the first term must be interpreted in light of the second. Begetting implies the communication of nature: what is begotten shares the nature of the one who begets. This is the logic behind the Nicene formula homoousios (“of one substance”)—the Son shares the Father’s being because he is begotten from it, not made as an external product.
  3. §8:27–31: The term harmosousa (ἁρμόζουσα) is the Septuagint’s rendering of the notoriously difficult Hebrew amon, which has been variously interpreted as “master worker,” “nursling,” or “faithful one.” The Greek choice—“fitting together, joining, harmonizing”—presents Wisdom as the cosmic architect, the principle that gives the world its order and coherence. This image converges with the Stoic concept of the Logos as the rational structure of reality, and with the Johannine Prologue’s claim that “all things came into being through him.” The passage closes with Wisdom “rejoicing among the sons of men”—an image that prefigures the Incarnation in patristic reading.
