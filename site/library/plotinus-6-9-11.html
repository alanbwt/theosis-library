Enneads VI.9.11: The Flight of the Alone to the Alone

Plotinus, 204-270 AD

Introduction

Enneads VI.9 is Plotinus’s treatise On the Good, or the One, and its penultimate section (VI.9.11) contains the phrase that has defined Neoplatonic mysticism ever since: “the flight of the alone to the Alone” (φυγὴ μόνου πρὸς μόνον). This is Plotinus’s account of the mystical union with the One—the moment when the soul, having ascended through the levels of reality (Matter, Soul, Nous), makes the final leap beyond thought itself into direct contact with the source of all being.

Porphyry reports in his biography that Plotinus achieved this union four times during the years they were together. The passage is not speculative philosophy but a description of experience. Plotinus insists that in the moment of union, there is no duality—no seer and seen, no subject and object. The soul does not contemplate the One; it becomes one with it. This is the highest claim of Neoplatonic theosis, and it set the terms for mystical theology in Judaism (Kabbalah), Christianity (Pseudo-Dionysius, Eckhart), and Islam (Sufism) for the next fifteen centuries.

§VI.9.11 [1–10] · Plotinus, Enneads VI.9.11
Manuscript
Greek

ἐπεὶ οὐ δύο ἦν, ἀλλὰ ἓν ἦν αὐτὸς ὁ ἰδὼν πρὸς τὸ ὑφ’ αὑτοῦ ὁρώμενον

English

Since there were not two things but the seer himself was one with the seen—for it was not really seen but united to him—if he should remember what he was when he merged with the One, he would have an image of the One in himself. He himself was one, having no distinction in himself either in relation to himself or to other things. For there was no movement in him, no passion, no desire for another thing was present in him after the ascent. Not even reason, not even thought was present. He himself was not there, if we must say even this. But as if caught away or possessed by a god, he had passed in stillness into a state of solitude, his being at rest and not inclining to any side.

§VI.9.11 [10–20] · Plotinus, Enneads VI.9.11
Manuscript
Greek

οὐδὲ πρὸς αὑτόν· πάντη ἀτρεμὴς καὶ οἷον στάσις γενόμενος

English

He did not turn to anything at all, not even to himself. He was altogether at rest and had become a kind of stillness. He did not concern himself even with the beautiful, but had already gone beyond beauty and gone beyond the choir of the virtues. He was like someone who, entering the inner sanctuary, has left the statues in the temple behind him—they will be the first things he sees when he comes out of the sanctuary, after the vision within, after communion not with a statue or an image but with the divine itself.

§VI.9.11 [20–35] · Plotinus, Enneads VI.9.11
Manuscript
Greek

οὐκ ἂν οὖν οὐδὲ ὅρασις λέγοιτο, ἀλλὰ ἄλλος τρόπος τοῦ ἰδεῖν, ἔκστασις καὶ ἁπλωσις καὶ ἐπίδοσις αὑτοῦ

English

Perhaps one should not speak of “seeing,” but rather of a different kind of seeing, an ecstasy and simplification and self-surrender, a reaching out toward contact and a stillness and a meditation directed toward fitting oneself to what is to be seen in the sanctuary. But anyone who tries to see in any other way will find nothing there. These then are but images, and the wise among the prophets expressed in riddles how God is seen. But a wise priest, understanding the riddle, may make the true vision of the sanctuary, going within.

§VI.9.11 [35–50] · Plotinus, Enneads VI.9.11
Manuscript
Greek

αὕτη ζωὴ θεῶν καὶ θείων καὶ εὐδαιμόνων ἀνθρώπων βίος· ἀπαλλαγὴ τῶν ἄλλων τῶν τῇδε... φυγὴ μόνου πρὸς μόνον.

English

This is the life of gods and of godlike and blessed men: liberation from all things here below, a life that takes no delight in the things of earth, the flight of the alone to the Alone.

Translator's Notes

  1. §VI.9.11 [1–10]: Plotinus’s insistence that in the mystical union “there were not two things” distinguishes his position from every form of devotional theism. In Christian, Jewish, and Islamic mysticism, a residual distinction between the soul and God is typically maintained. Plotinus abolishes it. The seer was the seen. This is not an analogy. Porphyry, Plotinus’s student and editor, reports that Plotinus experienced this state four times during the six years Porphyry studied with him (Life of Plotinus, 23).
  2. §VI.9.11 [10–20]: The temple metaphor is deeply significant. The “statues” left behind as one enters the inner sanctuary correspond to the Forms or Ideas in Nous (Intellect). Even the Platonic Forms are images, not the reality itself. The One is beyond all multiplicity, even the multiplicity of the Forms. To reach the One, the soul must leave behind not just the sensible world but the intelligible world as well.
  3. §VI.9.11 [35–50]: The phrase “flight of the alone to the Alone” (φυγὴ μόνου πρὸς μόνον) is the single most quoted line in Neoplatonism. The Greek monos means both “alone” and “single, simple, unique.” The soul, having become simple (stripped of all multiplicity), returns to the One, which is the ultimately Simple. The flight is not from something bad to something good but from complexity to simplicity, from the many to the one.
