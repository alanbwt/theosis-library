Works and Days: The Five Ages of Man

Works and Days: The Five Ages of Man
Hesiod, c. 700 BCE
Medieval MSS tradition

Introduction

The myth of the Five Ages — Gold, Silver, Bronze, Heroic, and Iron — is Hesiod's account of humanity's progressive decline from a state of divine blessedness to the present age of toil and injustice. This framework of cosmic degeneration influenced Plato, Ovid, Virgil, and all subsequent traditions of a lost Golden Age. Hesiod wrote from the Iron Age, and his poem is both a lament for what was lost and a practical guide for surviving in a diminished world.

§109-120 · WD 109-120
Greek

χρύσεον μὲν πρώτιστα γένος μερόπων ἀνθρώπων ἀθάνατοι ποίησαν Ὀλύμπια δώματ᾿ ἔχοντες. οἱ μὲν ἐπὶ Κρόνου ἦσαν, ὅτ᾿ οὐρανῷ ἐμβασίλευεν· ὥστε θεοὶ δ᾿ ἔζωον ἀκηδέα θυμὸν ἔχοντες νόσφιν ἄτερ τε πόνων καὶ ὀιζύος.

English

First of all, the immortals who dwell on Olympus made a golden race of mortal men. They lived in the time of Kronos, when he was king in heaven. Like gods they lived, with hearts free from care, without toil or sorrow.

§174-179 · WD 174-179
Greek

μηκέτ᾿ ἔπειτ᾿ ὤφελλον ἐγὼ πέμπτοισι μετεῖναι ἀνδράσιν, ἀλλ᾿ ἢ πρόσθε θανεῖν ἢ ἔπειτα γενέσθαι. νῦν γὰρ δὴ γένος ἐστὶ σιδήρεον.

English

Would that I had not been born among the fifth race of men, but had either died before or been born later. For now truly is the race of iron.
