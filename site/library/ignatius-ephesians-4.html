To the Ephesians 4: The Blood of God

Ignatius of Antioch, c. 35–108 AD

Introduction

Ignatius of Antioch, writing around 110 CE while being transported to Rome for execution, composed seven letters that are among the earliest Christian documents outside the New Testament. In his letter To the Ephesians, chapter 4, Ignatius speaks of the unity of the church with its bishop in language that assumes, without argument, the full divinity of Christ. The phrase “the blood of God” (haima theou, αἷμα θεοῦ) appears as if it were an established formula rather than a theological innovation.

This is significant because Ignatius writes decades before the great Christological controversies. For him, calling Christ “God” is not a position to be defended but an assumption so basic it can be stated in passing. The phrase “blood of God” makes the Incarnation shockingly concrete: the blood that was shed on the cross is divine blood. If God has blood, then the boundary between divine and material has already been crossed from the divine side. The theosis tradition begins here—not as doctrine but as liturgical instinct.

ὅθεν πρέπει ὑμῖν συντρέχειν τῇ τοῦ ἐπισκόπου γνώμῃ · ὅπερ καὶ ποιεῖτε · τὸ γὰρ ἀξιόνομαστον ὑμῶν πρεσβυτέριον τοῦ θεοῦ ἄξιον οὕτως συνήρμοσται τῷ ἐπισκόπῳ ὡς χορδαὶ κιθάρᾳ ·

English

So it is fitting that you should be in agreement with the mind of the bishop, which indeed you do. For your justly renowned council of elders, worthy of God, is attuned to the bishop as the strings to a lyre.

§4.2 · Ignatius, To the Ephesians 4.2 (Lightfoot/Holmes)
Manuscript
Greek

διὸ ἐν τῇ ὁμονοίᾳ ὑμῶν καὶ συμφώνῳ ἀγάπῃ Ιησοῦς Χριστὸς ἄδεται ·

English

Therefore in your concord and harmonious love, Jesus Christ is sung. And each one of you must join this chorus, so that being harmonious in concord, receiving the pitch from God in unison, you may sing with one voice through Jesus Christ to the Father, that he may both hear you and recognise, through what you do well, that you are members of his Son.

Manuscript
Greek

English

Being as you are imitators of God, once you took on new life through the blood of God (en haimati theou, ἐν αἵματι θεοῦ) you perfectly accomplished the task natural to you.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1.1 (cross-reference): The phrase “blood of God” (αἷμα θεοῦ) is one of the earliest and most direct claims of Christ’s full divinity in post-apostolic literature. Ignatius does not say “blood of Christ” or “blood of the Son”—he says “blood of God.” The textual tradition has been disputed; some scholars have argued for emendations that would soften the phrase. But the best manuscripts support the reading, and it is consistent with Ignatius’s theology throughout his letters. He refers to Christ as “our God” (ὁ θεὸς ἡμῶν) repeatedly and without qualification.
  2. §4.2: The musical metaphor—the church as a choir, the bishop as the pitch, Christ as the song being sung—is more than rhetoric. For Ignatius, unity is not an organisational principle but a participation in divine harmony. The Greek symphonos (σύμφωνος) carries Pythagorean overtones: the harmony of the cosmos, the music of the spheres, now enacted in the gathered community. When the church is in concord, Christ is “sung”—made present through harmonious action. This is an early form of the theology of the church as the Body of Christ: the community does not merely worship God but becomes the medium through which divinity is expressed in the world.
