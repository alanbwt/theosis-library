On the Resurrection I.6: Made for Immortality

Methodius of Olympus, d. c. 311 AD

Introduction

Methodius of Olympus (d. c. 311) was a bishop and theologian who wrote in the generation before the Council of Nicaea. His On the Resurrection is a dialogue defending the bodily resurrection against Origenist spiritualizers who argued that the resurrected body would be entirely different from the earthly one. Methodius insists that the same flesh that was created, fell, and suffered will be restored and glorified.

Book I, Chapter 6 grounds this defense in a theology of creation. Man was made to be immortal and incorruptible; death is not natural to the human condition but an intrusion caused by sin. The body is not a prison to be escaped but a divine creation destined for transformation. This anthropology—the human being as created for incorruption—provides the metaphysical foundation for the claim that deification involves the whole person, body and soul.

Manuscript
Greek

ὄτι ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἐπ᾽ ἀφθαρσίᾳ καὶ ἀθανασίᾳ γεγένηται· θεὸς γὰρ θάνατον οὐκ ἐποίησεν, οὐδὲ τέρπεται ἐπ᾽ ἀπωλείᾳ ζώντων.

English

Man was made to be immortal and incorruptible. For God did not create death, nor does he delight in the destruction of the living. He made all things for incorruption, and the generative forces of the world are wholesome.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.6: Methodius draws on Wisdom of Solomon 1:13–14 and 2:23–24 for his claim that God did not create death. His insistence on bodily incorruption as the original human state places him in direct opposition to Origen, who tended to view embodiment itself as a consequence of the soul’s pre-cosmic fall. For Methodius, the body is not a punishment but a gift, and its resurrection is not a concession but the fulfillment of God’s original creative intent. This has significant implications for theosis: if the body is destined for incorruption, then deification must encompass the material as well as the spiritual.
