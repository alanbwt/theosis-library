Introduction

The Popol Vuh is the creation narrative of the K'iche' Maya, preserved in a 16th-century transcription. Part One describes the primordial stillness before creation and the gods' first attempts to create beings who can worship them.

Translation

§Part One: The Primordial Stillness · Popol Vuh, Part One

This is the account of when everything was still, silent, and calm. There was nothing that existed. There was only the calm sea, alone and tranquil. Nothing existed. There was only immobility and silence in the darkness, in the night. Only the Maker and Modeler, Tepeu and Gucumatz, the Forefathers, were in the water, surrounded by light. They were hidden under green and blue feathers, and so they were called the Plumed Serpent. They spoke and they thought. They planned the creation of the earth, the mountains, the valleys, the rivers. "Let it be done!" they said, and the earth arose.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Part One: The creation through speech—"Let it be done!"—parallels Genesis ("Let there be light") and the Quranic kun fayakun ("Be! And it is"). The dual creator figures, Tepeu and Gucumatz, reflect the Mesoamerican principle of duality: creation requires two complementary forces working in concert rather than a single omnipotent will.
