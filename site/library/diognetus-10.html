Letter to Diognetus 10: Imitator of God's Goodness

Author of the Letter to Diognetus, c. 130–200 AD

Introduction

The Letter to Diognetus is one of the most elegant texts of early Christianity, probably dating to the late second century. Chapter 10 brings the letter to its climax with a sequence of claims that move from God’s love, to Christ’s mission, to the believer’s transformation into an “imitator of God.” The logic is breathtaking in its simplicity: God loved humanity and sent his Son; once you understand this love, you will love God in return; once you love God, you will imitate him; once you imitate him, you will become like him.

The Greek text survives in a single manuscript tradition that was nearly lost in a fire at the Strasbourg library in 1870. The letter’s author is unknown; its recipient may be fictional. What matters is the theological vision: the imitation of God is not a moral programme but an ontological trajectory. The one who imitates God’s generosity does not merely behave well—that person becomes a god to those who receive from them.

§10.2–3 · Letter to Diognetus 10.2–3 (Marrou, SC 33bis)
Manuscript
Greek

ὁ γὰρ θεὸς τοὺς ἀνθρώπους ἠγάπησεν · δι’ οὓς ἐποίησε τὸν κόσμον · οἷς ὑπέταξε πάντα τὰ ἐν τῇ γῇ · οἷς λόγον καὶ νοῦν ἔδωκεν ·

English

For God loved humankind, for whose sake he made the world, to whom he subjected all things that are on earth, to whom he gave reason and mind (logon kai noun, λόγον καὶ νοῦν), to whom alone he permitted to look up to him, whom he formed in his own image, to whom he sent his only-begotten Son, to whom he promised the kingdom in heaven—and he will give it to those who love him.

§10.4–6 · Letter to Diognetus 10.4–6 (Marrou, SC 33bis)
Manuscript
Greek

ἓν δὲ ἐπιγνῷς ταύτην τὴν γνῶσιν · ποίας οἴει χαρᾶς πληρωθήσεσθαι · ἢ πῶς ἀγαπήσεις τὸν οὕτως προαγαπήσαντά σε · ἀγαπήσας δὲ μιμητὴς ἔσῃ τῆς χρηστότητος αὐτοῦ ·

English

And when you have acquired this knowledge, with what joy do you think you will be filled? Or how will you love him who so first loved you? And by loving him you will be an imitator of his goodness. And do not wonder that a human being can become an imitator of God—he can, since God wills it.

§10.6b · Letter to Diognetus 10.6 (Marrou, SC 33bis)
Manuscript
Greek

ὃς δ’ ἂν τὸ τοῦ πλησίον ἀναδέχηται βάρος · ὃς ἐν ἷ κρείσσων ἐστὶν ἕτερον τὸν ἐλαττούμενον εὐεργετεῖν θέλει · οὗτος θεὸς γίνεται τῶν λαμβανόντων · οὗτος μιμητὴς θεοῦ ἐστίν ·

English

For happiness does not consist in dominating one’s neighbours, nor in wishing to have more than the weak, nor in being rich and able to compel those who are beneath you. In these things no one can imitate God. But whoever takes upon himself the burden of his neighbour, whoever wishes to benefit one who is worse off in something in which he himself is better off, whoever provides to those in need what he received from God—this one becomes a god (theos, θεός) to those who receive from him. This one is an imitator of God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §10.4–6: The sequence—knowledge, joy, love, imitation—is not a moral ladder but an ontological chain reaction. Each stage produces the next automatically. The author uses the word gnosis (γνῶσις) without apology, claiming it for orthodox Christianity against the Gnostic movements. True knowledge of God produces joy; joy produces love; love produces imitation; imitation produces deification. The statement “do not wonder that a human being can become an imitator of God—he can, since God wills it” is a pre-emptive defence against the objection that deification is presumptuous.
  2. §10.6b: The claim that one who gives to the needy “becomes a god to those who receive” (θεὸς γίνεται τῶν λαμβανόντων) is among the boldest statements of social theosis in early Christian literature. Deification is not achieved through contemplation or sacrament but through generosity. The giver becomes God to the recipient in the same way that God became human for the sake of humanity. This is theosis as ethical practice—a strand of the tradition often overshadowed by the mystical and liturgical dimensions.
