Havamal 138-141: Odin's Self-Sacrifice

Anonymous (Norse), c. 10th-13th c. AD

Introduction

The Hávamál (“Sayings of the High One”) is a poem in the Poetic Edda attributed to Odin himself. It is a composite work: the first sections are pragmatic wisdom poetry (how to behave as a guest, whom to trust, how much to drink), but stanzas 138–141 shift abruptly into myth. Odin recounts how he hung on Yggdrasil for nine nights, pierced by a spear, sacrificed to himself, in order to gain the runes—the sacred letters that contain the power of creation, healing, binding, and knowledge. No one gave him bread or drink. He looked down, took up the runes, and fell back screaming.

This is one of the most extraordinary passages in world mythology: a god who sacrifices himself to himself in order to gain knowledge. The structural parallel to the crucifixion of Christ is unmissable—a divine figure hung on a tree, pierced by a weapon, dying and returning with salvific knowledge. Whether the parallel reflects pre-Christian Norse theology, post-conversion Christian influence, or a shared Indo-European mythological pattern is one of the great unresolved questions in the study of Norse religion. What is certain is that the passage presents knowledge as something that can only be gained through self-sacrifice and suffering—not through study or contemplation, but through ordeal.

§Stanza 138 · Hávamál 138, Codex Regius
Manuscript
Old Norse

Veit ek at ek hekk vindga meiði á nætr allar níu, geiri undaðr ok gefinn Óðni, sjálfr sjálfum mér, á þim meiði er manngi veit hvers hann af rótum rennr.

English

I know that I hung on the wind-swept tree, nine full nights, wounded by a spear, given to Odin, myself to myself, on that tree of which no man knows from what roots it runs.

Við hleiði seldu mér né við hornigi; nýsta ek niðr, nam ek upp rúnar, œpandi nam, fell ek aptr þaðan.

They gave me no bread, they gave me no drink from a horn. I looked down, I took up the runes, screaming I took them, and I fell back from there.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanza 138: The phrase “given to Odin, myself to myself” (gefinn Óðni, sjálfr sjálfum mér) is the crux of the passage. A god sacrifices himself to himself. There is no higher power to receive the sacrifice; Odin is both the priest and the offering. This reflexive sacrifice has no precise parallel in other Indo-European mythologies. The closest analogy is Christ’s sacrifice on the cross—but Christ is sacrificed to the Father, not to himself. Odin’s self-sacrifice is genuinely unique: the divine is transformed by its own suffering.
  2. §Stanza 139: The runes are not merely an alphabet. In Norse cosmology, the runes are the fundamental forces of reality—patterns woven into the structure of existence. To “take up” the runes is to gain access to the deepest level of cosmic power. The word œpandi (“screaming” or “shrieking”) indicates that this is not serene enlightenment but an agonizing breakthrough. Knowledge of the ultimate is gained through pain, not contemplation.
  3. §Stanza 141: The cascading formula—“word led me to word, deed led me to deed”—describes knowledge as self-multiplying once the initial breakthrough is achieved. This is strikingly similar to the Hermetic concept of gnosis as a single transformative event that unlocks everything else, and to Plotinus’s description of the soul that, having once seen the One, sees all things in a new light. The means of access differ radically—ordeal versus contemplation—but the structure of the experience is the same: a threshold is crossed, and everything changes.
