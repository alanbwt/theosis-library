On the Beatitudes 7: Seeing Is Becoming

Gregory of Nyssa, c. 335–395 AD

Introduction

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 335–395) is the most philosophically sophisticated of the Cappadocian Fathers and arguably the deepest mystical theologian of the patristic era. His Homilies on the Beatitudes, a series of sermons on Matthew 5:1–12, represent some of his most accessible theological writing. Each homily takes a single beatitude and unfolds its implications for the spiritual life with a combination of philosophical rigor and pastoral warmth.

Homily 7 treats the sixth beatitude: “Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God.” Gregory presses the logic of this promise to its ultimate conclusion. To see God is not merely to observe God from a distance but to be transformed by the vision. Seeing and becoming converge: the soul that beholds the divine beauty becomes beautiful, and the soul that sees God becomes, in some real sense, divine. This is Gregory’s doctrine of epektasis—the infinite progress of the soul into God—applied to the beatific vision.

§Homily 7 · De Beatitudinibus, Oratio 7 (GNO 7.2, ed. Callahan)
Manuscript
Greek

Οἱ καθαροὶ τῇ καρδίᾳ τὸν θεὸν ὄψονται, καὶ τὸ ἴδεῖν γενέσθαί ἐστιν· ὁ γὰρ ἰδὼν τὸ ἀγαθὸν ἀγαθὸς γίνεται.

English

The pure in heart shall see God, and seeing is becoming. For the one who sees the Good becomes good, and the one who looks upon the True becomes true, and the one who beholds God becomes divine. Whoever sees God has obtained all that is counted among the things of blessedness.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Homily 7: Gregory’s claim that “seeing is becoming” (τὸ ἴδεῖν γενέσθαί ἐστιν) draws on the Platonic principle, articulated in the Republic and the Symposium, that the soul is assimilated to what it contemplates. But Gregory radicalizes this principle. For Plato, the contemplation of the Forms produces likeness to them; for Gregory, the contemplation of God produces participation in God’s own being. The threefold parallelism—seeing the Good makes one good, seeing the True makes one true, seeing God makes one divine—escalates deliberately from Platonic categories to the specifically Christian claim of deification.
