Introduction

Discourse 1 of Maximus of Tyre (2nd century CE) asks “Whether God Should Be Worshipped with Images.” The Middle Platonist argues that images are aids for weak minds but that the philosopher seeks God beyond all representation.

§Discourse 1.1–5 · Maximus of Tyre, Discourses
Manuscript
Greek

Θεὸς μὲν αὐτός, ὁ πατὴρ καὶ δημιουργὸς πάντων

English

God himself, the father and craftsman of all, older than the sun, older than the sky, greater than time and eternity and every flowing nature—no lawgiver can name him, no voice can utter him, no eye can see him. Unable to grasp his essence, we grasp at words and names and images, gold and ivory and silver, plants and rivers, mountain peaks and streams, longing through beauty of what we see to catch a glimpse of his nature.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Discourse 1: Maximus’s position—that images are legitimate aids but inferior to direct intellectual apprehension of God—prefigures the Byzantine iconoclast controversy by six centuries. His argument that the beauty of the visible world serves as a stepping-stone to invisible reality closely parallels the “ladder of beauty” in Plato’s Symposium.
