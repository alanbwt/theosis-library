Apology: Socrates' Defense

        
Apology: Socrates' Defense

        
Plato, c. 428-348 BCE

        
Various manuscripts

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Apology is Plato's account of the trial of Socrates in 399 BCE — the most famous trial in Western history. Accused of corrupting the youth and not believing in the gods of the city, Socrates delivers not a conventional defense but a philosophical manifesto. He explains how the oracle at Delphi declared him the wisest man in Athens; he describes his divine mission to examine the lives of his fellow citizens; and he delivers the line that has defined philosophy ever since: 'The unexamined life is not worth living.'
After the guilty verdict and death sentence, Socrates offers his final reflection: he is going to die, they to live, and which is better, only God knows. This calm in the face of death is what made Socrates the model philosopher for two millennia.

            §17a-18a · Apol. 17a-18a
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
ὅτι μὲν ὑμεῖς, ὦ ἄνδρες Ἀθηναῖοι, πεπόνθατε ὑπὸ τῶν ἐμῶν κατηγόρων, οὐκ οἶδα· ἐγὼ δ᾿ οὖν καὶ αὐτὸς ὑπ᾿ αὐτῶν ὀλίγου ἐμαυτοῦ ἐπελαθόμην, οὕτω πιθανῶς ἔλεγον.

          


          

            
English

            
How you, O men of Athens, have been affected by my accusers, I do not know. But I know that they almost made even me forget who I am, so persuasively did they speak. And yet they have hardly spoken a word of truth. But from me you shall hear the whole truth — though not, by Zeus, in the polished phrases of my accusers. You will hear me speak in the words and arguments that occur to me at the moment, for I am confident that what I say is just.

          

        

        
        


          

            §20c-23b · Apol. 20c-23b
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
ὑμῶν, ὦ ἄνδρες, σοφώτατός ἐστιν ὅστις ὥσπερ Σωκράτης ἔγνωκεν ὅτι οὐδενὸς ἄξιός ἐστι τῇ ἀληθείᾳ πρὸς σοφίαν.

          


          

            
English

            
Chaerephon went to Delphi and asked the oracle whether anyone was wiser than I was. The Pythia replied that no one was wiser. When I heard this, I said to myself: What does the god mean? I know that I am not wise at all. So I went to one of the men reputed to be wise, and after talking with him, I came away thinking: I am wiser than this man. Neither of us knows anything truly beautiful and good, but he thinks he knows when he does not, while I neither know nor think I know. In this small way, then, I seem to be wiser — that what I do not know, I do not think I know.

          

        

        
        


          

            §28b-29a · Apol. 28b-29a
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
ὅπου ἄν τις ἑαυτὸν τάξῃ ἡγησάμενος βέλτιστον εἶναι ἢ ὑπ᾿ ἄρχοντος ταχθῇ, ἐνταῦθα δεῖ, ὡς ἐμοὶ δοκεῖ, μένοντα κινδυνεύειν.

          


          

            
English

            
Wherever a man takes his post — whether he has chosen it himself as the best place, or been stationed there by his commander — there he must remain and face the danger, considering neither death nor anything else before disgrace. When God stationed me here to live the life of a philosopher, examining myself and others, if I were to desert my post through fear of death — that would be truly dreadful, and then someone might justly bring me to court for not believing in the gods.

          

        

        
        


          

            §29d-30b · Apol. 29d-30b
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
ὁ δὲ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς ἀνθρώπῳ.

          


          

            
English

            
Men of Athens, I honor and love you, but I shall obey the god rather than you. As long as I breathe and am able, I will not cease philosophizing, exhorting you, pointing out the truth to anyone I meet, saying: Are you not ashamed of caring so much for money and reputation and honor, and so little for wisdom and truth and the improvement of your soul? For I go around doing nothing but persuading you, young and old, not to care for your bodies or your wealth more than for the perfection of your souls. I tell you that virtue does not come from wealth, but from virtue comes wealth and every other good thing for human beings. If you put me to death, you will not easily find another like me — I am, if I may use a ridiculous figure, a sort of gadfly attached to the state, and all day long I never cease to settle upon you, arousing, persuading, reproaching you. The unexamined life is not worth living for a human being.

          

        

        
        


          

            §38c-42a · Apol. 38c-42a
            

              
              
              
            

          


          

          

            
Greek

            
ἀλλὰ γὰρ ἤδη ὥρα ἀπιέναι, ἐμοὶ μὲν ἀποθανουμένῳ, ὑμῖν δὲ βιωσομένοις· ὁπότεροι δὲ ἡμῶν ἔρχονται ἐπὶ ἄμεινον πρᾶγμα, ἄδηλον παντὶ πλὴν ἢ τῷ θεῷ.

          


          

            
English

            
The hour of departure has arrived, and we go our separate ways — I to die, and you to live. Which of us goes to the better lot is known to no one, except the god.
