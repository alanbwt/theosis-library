Commentary on John 1:9: Participation in the Divine Nature

Εἰς τὸ κατὰ Ἰωάννην
Cyril of Alexandria, c. 376–444 AD

Introduction

Cyril of Alexandria’s Commentary on the Gospel of John, written between 425 and 428 CE, is one of the great exegetical works of the patristic era. Cyril composed it in the years before the Nestorian controversy consumed his attention, and it represents his mature Christological theology applied verse by verse to the Fourth Gospel.

Commenting on John 1:9 (“the true light, which enlightens every person, was coming into the world”), Cyril argues that the light the Logos brings is not merely illumination but actual participation in the divine nature. We are called to share in God not as a metaphor for moral improvement but as an ontological reality communicated through the Incarnation and the Spirit.

§Commentary on John 1:9 · Commentarii in Joannem I (Pusey ed., vol. 1; PG 73.161C–164A)
Manuscript
Greek

Κεκλήμεθα γαρ εις μετουσίαν της θείας φύσεως, καθά φησιν ο σοφός Πέτρος, ου τροπικως αλλα αληθως· ότι το αγιον Πνευμα μετουσίαν εαυτου και δι εαυτου της θείας φύσεως εγκατασπείρει ημιν· μέτοχοι γαρ Θεου και του αγίου Πνεύματος γεγόναμεν.

English

For we are called to participate in the divine nature, as the wise Peter says, not metaphorically but truly, because the Holy Spirit plants in us a participation in himself and through himself in the divine nature. For we have been made partakers of Christ and of the Holy Spirit, and the beauty of the ineffable Godhead shines forth in our souls through the Spirit who dwells in us. And thus those who are worthy to participate in the Spirit are called gods, not by grace alone, as those who are promoted to some honour, but because they truly have God dwelling in them and with them.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Commentary on John 1:9: Cyril’s insistence on ου τροπικως αλλα αληθως (“not metaphorically but truly”) is a deliberate rejection of any minimizing interpretation of 2 Peter 1:4 (“partakers of the divine nature”). He is arguing against those who would reduce theosis to a title of honour or a moral analogy. The Spirit does not merely improve us; the Spirit “plants in us a participation in himself.” The verb εγκατασπείρει (“plants” or “sows within”) carries agricultural force: something genuinely new grows inside the human person. This is real ontological change, not mere relabelling.
