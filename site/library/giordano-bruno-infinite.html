Introduction

Giordano Bruno's On the Infinite, the Universe, and the Worlds (1584) argues that the universe is infinite, containing innumerable worlds with intelligent life. Bruno was burned at the stake in 1600 for these and related heresies.

Translation

§First Dialogue: The Infinite Universe · De l'Infinito, Universo e Mondi, First Dialogue

The universe is one, infinite, immovable. It is not capable of comprehension, and therefore is endless and limitless, and to that extent infinite and indeterminable. If the divine power and goodness are infinite, as they most certainly are, how can we suppose that the objects of that power and goodness are finite? Why should infinite cause produce finite effect? There is a single general space, a single vast immensity which we may freely call void. In it are innumerable globes like this one on which we live and grow. This space we declare to be infinite, since there is no reason, convenience, possibility, sense, or nature that would assign it a limit.

Translator's Notes

  1. §First Dialogue: Bruno's argument from divine infinity to cosmic infinity—if God is infinite, His creation must be too—transformed a theological attribute into a cosmological principle. This reasoning anticipated the Copernican revolution's implications more radically than Copernicus himself, who still placed the stars on a fixed sphere. Bruno's execution by the Roman Inquisition made him a symbol of free thought for centuries.
