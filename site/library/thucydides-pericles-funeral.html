History II.37-41: Pericles' Funeral Oration

Thucydides, c. 460-400 BCE

Introduction

Pericles’ Funeral Oration, delivered in the winter of 431/430 BCE over the Athenian dead of the first year of the Peloponnesian War, is the most celebrated defense of democracy in the ancient world. Thucydides, who almost certainly heard it, reconstructed it in his History (II.37–41). Whether the words are Pericles’ own or Thucydides’ literary creation, the speech articulates a vision of democratic life that has never been surpassed: a city where citizens govern themselves, where poverty is no bar to public service, where private freedom and public responsibility coexist.

The oration is also a piece of political rhetoric in wartime. Pericles is not delivering a philosophy lecture; he is justifying the sacrifices of the dead and steeling the living for the sacrifices to come. The portrait of Athens is idealized—Thucydides himself will spend the remaining six books of his History showing how this ideal unraveled. The plague, the demagogues, the Sicilian disaster, and the oligarchic coups of 411 BCE all stand as implicit commentary on the fragility of the democratic vision Pericles celebrates here.

§II.37 (The Constitution) · Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War II.37
Manuscript
Greek

χρώμεθα γὰρ πολιτείᾳ οὐ ζηλούσῃ τοὺς τῶν πέλας νόμους, παράδειγμα δὲ μᾶλλον αὐτοὶ ὄντες τισὶν ἢ μιμούμενοι ἑτέρους. καὶ ὄνομα μὲν διὰ τὸ μὴ ἐς ὀλίγους ἀλλ’ ἐς πλείονας οἰκεῖν δημοκρατία κέκληται.

English

Our constitution does not copy the laws of neighboring states; we are rather a pattern to others than imitators ourselves. Its administration favours the many instead of the few; this is why it is called a democracy. If we look to the laws, they afford equal justice to all in their private differences; if to social standing, advancement in public life falls to reputation for capacity, class considerations not being allowed to interfere with merit; nor again does poverty bar the way—if a man is able to serve the state, he is not hindered by the obscurity of his condition.

§II.38–39 (Freedom and Culture) · Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War II.38–39
Manuscript
Greek

ἀνεπαχθῶς δὲ τὰ ἴδια προσομιλοῦντες τὰ δημόσια διὰ δέος μάλιστα οὐ παρανομοῦμεν.

English

And as in private life we are not suspicious of one another, so neither are we angry with our neighbour for doing what he likes. We do not put on sour looks at him, which though harmless are not pleasant. While we are thus unconstrained in our private intercourse, in our public acts we are prevented from lawlessness chiefly by reverence: we obey those whom we put in authority, and we obey the laws, especially those which are ordained for the protection of the injured, and those unwritten laws whose transgression brings admitted shame.

§II.40 (The Citizen-Philosopher) · Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War II.40
Manuscript
Greek

φιλοκαλοῦμέν τε γὰρ μετ’ εὐτελείας καὶ φιλοσοφοῦμεν ἄνευ μαλακίας.

English

For we are lovers of the beautiful, yet with economy, and we cultivate the mind without loss of manliness. Wealth we employ not for talk and ostentation but when there is a real use for it. To acknowledge poverty is no disgrace among us; the true disgrace is in doing nothing to avoid it. The same persons attend at once to the concerns of their households and of the city, and those of us who are engaged in business have a very fair idea of politics. We alone regard the man who takes no part in public affairs not as one who minds his own business, but as useless.

§II.41 (The Education of Hellas) · Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War II.41
Manuscript
Greek

ξυνελών τε λέγω τήν τε πᾶσαν πόλιν τῆς Ἑλλάδος παίδευσιν εἶναι.

English

In short, I say that as a city we are the education of Greece, and I doubt if the world can produce a man who, where he has only himself to depend upon, is equal to so many emergencies and graced by so happy a versatility as the Athenian. That this is no mere boast of words but an actual truth, the power of the state acquired by these habits proves. For Athens alone of her contemporaries is found, when tested, to be greater than her reputation.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II.37: Pericles’ definition of democracy is strikingly modern in some respects and alien in others. The claim that advancement depends on merit rather than class is democratic in spirit, but Athenian democracy excluded women, slaves, and resident foreigners from citizenship—the majority of the population. Thucydides does not comment on this irony, but it haunts the speech.
  2. §II.40: The Greek φιλοσοφοῦμεν (“we philosophize” or “we cultivate the mind”) is a direct counter to Sparta’s anti-intellectual militarism. Pericles claims that Athens proves intellect and courage are not opposed. The phrase “we regard the man who takes no part in public affairs as useless” (not “quiet” but “useless”—ἀχρεῖον) encapsulates the Athenian expectation that every citizen is a political participant, not merely a private person.
