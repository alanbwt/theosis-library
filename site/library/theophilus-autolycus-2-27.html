To Autolycus II.27: Man Would Have Become God

Theophilus of Antioch, d. c. 183 AD

Introduction

Theophilus of Antioch (d. c. 185) is one of the earliest Christian apologists whose work survives. His To Autolycus, addressed to a pagan interlocutor, defends Christian belief against philosophical objections and presents the biblical account of creation as superior to Greek cosmogonies. Theophilus writes before the great theological controversies of the third and fourth centuries, and his language about human destiny is correspondingly unguarded and bold.

Book II, Chapter 27 addresses the question of human mortality. Theophilus argues that God created humanity neither fully mortal nor fully immortal but in an intermediate state, capable of progressing toward either. Had Adam kept the divine commandment, he would have received immortality as a reward and “become god.” This conditional theosis—deification as the intended trajectory of human obedience—is among the earliest explicit statements of the doctrine in Christian literature.

§II.27 · Ad Autolycum II.27 (ed. Grant)
Manuscript
Greek

Οὔτε γὰρ θνητὸν ἐποίησεν αὐτὸν οὔτε ἀθάνατον, ἀλλὰ δεκτικὸν ἀμφοτέρων· εἰ γὰρ ἔρεψεν ἐπὶ τὰ τῆς ἀθανασίας, τηρήσας τὴν ἐντολὴν τοῦ θεοῦ, μισθὸν ἐκομίσατο παρ᾽ αὐτοῦ τὴν ἀθανασίαν καὶ θεὸς ἐγένετο.

English

For God made man neither immortal nor mortal, but capable of both. If man had inclined toward the things of immortality by keeping the commandment of God, he would have received immortality as a reward and would have become god. But if he turned to the things of death by disobeying God, he would himself be the cause of his own death.

Translator's Notes

  1. §II.27: Theophilus’s phrase “capable of both” (δεκτικὸν ἀμφοτέρων) places humanity in a dynamic intermediate state that later theologians would develop into elaborate anthropologies. The idea that Adam was created with the potential for deification but had not yet achieved it anticipates Irenaeus’s distinction between the “image” and “likeness” of God. The direct statement “and would have become god” (καὶ θεὸς ἐγένετο) is remarkably unqualified for so early a text, predating the careful distinctions that later Fathers would introduce between divine essence and participation.
