Tablet XI: The Discovery That Shook Victorian England

        
Sumerian Scribes, c. 2100-1900 BCE

        
British Museum (K.3375)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
On December 3, 1872, George Smith stood before the Society of Biblical Archaeology in London and announced that he had found a Babylonian account of the Flood — on a clay tablet from the library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh, dating to the 7th century BCE but recording a story far older. The account was so close to Genesis 6-9 that there could be no doubt of a literary connection. According to one account, Smith was so excited during his initial translation that he began removing his clothes.
The discovery of Tablet XI of the Epic of Gilgamesh transformed biblical studies. It proved that the Genesis flood narrative was part of a much older Mesopotamian literary tradition. The text presented here is the complete flood narrative as told by Utnapishtim to Gilgamesh — the passage that changed our understanding of the Bible's relationship to the ancient world.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · flood-tablet-discovery
          
The assembly of the gods decided to send the Deluge. Ea whispered to the reed wall: 'Reed wall, reed wall! Wall, wall! Reed wall, hear! Wall, understand! O man of Shuruppak, son of Ubar-Tutu, tear down your house, build a boat! Give up possessions, seek life! Despise property, save life! Load the seed of all living things into the boat. The boat that you shall build — let her dimensions be measured: let her width and her length be equal. Cover her like the waters of the deep.' I loaded her with everything there was. I loaded her with the seed of all living things. I brought aboard my whole family and kin. The wild animals of the field, the tame animals — I made them all go aboard. For six days and seven nights the wind blew, the flood overwhelmed the land. When the seventh day arrived, the storm subsided. The sea grew calm. I opened a window and light fell on my face. I fell to my knees and sat weeping. On Mount Nisir the boat held fast. I sent out a dove, but finding no resting place, she came back. I sent out a swallow, but finding no resting place, she came back. I sent out a raven — she saw that the waters had receded, eating, bobbing, but not turning round. Then I set out offerings on the peak of the mountain.
