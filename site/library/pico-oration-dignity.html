Introduction

Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's Oration on the Dignity of Man (1486) is often called the manifesto of the Renaissance. It presents humanity as uniquely free—unbound by any fixed nature, capable of ascending to the divine or descending to the bestial.

Translation

§God's Address to Adam · Oration on the Dignity of Man, Opening Section

The Supreme Father, God the Master-builder, had already created this cosmic house according to the laws of His hidden wisdom. But when the work was finished, the Craftsman desired someone to ponder the meaning of so great a creation. At last He conceived of creating man. He addressed him thus: "Neither a fixed abode nor a form that is yours alone nor any function peculiar to yourself have I given you, Adam. You, constrained by no limits, shall determine for yourself the limits of your nature. I have placed you at the center of the world so that you may more easily observe what is in it. I have made you neither heavenly nor earthly, neither mortal nor immortal, so that you may, as the free and extraordinary shaper of yourself, fashion yourself in whatever form you prefer."

Translator's Notes

  1. §God's Address to Adam: Pico's argument that humanity has no fixed nature but is free to shape itself is a radical departure from the medieval chain of being, in which every creature has a predetermined place. It draws simultaneously on Hermeticism, Kabbalah, Neoplatonism, and Christian theology—reflecting Pico's ambition to synthesize all philosophical and religious traditions into a single universal wisdom.
