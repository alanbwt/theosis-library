Introduction

At Timaeus 41a–d, the Demiurge addresses the lesser gods he has created and instructs them to fashion mortal beings. This passage is the key text for Plato’s theological cosmology and the hierarchical delegation of creative power.

§41a–d · Plato, Timaeus 41a7–d3 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

θεοὶ θεῶν, ὧν ἐγὼ δημιουργὸς πατήρ τε ἔργων

English

Gods of gods, the works of which I am the maker and father are indissoluble so long as I will it. All that is bound may be dissolved, but only an evil will would dissolve what is fairly fitted together and in good condition. Turn now to the fashioning of mortal creatures, weaving the mortal to the immortal.

Translator's Notes

  1. §41a–d: The Demiurge’s address to the “gods of gods” (θεοὶ θεῶν) became a foundational text for Neoplatonic theology. Proclus devoted extensive commentary to this passage, reading the Demiurge as the divine intellect mediating between the One and the visible cosmos.
