Nicomachean Ethics X: The Contemplative Life

Nicomachean Ethics X: The Contemplative Life
Aristotle, 384-322 BCE
Codex Laurentianus 81.11

Introduction

The final book of the Nicomachean Ethics contains Aristotle's argument that the highest form of human happiness (eudaimonia) is the contemplative life — the activity of the rational soul engaged in theoretical knowledge. This is not merely an intellectual preference: Aristotle argues that contemplation makes us participate in the divine, since God's own activity is pure thought. His injunction to 'make ourselves immortal' (athanatizein) as far as possible anticipates the Christian doctrine of theosis.

§1177a-b · EN X.7 1177a-b
Greek

εἰ δὴ θεῖον ὁ νοῦς πρὸς τὸν ἄνθρωπον, καὶ ὁ κατὰ τοῦτον βίος θεῖος πρὸς τὸν ἀνθρώπινον βίον.

English

If the mind is divine in comparison with the human being, then the life according to it is divine in comparison with human life.

§1177b · EN X.7 1177b
Greek

οὐ χρὴ δὲ κατὰ τοὺς παραινοῦντας ἀνθρώπινα φρονεῖν ἄνθρωπον ὄντα... ἀλλ᾿ ἐφ᾿ ὅσον ἐνδέχεται ἀθανατίζειν.

English

We ought not to follow those who advise us, being human, to think human thoughts... but rather, so far as possible, to make ourselves immortal.
