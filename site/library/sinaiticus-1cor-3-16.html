1 Corinthians 3:16: You Are God's Temple

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

In 1 Corinthians 3:16, Paul asks a question whose implications the early Church spent centuries unpacking: “Do you not know that you are God’s temple and that God’s Spirit dwells in you?” The Greek word for temple here is naos (ναός), which refers specifically to the inner sanctuary—the holy of holies where God’s presence dwelt—not the outer courts (hieron, ἱερόν). Paul is not using a loose metaphor. He is claiming that believers have become the place where God lives.

The passage is addressed to the Corinthian community collectively (the “you” is plural in Greek), but the theological principle it establishes applies to the individual as well, as Paul makes explicit in 1 Corinthians 6:19. For the theosis tradition, the indwelling of the Spirit is the mechanism by which human beings participate in the divine nature. The temple is not merely visited by God; it is constituted by God’s presence. Without the indwelling Spirit, there is no temple at all.

English

If anyone destroys God’s temple, God will destroy that person. For God’s temple is holy, and you are that temple.

Translator's Notes

  1. §3:16: The distinction between naos (ναός) and hieron (ἱερόν) is theologically significant. Hieron refers to the entire temple complex, including the outer courts where commerce and teaching took place. Naos refers specifically to the inner sanctuary, the dwelling place of God. In the Jerusalem temple, only the high priest could enter the naos, and only once a year, on the Day of Atonement. Paul’s claim that the community is the naos effectively democratizes access to the divine presence.
  2. §3:17: The verb phtheirei (φθείρει) can mean “corrupt,” “destroy,” or “ruin.” The wordplay is intentional: God will do to the destroyer what the destroyer does to the temple. The severity of the warning reflects the seriousness of the claim. If the community truly is God’s inner sanctuary, then to damage it is not merely a social offense but a form of sacrilege.
