Hypostasis of the Archons: The Rulers of This World

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Hypostasis of the Archons (“The Reality of the Rulers”) is a Gnostic rewriting of Genesis found in the Nag Hammadi library (Codex II, Tractate 4). Composed in Coptic, likely translated from a Greek original of the second or third century, it opens by quoting Paul—“Our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers of this world” (Ephesians 6:12)—and then proceeds to explain exactly who those rulers are.

The text retells the creation of the world, the making of Adam, the role of Eve, and the expulsion from Eden, but inverts the meaning of each event. The archons (rulers) who create the material world are not the true God but blind, arrogant powers. The serpent is not the enemy but the instructor. Eve is not the cause of the fall but the agent of awakening. The true God is hidden, and the material creation is a prison. The text is essential for understanding how Gnostic communities read Genesis as a story of divine sparks trapped in matter, and how they understood salvation as the soul’s recognition of its true origin.

Translator's Notes

  1. §87:1-10: Samael (“blind god”) is the Gnostic name for the Demiurge, the creator of the material world. His declaration “I am God; there is none apart from me” quotes Isaiah 45:5 and 46:9. In the orthodox reading, these are statements of monotheistic truth. In the Gnostic reading, they are the arrogant boasts of a lesser, ignorant being who does not know the true God above him.
  2. §88:10-24: The identification of Adam’s “deep sleep” (Genesis 2:21) with ignorance is characteristic of Gnostic exegesis. In this reading, the creation of Eve is not a gift but a trap: the archons are trying to separate the spiritual element from Adam. However, the text also subverts this by making the “living woman” the spiritual principle herself.
  3. §91:2-15: The serpent as “instructor” is the most famous Gnostic inversion of Genesis. The knowledge of good and evil is not a curse but the beginning of liberation. The serpent represents the true divine wisdom breaking into the prison of the archons’ creation. This is why the Church Fathers sometimes called the Gnostics “Ophites” (from ophis, “serpent”).
