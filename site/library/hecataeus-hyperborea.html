On the Hyperboreans: The Spherical Temple of Apollo

Hecataeus of Abdera, c. 360-290 BCE

Introduction

Hecataeus of Abdera, a Greek historian and philosopher of the fourth century BCE, wrote a treatise On the Hyperboreans that does not survive independently but is preserved in extensive paraphrase by Diodorus Siculus. Hecataeus’s account is widely regarded as the single most important source for the Hyperborean tradition: it provides the detailed description of the island, the spherical temple, the 19-year cycle of Apollo’s visitation, and the close appearance of the moon that Diodorus transmits in his Library of History II.47.

Hecataeus wrote in the intellectual milieu of Alexander the Great’s conquests, when the boundaries of the known world were expanding rapidly and the Greeks were encountering cultures that challenged their assumptions. His Hyperborean account may represent a philosophical idealization—a thought experiment about what a society would look like if it lived in perfect harmony with the divine. The spherical temple, in particular, has theological resonance: the sphere was considered the most perfect geometric form by Greek philosophers, and a spherical temple to Apollo would represent worship housed in cosmic perfection. For the theosis tradition, Hecataeus’s Hyperborea is the most architecturally and astronomically precise vision of paradise in pagan literature.

Ἑκαταῖός τε καί τινες ἕτεροί φασιν ἐν τοῖς ἀντιπέραν τῆς Κελτικῆς τόποις κατὰ τὸν ὠκεανὸν εἶναι νῆσον οὐκ ἐλάττω τῆς Σικελίας.

English

Hecataeus and certain others say that in the regions beyond the land of the Celts there lies in the ocean an island no smaller than Sicily. This island, situated in the north, is inhabited by the Hyperboreans, who are so called because their home is beyond the point from which the north wind blows. The island has a fertile soil that is productive of every crop, and since it has an unusually temperate climate it produces two harvests each year.

§Fragment 7a (ap. Diodorus II.47.3–4) · Hecataeus of Abdera, Fragment 7a (preserved in Diodorus Siculus, Library of History II.47.3–4)

Greek

μυθολογεῖσθαι δ’ ἐν ταύτῃ γεγενῆσθαι τὴν Λητώ, καὶ διὰ τοῦτο τὸν Ἀπόλλωνα παρ’ αὐτοῖς τιμᾶσθαι μάλιστα τῶν θεῶν· εἶναι δὲ καὶ τέμενος Ἀπόλλωνος ἐπιφανὲς καὶ ναὸν ἀξιόλογον, σφαιροειδῆ τῷ σχήματι.

English

The myth relates that Leto was born on this island, and for this reason the inhabitants honor Apollo above all other gods, being as it were his priests, since they praise him daily and continuously with songs, and honor him especially. There is on the island a magnificent sacred precinct of Apollo and a notable temple, spherical in form (sphairoeidēs, σφαιροειδής), adorned with many votive offerings.

§Fragment 7a (ap. Diodorus II.47.6) · Hecataeus of Abdera, Fragment 7a (preserved in Diodorus Siculus, Library of History II.47.6)
Manuscript
Greek

λέγουσι δὲ καὶ τὴν σελήνην ἐκ ταύτης τῆς νήσου φαίνεσθαι σφόδρα πλησίον τῆς γῆς· τὸν δὲ θεὸν δι’ ἐτῶν ἐννεακαίδεκα παραγίνεσθαι πρὸς τὴν νῆσον. τοὺς δὲ βασιλεῖς τῆς πόλεως καὶ τοῦ τεμένους ἐπιστάτας Βορεάδας καλεῖσθαι.

English

They say also that the moon, as viewed from this island, appears to be but a little distance from the earth, and to have upon it prominences, like those of the earth, which are visible to the eye. The god is said to visit the island every nineteen years, the period in which the return of the stars to the same place in the heavens is accomplished. At the time of this appearance of the god he both plays on the lyre and dances continuously through the night from the vernal equinox until the rising of the Pleiades, expressing his delight at his successes. The kings of this city and the supervisors of the sacred precinct are called Boreades, since they are descendants of Boreas, and the succession to these positions is always kept in their family.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragment 7a (temple): The term sphairoeidēs (σφαιροειδής, “spherical in form”) is the most debated word in the entire Hyperborean corpus. Some translators render it as “circular” or “domed,” but the Greek literally means “sphere-shaped.” A truly spherical temple would be architecturally extraordinary and symbolically resonant: the sphere was for the Pythagoreans and Platonists the perfect form, the shape of the cosmos itself. A spherical temple to Apollo would be a microcosm—the entire universe in miniature, housing the god of light, reason, and music.
  2. §Fragment 7a (moon): The observation that the moon appears close to the earth and shows visible surface features from this island is striking. At high northern latitudes, the moon can appear dramatically large near the horizon due to atmospheric refraction, and clear Arctic air might theoretically allow unusual detail to be observed. Whether this reflects genuine astronomical observation from a northern location or pure mythological embellishment is impossible to determine, but the specificity of the claim sets Hecataeus apart from purely literary treatments of the theme.
  3. §Fragment 7a (Boreades): The detail that the kings are called Boreades—descendants of Boreas, the North Wind—and that their succession is hereditary within the same family connects the political and natural orders. The rulers of Hyperborea are literally children of the wind that defines their land. This fusion of lineage, geography, and cosmic force is characteristic of the Hyperborean worldview: everything is connected, everything participates in the divine order. There is no gap between nature and culture, between the human and the elemental.
