Introduction

Exodus 20:1–17 contains the Ten Commandments (Decalogue), delivered by God at Sinai. These commandments became the foundation of both Jewish halakha and Christian moral theology. From the Septuagint.

§20:1–7 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

καὶ ἐλάλησεν κύριος πάντας τοὺς λόγους τούτους

English

And the Lord spoke all these words, saying: “I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery. You shall have no other gods before me. You shall not make for yourself an idol, or any likeness of anything that is in heaven above, or on the earth beneath, or in the waters under the earth.”

§20:12–17 · Septuagint
Manuscript
Greek

τίμα τὸν πατέρα σου καὶ τὴν μητέρα σου

English

Honor your father and your mother, that it may be well with you, and that you may live long on the good land that the Lord your God is giving you. You shall not murder. You shall not commit adultery. You shall not steal. You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor. You shall not covet your neighbor’s house.

Translator's Notes

  1. §20:2: The commandments begin not with an imperative but with a declaration of identity and liberation: “I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of Egypt.” The law is grounded in prior grace. Obedience is a response to deliverance, not a condition for it.
