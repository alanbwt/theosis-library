Introduction

The Orpheus episode in Georgics IV (453–527) tells how the legendary poet descended to the underworld to reclaim his wife Eurydice, only to lose her by looking back. Virgil transforms this myth into a meditation on art, love, and the limits of human longing.

§IV.453–480 · Virgil, Georgics IV.453–480 (Mynors, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Non te nullius exercent numinis irae; magna luis commissa: tibi has miserabilis Orpheus haudquaquam ob meritum poenas, ni fata resistant, suscitat.

English

Not even Proteus could escape Aristaeus, who held him fast though he turned into every shape—flame, beast, flowing water. At last the old seer spoke: It is the anger of Orpheus that pursues you. For his lost wife he calls down upon you this punishment—not undeserved. Orpheus, stricken with grief, sought the shades below and crossed the Stygian lake to charm the dead with his lyre.

Translator's Notes

  1. §IV.453–480: The Orpheus myth became a central allegory in Neoplatonic thought: the descent to the underworld represents the soul’s fall into matter, and the backward glance symbolizes the attachment to sensible beauty that prevents the soul’s full return to the intelligible realm.
