Introduction

The Epistle of Jude warns against false teachers who have infiltrated the community and cites the apocryphal Book of Enoch and the Assumption of Moses. It is one of the shortest books in the New Testament. From Codex Sinaiticus.

§1:3–4 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

αγαπητοι πασαν σπουδην ποιουμενος γραφειν υμιν

English

Beloved, although I was very eager to write to you about our common salvation, I found it necessary to write appealing to you to contend for the faith that was once for all delivered to the saints. For certain people have crept in unnoticed who long ago were designated for this condemnation, ungodly people, who pervert the grace of our God into sensuality and deny our only Master and Lord, Jesus Christ.

§1:14–15 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

προεφητευσεν δε και τουτοις εβδομος απο αδαμ ενωχ

English

It was also about these that Enoch, the seventh from Adam, prophesied, saying, “Behold, the Lord comes with ten thousands of his holy ones, to execute judgment on all and to convict all the ungodly of all their deeds of ungodliness that they have committed in such an ungodly way.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §1:14: Jude’s quotation of 1 Enoch 1:9 is the only direct citation of a pseudepigraphal text in the New Testament. This citation contributed to the Ethiopian Church’s decision to include 1 Enoch in its biblical canon, while other traditions debated whether Jude’s use implied scriptural authority for the source.
