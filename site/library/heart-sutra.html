Heart Sutra: Form Is Emptiness

Nagarjuna (attributed), c. 150-250 AD

Introduction

The Heart Sutra (Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya) is the most recited text in Mahayana Buddhism—a condensation of the vast Perfection of Wisdom literature into approximately 260 words in its Chinese translation, fewer in Sanskrit. It is chanted daily in Zen, Tibetan, and Chinese Buddhist monasteries across the world. Its central declaration—“form is emptiness, emptiness is form”—is the most compressed statement of Buddhist ontology ever composed. Nothing exists with inherent, independent being. Every phenomenon is śūnya, empty of self-nature, arising only in dependence on other phenomena.

For the theosis question, the Heart Sutra represents a radical alternative. Where the Christian and Neoplatonic traditions posit a fullness (pleroma) to which beings ascend, the Heart Sutra posits an emptiness (śūnyatā) that is not absence but the very condition of all appearance. There is no God to become because there is no fixed self to do the becoming. And yet the sutra ends with a mantra—gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā—“gone, gone, gone beyond, gone utterly beyond: awakening!” Something is attained. The question is what, and by whom.

§Opening · Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Ārya-Avalokiteśvaro bodhisattvo gambhīrāṃ prajñāpāramitā-caryāṃ caramāṇo vyavalokayati sma: pañca-skandhās tāṃś ca svabhāva-śūnyān paśyati sma.

English

The noble Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, while practising the deep practice of the Perfection of Wisdom, looked upon the five aggregates and saw that in their own nature they were empty.

§The Core Declaration · Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Iha Śāriputra rūpaṃ śūnyatā, śūnyataiva rūpam; rūpān na pṛthak śūnyatā, śūnyatāyā na pṛthag rūpam; yad rūpaṃ sā śūnyatā, yā śūnyatā tad rūpam. Evam eva vedanā-saṃjñā-saṃskāra-vijñānāni.

English

“Śāriputra, form is not different from emptiness; emptiness is not different from form. Form itself is emptiness; emptiness itself is form. So too are feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness.”

§The Negations · Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Śāriputra sarva-dharmāḥ śūnyatā-lakṣaṇā, anutpannā aniruddhā, amalā avimalā, anūnā aparipūrṇāḥ. Tasmāc Chāriputra śūnyatāyāṃ na rūpaṃ, na vedanā, na saṃjñā, na saṃskārāḥ, na vijñānam.

English

“Śāriputra, all dharmas are marked with emptiness: they are neither produced nor destroyed, neither defiled nor pure, neither deficient nor complete. Therefore, in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, no perception, no mental formations, no consciousness.”

§No Path, No Attainment · Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Na avidyā nāvidyā-kṣayo, yāvan na jarā-maraṇaṃ na jarā-maraṇa-kṣayo; na duḥkha-samudaya-nirodha-mārgā; na jñānaṃ, na prāptir na aprāptiḥ.

English

“There is no ignorance, no extinction of ignorance, and so forth up to no ageing and death, no extinction of ageing and death. There is no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path. There is no wisdom, no attainment, and no non-attainment.”

§The Mantra · Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra
Manuscript
Sanskrit

Tasmāj jñātavyaṃ prajñāpāramitā mahā-mantro mahā-vidyā-mantro ’nuttara-mantro ’samasama-mantraḥ, sarva-duḥkha-praśamanaḥ, satyam amithyatvāt. Prajñāpāramitāyām ukto mantraḥ. Tadyathā: gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā.

English

Therefore, know the Perfection of Wisdom is the great mantra, the mantra of great knowledge, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequalled mantra, which removes all suffering. It is true, not false. So proclaim the Perfection of Wisdom mantra. Proclaim the mantra that says: gone, gone, gone beyond, gone utterly beyond—awakening!

Translator's Notes

  1. §The Core Declaration: The equation rūpaṃ śūnyatā (“form is emptiness”) is not a statement of nihilism. Śūnyatā does not mean “nothingness” but “emptiness of inherent existence” (svabhāva-śūnyatā). Things exist, but they exist dependently, relationally, without a fixed essence. The equation runs in both directions: form is emptiness, but emptiness is also form. This prevents the sutra from collapsing into mere negation.
  2. §The Negations: The sutra systematically negates every category of Buddhist teaching—the five aggregates, the twelve sense bases, the eighteen elements, the twelve links of dependent origination, the Four Noble Truths, and even wisdom and attainment themselves. This is not the Buddha contradicting his own teaching but the Perfection of Wisdom tradition insisting that even the dharma is a raft to be abandoned once the far shore is reached. The teaching that liberates must itself be emptied of inherent existence.
  3. §The Mantra: The mantra gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā is typically left untranslated in Buddhist practice, treated as a performative utterance rather than a propositional statement. The word gate is a past participle meaning “gone.” The progression—gone, gone, gone beyond, gone completely beyond—enacts the very crossing it describes. Bodhi means awakening or enlightenment. Svāhā is a ritual exclamation with no semantic content, like “amen” or “so be it.”
