Revelation 21:1-7: I Make All Things New

John (attributed), c. 90–110 AD

Introduction

Revelation 21:1–7 presents the consummation of the theosis narrative: a new heaven and a new earth, the holy city descending from God, and the divine voice declaring, “Behold, I make all things new.” The passage gathers every thread of biblical promise into a single vision of transformed reality. God dwells with humanity; death, mourning, and pain are abolished; the one seated on the throne announces the cosmic renewal that has been underway since the Incarnation.

The climactic promise belongs to verse 7: “The one who conquers shall inherit these things, and I will be his God and he shall be my son.” This is the covenant formula of the Old Testament—“I will be their God and they shall be my people”—elevated to its ultimate expression. The relationship is no longer between sovereign and subject but between Father and child. The conqueror inherits not a land or a kingdom but “these things”—the new creation itself. Human beings become, in the fullest sense, children of God, heirs of the new cosmos.

And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, “Behold, the dwelling of God is with humanity, and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his peoples, and God himself will be with them as their God. English

And the one seated on the throne said, “Behold, I make all things new.” And he said, “Write, for these words are faithful and true.” And he said to me, “It is done. I am the Alpha and the Omega, the beginning and the end. To the one who thirsts I will give from the spring of the water of life without payment. Translator's Notes

  1. §21:3: The word “dwelling” (σκηνή) is the same root as the verb used in John 1:14 (“the Word became flesh and dwelt among us”). The tabernacle imagery runs through the entire biblical narrative: from the wilderness tabernacle, to Solomon’s temple, to the incarnate Christ, to the eschatological city where God’s dwelling is permanently and universally with humanity. The trajectory of Scripture is God drawing ever closer to his creation.
  2. §21:7: The promise “I will be his God and he shall be my son” (ἔσομαι αὐτῷ θεὸς καὶ αὐτὸς ἔσται μοι υἱός) transforms the Old Testament covenant formula into a statement of divine filiation. In the old covenant, Israel was God’s people; in the new creation, each individual is God’s child. The language of inheritance (κληρονομήσει) connects to Paul’s theme of believers as “heirs of God and co-heirs with Christ” (Romans 8:17).
