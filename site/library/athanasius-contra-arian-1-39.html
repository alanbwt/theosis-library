Contra Arianos I.39: Made Man That We Might Be Made God

Athanasius of Alexandria, c. 296–373 AD

Introduction

In Contra Arianos I.39, Athanasius of Alexandria deploys the exchange formula that would become the single most cited statement in the theosis tradition: “He was made man that we might be made God.” (Autos enenthropisin hina hemeis theopoiethomen.) This is not a passing metaphor. It is the structural logic of Athanasius’s entire anti-Arian argument. If Christ is not truly God—as the Arians claimed—then his assumption of human nature accomplishes nothing. Only if the one who descends is fully divine can the humanity he assumes be truly deified.

Athanasius wrote the Orations against the Arians around 339–343 CE, during a period of exile when the Arian position dominated the imperial church. The Greek text survives in multiple manuscripts. The exchange formula has roots in Irenaeus (“he became what we are, that he might make us what he is”) and would be repeated by virtually every major Greek-speaking theologian for the next thousand years.

§I.39a · Contra Arianos I.39 (Metzler, AW I.1.2)
Manuscript
Greek

αὐτὸς γὰρ ἐνηνθρώπησεν ἵνα ἡμεῖς θεοποιηθῶμεν · καὶ ἐφανέρωσεν ἑαυτὸν διὰ σώματος ἵνα ἡμεῖς τοῦ ἀοράτου πατρὸς ἔννοιαν λάβωμεν ·

English

For he was made man that we might be made God (theopoiethomen, θεοποιηθῶμεν); and he manifested himself through a body that we might receive the idea of the unseen Father; and he endured the insolence of men that we might inherit immortality.

§I.39b · Contra Arianos I.39 (Metzler, AW I.1.2)
Manuscript
Greek

αὐτὸς γὰρ οὐκ ἐλαττώθη φορῶν τὸ σῶμα ἡμῶν · ἀλλὰ μᾶλλον ἐθεοποίησεν ὃ ἐνεδύσατο · καὶ προσέτι ἐχαρίσατο τοῦτο τῷ γένει τῶν ἀνθρώπων ·

English

For he himself was not diminished by bearing our body, but rather he deified (etheopoiesen, ἐθεοποίησεν) that which he put on, and further gave it graciously to the race of humankind.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.39a: The verb theopoieo (θεοποιέω) is stronger than “divinise” in its modern English sense. It means literally “to make God”—to produce the quality of divinity in something that did not previously possess it. Athanasius does not qualify or soften the claim. The exchange is symmetrical: genuine incarnation on one side, genuine deification on the other. If one is merely metaphorical, both are. The Arians understood this perfectly, which is why they denied the full divinity of Christ—to block the deification of humanity.
  2. §I.39b: The phrase “he deified that which he put on” uses the clothing metaphor common in patristic theology. Christ “puts on” human nature as a garment—but unlike the Gnostic version of this metaphor (as in the Trimorphic Protennoia), the garment is not discarded. It is permanently transformed. The human nature Christ assumed remains divine forever. This is the basis of the Orthodox doctrine that the Incarnation is irreversible: God did not visit humanity and leave. He became human permanently, and in doing so, opened a permanent path for humanity to participate in divine nature.
