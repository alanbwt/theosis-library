Introduction

The Vigrahavāvartanī is Nāgārjuna’s defense of śūnyatā against the charge that emptiness is self-refuting. If all statements are empty, is the statement “all is empty” also empty?

§1-5 · Vigrahavāvartanī
Manuscript
Greek

yadi sarvesham bhāvānāṃ śūnyatvaṃ | tava vacānaṃ api śūnyaṃ

English

The opponent says: “If all things are empty of inherent existence, then your words too are empty and cannot negate inherent existence.” Nāgārjuna replies: “My statement is itself empty. Being empty, it does not negate anything from its own side. It is like a magically created person negating another magically created person.”

§29-30 · Vigrahavāvartanī
Manuscript
Greek

yadi kācana pratijñā syān me tata eṣa me bhaved doṣaḥ | nāsti ca mama pratijñā tasmān naivāsti me doṣaḥ

English

If I had any thesis, I would have that fault. But I have no thesis. Therefore I have no fault. If, through perception and the other means of valid cognition, I were to apprehend any objects, I would affirm or deny things. But since such objects do not exist for me, I cannot be criticized.

Translator's Notes

  1. §29: Nāgārjuna’s claim “I have no thesis” is the most radical epistemological move in Indian philosophy. It immunizes his position against all objections by refusing to occupy a position. This is not sophistry but the logical consequence of thoroughgoing emptiness applied to emptiness itself.
