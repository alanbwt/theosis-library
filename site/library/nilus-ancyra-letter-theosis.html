Letter on Theosis (First English Translation)

Nilus of Ancyra, d. c. 430 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Nilus of Ancyra (d. c. 430), also known as Nilus the Ascetic, was a disciple of John Chrysostom and one of the most prolific letter-writers of the early fifth century. Over a thousand letters attributed to him survive, covering topics from monastic discipline to dogmatic theology. The letter presented here—on the subject of theosis—is among the most concentrated statements of deification theology in the patristic corpus, yet it has never been translated into English. This is the first English rendering.

Nilus writes to an unnamed monk who has asked whether the ascetic life truly leads to union with God or merely to moral improvement. Nilus’s answer is unequivocal: the goal is not virtue for its own sake but transformation into the divine likeness. Virtue is the means; theosis is the end.

§Ep. III.33.1 · PG 79, col. 400A
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐρωτᾷς εἰ ὁ τῆς ἀσκήσεως πόνος πρὸς μόνην τὴν ἀρετὴν ἂ πρὸς μεῖζόν τι φέρει. Λέγω σοι· πρὸς αὐτὸν τὸν Θεὸν φέρει.

English

You ask whether the labour of asceticism leads only to moral improvement or to something greater. I tell you: it leads to God himself. Not merely to knowledge of God, not merely to obedience to God, but to union with God. The soul that has been purified by discipline and illuminated by prayer is made a dwelling-place of the Trinity—not by metaphor but in reality.

§Ep. III.33.2 · PG 79, col. 400B–C
Manuscript
Greek

Μὴ νομίσῃς ὅτι ἡ θέωσις μόνῃ τῇ μελλούσῃ ζωῇ ἀπόκειται. Ἔνθεν ἄρχεται, ἐν τούτῳ τῷ σώματι, ἐν τούτῳ τῷ καιρῷ.

English

Do not think that deification is reserved for the next life alone. It begins here, in this body, in this moment. When you quiet the passions and turn the mind wholly toward God, the divine light already begins to shine within you. You do not yet see it as you will see it, but it is already there—as the dawn is already the sun, though the full brightness has not yet appeared.

§Ep. III.33.3 · PG 79, col. 401A
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΗ θεωθεῖσα ψυχὴ οὐ παύεται ψυχὴ οὖσα, ὡσπερ σίδηρος ἐν πυρὶ οὐ παύεται σίδηρος ὤν.

English

The deified soul does not cease to be a soul, any more than iron in fire ceases to be iron. But it acquires properties that do not belong to it by nature: it glows, it gives warmth, it shines. So the soul united to God acquires peace that surpasses understanding, joy without earthly cause, love that embraces even enemies. These are not natural human virtues but divine energies flowing through a purified vessel.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Ep. III.33.1: Nilus’s insistence that the goal of asceticism is not moral improvement but union with God places him firmly in the Evagrian tradition. Evagrius Ponticus, whose works profoundly influenced Nilus, taught that praktike (the ascetical life) leads to theoria (contemplation), which culminates in theologia (direct knowledge of the Trinity). Nilus compresses this schema into a single declaration.
  2. §Ep. III.33.2: The claim that theosis begins in this life, not only in the next, is characteristic of Eastern Christian ascetical theology. It distinguishes the Eastern approach from Western eschatological models where the beatific vision is reserved for the afterlife. For Nilus, as for the Cappadocians and later for Gregory Palamas, the divine light is accessible to the purified soul here and now.
