Oration on 'The Father Is Greater' (First English Translation)

Amphilochius of Iconium, c. 340-403 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Amphilochius of Iconium (c. 340–395), cousin of Gregory of Nazianzus and close associate of Basil of Caesarea, was one of the Cappadocian circle’s most effective defenders of Nicene orthodoxy. His Oration on “The Father is greater than I” (John 14:28) addresses the Arian challenge directly: if Christ himself says the Father is greater, how can the Son be fully God? This is the first English translation of this text.

Amphilochius resolves the problem through the theology of the two natures. The saying belongs to Christ’s humanity, not his divinity. But the implications run deeper: because divinity and humanity are united in Christ, humanity itself is elevated. The “greater” of the Father becomes the measure of the distance that theosis traverses—the gap between creature and Creator that the Incarnation bridges, not by lowering God but by raising man.

§§1 · Datema, Amphilochii Iconiensis Opera, fr. 15
Manuscript
Greek

Ὄταν λέγῃ ὁ Κύριος· ῊΟ Πατὴρ μείζων μου ἐστίν, οὐχ ὡς Θεὸς ἐλαττωθεὶς φθέγγεται, ἀλλ’ ὡς ἄνθρωπος προσληφθείς.

English

When the Lord says, “The Father is greater than I,” he speaks not as God diminished but as man assumed. For the same one who says “I and the Father are one” also says “The Father is greater than I.” If you take both sayings from the same nature, you create a contradiction. But if you understand one from his divinity and the other from his humanity, the harmony is perfect.

§§3 · Datema, Amphilochii Iconiensis Opera, fr. 15
Manuscript
Greek

Κατέβη ἵνα ἡμεῖς ἀναβῶμεν. Μείζονα τὸν Πατέρα προσεῖπεν, ἵνα μάθωμεν τὸ μέτρον τῆς ἡμετέρας ἀναβάσεως.

English

He descended that we might ascend. He called the Father greater so that we might learn the measure of our elevation. For if he who is equal to the Father became less by taking our nature, then our nature, by being taken up into him, has become greater than it was. The humility of Christ is the exaltation of humanity.

§§5 · Datema, Amphilochii Iconiensis Opera, fr. 15
Manuscript
Greek

Μὴ εἴπῃς ὅτι ἡ θέωσις ἀδύνατος ἐπειδὴ ὁ Πατὴρ μείζων. Μᾶλλον νόησον ὅτι τὸ μέγεθος τοῦ Πατρὸς αἰτία τῆς θεώσεώς ἐστιν.

English

Do not say that deification (theosis, θέωσις) is impossible because the Father is greater. Rather, understand that the Father’s greatness is the very reason deification is possible. For the Son did not come to make us equal to the Father—that would be madness—but to make us partakers of the divine nature through himself. As fire does not make iron into fire but makes it fiery, so God does not make man into God by nature but makes him godlike by grace.

§§7 · Datema, Amphilochii Iconiensis Opera, fr. 15
Manuscript
Greek

Τὸ ἡμέτερον ἔλαβεν ἵνα τὸ ἑαυτοῦ δώσῃ ἡμῖν. Δουλείαν προσέλαβεν ἵνα υἱοθεσίαν ἡμεῖς λάβωμεν.

English

He assumed what is ours so that he might give us what is his. He took servitude so that we might receive adoption. He tasted death so that we might taste immortality. This is the great exchange (antidosis, ἀντίδοσις): not that God became less, but that man became more—not in essence but in participation, not by nature but by the gift of the Spirit.

Translator's Notes

  1. §§1: The hermeneutical principle deployed here—assigning different sayings of Christ to his different natures—became standard in Chalcedonian exegesis. Amphilochius is an early witness to this approach, which was later systematised by Cyril of Alexandria and Leontius of Byzantium.
  2. §§5: The iron-and-fire analogy appears here in a form very close to what Didymus uses in De Spiritu Sancto. The distinction between becoming God “by nature” (impossible) and becoming godlike “by grace” (the goal of Christian life) is the essential safeguard that distinguishes orthodox theosis from pantheistic absorption.
  3. §§7: The term antidosis (ἀντίδοσις, “exchange”) is closely related to the more common patristic formula of the “wonderful exchange” (admirabile commercium). The pattern—“he took what is ours to give us what is his”—appears in Irenaeus, Athanasius, and Gregory of Nazianzus. Amphilochius’s version adds the pneumatological dimension: the exchange is accomplished specifically through the gift of the Spirit.
