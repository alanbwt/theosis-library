Introduction

Pyrrho of Elis (c. 365–275 BCE) founded the skeptical tradition that bears his name. He left no writings; his philosophy survives through his student Timon and later doxographers. The key testimony is Aristocles’s report via Timon on the nature of things.

Greek original and English translation.
§Aristocles Fragment · Aristocles ap. Eusebius, Praeparatio Evangelica XIV.18.1–5
Manuscript
Greek

τὰ πράγματα ἐπ᾽ ἴσης ἀδιάφορα καὶ ἀστάθμητα καὶ ἀνεπίκριτα

English

Whoever wishes to live well must consider these three questions: first, what are things like by nature? Second, what attitude should we adopt toward them? Third, what will be the outcome for those who adopt this attitude? Pyrrho declared that things are equally indifferent, unmeasurable, and undecidable. Therefore neither our sensations nor our judgments tell us truths or falsehoods.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Aristocles Fragment: The three terms adiaphora (indifferent), astathmeta (unmeasurable), and anepikrita (undecidable) constitute Pyrrho’s radical thesis: things are in themselves none of the things we say about them. This went further than later Pyrrhonism, which merely suspended judgment.
