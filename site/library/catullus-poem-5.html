Introduction

Poem 5, addressed to Lesbia, is Catullus’s most famous lyric: “Let us live, my Lesbia, and let us love.” Against the background of death’s perpetual night, the poet urges an infinity of kisses—too many to count, lest the envious cast a spell on their happiness.

§Carmen 5 · Catullus, Carmina 5 (Mynors, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

Vivamus, mea Lesbia, atque amemus, rumoresque senum severiorum omnes unius aestimemus assis. Soles occidere et redire possunt: nobis cum semel occidit brevis lux, nox est perpetua una dormienda.

English

Let us live, my Lesbia, and let us love, and let us value all the rumors of stern old men at a single penny. Suns can set and rise again; for us, when once our brief light has set, there is one perpetual night to be slept through. Give me a thousand kisses, then a hundred, then another thousand, then a second hundred, then yet another thousand, then a hundred.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Carmen 5: The phrase nox est perpetua una dormienda (“one perpetual night must be slept through”) reflects an Epicurean view of death as final unconsciousness. The poem’s urgency arises from this materialist premise: if there is no afterlife, the present moment is all we have.
