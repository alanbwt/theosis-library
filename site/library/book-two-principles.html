Book of the Two Principles: Cathar Dualism

Unknown (Gospel of Thomas), c. 50–140 AD

Introduction

The Liber de Duobus Principiis (Book of the Two Principles) is the most philosophically rigorous surviving Cathar theological text, composed in Latin in the mid-thirteenth century, likely in northern Italy. It was rediscovered in 1939 in a manuscript in the National Library of Florence. Unlike the polemical descriptions of Cathar belief written by their persecutors, this text presents the dualist position in the Cathars’ own words, with careful logical argumentation.

The core thesis is radical: there are two eternal principles, one good and one evil. The good God created the spiritual world; the evil principle created the material world. Human souls are divine sparks—fragments of the good God—imprisoned in material bodies by the evil creator. Salvation consists in the soul’s recognition of its true origin and its liberation from the flesh. This is Gnostic theosis in medieval dress: the Cathars understood themselves as continuing the original Christian teaching that the material world is a prison, and that the goal of the spiritual life is escape from matter and return to the divine source.

Latin original and English translation, with manuscript scans.
§I.1 · Florence MS, Conventi Soppressi I.II.44, f. 1r
Manuscript
Latin

Sciendum est quod duo sunt principia: unum boni et unum mali, sicut demonstrabimus per auctoritates et rationes claras.

English

It must be understood that there are two principles: one of good and one of evil, as we shall demonstrate by authorities and clear reasons. For it is necessary to posit two principles, one of good things and one of evil things, since it is impossible that the same tree should bring forth good fruit and bad fruit.

§I.2-3 · Florence MS, f. 1r-v
Manuscript
Latin

Dicit enim Dominus in Matthaeo: Non potest arbor bona malos fructus facere, neque arbor mala bonos fructus facere.

English

The Lord himself says in Matthew: “A good tree cannot bring forth evil fruit, neither can a corrupt tree bring forth good fruit.” From this it is clear that there exists another principle, that of evil, which is the cause of evil things. For if there were only one principle, namely the good God, there would be no evil in the world.

§II.1-2 · Florence MS, f. 2r
Manuscript
Latin

Praeterea verus Deus, cum sit totus bonus, non potuit creare res quas videmus in hoc mundo: dolorem, morbum, mortem, corruptionem.

English

Furthermore, the true God, being entirely good, could not have created the things we see in this world: suffering, disease, death, corruption, and the devouring of one creature by another. These things cannot proceed from a good principle. Therefore they must proceed from another principle, which is evil by nature.

§III.1-3 · Florence MS, f. 3r
Manuscript
Latin

Animae autem hominum non sunt de malo principio sed de bono Deo. Creatae fuerunt in mundo caelesti, in luce et in gaudio.

English

Now the souls of human beings are not from the evil principle but from the good God. They were created in the heavenly world, in light and in joy. But the evil principle, through envy and malice, drew them down and imprisoned them in bodies of flesh. The body is the prison of the soul. The material world is the kingdom of the evil principle.

§IV.1-2 · Florence MS, f. 4r
Manuscript
Latin

Christus venit in hunc mundum non in vera carne sed in apparentia carnis, quia bonus Deus non potuit vere intrare creaturam mali principii.

English

Christ came into this world not in true flesh but in the appearance of flesh, for the good God could not truly enter the creation of the evil principle. Christ came to reveal to souls their true origin: that they are from the good God, that the material world is not their home, and that through knowledge and the consolation of the Holy Spirit they may return to their heavenly Father.

§V.1-3 · Florence MS, f. 5r
Manuscript
Latin

Finis ergo vitae Christianae est liberatio animae a corpore et a mundo materiali. Hoc fit per consolamentum.

English

Therefore the goal of the Christian life is the liberation of the soul from the body and from the material world. This is accomplished through the consolamentum, the laying on of hands by which the Holy Spirit is transmitted. The one who receives the consolamentum and keeps the commandments shall, at the death of the body, return to the kingdom of the good God, from which the soul originally came.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1: The argument from Matthew 7:18 (“A good tree cannot bring forth evil fruit”) is characteristic of Cathar exegesis: they read the Bible carefully and literally, using Christ’s own words to argue for two principles. Catholic apologists struggled with this argument precisely because the Cathars were quoting Scripture, not rejecting it.
  2. §III.1-3: The Cathar doctrine of the soul’s heavenly origin and imprisonment in flesh is structurally identical to the Valentinian Gnostic account of divine sparks trapped in matter. Whether the Cathars inherited this directly from ancient Gnostic communities (through the Bogomils of the Balkans) or arrived at it independently through their own reading of Scripture is one of the great debates in medieval religious history.
  3. §IV.1-2: Cathar Christology was docetic: Christ appeared to have a body but did not truly possess one. This follows logically from their premises—if matter is the creation of the evil principle, then the good God cannot truly become material. The Catholic Church condemned this as heresy, insisting on the reality of the Incarnation, precisely because the orthodox theosis tradition requires that God truly became human so that humans might truly become divine.
