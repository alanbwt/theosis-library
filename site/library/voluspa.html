Voluspa: The Seeress's Prophecy

Anonymous (Norse), c. 10th-13th c. AD

Introduction

The Völuspá (“The Seeress’s Prophecy”) is the first and greatest poem of the Poetic Edda, preserved in the thirteenth-century Codex Regius manuscript but composed centuries earlier, likely in the tenth century in Iceland. A völva (seeress, prophetess) speaks to Odin, recounting the creation of the world from the void of Ginnungagap, the establishment of the cosmic order, the growth of the World Tree Yggdrasil, the coming of Ragnarök—the destruction of the gods and the world—and finally the rebirth of a renewed earth rising green from the sea.

The Völuspá is the Norse answer to the question that every theosis tradition must confront: is the divine permanent or does it cycle through destruction and renewal? The poem’s answer is unambiguous—the gods themselves die at Ragnarök, but the world is reborn. This is neither the linear eschatology of the Abrahamic traditions nor the eternal unchanging One of the Neoplatonists. It is a cosmic rhythm of creation, destruction, and recreation in which even the highest powers are subject to fate (urðr). The poem’s final stanzas, describing a renewed world where the gods return and unsown fields bear fruit, have been debated endlessly: are they a pre-Christian vision of cyclical renewal, or a later interpolation reflecting Christian influence?

§Stanza 1–2 · Völuspá 1–2, Codex Regius
Manuscript
Old Norse

Hlióðs bið ek allar helgar kindir, meiri ok minni mögu Heimdallar; viltu at ek, Valföðr, vel fyr telja forn spöll fira, þau er fremst um man.

Ek man jötna ár um borna, þá er forðum mik fœdda höfðu; níu man ek heima, níu íviðjur, mjötvið mæran fyr mold neðan.

English

Hear me, all you hallowed beings, both high and low of Heimdall’s children. You wish, Odin, that I recount the ancient tales of beings, the earliest I remember.

I remember giants born in ancient times, those who long ago reared me. Nine worlds I remember, nine wood-ogresses, the glorious tree of fate beneath the earth.

§Stanza 3–4 · Völuspá 3–4, Codex Regius
Manuscript
Old Norse

Ár var alda þat er ekki var, var-a sandr né sær né svalar unnir; jörð fannsk æva né upphiminn, gap var ginnunga en gras hvergi.

Áðr Burs synir bjöðum um ypðu, þair er Miðgarð mæran skópu; sól skín sunnan á salar steina, þá var grund gróin grœnum lauki.

English

In the beginning there was nothing: neither sand nor sea, nor cold waves. No earth existed, nor heaven above. There was only Ginnungagap—the Yawning Void—and grass was nowhere.

Before the sons of Bur raised up the lands, they who shaped the glorious Middle-Earth. The sun shone from the south on the stones of the hall; then the ground was grown with green plants.

§Stanza 19–20 · Völuspá 19–20, Codex Regius
Manuscript
Old Norse

Ask veit ek standa, heitir Yggdrasill, hár baðmr, ausinn hvita auri; þaðan koma döggvar þars er í dala falla, stendr æ yfir grœnn Urðar brunni.

Þaðan koma meyjar márg vitandi þrír ór þim sal er und þolli stendr; Urð hétu eina, aðra Verðandi, skáru á skiði, Skuld ina þriðju.

English

I know an ash tree that stands, called Yggdrasil, a tall tree, watered with white clay. From there come the dews that fall in the valleys. It stands ever green above the Well of Fate.

From there come three maidens, deep in knowledge, from the lake that stands beneath the tree. One is called Urð (“Fate”), another Verðandi (“Becoming”), the third Skuld (“Obligation”). They carved on wooden slips, they laid down laws, they chose the lives of the children of mankind.

§Stanza 44–46 · Völuspá 44–46, Codex Regius
Manuscript
Old Norse

Skelfr Yggdrasils askr standandi, ymr it aldna tré, en jötunn losnar... Hrymr ekr austan, hefisk lind fyrir, snýsk Jörmungandr í jötunmóði.

English

Yggdrasil trembles, the ash standing, the old tree groans, and the giant breaks free. All are in fear on the roads of Hel, before the kin of Surt swallows it.

What ails the Aesir? What ails the elves? All Jötunheim groans. The gods are in council. The dwarves moan before their doors of stone, wise ones of the cliff-walls. Do you know yet, or what?

Hrym drives from the east, holds his shield before him. Jörmungandr writhes in giant fury. The serpent churns the waves. The eagle screams, tears corpses pale-beaked. Naglfar breaks free.

Old Norse

Sól tér sortna, sígr fold í mar, hverfa af himni heiðar stjörnur; gæsir eimi ok aldrnari, leikr hár hiti við himin sjálfan.

Sér hon upp koma öðru sinni jörð ór ægi, iðjagrœna; falla forsar, flýgr örn yfir, sá er á fjalli fiska veiðir.

English

The sun turns black. Earth sinks into the sea. The bright stars fall from the sky. Flame rages against the World Tree. The heat soars high against heaven itself.

She sees rising a second time earth from the sea, eternally green. Waterfalls flow. The eagle flies above, hunting fish on the mountain.

The Aesir meet on Idavoll and speak of the mighty World Serpent. They remember the great events and the ancient runes of the Mighty One.

§Stanza 59–62 · Völuspá 59–62, Codex Regius
Manuscript
Old Norse

Þar munu eftir undrsamligar gullnar töflur í grasi finnask... Ser hon sal standa, sólu fegra, gulli þakðan, á Gimlé; þar skulu dyggar drottir byggja ok um aldrdaga yndi njóta.

English

There the wondrous golden game-pieces shall be found in the grass, those which they possessed in the earliest days.

Unsown shall the fields bear fruit. All ills shall be healed. Baldr shall come. Baldr and Höðr shall dwell in Hropt’s battle-hall, the shrines of the slain gods. Do you know yet, or what?

Then Hœnir shall choose the prophetic wooden slip, and the sons of two brothers shall dwell broadly in the wide Wind-Home. Do you know yet, or what?

She sees a hall standing, fairer than the sun, roofed with gold, at Gimlé. There shall worthy people dwell and enjoy delights for all ages.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanza 3–4: The description of the primordial void—Ginnungagap—where “there was nothing: neither sand nor sea” resonates with multiple Near Eastern creation texts. Compare Genesis 1:2 (“the earth was formless and void”) and the Enuma Elish, where Apsu and Tiamat exist before any form. The Norse cosmogony, however, does not begin with a divine act of will or speech but with a spontaneous interaction of fire (Muspelheim) and ice (Niflheim) in the void—a materialist cosmogony in which the gods themselves are products of cosmic process, not its authors.
  2. §Stanza 19–20: Yggdrasil, the World Tree, is the cosmic axis connecting all nine worlds. The name likely means “Odin’s Horse” (Yggr = Odin, drasill = horse), referring to Odin’s self-sacrifice on the tree (see Hávamál 138). The three Norns—Urð (Fate/Past), Verðandi (Becoming/Present), Skuld (Obligation/Future)—carve the destinies of all beings on wooden slips. Even the gods are subject to their decrees. This places fate above divinity in the Norse cosmic order.
  3. §Stanza 56–58: The destruction and rebirth sequence is the theological heart of the poem. The sun turns black, the earth sinks, the stars fall—and then a new earth rises “eternally green.” The question of whether these final stanzas represent original pre-Christian Norse eschatology or Christian influence (the new creation of Revelation 21) has been debated since the nineteenth century. The most likely answer is that the cyclical pattern is genuinely Norse, but the specific imagery of the golden hall at Gimlé and the return of Baldr may reflect Christian contact.
  4. §Stanza 59–62: The “golden game-pieces” found in the grass are the same ones the gods played with before the corruption of the world. Their rediscovery signals a return to the original state of innocence—a restoration, not merely a new creation. The phrase “unsown shall the fields bear fruit” is a paradise motif found across Indo-European traditions (compare the Golden Age of Hesiod, the Garden of Eden). The Norse vision of the renewed world is not an eternal stasis but a return to the beginning of the cycle.
