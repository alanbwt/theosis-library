Institutes I.1: The Finite Cannot Contain the Infinite

John Calvin, 1509-1564 AD

Introduction

John Calvin (1509–1564) opens his Institutes of the Christian Religion with a meditation on the relationship between knowledge of God and knowledge of self. The first chapter establishes the foundational Reformed principle: the distance between God and the human being is absolute. Human beings can know God only because God condescends to reveal himself, not because humanity possesses any natural capacity to ascend to the divine. The later Reformed scholastic tradition distilled this into the axiom finitum non capax infiniti—the finite cannot contain the infinite.

This is the Protestant rejection of theosis. Where the Eastern Orthodox tradition teaches that human beings can participate in God’s uncreated energies, and where Catholic theology speaks of the beatific vision as a genuine sharing in divine knowledge, Calvin insists on an unbridgeable qualitative gap. Grace does not elevate human nature into the divine nature; it restores human nature to its proper creaturely function. The creature is perfected as a creature, never deified. God remains wholly other.

§I.1.1 · Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion I.1.1 (1559 edition)
Manuscript
Latin

Tota fere sapientiae nostrae summa, quae vera demum ac solida sapientia censeri debeat, duabus partibus constat: Dei cognitione et nostri.

English

Nearly all the wisdom we possess, that is to say, true and sound wisdom, consists of two parts: the knowledge of God and of ourselves. But as these are connected together by many ties, it is not easy to determine which of the two precedes and gives birth to the other.

§I.1.2 · Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion I.1.2 (1559 edition)
Manuscript
Latin

Quia primo nemo se ipse intueri potest quin ad Dei, in quo vivit et movetur, contemplationem sensus suos statim convertat.

English

For, in the first place, no man can survey himself without forthwith turning his thoughts toward the God in whom he lives and moves. Every person, upon contemplating his own nature, is immediately driven to contemplate God, for the misery that weighs upon us from every side compels us to look upward.

§I.1.3 · Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion I.1.3 (1559 edition)
Manuscript
Latin

Rursum hominem in sui cognitionem nequaquam pervenire constat, nisi prius Dei faciem fuerit contemplatus.

English

On the other hand, it is evident that no one can arrive at true self-knowledge until he has previously contemplated the face of God, and come down after such contemplation to look into himself. For so deeply is the arrogance ingrained in our nature that we always seem to ourselves righteous and wise and holy—until we are confronted by the majesty of God.

§I.1.4 · Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion I.1.3 (continued)
Manuscript
Latin

Hinc ille horror et stupor quo Scriptura passim refert sanctos fuisse percussos, quoties praesentiam Dei sentiebant.

English

Hence that dread and wonder with which Scripture constantly represents the saints as stricken whenever they felt the presence of God. When we see those who previously stood firm and secure so shaken with terror that they are almost swallowed up by the fear of death—we must infer that man is never sufficiently touched and affected by awareness of his lowliness until he has compared himself with God’s majesty.

Translator's Notes

  1. §I.1.1: Calvin wrote the first edition of the Institutes in Latin in 1536 and revised it repeatedly until the definitive edition of 1559. He also produced a French translation. The opening sentence establishes the mutual dependence of knowledge of God and knowledge of self—but the rest of the chapter makes clear that this is an asymmetrical relationship. Knowledge of self drives the human being toward God, but the human being can never close the gap through his own effort.
  2. §I.1.3–4: Calvin’s examples of saints “stricken with terror” before God’s majesty (Isaiah 6:5, Ezekiel 1:28, Job 42:5–6) are precisely the passages that the theosis tradition reads as transformative encounters. For the Orthodox, Isaiah’s vision of God is the beginning of deification; for Calvin, it is the beginning of self-knowledge through humiliation. The same texts yield opposite theologies depending on whether the divine-human gap is seen as something to be bridged or something to be acknowledged.
