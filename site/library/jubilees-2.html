Jubilees 2: Creation Through Angels

Anonymous (Enochic), c. 3rd c. BCE

Introduction

The Book of Jubilees is a Second Temple Jewish text, likely composed in Hebrew in the second century BCE. It retells Genesis and the first part of Exodus within a framework of angelic revelation: the Angel of the Presence dictates the entire history to Moses on Mount Sinai. The text survives complete only in Ethiopic, though Hebrew fragments were found among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran, confirming its antiquity and its importance to at least some Jewish communities before the Common Era.

Chapter 2 retells the creation account of Genesis 1, but with a crucial difference: God creates through angels. The angels of the presence, the angels of sanctification, and other angelic orders are themselves created on the first day and then participate in the rest of creation. This angelic framework transforms the Genesis narrative from a simple creation story into a cosmological hierarchy. For the theosis question, Jubilees matters because it establishes a model in which the boundary between divine and created beings is permeable—angels are created, yet they stand in God’s presence and participate in God’s creative work.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:2: The creation of angels on the first day is Jubilees’ most significant departure from Genesis. Genesis 1 mentions no angels; Jubilees places them at the very beginning, as part of the foundational structure of reality. The two highest orders—angels of the presence and angels of sanctification—are distinguished from the nature-angels who govern weather and seasons. This hierarchy would become enormously influential in later Jewish and Christian angelology.
  2. §2:4-6: The shift from third person to first person (“then we saw his works, and praised him”) reveals the narrator: the Angel of the Presence is speaking as a witness to creation. This makes the entire text an angelic testimony, adding a layer of authority that the Genesis account lacks. The seven great works on the first day (heavens, earth, waters, spirits, darkness, light, and the abyss) establish the Jubilees cosmology as more elaborate than the Genesis original.
  3. §2:14-16: The calendar is central to Jubilees’ theology. The sun, not the moon, governs the sacred calendar—a point of fierce controversy in Second Temple Judaism. The Qumran community followed a solar calendar, and the presence of Jubilees fragments at Qumran confirms this connection. The word “jubilees” in the title refers to the 49-year cycles that structure all of sacred history.
