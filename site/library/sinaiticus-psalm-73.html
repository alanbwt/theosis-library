Introduction

Psalm 73 is an Asaph psalm wrestling with the prosperity of the wicked, moving from near-collapse of faith to restored vision in God’s sanctuary. Its conclusion—“Whom have I in heaven but you?”—is one of the Old Testament’s most intimate expressions of devotion.

§73:1–3, 25–28 · Codex Sinaiticus, Psalm 73
Manuscript
Greek

ὡς ἀγαθὸς ὁ θεὸς τῷ Ισραηλ τοῖς εὐθέσι τῇ καρδίᾳ

English

Truly God is good to Israel, to those who are pure in heart. But as for me, my feet had almost stumbled. For I was envious of the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked. Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you. My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever.

Translator's Notes

  1. §73:25–26: The confession that God is the psalmist’s “portion” (μερίς) forever transcends the problem of theodicy by redefining the good: the ultimate good is not earthly prosperity but God himself. Augustine built his theology of desire on this insight.
