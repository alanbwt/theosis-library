Romans 6:3-11: Baptized into His Death

Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD

Introduction

Romans 6:3–11 is Paul’s most concentrated statement on the believer’s participation in Christ’s death and resurrection. The logic is not metaphorical: to be baptized “into Christ” is to be baptized “into his death.” The Greek preposition eis (εἰς) marks real entrance, real incorporation. The old self is co-crucified (synestaurōthē, συνεσταυρώθη) with Christ so that the body of sin might be rendered powerless, and the believer might “walk in newness of life.”

This passage became foundational for the patristic understanding of baptism as genuine ontological transformation rather than mere ritual washing. For Cyril of Jerusalem, the baptismal pool was a real tomb and a real womb—the old nature dies in the water and a new nature rises from it. The repeated syn- compounds (co-buried, co-crucified, co-planted, co-living) insist that what happened to Christ happens to the believer in him.

§6:3–4 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

ἢ ἀγνοεῖτε ὅτι ὅσοι ἐβαπτίσθημεν εἰς Χριστὸν Ἰησοῦν · εἰς τὸν θάνατον αὐτοῦ ἐβαπτίσθημεν · συνετάφημεν οὖν αὐτῷ διὰ τοῦ βαπτίσματος εἰς τὸν θάνατον · ἵνα ὥσπερ ἠγέρθη Χριστὸς ἐκ νεκρῶν διὰ τῆς δόξης τοῦ πατρός · οὕτως καὶ ἡμεῖς ἐν καινότητι ζωῆς περιπατήσωμεν ·

English

Or do you not know that all of us who were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death? Therefore we were buried with him through baptism into death, so that just as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, so also we might walk in newness of life.

§6:5–7 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ γὰρ σύμφυτοι γεγόναμεν τῷ ὁμοιώματι τοῦ θανάτου αὐτοῦ · ἀλλὰ καὶ τῆς ἀναστάσεως ἐσόμεθα · τοῦτο γινώσκοντες ὅτι ὁ παλαιὸς ἡμῶν ἄνθρωπος συνεσταυρώθη · ἵνα καταργηθῇ τὸ σῶμα τῆς ἁμαρτίας · τοῦ μηκέτι δουλεύειν ἡμᾶς τῇ ἁμαρτίᾳ · ὁ γὰρ ἀποθανὼν δεδικαίωται ἀπὸ τῆς ἁμαρτίας ·

English

For if we have been united with him in the likeness of his death, we shall certainly also be united with him in the likeness of his resurrection. We know that our old self was crucified with him, so that the body of sin might be rendered powerless, so that we would no longer be enslaved to sin. For the one who has died has been set free from sin.

§6:8–11 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ δὲ ἀπεθάνομεν σὺν Χριστῷ · πιστεύομεν ὅτι καὶ συνζήσομεν αὐτῷ · εἰδότες ὅτι Χριστὸς ἐγερθεὶς ἐκ νεκρῶν οὐκέτι ἀποθνήσκει · θάνατος αὐτοῦ οὐκέτι κυριεύει · ὃ γὰρ ἀπέθανεν · τῇ ἁμαρτίᾳ ἀπέθανεν ἐφάπαξ · ὃ δὲ ζῇ · ζῇ τῷ θεῷ · οὕτως καὶ ὑμεῖς λογίζεσθε ἑαυτοὺς νεκροὺς μὲν τῇ ἁμαρτίᾳ · ζῶντας δὲ τῷ θεῷ ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ ·

English

Now if we died with Christ, we believe that we shall also live with him, knowing that Christ, having been raised from the dead, dies no more; death no longer has dominion over him. For the death he died, he died to sin once for all; but the life he lives, he lives to God. So you also must consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus.

Translator's Notes

  1. §6:5: The word symphytoi (σύμφυτοι) means “grown together” or “planted together,” carrying the imagery of organic union. Like a graft that fuses with the rootstock, the believer’s life becomes inseparable from Christ’s death. This botanical metaphor anticipates the vine imagery of John 15.
  2. §6:6: The compound verb synestaurōthē (συνεσταυρώθη), “was co-crucified,” is one of Paul’s most daring syn- compounds. Paul uses over a dozen such compounds to describe the believer’s participation in Christ: co-buried, co-raised, co-seated, co-glorified. These are not figures of speech. For Paul, what happened to Christ at Golgotha and the empty tomb happened to every person baptized into him.
