Introduction

Jakob Bohme's Aurora (1612) is the foundational text of Christian theosophy. Written by an unlettered shoemaker after a mystical experience, it describes the inner dynamics of God's nature and the origin of good and evil.

Translation

§Chapter 1: The Investigation of the Divine Being · Aurora, Chapter 1

If we behold the whole of nature, the trees, the herbs, the grass, and everything that grows, we find in it the likeness of the Divine Being. The whole of visible nature is a manifestation of the interior spiritual world. For the external was born from the internal. The material world is like a great mirror of eternity, in which the spirit sees its own face. Every tree, stone, and creature carries within it a hidden fire—the divine spark from which it first came into being.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 1: Bohme's vision of nature as the visible expression of invisible spiritual reality influenced German Romanticism, Hegel's dialectical philosophy, and William Blake's poetry. His concept of the Ungrund (the abyss or groundless ground of God's being) anticipated Schelling's philosophy of nature and influenced depth psychology through its impact on Jung.
