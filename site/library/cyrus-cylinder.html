The Cyrus Cylinder: The First Declaration of Human Rights

        
Cyrus the Great, c. 600-530 BCE

        
British Museum (BM 90920)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Cyrus Cylinder, discovered in Babylon in 1879, records the conquest of Babylon by Cyrus the Great in 539 BCE. What makes it extraordinary is not the conquest but the aftermath: Cyrus declares religious freedom, frees captive peoples (including the Jews of the Babylonian Exile, as recorded in the Book of Ezra), and restores temples of foreign gods.
The cylinder has been called the world's first declaration of human rights. A replica is displayed at the United Nations headquarters. The text confirms the biblical account of Cyrus' decree allowing the Jews to return to Jerusalem and rebuild the Temple (Ezra 1:1-4, Isaiah 45:1-7).

Translation

        
        

          §1 · cyrus-cylinder
          
I am Cyrus, king of the universe, the great king, the powerful king, king of Babylon, king of Sumer and Akkad, king of the four quarters of the world. When I entered Babylon in peace, I established my royal residence in the palace amid jubilation and rejoicing. Marduk, the great lord, turned the hearts of the people of Babylon toward me. My vast army marched peacefully into Babylon. I did not allow anyone to terrorize the land of Sumer and Akkad. I sought the welfare of the city of Babylon and all its sanctuaries. As for the citizens of Babylon, upon whom Nabonidus had imposed a yoke not befitting them, I relieved their weariness and freed them from their service. I gathered all their people and returned them to their dwelling places. And the gods of Sumer and Akkad, whom Nabonidus had brought into Babylon, I returned to their places and let them dwell in eternal abodes.
