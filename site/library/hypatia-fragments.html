Introduction

Hypatia of Alexandria (c. 360–415 CE) was the most celebrated Neoplatonic philosopher of her age. No writings survive directly, but testimonies from Synesius of Cyrene, Socrates Scholasticus, and the Suda preserve her intellectual legacy and her violent death at the hands of a Christian mob.

Greek

ἡμεῖς οἱ σοὶ μαθηταὶ λέγομεν σε καθηγήτριαν

English

Synesius writes to Hypatia: “We who have been your students must speak of you as our teacher in all that is sacred and true.” The Suda records that she wrote commentaries on Diophantus’s Arithmetica, on Apollonius’s Conics, and on the astronomical tables of Ptolemy. She surpassed all the philosophers of her time and lectured publicly on Plato, Aristotle, and the sciences.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Testimonia: Hypatia’s murder in 415 CE by a mob of Alexandrian parabalani (church-associated paramilitary) has come to symbolize the conflict between classical learning and militant Christianity. Synesius, himself a Christian bishop, shows that her philosophical circle included both pagans and Christians.
