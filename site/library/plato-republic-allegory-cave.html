Republic VII: The Allegory of the Cave

Plato, c. 428-348 BCE

Introduction

The Allegory of the Cave opens Book VII of the Republic and is the single most influential image in Western philosophy. Prisoners chained in a cave since childhood see only shadows cast on the wall by a fire behind them. They take the shadows for reality. One prisoner is freed, dragged painfully upward into the sunlight, and eventually sees the sun itself—the Form of the Good. But the allegory does not end with enlightenment. The freed prisoner must return to the cave and attempt to govern those who remain in darkness.

This return is the political core of the allegory. Plato is not describing a private mystical ascent but a public obligation. Those who have seen the truth are the only ones fit to rule, and they must be compelled to do so against their own preference for contemplation. The tension between the philosopher’s desire to remain in the light and the city’s need for his governance is never resolved. It is the permanent problem of politics: those who most want power are least fit to wield it, and those most fit to wield it want nothing to do with it.

§514a–515a (The Cave) · Plato, Republic VII, 514a–515a
Manuscript
Greek

Ἰδὲ γὰρ ἀνθρώπους οἷον ἐν καταγείῳ οἰκήσει σπηλαιώδει, ἀναπεπταμένην πρὸς τὸ φῶς τὴν εἴσοδον ἔχουσαν μακρὰν παρὰ πᾶν τὸ σπήλαιον, ἐν ταύτῃ ἐκ παίδων ὄντας ἐν δεσμοῖς καὶ τὰ σκέλη καὶ τοὺς αὐχένας.

English

Picture human beings living in an underground cave-like dwelling, with an entrance open to the light a long way up across the whole width of the cave. They have been there since childhood, with their legs and necks in bonds, so that they remain in the same place and can only see ahead of them, as the bonds prevent them from turning their heads. Light reaches them from a fire burning far above and behind them. Between the fire and the prisoners there is a raised path, along which imagine a low wall has been built—like the screens puppet-showmen put up in front of their audience, over which they show their puppets.

§515c–516a (The Ascent) · Plato, Republic VII, 515c–516a
Manuscript
Greek

σκόπει δή, ἦν δ’ ἐγώ, αὐτῶν λύσιν τε καὶ ἴασιν τῆς ἀφροσύνης, οἵα τις ἂν εἴη εἰ φύσει τοιάδε συμβαίνοι αὐτοῖς.

English

Consider, then, what being released from their bonds and cured of their ignorance would be like. When one of them was freed and suddenly compelled to stand up, turn his head, walk, and look toward the light, all these actions would be painful, and because of the glare he would be unable to see the things whose shadows he had formerly seen. What do you think he would say if someone told him that what he had seen before was foolishness, but that now he was closer to what is and was turned toward things that are more, and that he saw more correctly?

§516b–c (Seeing the Sun) · Plato, Republic VII, 516b–c
Manuscript
Greek

τελευταῖον δή, οἶμαι, τὸν ἥλιον, οὐκ ἐν ὕδασιν οὐδ’ ἐν ἀλλοτρίᾳ ἕδρᾳ φαντάσματα αὐτοῦ, ἀλλ’ αὐτὸν καθ’ αὑτὸν ἐν τῇ αὑτοῦ χώρᾳ δύναιτ’ ἂν κατιδεῖν καὶ θεάσασθαι.

English

Finally, I suppose, he would be able to look at the sun—not its image in water or some alien place, but the sun itself, in its own place—and be able to contemplate it. After this he would already be able to conclude about it that it provides the seasons and the years and governs everything in the visible realm, and is in some way the cause of all the things that he and his fellows used to see.

§519c–520a (The Duty to Return) · Plato, Republic VII, 519c–520a
Manuscript
Greek

ἡμέτερον δὴ ἔργον τῶν οἰκιστῶν τάς τε βελτίστας φύσεις ἀναγκάσαι ἀφικέσθαι πρὸς τὸ μάθημα.

English

It is the task of us who are founding this city, then, to compel the best natures to reach the study that we said before is the greatest—to see the Good—and to make the ascent. And when they have made it and have looked sufficiently, we must not allow them to do what they are allowed to do now. They must be made to go back down again among those prisoners and share their labours and honours, whether these are worth having or not.

Translator's Notes

  1. §514a–515a: The cave allegory maps directly onto Plato’s metaphysics: the shadows represent sensory appearances, the fire represents the visible sun, and the real sun outside the cave represents the Form of the Good. The journey upward is the education described in Books VI–VII—from opinion (doxa, δόξα) through mathematical reasoning (dianoia, διάνοια) to direct intellectual apprehension (noesis, νόησις) of the Forms.
  2. §519c–520a: The compulsion to return is essential to Plato’s political philosophy. Unlike the mystic who ascends and stays, the philosopher-king is forced back into the cave. Plato insists that this is just, because the city educated the philosopher and is owed service in return. The philosopher who governs does so as a debt, not from desire—and this reluctance is precisely what makes him fit to rule.
