Politics I.1-2: Man Is a Political Animal

Aristotle, 384-322 BCE

Introduction

The opening chapters of Aristotle’s Politics establish the foundation of his entire political philosophy: the state (polis, πόλις) exists by nature, not by convention. Against the Sophists who argued that political association is merely a useful arrangement, and against social contract theories avant la lettre, Aristotle insists that human beings are by nature political animals (zoon politikon, ζῷον πολιτικόν). The person who lives outside the city is either a beast or a god.

Aristotle builds his case teleologically. The household exists for daily needs, the village for more-than-daily needs, and the city for the sake of living well—not merely living. Because the city is the culmination of these natural associations, it is prior in nature to the individual, just as the whole body is prior to the hand. A hand severed from the body is a hand in name only. So too a human being severed from political community is human in name only. What distinguishes humans from other animals is speech (logos, λόγος), which exists not merely to signal pleasure and pain but to deliberate about justice and injustice—and this deliberation is the substance of politics.

§I.1 (1252a1–10) · Aristotle, Politics I.1, 1252a1–10 (Bekker pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

Ἐπειδὴ πᾶσαν πόλιν ὁρῶμεν κοινωνίαν τινὰ οὖσαν, πᾶσαν δὲ κοινωνίαν ἀγαθοῦ τινος ἕνεκεν συνεστηκυῖαν (τοῦ γὰρ εἶναι δοκοῦντος ἀγαθοῦ χάριν πάντες πράττουσι πάντα), δῆλον ὡς πᾶσαι μὲν ἀγαθοῦ τινος στοχάζονται, μάλιστα δὲ καὶ τοῦ κυριωτάτου πάντων ἡ πασῶν κυριωτάτη καὶ πάσας περιέχουσα τὰς ἄλλας. αὕτη δ’ ἐστὶν ἡ καλουμένη πόλις καὶ ἡ κοινωνία ἡ πολιτική.

English

Every city is a kind of association, and every association is established for the sake of some good; for human beings always act for the sake of what they believe to be good. If all associations aim at some good, then clearly the most sovereign and most comprehensive association, which embraces all the rest, aims at the most sovereign of all goods. This is the association called the city, or the political association.

§I.2 (1253a1–18) · Aristotle, Politics I.2, 1253a1–18 (Bekker pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

ἐκ τούτων οὖν φανερὸν ὅτι τῶν φύσει ἡ πόλις ἐστί, καὶ ὅτι ὁ ἄνθρωπος φύσει πολιτικὸν ζῷον, καὶ ὁ ἄπολις διὰ φύσιν καὶ οὐ διὰ τύχην ἤτοι φαῦλός ἐστιν ἢ κρείττων ἢ ἄνθρωπος.

English

From these things it is clear that the city belongs among the things that exist by nature, and that man is by nature a political animal (zoon politikon, ζῷον πολιτικόν). He who is without a city through nature rather than through chance is either a poor sort of being or a being superior to man. He is like the man reviled by Homer as “clanless, lawless, hearthless.” Such a person is by nature a lover of war, like an isolated piece in a game of draughts.

§I.2 (1253a7–18) · Aristotle, Politics I.2, 1253a7–18 (Bekker pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

διότι δὲ πολιτικὸν ὁ ἄνθρωπος ζῷον πάσης μελίττης καὶ παντὸς ἀγελαίου ζώου μᾶλλον, δῆλον. οὐθὲν γάρ, ὡς φαμέν, μάτην ἡ φύσις ποιεῖ, λόγον δὲ μόνον ἄνθρωπος ἔχει τῶν ζώων.

English

The reason why man is a political animal to a greater degree than any bee or any herd animal is clear. For nature, as we say, does nothing in vain, and man alone among animals has speech (logos, λόγος). The voice indeed is a sign of the painful and the pleasant, and for this reason it belongs to the other animals as well. But speech is for making clear what is advantageous and what is harmful, and therefore also what is just and what is unjust. For it is peculiar to man, in contrast to the other animals, that he alone has perception of good and evil, of just and unjust, and of similar qualities; and it is the sharing of these things that makes a household and a city.

Translator's Notes

  1. §1253a1–18: The phrase zoon politikon (ζῷον πολιτικόν) is usually translated “political animal,” but politikon derives from polis and means specifically “of or pertaining to the city.” Aristotle’s claim is not that humans are merely sociable—bees and ants are sociable—but that they are constituted for the particular form of association that is the city, which involves rational deliberation about justice. The distinction between voice (phone, φωνή) and speech (logos, λόγος) is central: animals communicate sensation, but only humans communicate about justice.
