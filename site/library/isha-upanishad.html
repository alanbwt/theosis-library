Isha Upanishad 1-3: The Self Is Everywhere

Upanishadic Sages, c. 800-200 BCE

Introduction

The Isha Upanishad (also spelled Isha Vasya) takes its name from its opening word: īśā, “by the Lord.” It is among the shortest and most concentrated of the Upanishads, and its opening verse may be the single most influential sentence in Hindu philosophy. In eighteen verses, it addresses the relationship between the one and the many, between action and renunciation, between the visible world and its invisible ground.

The first three verses, presented here, make a claim that is simultaneously world-affirming and world-transcending. Everything that exists is pervaded by the Lord. Therefore, enjoy what has been given to you—but do not covet, for nothing belongs to anyone. He who sees all beings in the Self, and the Self in all beings, never shrinks away from anything. For the theosis question, the Isha Upanishad provides a startlingly concise answer: the divine is not somewhere else. It is the fabric of what is right here.

§Verse 1 · Isha Upanishad, Verse 1
Manuscript
Sanskrit

īśā vāsyam idaṃ sarvaṃ yat kiṃ ca jagatyaṃ jagat | tena tyaktena bhuñjīthā mā gṛdhaḥ kasya svid dhanam

English

All this—whatever moves in this moving world—is pervaded by the Lord. Therefore find your enjoyment in renunciation. Do not covet the wealth of anyone.

§Verse 2 · Isha Upanishad, Verse 2
Manuscript
Sanskrit

kurvann eveh a karmāṇi jijīviṣec chataṃ samāḥ | evaṃ tvayi nānyathet o’sti na karma lipyate nare

English

By performing action in this world, one should wish to live a hundred years. Thus, and in no other way, action does not cling to you.

§Verse 3 · Isha Upanishad, Verse 3
Manuscript
Sanskrit

asuryā nāma te lokā andhena tamasāvṛtāḥ | tāṃs te pretyabhigacchanti ye ke cātmahano janāḥ

English

Those worlds, shrouded in blind darkness, are called “sunless.” To them, after death, go those who slay the Self.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Verse 1: The phrase īśā vāsyam idaṃ sarvam (“all this is pervaded by the Lord”) establishes panentheism—not pantheism (God is the world) but the claim that God dwells within everything without being exhausted by it. Gandhi considered this single verse sufficient as a scripture, saying that if the entire Hindu canon were lost and only this verse survived, Hinduism would survive with it.
  2. §Verse 2: This verse resolves an apparent contradiction. If everything is divine, why act at all? The Isha’s answer: act fully, live fully, but without attachment to results. This teaching anticipates the Bhagavad Gita’s central doctrine of niṣkāma karma (desireless action) by several centuries. Action performed without grasping does not bind; it liberates.
  3. §Verse 3: The phrase ātmahanaḥ (“slayers of the Self”) does not mean those who commit suicide. It means those who deny or ignore the true Self—who live as if they were merely bodies, merely egos, with no connection to the divine ground. To “slay the Self” is to live in ignorance of one’s own deepest nature. The punishment is not inflicted from outside; it is the natural consequence of self-ignorance: continued existence in darkness.
