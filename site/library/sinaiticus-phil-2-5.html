Philippians 2:5-11: The Kenosis Hymn

Πρὸς Φιλιππησίους
Paul (attributed), c. 50-65 AD
Codex Sinaiticus

Introduction

Philippians 2:5–11, the Carmen Christi, is the earliest and most influential christological hymn in the New Testament. Its structure traces a descent from divine equality to the lowest human condition—death on a cross—and then an exaltation above every name in the cosmos. The theological term kenosis (κένωσις, self-emptying) derives from this passage, and it has shaped every subsequent account of how divinity relates to humanity.

For the theosis tradition, the hymn provides the pattern in reverse. If Christ descended from the form of God into the form of a servant, then the path of human salvation is an ascent from servitude into the divine form. The Greek Fathers read the descent of Christ and the ascent of the believer as a single movement: God became human so that humans might become God. The text follows Codex Sinaiticus.

§2:5–8 · Codex Sinaiticus
Manuscript
Greek

τοῦτο φρονεῖτε ἐν ὑμῖν ὃ καὶ ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ · ὃς ἐν μορφῆ θεοῦ ὑπάρχων · οὐχ ἁρπαγμὸν ἡγήσατο τὸ εἶναι ἴσα θεῷ · ἀλλὰ ἑαυτὸν ἐκένωσεν · μορφὴν δούλου λαβών · ἐν ὁμοιώματι ἀνθρώπων γενόμενος · καὶ σχήματι εὑρεθεὶς ὡς ἄνθρωπος · ἐταπείνωσεν ἑαυτὸν · γενόμενος ὑπήκοος μέχρι θανάτου · θανάτου δὲ σταυροῦ ·

English

Have this mind among yourselves, which was also in Christ Jesus, who, being in the form (morphe, μορφή) of God, did not regard equality with God as something to be grasped, but emptied himself, taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of human beings. And being found in appearance as a human being, he humbled himself, becoming obedient to the point of death—even death on a cross.

English

Therefore God also highly exalted him and gave him the name that is above every name, so that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

Translator's Notes

  1. §2:6: The phrase en morphe theou hyparchon (ἐν μορφῆ θεοῦ ὑπάρχων) is one of the most debated expressions in Pauline theology. Morphe (μορφή) is not schema (outward appearance) but the essential form or nature of a thing. To exist in the morphe of God is to possess the divine nature. The word harpagmon (ἁρπαγμόν), rendered “something to be grasped,” has been translated variously as “a thing to be seized,” “a thing to be clung to,” or “an act of robbery.” The sense is that Christ possessed divine equality but did not treat it as a privilege to be exploited—he released it freely.
  2. §2:7: The verb ekenosen (ἐκένωσεν, “emptied”) gives the theological tradition its term kenosis. What exactly Christ emptied himself of has been debated for two millennia. The hymn does not say he ceased to be God; it says he took on the form of a servant. The descent–ascent pattern—from the form of God through the form of a servant to exaltation above every name—became the template for the theosis tradition’s understanding of human salvation as the inverse movement: from servitude through participation in Christ to glorification. Irenaeus, Athanasius, and Gregory of Nazianzus all read this passage as the archetype of the exchange formula: God descends so that humanity may ascend.
  3. §2:9–11: The exaltation of the humiliated Christ to universal lordship draws on Isaiah 45:23, where it is YHWH before whom every knee bows. The application of this text to Jesus is among the highest christological claims in the New Testament. For the theosis tradition, the critical point is that the one who is exalted is the one who descended—the movement is not from humanity to divinity by human effort but from divinity through humanity and back to divinity by divine initiative. Human participation in this movement is possible only because God initiated it.
