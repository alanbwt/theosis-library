Introduction

The Deor (c. 10th century) is an Old English poem in which a displaced court poet consoles himself by cataloguing famous sufferings from Germanic legend, each stanza concluding with the refrain: “That passed over; so may this.”

§Stanzas 1–2 and refrain · Deor, Exeter Book
Manuscript
Greek

Welund him be wurman wrǣces cunnade, ānhȳdig eorl earfoþa drēag; hæfde him tō gesīþhe sorge ond longaþ. Þæs ofereode, þisses swā mæg.

English

Weland knew the torment of exile; the strong-minded smith suffered hardships. He had for companions sorrow and longing, winter-cold wretchedness; often he found woe after Niðhad laid constraint upon him. That passed over; so may this. Beadohild was not so grieved by her brothers’ death as by her own condition, when she clearly perceived that she was with child. That passed over; so may this.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Stanzas 1–2: The refrain “þæs ofereode, þisses swā mæg” (“That passed over; so may this”) is the most famous expression of stoic consolation in Old English literature. It refuses both despair and false hope, offering instead the bare observation that suffering is impermanent.
