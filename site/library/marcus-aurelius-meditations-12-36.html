Introduction

The final entry of the Meditations (XII.36) is Marcus Aurelius’s last recorded reflection. He reminds himself that human life is brief, that the stage is small, and that the time to depart has come—so leave graciously, as the one who dismisses you is gracious.

§XII.36 · Marcus Aurelius, Meditations XII.36 (Dalfen, Teubner)
Manuscript
Greek

ἄνθρωπε, ἐπολιτεύσω ἐν τῇδε τῇ μεγάλῃ πόλει

English

Mortal man, you have been a citizen in this great city. What does it matter whether for five years or fifty? What is in accordance with the laws is equal for all. What is so dreadful, then, if it is not a tyrant or an unjust judge who sends you away, but nature who brought you in? It is as if a praetor who has engaged an actor should dismiss him from the stage. But I have not played all five acts. True, but in life three acts may be the whole drama. Depart then satisfied, for he who dismisses you is also satisfied.

Translator's Notes

  1. §XII.36: The theatrical metaphor—life as a play in which we are assigned our roles—runs through Stoic literature from Epictetus onward. Marcus’s final word is one of acceptance: the length of the performance matters less than the grace with which one exits.
