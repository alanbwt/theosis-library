The Cloud of Unknowing: He May Be Loved But Not Thought

Anonymous (Cloud of Unknowing), c. 14th c.

Introduction

The Cloud of Unknowing is an anonymous English mystical treatise written in the late fourteenth century, probably by a Carthusian monk. Its central teaching is that God cannot be known by the intellect but only by love. Between the soul and God there stands a “cloud of unknowing” that no amount of theological reasoning can penetrate. The contemplative must beat upon this cloud with “a sharp dart of longing love” and abandon all thought, all images, all concepts—even thoughts about God’s attributes.

The text stands in the apophatic tradition of Pseudo-Dionysius, whose Mystical Theology is explicitly cited. But where Dionysius writes in the language of Neoplatonic philosophy, the Cloud author writes in plain English, addressed to a young contemplative. The result is one of the most accessible introductions to apophatic mysticism ever written. Its declaration that God “may well be loved, but not thought” is the English equivalent of the Upanishadic “not this, not this” and the Taoist insistence that the Tao which can be spoken is not the eternal Tao.

§Chapter 3 (excerpt) · The Cloud of Unknowing, Chapter 3
Manuscript
Middle English

Lifte vp thin herte vnto god with a meek steryng of loue and mene him self and none of his goodes. And therto loke that thou loth to think on ought bot on him self so that nought worche in thi witte ne in thi wille bot only him self.

English

Lift up your heart unto God with a meek stirring of love; and mean himself, who made you, and bought you, and graciously called you to your state of life. And think no other thought of him. It suffices to have only a naked intent directed unto God, without any other cause than himself.

§Chapter 4 (excerpt 1) · The Cloud of Unknowing, Chapter 4
Manuscript
Middle English

For he may wel be louyd bot not thought. By loue may he be getyn and holden bot bi thought neither. And therfore though it be good sumtyme to think of the kyndnes and the worthines of god in specyal and though it be a light and a party of contemplacion neuertheles in this werke it schal be casten doun and coueryd with a cloude of forgetyng.

English

For he may well be loved, but not thought. By love he may be gotten and held, but by thought never. And therefore, though it is good sometimes to think of the kindness and the worthiness of God in particular, and though this is a light and a part of contemplation, nevertheless in this work it shall be cast down and covered with a cloud of forgetting.

§Chapter 4 (excerpt 2) · The Cloud of Unknowing, Chapter 4
Manuscript
Middle English

And step abouen it stalwortly bot listely with a deuoute and a plesing steryng of loue and fonde for to peerse that derknes abouen thee. And smyte apon that thicke cloude of vnknowyng with a scharpe darte of longing loue and go not thens for thing that befalleth.

English

And step above it stoutly but with a soft demeanour, with a devout and a pleasing stirring of love, and try to pierce that darkness above you. And smite upon that thick cloud of unknowing with a sharp dart of longing love; and do not leave that work for anything that may befall.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 4, excerpt 1: The declaration “by love he may be gotten and held, but by thought never” is the Cloud author’s central thesis. It reverses the Aristotelian-Thomistic priority of intellect over will. For Thomas Aquinas, the beatific vision is primarily an act of the intellect, seeing God’s essence. For the Cloud author, following Pseudo-Dionysius, the intellect must be left behind entirely, and only the will—expressed as love—can reach God. This debate between the primacy of intellect and the primacy of love runs through the entire history of Christian mysticism.
  2. §Chapter 4, excerpt 2: The “sharp dart of longing love” is the Cloud author’s most vivid image. The contemplative does not passively wait for God but actively strikes at the cloud with a single, focused impulse of desire. The violence of the metaphor is deliberate: contemplation is not peaceful withdrawal but an aggressive assault on the barriers between the soul and God. The dart is “sharp” because it must be concentrated in a single point—one word, one intention, stripped of all elaboration.
