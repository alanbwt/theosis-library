Introduction

Heraclitus of Ephesus (c. 535–475 BCE) opened his philosophical treatise with this fragment on the Logos, the rational principle underlying all things. Though all things happen according to this Logos, most people live as though they have a private understanding of their own.

§Fragment 1 · DK 22 B1 (Sextus Empiricus, Adversus Mathematicos VII.132)
Manuscript
Greek

τοῦ δὲ λόγου τοῦδὲ ἐόντος ἀεὶ ἀξύνετοι γίνονται ἄνθρωποι καὶ πρόσθεν ἢ ἀκοῦσαι καὶ ἀκούσαντες τὸ πρῶτον· γινομένων γὰρ πάντων κατὰ τὸν λόγον τόνδε ἀπείροισιν ἐοίκασι

English

Of this Logos, which holds always, people prove to be uncomprehending, both before they hear it and once they have heard it. For although all things come to pass in accordance with this Logos, they are like the inexperienced experiencing the words and deeds such as I set forth.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Fragment 1: The term Logos (λόγος) in Heraclitus carries multiple senses: “account,” “reason,” “proportion,” and “word.” It prefigures the Stoic concept of a rational cosmic principle and ultimately the Johannine use of the term.
