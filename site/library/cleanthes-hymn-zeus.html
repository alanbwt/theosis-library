Introduction

The Hymn to Zeus by Cleanthes (c. 330–230 BCE), the second head of the Stoic school, is the most complete surviving expression of Stoic theology in verse. It addresses Zeus as the rational principle governing the cosmos through the universal Logos.

§Lines 1–15 · SVF I.537 (Stobaeus, Eclogae I.1.12)
Manuscript
Greek

κύδιστ᾽ ἀθανάτων, πολυώνυμε, παγκρατὲς αἰεί, Ζεῦ, φύσεως ἀρχηγέ, νόμου μέτα πάντα κυβερνῶν

English

Most glorious of immortals, many-named, almighty forever, Zeus, ruler of nature, who governs all things with law—hail! For it is right that all mortals should address you. For we are your offspring, and alone of mortal creatures that live and creep upon the earth, we bear the likeness of God.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Lines 1–15: The phrase “we are your offspring” (ἐκ σοῦ γὰρ γένος ἐσμέν) was quoted by the apostle Paul in his Areopagus speech (Acts 17:28), establishing a direct link between Stoic theology and early Christian thought about humanity’s divine kinship.
