Introduction

Aristophanes’s speech in the Symposium (189c–193d) presents the myth of the double beings—originally whole creatures split in two by Zeus. Eros is the desire to reunite with one’s other half and restore the primordial wholeness.

§189d–190a · Plato, Symposium 189d5–190a7 (Burnet, OCT)
Manuscript
Greek

πρῶτον μὲν γὰρ τρία ἦν τὰ γένη τὰ τῶν ἀνθρώπων, οὐχ ὥσπερ νῦν δύο

English

First you must learn about human nature and what has happened to it. For our nature was not originally what it is now. In the beginning there were three kinds of human beings, not two as now—male, female, and a third combining both. Each person was round, with four hands and four legs and two faces upon a circular neck.

Translator's Notes

  1. §189d–190a: Aristophanes’s myth of original wholeness and subsequent division has been read as comic entertainment, as genuine Platonic theology, and as a critique of merely physical desire. The theme of reunification with a lost whole resonates with Neoplatonic accounts of the soul’s return to the One.
