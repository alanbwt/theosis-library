Homily on Genesis 1:26: Image and Likeness (First English Translation)

Severianus of Gabala, d. c. 408 AD
First English Translation

Introduction

Severianus of Gabala (d. c. 408) was a celebrated preacher in Constantinople, a rival of John Chrysostom, and a prolific homilist whose works have been largely neglected in modern scholarship. His Homily on Genesis 1:26 treats the creation of humanity “in the image and likeness” of God—the verse that grounds the entire patristic theology of theosis. This is the first English translation of this text. No rendering into English has previously been published.

Severianus distinguishes sharply between “image” (eikon, εἰκών) and “likeness” (homoiosis, ὁμοίωσις). The image is given at creation and cannot be lost; the likeness is a task, a destination, the full flowering of the divine potential implanted in human nature. This distinction—image as gift, likeness as goal—became a cornerstone of Eastern Christian anthropology.

§§1 · PG 56, col. 429A
Manuscript
Greek

Εἶπεν ὁ Θεός· Ποιήσωμεν ἄνθρωπον κατ’ εἰκόνα ἡμετέραν καὶ καθ’ ὁμοίωσιν. Ὄρα τὴν βουλήν. Οὐκ εἶπεν· Γενηθήτω ἄνθρωπος, ὡς εἶπεν· Γενηθήτω φῶς.

English

God said, “Let us make man in our image, after our likeness.” Observe the deliberation. He did not say “Let there be man,” as he said “Let there be light.” For light was made by command, but man was made by counsel. The creation of the world was a work of power; the creation of man was a work of wisdom and love.

§§2 · PG 56, col. 429B–C
Manuscript
Greek

Τί ἐστιν ἡ εἰκών, καὶ τί ἐστιν ἡ ὁμοίωσις; Εἰκών ἐστιν ἡ λογικὴ ψυχή, ἡ τοῦ νοεῖν δύναμις, ἡ τῆς προαιρέσεως ἐξουσία. ῊΗ δὲ ὁμοίωσις ἡ τῆς ἀρετῆς τελείωσις.

English

What is the image, and what is the likeness? The image is the rational soul, the capacity for thought, the power of free choice. These belong to man by nature and cannot be destroyed—not even by sin. But the likeness is the perfection of virtue, the attainment of God-likeness through righteous living. The image is the foundation; the likeness is the building that rises upon it.

§§4 · PG 56, col. 432A
Manuscript
Greek

Διὰ τί εἶπεν· Κατ’ εἰκόνα ἡμετέραν, καὶ οὐκ εἶπεν· Κατ’ εἰκόνα ἐμήν; Ὄτι ἡ Τριὰς ὁμοῦ ἐνεργεῖ.

English

Why did God say “in our image” and not “in my image”? Because the Trinity acts together. The Father wills, the Son creates, the Spirit perfects. Man is the work of the undivided Trinity, and he bears the stamp of the Three in his very being: mind, word, and spirit—one nature in three powers, an image of the one God in three persons.

§§6 · PG 56, col. 433B
Manuscript
Greek

ῊΗ εἰκών ἐδόθη· ἡ ὁμοίωσις προετέθη ὡς σκοπός. Δυνατούς ἡμᾶς ἐποίησεν ὁ Θεὸς γενέσθαι ὅμοιοι αὐτῷ, οὐκ ἠνάγκασεν.

English

The image was given; the likeness was set before us as a goal. God made us capable of becoming like him, but he did not compel us. He planted the seed; we must cultivate the harvest. This is why Scripture says “in our image” (what is given) and “after our likeness” (what is to be attained). Theosis is not a violation of nature but its fulfilment.

Translator's Notes

  1. §§2: The image/likeness distinction originates with Irenaeus (Against Heresies V.6.1) and was developed extensively in the Alexandrian tradition by Clement and Origen. Severianus’s formulation—image as inalienable gift, likeness as task—became the standard Eastern Christian reading. The Western tradition, following Augustine, tended to collapse the two terms.
  2. §§6: The final sentence—“Theosis is not a violation of nature but its fulfilment”—represents a key principle of patristic anthropology. Deification does not destroy or replace human nature; it brings human nature to the end for which it was created. This stands in contrast to Gnostic systems where salvation requires escape from the material world and its creator.
