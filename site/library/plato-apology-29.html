Apology 29-30: The Unexamined Life Is Not Worth Living

Plato, c. 428-348 BCE

Introduction

The Apology is Plato’s account of Socrates’ speech at his trial in 399 BCE, where he was charged with impiety and corrupting the youth of Athens. The passage at 29c–30b contains Socrates’ most defiant statement: even if the jury were to acquit him on condition that he cease philosophizing, he would refuse. He will obey God rather than Athens. The “unexamined life is not worth living” is not a platitude but a man’s explanation of why he would rather die than stop asking questions.

The political dimension of the Apology is as important as the philosophical. Athens was a democracy, and Socrates was convicted by a democratic jury of 501 citizens. The trial is therefore not merely a conflict between one man and his accusers but between philosophy and democratic politics. Democracy demands that the majority rules; Socrates insists that truth is not decided by vote. The execution of Socrates became the founding trauma of Western philosophy—the event that convinced Plato that democracy, left unchecked by wisdom, will destroy the best it produces.

§29c–d (The Refusal to Stop Philosophizing) · Plato, Apology 29c–d (Stephanus pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ οὖν με τούτοις ἀφίοιτε ἐπὶ τούτοις, εἴποιμ’ ἂν ὑμῖν ὅτι ἐγὼ ὑμᾶς, ὦ ἄνδρες Ἀθηναῖοι, ἀσπάζομαι μὲν καὶ φιλῶ, πείσομαι δὲ μᾶλλον τῷ θεῷ ἢ ὑμῖν, καὶ ἕωσπερ ἂν ἐμπνέω καὶ οἷός τε ὦ, οὐ μὴ παύσομαι φιλοσοφῶν.

English

If you said to me, “Socrates, this time we will not listen to Anytus; we will let you go, but on this condition: that you no longer spend your time in this investigation or in philosophy, and if you are caught doing so you will die”—if, as I say, you were to let me go on these terms, I would reply: “Men of Athens, I respect and love you, but I will obey the god rather than you. As long as I breathe and am able, I will not cease philosophizing, exhorting you and pointing out the truth to any one of you I happen to meet.”

§29d–30a (The Gadfly) · Plato, Apology 29d–30a (Stephanus pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

οὐδὲν γὰρ ἄλλο πράττων ἐγὼ περιέρχομαι ἢ πείθων ὑμῶν καὶ νεωτέρους καὶ πρεσβυτέρους μήτε σωμάτων ἐπιμελεῖσθαι μήτε χρημάτων πρότερον μηδὲ οὕτω σφόδρα ὡς τῆς ψυχῆς ὅπως ὡς ἀρίστη ἔσται.

English

“I go around doing nothing but persuading both young and old among you not to care for your bodies or your wealth in preference to or as strongly as for the best possible state of your soul, saying: ‘Wealth does not bring virtue, but virtue brings wealth and every other good thing to man, both individually and to the state.’ If by saying this I am corrupting the young, these things must be harmful. But if anyone says that I say other than these things, he speaks falsely. On this basis, men of Athens, I would say: either listen to Anytus or not, either acquit me or not, knowing that I will not alter my ways, even if I am to die many times over.”

§30a–b (The Unexamined Life) · Plato, Apology 30a–b (Stephanus pagination)
Manuscript
Greek

οἷον δὴ ἐγὼ τυγχάνω ὢν τοιοῦτος οἷος ὑπὸ τοῦ θεοῦ τῇ πόλει δεδόσθαι — ὁ δὲ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς ἀνθρώπῳ.

English

“For I am that gadfly which God has attached to the state, and all day long and in all places I am always fastening upon you, arousing and persuading and reproaching every one of you. You will not easily find another like me, and therefore I would advise you to spare me. If you strike me down, as Anytus advises, then you will sleep on for the remainder of your lives, unless God in his care for you sends you another gadfly. The unexamined life is not worth living for a human being.”

Translator's Notes

  1. §29c–d: The phrase “I will obey the god rather than you” (πείσομαι δὲ μᾶλλον τῷ θεῷ ἢ ὑμῖν) establishes a principle of civil disobedience that would echo through Western thought—from the Apostles’ “we must obey God rather than men” (Acts 5:29) to Thoreau and Martin Luther King Jr. Socrates does not deny the authority of the state; he subordinates it to a higher obligation.
  2. §30a–b: The famous phrase “the unexamined life is not worth living” (ὁ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς ἀνθρώπῳ) actually appears later in the Apology, at 38a, during Socrates’ remarks after the guilty verdict. It is included here because the argument of 29c–30b is inseparable from that conclusion. The gadfly metaphor reveals Socrates’ understanding of his political role: he is not a revolutionary but an irritant, a persistent questioner whose function is to keep the democratic body politic from falling asleep.
