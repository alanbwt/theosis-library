Introduction

Paracelsus's Archidoxis (c. 1526) lays out his revolutionary medical and alchemical philosophy, replacing the Galenic system with a new framework based on the three principles of salt, sulfur, and mercury. It helped inaugurate the chemical revolution in medicine.

Translation

§Book 1: The Three Principles · Archidoxis, Book 1

Know then that all things of this world are composed of three substances, and from these three all things proceed. They are Sulfur, Mercury, and Salt. When these three are united, they form a body. When they separate, the body is destroyed. Sulfur is that which burns. Mercury is that which smokes and evaporates. Salt is that which remains as ash. These are not the common sulfur, mercury, and salt of the merchants, but the philosophical principles hidden within all substances. He who understands these three understands the foundation of all natural knowledge.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Book 1: Paracelsus's three principles (the tria prima) replaced the Aristotelian four elements as the fundamental explanatory framework for chemistry and medicine. The insistence that these are "philosophical" rather than literal substances reflects the alchemical distinction between inner essences and outer appearances. This framework, though ultimately superseded, was a crucial step toward modern chemistry.
