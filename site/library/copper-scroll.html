The Copper Scroll: A Treasure Map from Qumran

        
The Copper Scroll: A Treasure Map from Qumran

        
Unknown Jewish Author, c. 100 AD

        
Jordan Archaeological Museum, Amman (3Q15)

        
      


      

        
Introduction

        
The Copper Scroll (3Q15), discovered in Cave 3 at Qumran in 1952, is unique among the Dead Sea Scrolls. It was not a literary or religious text but an inventory — a list of 64 hiding places containing vast quantities of gold, silver, and sacred vessels, totaling an estimated 65 tons of precious metal. The scroll was so corroded that it had to be cut into strips to be read.
Despite decades of searching, none of the treasure has ever been found. Scholars debate whether the treasure was real (perhaps the Temple treasury hidden before the Roman destruction of 70 AD), symbolic, or fictional. The locations are described with enough specificity to suggest real places, but not enough for modern identification.

Translation

        
        

          §1 · copper-scroll
          
In the ruin which is in the valley of Achor, under the steps leading to the east, forty cubits: a chest of silver and its vessels — a weight of seventeen talents. In the sepulchral monument, in the third course of stones: one hundred gold bars. In the big cistern of the courtyard of the peristyle, in a hollow in the floor covered with sediment, in front of the upper opening: nine hundred talents. In the hill of Kohlit: vessels of tribute of the Lord of the peoples, and sacred vestments. Its total, and the seventh treasure: a tithe of second tithe rendered unfit. Its opening lies on the edge of the aqueduct, six cubits toward the immersion pool.
