Contra Celsum III.28: Why God Became Man

Origen of Alexandria, c. 185–253 AD

Introduction

In Contra Celsum III.28, Origen of Alexandria confronts the pagan philosopher Celsus’s central objection to Christianity: the claim that God became man is absurd and degrading to the divine nature. Celsus argued that for an immutable, perfect God to descend into human flesh would require a “change” (metabole) in the divine nature—and change, in Greek philosophy, means deterioration. Origen’s response is one of the most sophisticated defences of the Incarnation in early Christian literature: God’s descent does not diminish God, because the divine nature is not altered by what it touches.

Origen wrote Contra Celsum around 248 CE, making it one of the earliest sustained intellectual engagements between Christianity and classical philosophy. The Greek text survives in a single manuscript tradition. For the theosis question, Origen’s argument is foundational: if God can enter humanity without being diminished, then the door is open for humanity to participate in divinity without divinity being compromised.

§III.28a · Contra Celsum III.28 (Koetschau, GCS)
Manuscript
Greek

οὐ μεταβολὴν ὑπέμεινεν ἡ θεία φύσις αὐτοῦ σῶμα ἀνθρώπινον καὶ ψυχὴν ἀνθρωπίνην ἀναλαβοῦσα · μένουσα δὲ ἐν τῇ ἑαυτῆς οὐσίᾳ κατελήλυθεν εἰς τὰ ἀνθρώπινα

English

The divine nature did not change when it assumed a human body and a human soul. Remaining in its own essence unchanged, it descended to human affairs out of its love for humankind. We do not say that God suffered change or transformation, for the divine Word remained divine Word in essence.

§III.28b · Contra Celsum III.28 (Koetschau, GCS)
Manuscript
Greek

εἰ γὰρ καὶ κατὰ τοὺς Ἕλληνας ἡ πρόνοια τοῦ θεοῦ διήκει διὰ πάντων οὐ μολυνομένη ὑπὸ οὐδενός

English

For if, as even the Greeks admit, the providence of God pervades all things without being defiled by any, why should it be thought incredible that the divine nature touched a human body and human soul without thereby being contaminated? Just as the sun’s rays touch filth without being polluted, so the divine nature assumed humanity without undergoing any defilement.

Translator's Notes

  1. §III.28a: Origen’s key move is to deny that incarnation requires metabole (change/alteration) in the divine nature. Celsus assumed that contact with matter must degrade the divine—a standard Platonic position. Origen counters by distinguishing between the divine ousia (essence/substance) and divine activity. The essence remains unchanged; the activity extends into the human sphere. This distinction anticipates the later Palamite distinction between divine essence and divine energies by over a millennium.
  2. §III.28b: The sun analogy—light touches filth without being polluted—became one of the standard metaphors of patristic theology. Athanasius, the Cappadocians, and John of Damascus all use versions of it. The argument is designed to meet the pagan philosopher on his own ground: even Stoic physics teaches that the divine pneuma pervades all matter without being affected by it. If Celsus accepts this as a philosophical principle, he has no basis for objecting to the Incarnation as a theological fact.
