Introduction

Chapter 13 of Hobbes's Leviathan (1651) describes the "state of nature"—the condition of humanity without government. It contains the famous characterization of natural life as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

Translation

§Chapter 13: Of the Natural Condition of Mankind · Leviathan, Part 1, Chapter 13

Nature hath made men so equal in the faculties of body and mind as that, though there be found one man sometimes manifestly stronger in body or of quicker mind than another, yet when all is reckoned together the difference between man and man is not so considerable as that one man can thereupon claim to himself any benefit to which another may not pretend as well as he. From this equality of ability ariseth equality of hope in the attaining of our ends. And therefore if any two men desire the same thing, which nevertheless they cannot both enjoy, they become enemies. Hereby it is manifest that during the time men live without a common power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called war; and such a war as is of every man against every man. In such condition there is no place for industry, because the fruit thereof is uncertain, and consequently no culture of the earth, no arts, no letters, no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.

Translator's Notes

  1. §Chapter 13: Hobbes's state of nature is not a historical claim but a thought experiment: what would human life be like without political authority? His answer—universal war—justifies absolute sovereignty as the rational solution to collective insecurity. This argument, written during the English Civil War, remains the starting point for all modern political philosophy and is directly challenged by Locke and Rousseau.
